# Kavanagh et al. 2026 — UPF and Childhood Behavioral Outcomes

## Citation
Kavanagh ME, Chen ZH, Tamana SK, et al. Ultraprocessed Food Consumption and Behavioral Outcomes in Canadian Children. *JAMA Netw Open*. 2026;9(3):eXXXXXX. doi:10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2026.xxxx

## Source
- **Journal:** JAMA Network Open
- **Date:** March 3, 2026
- **Study:** Canadian children cohort

---

## Key Findings

### Main Result
High UPF consumption at age 3 → more behavioral problems at age 5.

### Behavioral Issues Observed
- **Internalizing:** Anxiety, fear, depressive mood
- **Externalizing:** Aggression, hyperactivity

### Top UPF Contributors
- Sweets and desserts (cookies, cakes, donuts, chocolates, ice cream)
- Bread and cereals (packaged toast bread, breakfast cereals, cereal bars, crackers)
- Processed animal foods (cold cuts, sausages, chicken nuggets, processed burgers)
- Ready meals (frozen pizzas, instant pasta, microwave meals)
- Sugary drinks and artificial sweeteners (sodas, energy drinks, iced tea, juices with sugar)
- Sauces and dressings (ketchup, mayonnaise, BBQ sauce, salad dressings, pasta sauces)
- Salty snacks (chips, cheese puffs, nachos, flavored crackers)
- Plant-based substitutes (plant "burgers", plant sausages, plant cheeses with additives)

### Dose-Response
- Even **10% substitution** of UPF with minimally processed foods improved behavior scores
- Small dietary changes have measurable impact

### Clinical Implications
- UPF restriction important for child mental, emotional, and behavioral development
- Early dietary patterns shape later outcomes
- Modifiable risk factor for pediatric mental health
