# Clinical Assessment of B12 Status — MMA

## Core Claim
**Methylmalonic acid (MMA) is the most sensitive and specific functional marker of B12 deficiency, reflecting mitochondrial B12 (adenosylcobalamin) status, while homocysteine is elevated in both B12 and folate deficiency** — serum B12 alone is a poor indicator of functional status.

## Evidence

### Biomarker Comparison
| Marker | Reference | Specificity | Sensitivity |
|--------|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| **Serum B12** | 200-900 pg/mL | Poor | Late marker |
| **MMA** | <0.4 μmol/L | **Specific for B12** | **Most sensitive** |
| **Homocysteine** | <10 μmol/L | Non-specific (B12 or folate) | Moderate |

### Interpretation Patterns
| Pattern | Interpretation |
|---------|----------------|
| **Low B12 + High MMA** | B12 deficiency |
| **Normal B12 + High MMA** | Functional/tissue deficiency |
| **High homocysteine only** | Likely folate deficiency |
| **MMA + homocysteine elevated** | B12 deficiency |

### Why MMA is Superior
- **Functional marker:** Reflects actual enzyme (mutase) activity
- **Early indicator:** Elevates before serum B12 drops
- **Tissue specific:** Indicates mitochondrial B12 status
- **Short half-life:** Responds quickly to supplementation

## Clinical Implication
- **Always check MMA** when assessing B12 status, especially with normal serum B12
- **Functional deficiency** (normal serum B12, high MMA) is common and treatable
- **METHAP protocol:** Uses high-dose B12; MMA monitoring confirms adequacy
- **Optimal target:** MMA <0.27 μmol/L (lower than standard reference)
