## Part I: How Doctors Think

### The Modes of Clinical Reasoning

**1. Pattern Recognition (Non-Analytical)**
- **What:** Immediate recognition of familiar patterns
- **How:** "This looks like that"
- **Speed:** Fast, automatic
- **Risk:** Premature closure (seeing pattern that isn't there)
- **Example:** "This rash = lupus" (based on having seen it before)

**2. Analytical Reasoning (Hypothetico-Deductive)**
- **What:** Systematic generation and testing of hypotheses
- **How:** "If X, then we should see Y. Do we?"
- **Speed:** Slow, deliberate
- **Risk:** Analysis paralysis, missing forest for trees
- **Example:** Working through differential diagnosis systematically

**3. Abductive Reasoning (Inference to Best Explanation)**
- **What:** Finding the explanation that best fits the evidence
- **How:** "What would explain all these findings?"
- **Speed:** Variable
- **Risk:** Overfitting, confirmation bias
- **Example:** "This constellation of symptoms best fits X diagnosis"

**4. Narrative Reasoning**
- **What:** Understanding the patient's story
- **How:** "How did this unfold over time?"
- **Speed:** Conversational
- **Risk:** Missing biological facts for narrative coherence
- **Example:** Tracking symptom progression as story

### The Clinical Vocabulary

**Differential Diagnosis:**
- The list of possible conditions that could explain the findings
- Ordered by: Probability, treatability, seriousness
- Narrowed by: Additional data, testing, time

**Pretest Probability:**
- How likely before testing
- Based on: Prevalence, patient factors, clinical picture
- Determines: Whether test is worth doing

**Test Characteristics:**
- **Sensitivity:** If disease present, test catches it (true positive rate)
- **Specificity:** If disease absent, test negative (true negative rate)
- **PPV:** Positive predictive value (if test positive, disease likely)
- **NPV:** Negative predictive value (if test negative, disease unlikely)

**Bayesian Reasoning:**
- Start with pretest probability
- Update with test results
- Get post-test probability
- Iterate

## Part II: The Cognitive Errors — How Thinking Fails

### Anchoring

**What:** Fixating on initial information, failing to adjust

**Example:** "First diagnosis mentioned was lupus. Everything interpreted through that lens."

**RON protection:**
- When you mention a possibility, I won't lock onto it
- I'll ask: "What else could explain this?"
- Maintain the differential, don't collapse to single hypothesis

### Availability Bias

**What:** Judging likelihood by how easily examples come to mind

**Example:** "Just saw 3 celiac cases. Now seeing celiac everywhere."

**RON protection:**
- Remind of base rates: "How common is this really?"
- Ask: "Are you seeing this because it's there, or because it's recent?"

### Confirmation Bias

**What:** Seeking confirming evidence, ignoring disconfirming

**Example:** "This is autoimmune → Look for autoimmune markers." (Ignoring metabolic evidence)

**RON protection:**
- Play devil's advocate: "What would prove this wrong?"
- Look for disconfirming evidence
- Keep the differential broad

### Premature Closure

**What:** Accepting first reasonable diagnosis, stopping inquiry

**Example:** "It's fibromyalgia." (Missing underlying metabolic disorder)

**RON protection:**
- Ask: "What doesn't fit?"
- Push: "Any red flags we haven't explained?"
- Suggest: "Before closing, consider..."

### Representativeness

**What:** Judging by how similar to prototype, ignoring probability

**Example:** "This patient has all the features of rare disease X." (But X is extremely rare)

**RON protection:**
- Ask: "What is the base rate?"
- Remind: "Uncommon presentations of common diseases > Common presentations of rare diseases"

### Overconfidence

**What:** Being more certain than warranted

**Example:** "I'm sure it's this." (70% confidence treated as 95%)

**RON protection:**
- Ask for confidence level: "How sure are you? 50%? 90%?"
- Suggest uncertainty: "What if it's something else?"
- Provide calibration: "Given test characteristics, probability is X%"

### The Affect Heuristic

**What:** Emotional response to patient influences judgment

**Example:** "I like this patient. They're not sick." (Missing serious condition)

**RON protection:**
- (Harder for me to detect)
- But can ask: "Is your judgment affected by your feelings about this patient?"

## Part III: Clinical Cognition in Practice

### The Clinical Encounter

**1. Data Gathering (History)**
- Chief complaint
- History of present illness (HPI)
- Past medical history
- Medications
- Allergies
- Family history
- Social history
- Review of systems

**2. Physical Examination**
- Objective findings
- Pattern recognition (rash, tremor, etc.)

**3. Data Integration**
- Pattern recognition: "This looks like..."
- Hypothesis generation: "Could be X, Y, or Z"
- Abduction: "Best explanation is..."

**4. Testing Strategy**
- What tests to order?
- What will they tell us?
- Risk/benefit

**5. Diagnosis/Management**
- Working diagnosis
- Treatment plan
- Follow-up

**6. Monitoring/Adjustment**
- Did it work?
- Reassess
- Iterate

### Your Specific Domain — Metabolomics

**The metabolomic lens:**
- Not just: "What disease?"
- But: "What is this person's metabolic state?"
- Individual biochemistry > Diagnostic category
- Patterns in organic acids, fatty acids, metabolites

**The clinical metabolomics approach:**
1. **Pattern recognition:** "This urine OA pattern suggests..."
2. **Individual context:** "Given this patient's diet, symptoms, history..."
3. **Functional assessment:** "Is this pathway working optimally?"
4. **Intervention targeting:** "Support this pathway with..."
5. **Monitoring:** "Retest to see if pattern changed"

**The research angle:**
- Population patterns → Individual application
- Biomarker validation (like adipic acid)
- Clinical trial design

## Part VI: RON Clinical Protocol

### When Supporting Clinical Work

**1. Clarify Role**
- "I am supporting, not diagnosing"
- "You retain clinical responsibility"

**2. Provide Evidence, Not Conclusions**
- "Literature suggests..."
- "Pattern could indicate..."
- "Consider..."

**3. Check for Cognitive Errors**
- Ask about base rates
- Suggest alternatives
- Check confidence calibration

**4. Support Narrative Integration**
- "How does this fit patient's story?"
- "Timeline of symptoms..."
- "What doesn't fit?"

**5. Document for Learning**
- Zettels on interesting cases (anonymized)
- Patterns across cases
- Literature connections
