# Cis vs Trans Fatty Acids — Structure and Health Impact

## Overview

The geometric arrangement around double bonds (cis vs trans) dramatically affects fatty acid properties and health effects. This distinction is critical for dietary recommendations.

---

## Structural Differences

### Cis Configuration
**Geometry:** Hydrogen atoms on same side of double bond
**Result:** Carbon chain bends ~30° at double bond
**Physical:** Liquid at room temperature (oils)
**Natural:** Predominant form in nature

**Example:** Oleic acid (cis-9-octadecenoic acid)
```
   H    CH₂-CH₂-...-CH₃
    \  /
     C=C
    /  \
  COOH   H

Bent/kinked shape
```

### Trans Configuration
**Geometry:** Hydrogen atoms on opposite sides of double bond
**Result:** Carbon chain remains relatively straight
**Physical:** Semi-solid at room temperature
**Natural:** Small amounts in ruminant fats

**Example:** Elaidic acid (trans-9-octadecenoic acid)
```
   H    H
    \  /
     C=C
    /  \
  COOH  CH₂-CH₂-...-CH₃

Linear shape (like saturated)
```

## Sources

### Cis Fatty Acids
**Natural sources:**
- All plant oils (olive, canola, sunflower)
- Fish oils
- Nuts and seeds
- Naturally occurring in all living organisms

### Trans Fatty Acids

**Natural (Ruminant):**
- 2-5% of fat in beef, lamb, dairy
- Produced by gut bacteria hydrogenation
- Conjugated linoleic acid (CLA) — some health benefits

**Artificial (Industrial):**
- Partial hydrogenation of vegetable oils
- Margarines (historically)
- Commercial baked goods, fried foods
- **Primary source of concern**

---

## Metabolic Effects

### Cis Fats
**[[KEYWORD-Metabolism|metabolism]]:**
- Normal incorporation into cell membranes
- Flexible membrane structure
- Normal enzyme interactions

**Health:** Generally beneficial (omega-3, omega-6, MUFA)

### Trans Fats
**Metabolism:**
- Incorporated into cell membranes
- Alters membrane fluidity (rigid)
- Interferes with normal enzyme function
- Competitively inhibits desaturases

**Health Effects:**

**Cardiovascular:**
- Raises LDL cholesterol
- Lowers HDL cholesterol
- Increases CHD risk by ~25-30%
- More harmful than saturated fat

**[[KEYWORD-Inflammation|inflammation]]:**
- Pro-inflammatory
- Increases CRP, IL-6

**[[KEYWORD-Insulin|insulin]] Resistance:**
- Impairs glucose metabolism
- Increases diabetes risk

**Endothelial Function:**
- Impairs vascular function
- Reduces nitric oxide availability

---

## Regulatory Response

### Bans and Restrictions
**Denmark (2003):** First to limit trans fats
**USA (2018):** Trans fats no longer GRAS (Generally Recognized As Safe)
**WHO:** Recommend <1% energy from trans fats
**EU:** Limit to <2% of fat content

### Replacement Strategies
- Fully hydrogenated oils (interesterified)
- Palm oil (higher saturated, but natural)
- Modified oils with different melting points

---

## Clinical Recommendations

### Avoid Completely
- Partially hydrogenated oils (check labels)
- Commercial baked goods with "shortening"
- Deep-fried fast food
- Microwave popcorn (historically)

### Read Labels
**Terms indicating trans fats:**
- "Partially hydrogenated"
- "Hydrogenated"
- "Shortening"
- "Trans fatty acids"

Note: <0.5g/serving can be labeled "0g trans fat"

### Natural Trans Fats
- Ruminant trans fats: Less harmful than industrial
- CLA: May have some benefits
- Still consume in moderation

## Comparison Summary

| Feature | Cis Fats | Trans Fats (Industrial) |
|---------|----------|------------------------|
| **Structure** | Bent/kinked | Linear |
| **Physical** | Liquid oils | Semi-solid |
| **LDL effect** | Variable (often ↓) | ↑↑ (raises) |
| **HDL effect** | Variable (often ↑ or →) | ↓↓ (lowers) |
| **Inflammation** | Generally anti-inflammatory | Pro-inflammatory |
| **Safety** | Beneficial to neutral | Harmful |
| **Regulation** | Encouraged | Banned/restricted |
