# Prolactinoma — Diagnosis,  Effects, and Management

## Overview

Prolactinoma is the most common functioning pituitary adenoma, causing hyperprolactinemia and secondary hypogonadism through inhibition of GnRH pulsatility.

## Pathophysiology

###  of Hypogonadism

```
Prolactinoma → Excess prolactin
    ↓
Inhibits hypothalamic GnRH pulsatility
    ↓
Reduced LH and FSH secretion
    ↓
Testicular stimulation decreases
    ↓
Low testosterone + impaired spermatogenesis
    ↓
Hypogonadism + Infertility
```

**Direct Effects:**
- Prolactin inhibits GnRH neurons
- Dopamine normally inhibits prolactin (dopamine antagonist effect reversed)

## Clinical Presentation

### In Men (Often Delayed Diagnosis)

**Symptoms:**
| System | Manifestation | Mechanism |
|--------|--------------|-----------|
| **Sexual** | Decreased libido, erectile dysfunction | Low testosterone |
| **Reproductive** | Infertility, reduced ejaculate volume | Impaired spermatogenesis |
| **Physical** | Gynecomastia (rare), reduced body hair | Low testosterone/estradiol imbalance |
| **Mass effects** | Headaches, visual field defects (bitemporal hemianopia) | Tumor compression of optic chiasm |
| **Rare** | Galactorrhea (<5% of men) | Breast tissue less responsive |

**Why Delayed in Men:**
- Symptoms nonspecific (fatigue, low libido common)
- No menstrual irregularity as warning sign
- Often large (macroadenoma) at diagnosis

### In Women (For Comparison)

- **Classic triad:** Amenorrhea/oligomenorrhea, galactorrhea, infertility
- **Earlier diagnosis:** Menstrual changes prompt evaluation
- **Often microadenomas:** Smaller at presentation

## Diagnosis

### Laboratory

**Prolactin Level:**
| Level (ng/mL) | Interpretation |
|---------------|----------------|
| **<20** | Normal |
| **20-100** | Mild elevation (drugs, stress, hypothyroidism) |
| **100-200** | Moderate elevation (prolactinoma possible) |
| **>200** | Highly suggestive of prolactinoma |
| **>500** | Almost diagnostic |

**Hook Effect (False Low):**
- Very high prolactin (>1000) can overwhelm assay
- Reported as falsely normal/low
- **Solution:** Request dilution study if large tumor with "normal" prolactin

**Additional Labs:**
- **Testosterone:** Low in men
- **LH/FSH:** Low or inappropriately normal
- **TSH:** Rule out primary hypothyroidism (can elevate prolactin)
- **Pregnancy test:** Rule out in women
- **Creatinine:** Chronic kidney disease elevates prolactin

### Imaging

**MRI Pituitary with Contrast:**
- **Gold standard**
- Microadenoma: <10 mm
- Macroadenoma: ≥10 mm
- Assess for compression of optic chiasm
- Cavernous sinus invasion

**CT:** Only if MRI contraindicated

### Differential Diagnosis of Hyperprolactinemia

**Physiologic:**
- Pregnancy
- Lactation
- Stress, exercise
- Sleep
- Nipple stimulation

**Pharmacologic:**
- Antipsychotics (dopamine antagonists)
- Antidepressants (SSRIs, TCAs)
- Antihypertensives (methyldopa, verapamil)
- Opioids
- Metoclopramide
- Estrogens

**Pathologic:**
- Prolactinoma (most common pathologic)
- Other pituitary tumors (stalk effect)
- Hypothyroidism
- Chronic kidney disease
- Cirrhosis
- Chest wall lesions/herpes zoster
- Epileptic seizures

**Stalk Effect:**
- Non-functioning pituitary tumors compress stalk
- Disrupt dopamine delivery to pituitary
- Elevates prolactin (usually <100 ng/mL)

## Classification

### By Size

**Microprolactinoma:**
- <10 mm
- 60-70% of prolactinomas
- Rarely grow
- Medical therapy usually sufficient

**Macroprolactinoma:**
- ≥10 mm
- More common in men
- Can compress optic chiasm
- May require surgery + medical therapy

### Giant Prolactinoma
- >40 mm
- Rare
- Aggressive behavior
- Often invasive

## Treatment

### Goals

1. Normalize prolactin
2. Restore gonadal function
3. Reduce tumor size
4. Prevent recurrence
5. Preserve pituitary function

### Medical Therapy — First Line

**Dopamine Agonists:**

| Drug | Dosing | Side Effects | Notes |
|------|--------|--------------|-------|
| **Cabergoline** | 0.25-1 mg twice weekly | Nausea, dizziness, fatigue, valvulopathy (rare at doses used) | Preferred; more effective, better tolerated, weekly dosing |
| **Bromocriptine** | 1.25-2.5 mg 2-3x daily | More nausea, dizziness, hypotension | Older; use if cabergoline contraindicated |
| **Quinagolide** | 75-150 mcg daily | Similar to cabergoline | Not available in all countries |

**Response:**
- Prolactin normalizes: 80-90% with cabergoline
- Tumor shrinkage: 50-70% (macroadenomas)
- Visual improvement: 60-80% if impaired

**Monitoring:**
- Prolactin at 1 month, then every 3 months initially
- MRI at 6-12 months, then annually if stable

### Surgery

**Indications:**
- Intolerance/resistance to dopamine agonists
- Cerebrospinal fluid rhinorrhea (rare complication)
- Acute apoplexy with vision loss
- Patient preference

**Approach:**
- Transsphenoidal (through nose)
- Microscopic or endoscopic
- Experienced neurosurgeon essential

**Outcomes:**
- Microadenomas: 60-80% cure rate
- Macroadenomas: 20-50% cure rate
- Often need adjuvant medical therapy

### Radiation

**Indications (Rare):**
- Resistant to medical and surgical therapy
- Malignant prolactinoma (very rare)

**Risks:**
- Hypopituitarism (50-60%)
- Secondary malignancies
- Cognitive effects

## Special Considerations

### Fertility

**Restoration:**
- Dopamine agonists restore fertility in 70-80%
- Must counsel about pregnancy risk
- Stop medication when pregnancy confirmed (usually safe)
- Monitor visual fields each trimester if macroadenoma

### Pregnancy

**Management:**
- Microadenoma: Usually stop cabergoline, observe
- Macroadenoma: Continue therapy or careful monitoring
- Risk of tumor enlargement: 2-3% (micro), 15-30% (macro)

### Resistance

**Definition:**
- Failure to normalize prolactin on maximally tolerated cabergoline
- Occurs in 10-20%

**Options:**
- Surgery
- Temozolomide (aggressive tumors)
- Experimental therapies

### Atypical Antipsychotics

**Challenge:**
- Many cause hyperprolactinemia
- May need to switch antipsychotic or add dopamine agonist (risk of psychosis)

## Follow-Up

### Long-Term Monitoring

**On Therapy:**
- Prolactin: Every 6-12 months
- MRI: Annually until stable, then less frequently
- Testosterone: Restore to normal range

**After Discontinuation (if attempted):**
- Prolactin: Every 3 months
- Recurrence common if macroadenoma

## Summary

**Key Points:**
1. Most common functioning pituitary tumor
2. Causes secondary hypogonadism via GnRH inhibition
3. Diagnosed by elevated prolactin + MRI
4. First-line treatment: Dopamine agonists (cabergoline preferred)
5. Often delayed in men (present with mass effects)
6. Fertility usually restorable
7. Long-term follow-up required

**Clinical Pearl:** Unexplained hypogonadism in men → Check prolactin. Delayed diagnosis common; often macroadenoma at presentation.
