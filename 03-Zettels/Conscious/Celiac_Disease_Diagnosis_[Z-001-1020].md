# Celiac Disease Diagnosis — Serology, Biopsy, and Gluten-Free Diet

## Overview

Celiac disease is an immune-mediated enteropathy triggered by gluten ingestion in genetically susceptible individuals (HLA-DQ2 or HLA-DQ8). Characterized by villous atrophy, crypt hyperplasia, and intraepithelial lymphocytosis.

## Pathophysiology

### Gluten and Immune Response

**Gluten Proteins:**
- **Gliadin** (wheat)
- **Hordein** (barley)
- **Secalin** (rye)

**Sequence of Events:**
```
Gluten ingestion
    ↓
Tissue transglutaminase (tTG) deamidates gliadin
    ↓
Increases binding affinity to HLA-DQ2/DQ8
    ↓
Presented to CD4+ T-cells
    ↓
Th1 response, cytokine release (IFN-γ)
    ↓
Mucosal damage, autoantibody production
```

### Autoantibodies

**Tissue Transglutaminase (tTG):**
- Enzyme that cross-links proteins
- Target of autoimmune response
- IgA-tTG primary diagnostic test

**Endomysial Antibodies (EMA):**
- Antibodies against endomysium (connective tissue)
- Highly specific
- More labor-intensive test

**Deamidated Gliadin Peptides (DGP):**
- IgG-DGP useful in IgA deficiency
- IgA-DGP alternative to tTG

## Clinical Presentation

### Classic Symptoms

**GI:**
- Chronic diarrhea
- Steatorrhea
- Bloating, abdominal pain
- Weight loss
- Failure to thrive (children)

**Systemic:**
- Iron deficiency anemia
- Fatigue
- Dermatitis herpetiformis
- Osteoporosis/osteopenia
- Infertility
- Neurological symptoms (ataxia, neuropathy)

### Silent/Subclinical

- No symptoms
- Detected by screening
- Biopsy evidence of damage

### Latent

- Genetic susceptibility
- Positive serology
- Normal biopsy
- May progress to active disease

### Associated Conditions

**Autoimmune:**
- Type 1 
- Thyroid disease (Hashimoto's)
- Sjögren's syndrome
- Autoimmune hepatitis

**Genetic:**
- Down syndrome
- Turner syndrome
- Williams syndrome

**Other:**
- IgA deficiency (10-15%)
- Selective IgA deficiency affects testing

## Diagnosis

### Serological Testing

**1. IgA Anti-tTG (Tissue Transglutaminase):**
- **First-line test**
- Sensitivity: 95-98%
- Specificity: 95-97%
- Inexpensive, widely available

**2. IgA Anti-Endomysial (EMA):**
- Highly specific (>99%)
- More expensive, operator-dependent
- Confirmatory role

**3. Total IgA:**
- Check in all patients
- 2-3% of celiacs have IgA deficiency
- If low, use IgG-based tests

**4. IgG-DGP (Deamidated Gliadin Peptide):**
- Alternative in IgA deficiency
- IgG-tTG also available

### HLA Testing

**HLA-DQ2 and HLA-DQ8:**
- Present in 95% of celiacs
- DQ2 (HLA-DQA1*05/DQB1*02)
- DQ8 (HLA-DQA1*03/DQB1*0302)

**Role:**
- **Exclude diagnosis:** If negative (very high NPV)
- Not diagnostic if positive (30% of population has DQ2)
- Useful in:
  - Unclear diagnosis
  - Already on gluten-free diet
  - Equivocal histology

### Endoscopy and Biopsy

**Indications:**
- Positive serology
- High clinical suspicion with negative serology
- Monitoring (refractory disease)

**Procedure:**
- **Multiple biopsies** required
- At least 4-6 samples
- Distal duodenum (bulb and beyond)
- Orient specimens properly

**Histology (Marsh Classification):**

| Stage | Villi | Crypts | IELs/100 enterocytes |
|-------|-------|--------|---------------------|
| **0** | Normal | Normal | <25 |
| **1** | Normal | Normal | >25 |
| **2** | Normal | Hyperplastic | >25 |
| **3a** | Partial atrophy | Hyperplastic | >25 |
| **3b** | Subtotal atrophy | Hyperplastic | >25 |
| **3c** | Total atrophy | Hyperplastic | >25 |

**Key Features:**
- Villous atrophy (partial to total)
- Crypt hyperplasia (lengthening)
- Increased intraepithelial lymphocytes (IELs)
- Plasma cell infiltrate in lamina propria

### Diagnostic Algorithm

```
Suspected Celiac Disease
    ↓
Serological Testing
- IgA-tTG
- Total IgA
- IgG-DGP (if IgA deficient)
    ↓
Positive Serology? → Endoscopy with biopsy
Negative but High Suspicion? → Consider HLA, biopsy, gluten challenge
    ↓
Histology consistent? → CONFIRMED CELIAC DISEASE
    ↓
Start Gluten-Free Diet
```

**Exception:** Children with:
- tTG-IgA >10x ULN
- Positive EMA
- HLA-DQ2/DQ8 positive
- May skip biopsy (ESPAGHAN guidelines)

### Gluten Challenge

**Indications:**
- Already on gluten-free diet (undiagnosed)
- Need confirmation

**Protocol:**
- 3-6g gluten/day (2 slices bread)
- Continue 6-8 weeks
- Test serology
- Biopsy if needed

**Caution:**
- Symptomatic patients may refuse
- Risk of severe symptoms
- Proceed cautiously

## Gluten-Free Diet (GFD)

### Principles

**Lifelong:**
- No cure except GFD
- Strict adherence required
- Even small amounts cause damage

**Allowed:**
- Rice, corn, quinoa, buckwheat, millet
- Fresh fruits, vegetables
- Fresh meats, fish, poultry (unprocessed)
- Most dairy
- Nuts, seeds

**Prohibited:**
- Wheat, barley, rye
- All derivatives (flour, bran, germ)
- Malt, malt vinegar
- Oats (unless certified gluten-free)
- Beer (unless gluten-free)

### Hidden Gluten

**Sources:**
- Processed foods
- Sauces, gravies, soups
- Soy sauce (use tamari)
- Medications, supplements
- Communion wafers
- Lipstick, toothpaste (trace)

**Labeling:**
- "Gluten-free" <20 ppm (FDA standard)
- "Wheat-free" ≠ gluten-free
- Check all packaged foods

### al Considerations

**Deficiencies (common at diagnosis):**
- Iron
- Folate
- Vitamin B12
- 
- Calcium
- Zinc

**Monitoring:**
- Correct deficiencies
- Annual DEXA (osteoporosis)
- Nutritional labs yearly

**Fiber:**
- May be low on GFD
- Emphasize whole grains (quinoa, brown rice)
- Fruits, vegetables

### Follow-Up

**Serology:**
- tTG-IgA should normalize
- 6-12 months after GFD initiation
- Indicates compliance

**Biopsy:**
- Not routine
- Consider if:
  - Symptoms persist
  - Serology doesn't normalize
  - Refractory disease suspected

**Symptom Assessment:**
- Most improve within weeks
- Complete healing may take 1-2 years
- Some symptoms (neurological) slow to resolve

## Complications

### Untreated Celiac Disease

**GI:**
- Refractory sprue
- Ulcerative jejunoileitis
- Enteropathy-associated T-cell lymphoma (EATL)
- Small bowel adenocarcinoma

**Systemic:**
- Osteoporosis/fractures
- Infertility/miscarriage
- Neurological (ataxia, epilepsy)
- Cardiomyopathy (rare)

### Refractory Celiac Disease (RCD)

**Definition:**
- Persistent villous atrophy despite GFD >12 months
- Rule out: Gluten exposure, other causes

**Type I:**
- Normal IEL phenotype
- Usually improves with steroids
- Better prognosis

**Type II:**
- Aberrant IELs (clonal)
- High risk of lymphoma
- Poor prognosis
- May need chemotherapy

## Screening

**High-Risk Groups:**
- First-degree relatives (10-15% prevalence)
- Type 1 diabetes
- Autoimmune thyroid disease
- Down syndrome, Turner syndrome
- Unexplained iron deficiency
- Osteoporosis
- Infertility

**Not general population** (controversial)

## Summary

**Diagnostic Criteria:**
1. Compatible symptoms
2. Positive serology (tTG-IgA)
3. Characteristic histology (villous atrophy)
4. Response to GFD
5. HLA-DQ2/DQ8 (supportive)

**Key Points:**
1. tTG-IgA first-line test (check total IgA)
2. Biopsy required for confirmation (except high-titer pediatric cases)
3. HLA testing excludes diagnosis if negative
4. GFD is lifelong and strict
5. Monitor adherence with serology
6. Screen high-risk groups

**Clinical Pearl:** Don't diagnose celiac disease without biopsy confirmation (except in specific pediatric scenarios). The diagnosis commits patients to lifelong GFD—be certain. Check IgA levels, as deficiency can cause false-negative testing.
