# Industry Funding Bias — Early PUFA Research and Suppressed Science

## Historical Context

The rise of seed oils coincided with a transformation of the American diet and scientific establishment. Industry funding shaped research agendas, publication patterns, and ultimately public health policy.

## Key Industry Players

### Procter & Gamble — Crisco

**1911:** P&G introduces Crisco (crystallized cottonseed oil)
**Strategy:**
- Market as "modern," "scientific" alternative to lard
- Fund research on saturated fat dangers
- Promote unsaturated fat health benefits
- Cookbook publication to drive adoption

**Research Funding:**
- Supported early cholesterol research
- Funded Keys' early work
- Created favorable scientific environment for PUFA

### Vegetable Oil Industry Consortium

**Members:**
- American Soybean Association
- Corn Refiners Association
- National Cottonseed Products Association
- Various margarine manufacturers

**Coordinated Efforts:**
- Joint funding of research
- Shared public relations messaging
- Influence on professional societies
- Lobbying for dietary guidelines

## s of Influence

### 1. Research Funding

**Selective Support:**
- Fund studies likely to show benefit
- Defund or ignore unfavorable research
- Create research centers with industry-friendly leadership

**Examples:**
- Multiple university lipid research centers
- American Heart Association early funding
- NHLBI (government) also influenced by industry advisory

### 2. Publication Bias

**The File Drawer Problem:**
- Negative results go unpublished
- Sydney Diet-Heart mortality data (unpublished 1973-2013)
- Minnesota Coronary full results (unpublished 1973-2016)

**Editorial Influence:**
- Industry representation on journal boards
- Reviewer selection
- Acceptance criteria

### 3. Professional Society Capture

**American Heart Association:**
- Industry funding of conferences
- Industry-sponsored educational materials
- Expert panels with industry ties

**Academy of  and Dietetics:**
- Corporate sponsorship structure
- Industry-influenced position statements

### 4. Guidelines Committee Stacking

**Dietary Guidelines Process:**
- Expert selection influenced by industry
- Exclusion of critics
- Emphasis on observational over trial data

**Specific Examples:**
- 1977 McGovern Committee (Dietary Goals)
- 1980 first Dietary Guidelines
- Ongoing conflicts of interest

## The Suppressed Studies

### Sydney Diet-Heart Study (1966-1973)

**What Happened:**
- Full mortality data never published
- Only cholesterol results reported (1978)
- Original investigator died
- Data stored, essentially lost

**Recovery:**
- Ramsden et al. found data in National Heart Institute archives (2012)
- Revealed higher mortality in  group
- Published 2013 in BMJ

**Why Suppressed?**
- Contradicted prevailing hypothesis
- Unfavorable to industry interests
- Easier to let it fade away

### Minnesota Coronary Experiment (1968-1973)

**What Happened:**
- Data tapes stored in basement
- Only partial results published (1989)
- Full mortality data not analyzed

**Recovery:**
- Ramsden et al. recovered and analyzed (2016)
- Showed no benefit, possible harm in elderly
- Published in BMJ

**Institutional Context:**
- NHLBI funding
- Uncomfortable results for public health messaging
- No conspiracy needed — just lack of enthusiasm for publication

### Other Missing Data

**Pattern Recognition:**
- Multiple large trials had incomplete publication
- Tendency: Cholesterol results published, mortality not
- Industry-aligned researchers prioritized

## Documented Tactics

### The Sugar Association Playbook (Revealed 2016)

**1960s Documents:**
- Sugar Research Foundation paid Harvard researchers
- To downplay sugar, emphasize fat as heart disease cause
- Published 2016 in JAMA Internal Medicine

**Relevance:**
- Same era as seed oil rise
- Same research institutions
- Similar playbook likely for oils

### Marketing as Science

**"Heart Healthy" Labeling:**
- Industry-funded certifications
- AHA "Heart Check" program (industry fees)
- Confuses consumers about actual evidence

**Cookbook Infiltration:**
- Crisco cookbook (1910s onward)
- Recipes replaced lard/butter with shortening
- Cultural transformation through domestic science

## Consequences for Public Health

### Dietary Guidelines

**1977 Dietary Goals (McGovern Committee):**
- Reduce saturated fat
- Increase PUFA
- Based on limited evidence
- Industry influence acknowledged later

**1980 Dietary Guidelines:**
- First official US guidelines
- "Choose fats and oils high in polyunsaturated fat"
- Supported by industry, not robust science

### Food Environment

**Seed Oil Proliferation:**
- Subsidized commodity crops (soy, corn)
- Cheap inputs for processed foods
- Ubiquity in restaurant and packaged food
- Self-reinforcing cycle

### Scientific Discourse

**Chilling Effect:**
- Researchers avoided criticizing PUFA
- Dissenters marginalized (Yudkin on sugar, others on fat)
- Paradigm persistence despite contradictory evidence

## Modern Continuation

### Current Funding Patterns

**Still Common:**
- Industry-funded nutrition research
- Industry-sponsored reviews
- Conflict of interest in guidelines

**Disclosure Requirements:**
- Improved but insufficient
- Doesn't eliminate bias
- Only reveals, doesn't prevent

### Unfinished Business

**Needed Reforms:**
1. Independent research funding
2. Pre-registration of dietary trials
3. Publication of all trial data
4. Guidelines without industry influence
5. Conflict-free systematic reviews

## Lessons for Evaluating Evidence

### Red Flags

1. **Industry funding** — Not disqualifying but requires scrutiny
2. **Publication bias** — Look for unpublished trials
3. **Selective outcome reporting** — Mortality vs surrogate markers
4. **Researcher financial ties** — Stock, consulting, speaking fees
5. **Guideline conflicts** — Industry ties of panel members

### Best Practices

1. **Tri hierarchy:** Prioritize RCTs > cohorts > mechanistic
2. **Follow the money:** Identify all funding sources
3. **Look for nulls:** Published negative results valuable
4. **Reanalysis:** Independent reanalysis of raw data
5. **Consistency:** Across study types and populations

## Conclusion

The seed oil story exemplifies how:
- Commercial interests can shape scientific consensus
- Research funding influences questions asked
- Negative results can disappear
- Public health can be compromised

**Current status:** Recognition of problems growing, but institutional capture persists.
