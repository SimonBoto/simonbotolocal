## Core Claim
**B12 deficiency impairs exercise performance through reduced ATP production from amino acid and odd-chain fatty acid oxidation, leading to fatigue, weakness, and reduced exercise tolerance** — athletes and active individuals may have increased B12 requirements due to high metabolic turnover and protein intake.

## Evidence

### Mechanisms of Impairment
| Pathway | Effect of B12 Deficiency |
|---------|-------------------------|
| **Amino acid oxidation** | Isoleucine, methionine, threonine, valine not fully oxidized → reduced energy from protein |
| **Odd-chain fatty acids** | Cannot extract energy from dairy fats (15:0, 17:0) |
| **Mitochondrial dysfunction** | MMA accumulation (toxic), impaired Krebs cycle anaplerosis |

### Clinical Observations
| Deficiency | Repletion |
|------------|-----------|
| Fatigue | Increased energy |
| Weakness | Better exercise tolerance |
| Reduced exercise tolerance | Resolution of fatigue |
| Shortness of breath (anemia) | Improved oxygen capacity |

### Athletic Considerations
- **High protein intake** increases propionate load
- **Increased metabolic turnover** raises B12 demand
- **Marginal deficiency** may impair performance before clinical symptoms

## Clinical Implication
- **B12 deficiency should be ruled out** in athletes with unexplained fatigue
- **Supplementation** often beneficial even without overt deficiency
- **B-complex** may be preferable for comprehensive support
- **METHAP relevance:** High-dose nutrient protocols include B12 for mitochondrial support; exercise performance marker of adequacy
