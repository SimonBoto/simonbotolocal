# Vitamin C Biochemical Functions

Vitamin C serves as an essential cofactor for multiple critical physiological processes: collagen synthesis, free radical neutralization, antioxidant regeneration, neurotransmitter production, and immune function — explaining why deficiency has systemic consequences.

## 1. Collagen Synthesis

**Function:**
- Hydroxylation of proline and lysine residues
- Essential for collagen triple helix formation
- Required for connective tissue integrity

**Consequences of deficiency:**
- Poor wound healing
- Fragile blood vessels (bruising)
- Gum disease
- Skin aging
- Joint problems

**Pauling's framing:** "Literally holds your body together"

## 2. Free Radical Neutralization

**Function:**
- Electron donor to neutralize ROS
- Water-soluble antioxidant
- Protects aqueous compartments

**Mechanism:**
- Ascorbate → semidehydroascorbate → dehydroascorbate
- Regenerable from oxidized forms
- First line of antioxidant defense

## 3. Antioxidant Regeneration

**Vitamin C regenerates:**
- **Vitamin E** — tocopherol radical back to tocopherol
- **Glutathione** — oxidized GSSG back to GSH
- **Other antioxidants** — indirect protection

**Significance:**
- Central node in antioxidant network
- Spares other antioxidants
- Amplifies overall antioxidant capacity

## 4. Neurotransmitter Synthesis

**Cofactor for:**
- **Dopamine** → norepinephrine conversion (dopamine β-hydroxylase)
- **Serotonin** synthesis pathway
- **Carnitine** synthesis (energy metabolism)

**Consequences of deficiency:**
- Mood changes
- Fatigue
- Cognitive effects

## 5. Immune Function

**Roles:**
- Accumulates in immune cells (neutrophils, lymphocytes)
- Enhances phagocytosis
- Supports lymphocyte proliferation
- Antiviral effects (in vitro)

**High-dose rationale:**
- Immune cells concentrate vitamin C
- Needs increase during infection
- Pharmacological doses may be required
