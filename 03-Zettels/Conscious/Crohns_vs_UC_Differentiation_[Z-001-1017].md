# Crohn's Disease vs Ulcerative Colitis — Clinical and Pathological Differentiation

## Core Differences

### Distribution Pattern

**Crohn's Disease:**
- **Any segment** of GI tract from mouth to anus
- **Ileum** most commonly affected (terminal ileum)
- **Skip lesions** — discontinuous involvement
- Can affect multiple segments with normal bowel between

**Ulcerative Colitis:**
- **Colon only**
- **Continuous** involvement from rectum proximally
- No skip lesions
- Rectum always involved (100%)

### Depth of Inflammation

**Crohn's — Transmural:**
- Full thickness of bowel wall
- Mucosa, submucosa, muscularis, serosa
- Leads to complications:
  - Fistulas
  - Abscesses
  - Strictures
  - Perforation

**UC — Mucosal Only:**
- Limited to mucosa and superficial submucosa
- Preserves deeper layers
- No fistulas or strictures
- Mucosal healing achievable

### Histological Features

**Crohn's:**
- **Non-caseating granulomas** (40-60% of cases)
- Transmural lymphoid aggregates
- Fissuring ulcers
- Submucosal fibrosis

**UC:**
- **No granulomas**
- Crypt architectural distortion
- **Crypt abscesses**
- Basal plasmacytosis
- Goblet cell depletion

## Clinical Presentation

### Crohn's Disease

**Symptoms:**
- Abdominal pain (often RLQ)
- Diarrhea (may be less than UC)
- Weight loss (common)
- Fatigue
- Growth retardation (children)

**Complications:**
- Fistulas (perianal, enterocutaneous, enterovesical)
- Strictures (obstruction)
- Abscesses
- Perforation
- Malnutrition

**Perianal Disease:**
- Tags, fissures, fistulas
- Occurs in 30-40%
- Major differentiator from UC

### Ulcerative Colitis

**Symptoms:**
- Bloody diarrhea (hallmark)
- Mucus in stool
- Urgency, tenesmus
- Lower abdominal pain/cramping
- Fever (severe disease)

**Complications:**
- Toxic megacolon (rare)
- Perforation (severe disease)
- Severe hemorrhage
- Colon cancer risk

## Serological Markers

### ASCA (Anti-Saccharomyces cerevisiae Antibodies)

**Crohn's Association:**
- Positive in 50-60% of Crohn's
- Only 10-15% of UC
- Associated with:
  - Small bowel disease
  - Stricturing disease
  - Internal penetrating disease

### p-ANCA (Perinuclear Anti-Neutrophil Cytoplasmic Antibody)

**UC Association:**
- Positive in 60-70% of UC
- Only 10-20% of Crohn's
- Associated with:
  - Left-sided colitis
  - Primary sclerosing cholangitis

### Combined Interpretation

| Pattern | Interpretation |
|---------|----------------|
| ASCA+/p-ANCA- | Classic Crohn's |
| ASCA-/p-ANCA+ | Classic UC |
| ASCA+/p-ANCA+ | IBD, less specific |
| ASCA-/p-ANCA- | IBD, less specific |

## Endoscopic Appearance

### Crohn's

- **Skip lesions** (patchy involvement)
- **Cobblestoning** (mucosa)
- Deep linear ulcers (fissures)
- **Strictures** (stenotic segments)
- Rectal sparing (30-50%)

### UC

- **Continuous** inflammation
- Loss of vascular pattern
- Erythema, friability
- **Pseudopolyps**
- **Rectal involvement** always
- No cobblestoning

## Imaging Differences

### CT Enterography/MR Enterography

**Crohn's:**
- **Comb sign** (vascular engorgement)
- **Fat wrapping** (creeping fat)
- Fibrofatty proliferation
- **Strictures**
- Fistulas
- Skip lesions

**UC:**
- **Haustral loss**
- **Lead pipe** appearance (chronic)
- Mucosal enhancement only
- No strictures or fistulas

## Treatment Differences

### Surgical Approach

**Crohn's:**
- Surgery not curative (recurrence common)
- Resection for complications
- Strictureplasty
- **70% require surgery** in lifetime
- Perianal surgery common

**UC:**
- Surgery **curative** (proctocolectomy)
- Colectomy for refractory disease
- Ileal pouch-anal anastomosis (IPAA)
- **15-30% require colectomy**

### Medical Therapy Nuances

**Crohn's:**
- Need transmural healing
- Anti-TNF earlier
- Metronidazole/ciprofloxacin for perianal
- Thiopurines effective

**UC:**
- Topical (rectal) therapy important
- 5-ASA first-line
- JAK inhibitors (tofacitinib)
- S1P modulators (ozanimod)

## Extraintestinal Manifestations

### More Common in Crohn's
- Ankylosing spondylitis
- Sacroiliitis
- Nephrolithiasis (oxalate)
- Gallstones (ileal disease)

### More Common in UC
- Primary sclerosing cholangitis
- Pyoderma gangrenosum

### Similar in Both
- Peripheral arthritis
- Erythema nodosum
- Uveitis/episcleritis
- Osteoporosis

## Summary Table

| Feature | Crohn's Disease | Ulcerative Colitis |
|---------|-----------------|-------------------|
| **Location** | Any GI tract | Colon only |
| **Distribution** | Skip lesions | Continuous |
| **Rectal** | Often spared | Always involved |
| **Depth** | Transmural | Mucosal only |
| **Granulomas** | Yes (40-60%) | No |
| **Fistulas** | Common | Rare |
| **Strictures** | Common | Rare |
| **Perianal** | Common (30%) | Rare |
| **Bleeding** | Variable | Common (bloody diarrhea) |
| **ASCA** | + in 50-60% | + in 10-15% |
| **p-ANCA** | + in 10-20% | + in 60-70% |
| **Smoking** | Worsens | Protective (paradox) |
| **Surgery** | Not curative | Curative |
| **Cancer risk** | Small bowel + colon | Colon only |

## Clinical Pearl

**Key differentiators:**
1. **Perianal disease** = Crohn's until proven otherwise
2. **Rectal sparing** = suggests Crohn's
3. **Fistulas/strictures** = Crohn's
4. **Bloody diarrhea** = UC hallmark
5. **Granulomas on biopsy** = Crohn's

**When uncertain:** 
- "Indeterminate colitis" (10-15%)
- May clarify over time
- Treat as IBD
- Serial evaluation
