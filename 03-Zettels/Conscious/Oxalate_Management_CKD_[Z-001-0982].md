# Oxalate Management in CKD — Kidney Stone Prevention and Progression

## Clinical Significance

Calcium oxalate is the most common kidney stone type. CKD patients have increased stone risk due to reduced oxalate clearance, and stones can accelerate CKD progression.

## Oxalate Physiology

### Sources

**Endogenous Production:**
- Glyoxylate  (primary source)
- Ascorbic acid (vitamin C) conversion
- Amino acid metabolism (hydroxyproline)
- ~50-100 mg/day produced internally

**Dietary Intake:**
- Absorbed from gut (~10-15% of intake)
- Varies with diet: 50-200 mg/day
- Bioavailability depends on food matrix

### Handling in CKD

**Normal Kidney Function:**
- Filtered at glomerulus
- Minimal reabsorption
- Excreted in urine

**Impaired Kidney Function:**
- Reduced filtration
- Plasma oxalate rises
- Tissue deposition risk (oxalosis)
- Stone formation risk increased

## High-Oxalate Foods to Limit

### Very High (>50 mg/serving)

| Food | Serving | Oxalate (mg) |
|------|---------|--------------|
| **Spinach** | 1 cup cooked | 750+ |
| **Rhubarb** | 1 cup | 540 |
| **Beet greens** | 1 cup | 500+ |
| **Almonds** | 1 oz (22 nuts) | 120 |
| **Baked beans** | 1 cup | 110 |
| **Miso** | 1 oz | 110 |

### High (10-50 mg/serving)

| Category | Foods |
|----------|-------|
| **Nuts/Seeds** | Cashews, peanuts, sesame, hazelnuts |
| **Vegetables** | Beets, okra, sweet potatoes, turnips |
| **Fruits** | Starfruit, raspberries, oranges, kiwi |
| **Grains** | Wheat germ, bran, quinoa, bulgur |
| **Legumes** | Soybeans, lentils, navy beans |
| **Beverages** | Black tea, dark beer, cocoa, chocolate drinks |
| **Other** | Chocolate, tofu, wheat bran |

### Moderate (5-10 mg/serving)

- Berries (strawberries, blackberries)
- Coffee
- Potatoes
- Navy beans
- Oranges

## Dietary Management Strategies

### 1. Calcium with Meals

**Mechanism:**
- Dietary calcium binds oxalate in gut
- Forms insoluble calcium oxalate
- Reduces absorption
- Excreted in stool

**Recommendation:**
- 800-1,200 mg dietary calcium daily
- Spread across meals
- Prefer food sources over supplements

**Sources:**
- Dairy (milk, yogurt, cheese)
- Fortified alternatives
- Some leafy greens (low-oxalate)

**Timing:**
- Most important with high-oxalate meals
- Calcium supplement with spinach, nuts, chocolate

### 2. Adequate Fluid Intake

**Goal:**
- Urine output >2 liters/day
- Dilutes urinary oxalate concentration
- Reduces supersaturation

**CKD Considerations:**
- Volume status may limit fluid
- Heart failure, edema constraints
- Individualize based on clinical status

### 3. Moderate Protein Intake

**Concern:**
- High protein increases oxalate
- Animal protein → acidic urine
- Acidosis increases calcium and decreases citrate

**Recommendation:**
- Follow CKD protein targets (0.6-0.8 g/kg)
- Avoid high-protein fad diets
- Emphasize quality over quantity

### 4. Limit Vitamin C Supplements

**Conversion:**
- Vitamin C (ascorbic acid) metabolized to oxalate
- Doses >500 mg/day increase stone risk
- Food sources OK (self-limiting)

**Recommendation:**
- Avoid high-dose vitamin C supplements
- RDA: 75-90 mg/day (easily achieved from food)

### 5. Reduce Sodium

**Mechanism:**
- High sodium increases calcium excretion
- Higher calcium-oxalate supersaturation

**Target:**
- <2,000 mg sodium/day
- Aligns with CKD recommendations

### 6. Increase Citrate (if not contraindicated)

**Benefits:**
- Citrate inhibits stone formation
- Alkalinizes urine
- Binds calcium

**Sources:**
- Citrus fruits (lemons, limes, oranges)
- Lemon juice in water
- Potassium citrate (prescription)

**CKD Caution:**
- Potassium load may be problematic
- Monitor potassium if using supplements

## Medical Management

### When to Consider Medication

**Indications:**
- Recurrent calcium oxalate stones
- Hyperoxaluria on 24-hour urine
- Progressive nephrocalcinosis
- Oxalate nephropathy

### Potassium Citrate

**Benefits:**
- Alkalinizes urine (target pH 6.5-7.0)
- Increases urinary citrate
- Inhibits stone formation

**Dosing:**
- Start 10-20 mEq twice daily
- Titrate to urine pH

**CKD Cautions:**
- Hyperkalemia risk
- Use cautiously if eGFR <30
- Monitor potassium closely

### Thiazide Diuretics

**Benefits:**
- Reduce urinary calcium
- Decrease stone formation

**CKD Limitations:**
- Less effective if eGFR <30
- May worsen electrolytes
- Volume depletion risk

### Pyridoxine (Vitamin B6)

**Mechanism:**
- Cofactor for alanine:glyoxylate aminotransferase
- Reduces endogenous oxalate production

**Dosing:**
- 10-200 mg/day
- Higher doses for primary hyperoxaluria

**Safety:**
- Generally safe
- Peripheral neuropathy with very high doses (>500 mg)

## 24-Hour Urine Testing

### When to Order

**Indications:**
- Recurrent stone formers
- First stone + CKD
- Solitary kidney
- High-risk occupation (pilots, surgeons)

### Key Parameters

| Parameter | Normal | Stone Risk |
|-----------|--------|------------|
| **Volume** | >2 L/day | <2 L/day |
| **Calcium** | <250 mg/day (men) | >300 mg/day |
| | <200 mg/day (women) | >250 mg/day |
| **Oxalate** | <40 mg/day | >40 mg/day |
| **Citrate** | >320 mg/day | <320 mg/day |
| **Uric acid** | <750 mg/day | >750 mg/day |
| **Sodium** | <150 mEq/day | >200 mEq/day |
| **pH** | 5.5-6.5 | <5.5 or >7.0 |

### CKD Modifications

**Limitations:**
- May not collect accurately if oliguric
- Interpretation different in advanced CKD
- Nephrologist involvement helpful

## Primary vs Secondary Hyperoxaluria

### Primary Hyperoxaluria (Genetic)

**Characteristics:**
- Autosomal recessive
- Enzyme defects in glyoxylate metabolism
- Very high oxalate production
- Childhood onset
- Rapid CKD progression
- Systemic oxalosis

**Diagnosis:**
- Genetic testing
- Very high urine/plasma oxalate
- Family history

**Management:**
- Aggressive hydration
- Pyridoxine (B6)
- Lumasiran (RNAi therapy) — new
- Liver-kidney transplant

### Secondary Hyperoxaluria (Acquired)

**Causes:**
- High oxalate diet
- Fat malabsorption (enteric hyperoxaluria)
- Bariatric surgery
- Low calcium intake
- High vitamin C intake
- Ethylene glycol poisoning

**Management:**
- Address underlying cause
- Dietary modifications
- Calcium supplementation with meals
- Cholestyramine (binds oxalate in gut)

## CKD-Specific Considerations

### Enteric Hyperoxaluria Risk

**Post-Bariatric Surgery:**
- Fat malabsorption
- Increased free  in gut
- Bind calcium
- More oxalate available for absorption

**Management:**
- Low-oxalate diet
- Calcium with every meal
- Cholestyramine
- Monitor kidney function

### Oxalate Nephropathy

**Definition:**
- Acute or chronic kidney injury from oxalate crystal deposition
- Tubular injury
- Interstitial 
- Progressive fibrosis

**Causes:**
- Primary hyperoxaluria
- Massive vitamin C ingestion
- Ethylene glycol poisoning
- Severe enteric hyperoxaluria

**Diagnosis:**
- High plasma oxalate
- Urine oxalate elevated
- Kidney biopsy: Calcium oxalate crystals

**Treatment:**
- Intensive dialysis (oxalate removal)
- Address underlying cause
- May need transplant

## Summary

**Key Points:**
1. Oxalate management important in CKD (stone risk + progression)
2. Limit high-oxalate foods (spinach, nuts, chocolate, beets)
3. Ensure adequate calcium with meals (binds oxalate)
4. Hydration goal: >2L urine output daily
5. Avoid high-dose vitamin C supplements
6. Moderate protein (aligns with CKD targets)
7. 24-hour urine testing for recurrent stone formers
8. Consider potassium citrate if hypocitraturic (watch K+)
9. Screen for primary hyperoxaluria in young, progressive cases
10. Nephrology referral for complex cases
