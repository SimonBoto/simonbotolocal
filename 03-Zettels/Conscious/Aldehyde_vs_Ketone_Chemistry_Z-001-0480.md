# Aldehyde vs Ketone Chemistry

## Core Claim
**Aldehydes (terminal carbonyl) and ketones (internal carbonyl) differ fundamentally in reactivity and biological function: aldehydes are more reactive (easily oxidized) and define aldose sugars like glucose, while ketones are more stable and define ketose sugars like fructose** — this chemical distinction underlies their different metabolic fates and reducing properties.

## Evidence

### Structural Comparison
| Feature | Aldehyde (R-CHO) | Ketone (R-CO-R') |
|---------|------------------|------------------|
| **Position** | Terminal (end of chain) | Internal (within chain) |
| **Bonds** | C=O, C-H, C-R | C=O, C-R, C-R' |
| **Reactivity** | High (easily oxidized) | Lower (more stable) |

### In Sugars
| Property | Aldose (e.g., Glucose) | Ketose (e.g., Fructose) |
|----------|------------------------|-------------------------|
| **Carbonyl position** | C1 (aldehyde) | C2 (ketone) |
| **Ring form** | Pyranose (6-membered) | Furanose (5-membered) |
| **Reducing property** | Strong (positive Benedict's) | Weaker |
| **Isomerization** | Can convert to ketose | Can convert to aldose (base) |

### Reactivity Differences
| Reaction | Aldehyde | Ketone |
|----------|----------|--------|
| **Oxidation** → Carboxylic acid | Easy | Requires strong conditions |
| **Reduction** → Alcohol | Primary alcohol | Secondary alcohol |

## Clinical Implication
- **Glucose (aldose) is more reducing than fructose (ketose)** — important in glycation reactions
- **Fructose metabolism bypasses PFK-1 regulation** — enters glycolysis downstream, unregulated
- **Aldehyde toxicity:** Reactive aldehydes (lipid peroxidation products) damage proteins/DNA
- **METHAP relevance:** Understanding sugar chemistry informs metabolic interventions; glucose monitoring central to metabolic syndrome management
