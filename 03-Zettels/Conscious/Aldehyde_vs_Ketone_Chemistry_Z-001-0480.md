# Aldehyde vs Ketone 

## Core Structures

### Aldehyde
```
R-CHO
```
- Carbonyl carbon bonded to **H and R**
- **Terminal position** (end of chain)
- Highly reactive (easily oxidized)

### Ketone
```
R-CO-R'
```
- Carbonyl carbon bonded to **two carbon groups**
- **Internal position** (within chain)
- More stable than aldehyde

## In Sugars

### Aldose (Aldehyde Sugar)
- Carbonyl at C1
- Example: Glucose
- Ring form: Pyranose (6-membered)
- More reducing (positive Benedict's test)

### Ketose (Ketone Sugar)
- Carbonyl at C2
- Example: Fructose
- Ring form: Furanose (5-membered)
- Can isomerize to aldose under basic conditions

## Reactivity Differences

### Oxidation
| Compound | Oxidation Product | Ease |
|----------|-------------------|------|
| **Aldehyde** | Carboxylic acid | Easy |
| **Ketone** | (Requires strong conditions) | Harder |

### Reduction
Both reduce to alcohols:
- Aldehyde → Primary alcohol
- Ketone → Secondary alcohol

## Biological Significance

### Reducing Sugars
**Aldoses are reducing sugars** (can reduce Cu²⁺ to Cu⁺):
- Glucose, galactose, ribose
- Used in diagnostic tests

**Ketoses can act as reducing sugars** via isomerization to aldoses under basic conditions.

### Metabolic Implications
- **Aldehyde groups:** More reactive, often involved in signaling/modifications
- **Ketone groups:** More stable, preferred for energy storage (fructose)
