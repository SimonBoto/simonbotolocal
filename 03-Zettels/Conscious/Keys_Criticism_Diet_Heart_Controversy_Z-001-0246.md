## Core Principle

> Despite its influence, Ancel Keys' Seven Countries Study faced significant methodological criticisms regarding country selection, data analysis, and the leap from correlation to causation, fueling ongoing debate about saturated fat and heart disease.

## Major Criticisms

### 1. Cherry-Picked Countries
**The Issue:**
- Data available for 16 countries
- Only 7 published (those supporting hypothesis)
- Excluded nations with contradictory patterns

**Notable Exclusions:**
| Country | Fat Intake | CVD Rate | Fit Hypothesis? |
|---------|------------|----------|-----------------|
| **France** | High | Low | No |
| **Switzerland** | High | Low | No |
| **West Germany** | Moderate | Variable | No |
| **Sweden** | High | Moderate | No |

**The French Paradox:**
- High saturated fat intake (butter, cheese, meat)
- Low cardiovascular disease rates
- Directly contradicts Keys' hypothesis
- Excluded from analysis

### 2. Ecological Fallacy
**The Error:**
- Country-level data applied to individuals
- "Averaging problem"
- Doesn't account for within-country variation

**Example:**
- Greek average ≠ individual Greek diet
- Within-country heterogeneity ignored
- Individual-level studies needed for causation

### 3. Ignored Confounders
**Sugar and Carbohydrates:**
- Not analyzed in Seven Countries
- Sugar consumption correlated with CVD
- Carbohydrate quality matters
- High-carb diets may be worse

**Other Factors:**
- Smoking rates differed enormously
- Socioeconomic status
- Physical activity levels
- Healthcare quality
- Other dietary components

### 4. Methodological Weaknesses

**Dietary Assessment:**
- Food frequency questionnaires (unreliable)
- Recall bias significant
- Single time-point measurements
- Limited food composition databases

**Biological  Assumed:**
- Saturated fat →  → CVD
- Cholesterol hypothesis later questioned
- HDL vs. LDL distinction missed initially
- Particle size matters (not known then)
