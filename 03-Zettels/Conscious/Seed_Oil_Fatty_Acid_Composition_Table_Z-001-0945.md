## Key Insights

### Highest Risk Oils (Linoleic Acid >50%)
1. **Safflower oil** — 75-80% linoleic acid
2. **Sunflower oil** — 65-70% linoleic acid
3. **Corn oil** — 55-60% linoleic acid
4. **Soybean oil** — 50-55% linoleic acid

** Significance:** These oils rapidly increase tissue omega-6 levels, driving pro-inflammatory eicosanoid production.

### Moderate Risk Oils (Linoleic Acid 20-50%)
- **Canola** — Better omega-6:omega-3 ratio (~2:1)
- **Peanut** — Moderate omega-6 with higher monounsaturated fat

### Low Risk Traditional Fats
- **Olive oil** — High oleic acid (monounsaturated), low omega-6
- **Coconut oil** — Saturated, stable, minimal omega-6
- **Butter/Tallow** — Natural fats with balanced 

## Metabolic Implications

### Omega-6 Load Calculation
**Daily linoleic acid intake from 2 tablespoons oil:**
- Safflower oil: ~20g linoleic acid
- Sunflower oil: ~17g linoleic acid
- Corn oil: ~15g linoleic acid
- Soybean oil: ~14g linoleic acid
- Olive oil: ~3g linoleic acid

**Evolutionary context:** Hunter-gatherers consumed ~3-5g/day linoleic acid.

### Smoke Point Considerations
> High smoke point ≠ Healthy oil

- Seed oils have high smoke points (230°C+) due to refining
- But high heat generates toxic aldehydes from polyunsaturated fats
- Saturated fats (coconut, butter) more stable despite lower smoke points

## Historical Context

### Pre-1900 Dietary Fats
- **Butter, lard, tallow** — Primary cooking fats
- **Olive oil** — Mediterranean regions
- **Coconut oil** — Tropical regions
- **Linoleic acid intake:** ~3-5g/day (1-2% calories)

### Post-1980 Shift
- **Seed oil consumption increased 5-10×**
- **Linoleic acid intake:** 20-30g/day (7-10% calories)
- **Result:** Tissue omega-6 levels increased 3-5×
