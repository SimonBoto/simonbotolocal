## Core Claim
**Eighteen of 20 amino acids are glucogenic, meaning they can be converted to glucose via gluconeogenesis — this provides a critical mechanism for maintaining blood glucose during fasting, starvation, and carbohydrate restriction when glycogen stores are depleted** — only leucine and lysine are exclusively ketogenic.

## Evidence

### Glucogenic Amino Acids by Precursor
| Precursor | Amino Acids | Pathway |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| **Pyruvate** | Ala, Ser, Gly, Cys | Transamination/dehydratase |
| **Oxaloacetate** | Asp, Asn | Direct transamination |
| **Alpha-ketoglutarate** | Glu, Gln, Pro, Arg, His | Deamination/degradation |
| **Succinyl-CoA** | Val, Met, Ile, Thr | Propionyl-CoA pathway (B12) |
| **Fumarate** | Phe, Tyr | Via tyrosine degradation |

### Critical Role in Fasting
| Time | Glucose Source | Amino Acid Contribution |
|------|---------------|------------------------|
| **0-12 hours** | Glycogenolysis | Minimal |
| **12-24 hours** | Gluconeogenesis | Moderate (alanine from muscle) |
| **24-48 hours** | Gluconeogenesis | High (protein catabolism) |
| **48+ hours** | Gluconeogenesis + ketones | Moderate (ketones spare glucose) |

### Alanine Cycle (Muscle-Liver)
- **Muscle:** Alanine released from protein breakdown
- **Liver:** Alanine → pyruvate → glucose
- **Glucose:** Returns to muscle

## Clinical Implication
- **Protein is essential for glucose homeostasis** during fasting
- **B12 deficiency impairs** valine, methionine, isoleucine, threonine contribution
- **High protein intake supports** gluconeogenic capacity
- **METHAP relevance:** Adequate protein + B12/folate/B6 ensures robust gluconeogenesis
