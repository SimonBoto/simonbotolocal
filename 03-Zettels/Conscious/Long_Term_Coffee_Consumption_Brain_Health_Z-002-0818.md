## Core Claim
**Long-term coffee consumption over decades supports brain health and reduces dementia risk** — the 37-year follow-up demonstrates that coffee's neuroprotective effects are sustained over long periods, making it a viable lifelong prevention strategy.

## The 37-Year Evidence

### Study Robustness
| Feature | Significance |
|---------|--------------|
| **37-year follow-up** | Exceptionally long for nutritional epidemiology |
| **132,000 participants** | Robust statistical power |
| **11,000 dementia cases** | Reliable risk estimation |
| **Consistency across decades** | True protective effect, not confounding |

### Pattern Consistency
- Both coffee and tea show similar protective patterns
- Caffeine-containing beverages consistently beneficial
- Effect persists from middle age into older age

## Practical Implications

### Timing
- Starting moderate coffee consumption in **middle age**
- Provides protection when dementia risk is **highest** (older age)
- Lifelong prevention strategy

### Population Impact
- High coffee consumption globally
- Even modest individual risk reduction
- Translates to **significant public health benefit**

## Significance

**Long-term coffee consumption over decades supports brain health and reduces dementia risk** — the 37-year follow-up demonstrates that coffee's neuroprotective effects are sustained over long periods, making it a viable lifelong prevention strategy.

## Conclusion
Coffee's neuroprotective effects are not short-term but sustained across decades, supporting its role in long-term dementia prevention strategies.
