# Peer Review Best Practices — Structure, Tone, and Decision Framework

## Overview

Peer review is the quality control mechanism of scientific publishing. Effective review requires technical expertise, critical thinking, and professional communication skills.

## The Reviewer's Responsibility

### Core Duties

**Scientific Integrity:**
- Verify methodological soundness
- Check data support conclusions
- Identify errors or flaws
- Ensure ethical compliance

**Constructive Critique:**
- Provide actionable feedback
- Suggest improvements
- Maintain professional tone
- Respect author effort

**Confidentiality:**
- Protect unpublished work
- Don't share manuscripts
- Don't use ideas prematurely
- Maintain anonymity (if blind)

**Timeliness:**
- Meet deadlines
- Request extensions if needed
- Don't delay unnecessarily
- Decline if unable

## Review Structure

### 1. Summary Paragraph

**Purpose:**
- Brief overview for editor
- Contextualize your expertise
- Overall impression

**Content:**
- Study objective
- Main methods
- Key findings
- Your assessment of contribution

**Example:**
> "This manuscript investigates the relationship between X and Y in Z population using a cohort design. The authors report finding A, B, and C. The study addresses an important question with generally appropriate methods, though several issues need attention before publication."

### 2. Major Concerns

**Critical Issues:**
- Must be addressed for acceptance
- Scientific validity problems
- Methodological flaws
- Ethical concerns

**Formatting:**
- Numbered list
- Page/line references
- Clear explanation
- Suggested solution

**Examples:**

**Major Issue 1 — Sample Size:**
> "The study is underpowered. With the observed effect size and variance, approximately 200 participants would be needed for 80% power. The current n=85 risks Type II error. Consider power analysis and acknowledge limitations or expand sample." (Page 8, Methods)

**Major Issue 2 — Confounding:**
> "The authors claim causation from observational data. The association between X and Y may be confounded by Z, which was not measured. Revise to association rather than causation, or address confounding through sensitivity analyses." (Page 12, Discussion)

### 3. Minor Concerns

**Improvements:**
- Clarifications
- Additional citations
- Formatting
- Typos
- Figure legends

**Tone:**
- Helpful suggestions
- Optional improvements
- Not barriers to acceptance

**Examples:**

**Minor Issue 1 — Figure Clarity:**
> "Figure 2 would benefit from clearer labeling of the y-axis. Consider adding units."

**Minor Issue 2 — Citation:**
> "Recent work by Smith et al. (2024) supports these findings and should be cited."

### 4. Recommendation

**Clear Statement:**
- Accept
- Minor revision
- Major revision
- Reject

**Justification:**
- Brief rationale
- Specific to this manuscript

## Decision Framework

### Accept

**Criteria:**
- Methods sound
- Data support conclusions
- Novel contribution
- Minor editorial issues only

**Typical Issues:**
- Typos
- Minor formatting
- Optional clarifications

### Minor Revision

**Criteria:**
- Good science
- Small improvements needed
- No re-review necessary (usually)
- Author can address quickly

**Typical Issues:**
- Clarify methods
- Additional citations
- Figure improvements
- Statistical presentation

### Major Revision

**Criteria:**
- Substantive problems
- Requires re-review
- Salvageable with work
- Worth the effort

**Typical Issues:**
- Additional analyses
- Major rewriting
- New experiments
- Significant clarifications

### Reject

**Criteria:**
- Fatal flaws
- Beyond salvage
- Out of scope
- Not novel
- Ethical violations

**Types:**

**1. Reject (do not encourage resubmission):**
- Fatally flawed
- No path to adequacy
- Out of scope
- Duplicate publication

**2. Reject (encourage resubmission):**
- Major problems but fixable
- Different direction needed
- Better fit elsewhere
- Worth pursuing

## Review Tone

### Professional and Respectful

**Good:**
> "The authors' conclusion that X causes Y is not fully supported by the data. The observational design cannot establish causality. Consider revising to state 'X is associated with Y' and discussing alternative explanations."

**Bad:**
> "The authors clearly don't understand causation. This claim is ridiculous and shows poor scientific training."

### Specific and Constructive

**Good:**
> "The sample size calculation appears incorrect. Using G*Power with the reported effect size (d=0.4), α=0.05, and power=0.80 requires n=100 per group. The current n=60 per group provides only 60% power."

**Bad:**
> "Sample size is wrong."

### Focused on Science, Not Personalities

**Good:**
> "The methodology section lacks detail on the randomization procedure. Please specify: (1) randomization method, (2) allocation concealment, (3) who generated the sequence."

**Bad:**
> "The authors are careless and didn't describe their methods properly. This is typical of their sloppy work."

## Common Reviewer Errors

### The Dismissive Reviewer
- Rejects without explanation
- Provides no constructive feedback
- Unhelpful to editors and authors

### The Nitpicker
- Focuses on trivial issues
- Misses major flaws
- Delays publication unnecessarily

### The Overreacher
- Demands new experiments
- Wants a different study
- Unreasonable expectations

### The Biased Reviewer
- Competitive blocking
- Favors friends/punishes rivals
- Lets personal feelings interfere

### The Ghost Reviewer
- Accepts assignment
- Never submits review
- Wastes everyone's time

## Handling Specific Situations

### Conflict of Interest

**Declare:**
- Competing research
- Personal relationships
- Financial interests

**Action:**
- Decline review
- Inform editor of COI
- Let editor decide

### Inadequate Expertise

**Recognize:**
- Methods outside your knowledge
- Unfamiliar field
- Too specialized

**Action:**
- Decline promptly
- Suggest alternative reviewers
- Don't attempt half-informed review

### Suspected Misconduct

**Red Flags:**
- Image manipulation
- Duplicate publication
- Plagiarism
- Fabricated data

**Action:**
- Document specific concerns
- Report to editor confidentially
- Don't accuse publicly
- Let institution investigate

### Previous Review

**Resubmission:**
- Check if you reviewed before
- Assess response to previous comments
- Verify changes made
- Note persistent issues

## Review Timeline

### Initial Review

**Standard:** 2-4 weeks
**Rapid:** 1-2 weeks (if agreed)
**Extension:** Request if needed

**Process:**
1. First read: Overall impression
2. Second read: Detailed notes
3. Write review: Structured format
4. Final check: Tone, clarity
5. Submit: Before deadline

### Re-review

**After Revision:**
- Check if all concerns addressed
- Verify changes adequate
- New issues?
- Recommendation update

**Timeline:** Usually shorter (1-2 weeks)

## For Authors: Responding to Reviews

### Point-by-Point Response

**Format:**
- Copy each reviewer comment
- Provide detailed response
- Indicate changes made
- Justify if not changed

**Example:**

**Comment 1:**
> "The sample size seems inadequate. Please provide power calculation."

**Response:**
> "Thank you for this important point. We have added a power calculation in the Methods section (Page 8, lines 45-50). Using G*Power with effect size d=0.5, α=0.05, and power=0.80, we required 64 participants per group. We enrolled 70 per group to account for 10% dropout."

### Tone

**Be Respectful:**
- Thank reviewers
- Acknowledge valid points
- Explain disagreements calmly
- Don't be defensive

**Be Specific:**
- Page/line references
- Exact changes
- Copy relevant text

### Strategy

**Major Concerns:**
- Address completely
- Make requested changes
- If disagreeing, explain scientifically
- May need new analyses

**Minor Concerns:**
- Implement if reasonable
- Most should be addressed
- Some negotiable

## Summary

**Key Principles:**
1. **Structured review:** Summary → Major → Minor → Recommendation
2. **Professional tone:** Respectful, specific, constructive
3. **Timely completion:** Meet deadlines or decline
4. **Honest assessment:** Call out flaws, acknowledge strengths
5. **Constructive critique:** Suggest solutions, not just problems
6. **Confidentiality:** Protect unpublished work
7. **Scientific focus:** On methodology, data, ethics—not personalities

**Clinical Pearl:** The best reviewers improve science by helping authors make their work better. Be the reviewer you'd want reviewing your work—thorough, fair, constructive, and timely.
