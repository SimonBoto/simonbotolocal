## Core Claim
**Four essential amino acids—isoleucine, methionine, threonine, and valine—undergo catabolism producing propionyl-CoA or methylmalonyl-CoA, requiring vitamin B12 for complete oxidation to succinyl-CoA** — this makes B12 essential for energy extraction from dietary protein.

## Evidence

### Amino Acid Pathways
| Amino Acid | Class | Intermediate | B12 Required |
|------------|-------|--------------|--------------|
| **Isoleucine** | BCAA | Propionyl-CoA | Yes |
| **Valine** | BCAA | Methylmalonyl-CoA | Yes |
| **Methionine** | Sulfur | Propionyl-CoA | Yes |
| **Threonine** | Hydroxy | Propionyl-CoA | Yes |

### Common Features
- **All essential:** Must be obtained from diet
- **All glucogenic:** Can produce glucose via succinyl-CoA
- **All B12-dependent:** Cannot fully oxidize without B12
- **All anaplerotic:** Replenish Krebs cycle intermediates

### Catabolic Pattern
1. **Transamination** — removes amino group
2. **Oxidative decarboxylation** — produces acyl-CoA
3. **Multiple steps** — to propionyl-CoA or methylmalonyl-CoA
4. **B12-dependent conversion** — to succinyl-CoA

## Clinical Implication
- **High protein intake increases B12 demand** — each gram of protein from these amino acids requires B12 for complete oxidation
- **B12 deficiency limits protein energy extraction** — propionyl-CoA accumulates
- **Athletes on high-protein diets** particularly vulnerable to marginal B12 deficiency
- **METHAP relevance:** High-dose B12 supports protein metabolism; important for high-protein therapeutic protocols
