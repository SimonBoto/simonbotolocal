# Aconitase Neurodegeneration Marker

## Core Claim

**Mitochondrial aconitase (ACO2) is a sentinel biomarker for neurodegenerative diseases** — its Fe-S cluster vulnerability to ROS makes it an early indicator of mitochondrial dysfunction in Parkinson's and Alzheimer's disease.

## Mechanism

### Why Neurons Are Vulnerable

| Feature | Consequence |
|---------|-------------|
| **High metabolic demand** | Mitochondria work near capacity |
| **High iron content** | Fenton chemistry → ROS |
| **Low antioxidant defenses** | Less catalase, glutathione |
| **Post-mitotic cells** | No replacement, cumulative damage |

### Aconitase in PD/AD

**Parkinson's Disease:**
- Complex I inhibition → electron leak → O₂⁻ ↑
- Aconitase [4Fe-4S] destroyed → citrate accumulates
- Dopaminergic neurons particularly vulnerable

**Alzheimer's Disease:**
- Aβ oligomers enter mitochondria
- Fe-S cluster destabilization
- Energy failure precedes plaque formation

## Evidence

| Study | Finding |
|-------|---------|
| **Ghosh et al., 2021** | ACO2 activity ↓ 40% in PD patient fibroblasts |
| **Mandel et al., 2005** | Aconitase inactivation precedes dopaminergic loss |
| **Crouch et al., 2005** | Aβ binds ACO2, inhibits activity |

## Clinical Potential

| Application | Status |
|-------------|--------|
| **Early biomarker** | Promising (peripheral cells mirror brain) |
| **Therapeutic target** | Fe-S biogenesis inhibitors protect neurons |
| **Drug screening** | Aconitase activity as readout |
