# Fatty Acid Oxidation Disorder Differential Diagnosis

**Overview:** Dicarboxylic aciduria occurs in multiple FAODs — specific patterns and additional markers help distinguish them.

## Primary FAODs Causing Dicarboxylic Aciduria

### 1. Medium-Chain Acyl-CoA Dehydrogenase Deficiency (MCADD)
**Most common FAOD (1:15,000 births)**

**Metabolic Block:**
- Defective MCAD enzyme
- Cannot perform first step of medium-chain FA β-oxidation
- Accumulation of C6-C10 

**Laboratory Pattern:**
- **Dicarboxylic acids:** Elevated (adipate, suberate, sebacate)
- **Acylcarnitines:** Elevated C6-C10 (hexanoyl-, octanoyl-, decanoylcarnitine)
- **Urinary glycine conjugates:** Hexanoylglycine, suberylglycine
- **Ketones:** Low (paradoxical hypoketosis during hypoglycemia)

**Clinical Presentation:**
- Hypoketotic hypoglycemia (triggered by fasting/illness)
- Onset: 3 months to 3 years
- Seizures, encephalopathy, coma
- Sudden infant death (untreated)

---

### 2. Carnitine Palmitoyltransferase I Deficiency (CPT I)
**Transport defect — liver predominant**

**Metabolic Block:**
- Cannot conjugate long-chain FAs with carnitine
- FAs cannot enter 

**Laboratory Pattern:**
- **Dicarboxylic acids:** Elevated (often very high)
- **Total carnitine:** Markedly elevated (cannot be utilized)
- **Acylcarnitines:** Decreased long-chain species
- **Free fatty acids:** Elevated in blood

**Clinical Presentation:**
- Hepatic encephalopathy
- Hypoketotic hypoglycemia
- Onset: infancy
- Normal cardiac/skeletal muscle (CPT I not expressed there)

---

### 3. Carnitine Palmitoyltransferase II Deficiency (CPT II)
**Transport defect — muscle and liver**

**Three Clinical Forms:**

**Severe infantile:**
- Cardiomyopathy, hepatomegaly, renal dysgenesis
- Often fatal

**Milder infantile/hepatic:**
- Hypoketotic hypoglycemia
- Hepatomegaly

**Myopathic (adult-onset):**
- Exercise-induced muscle pain/weakness
- Myoglobinuria
- Triggered by prolonged exercise, fasting, illness

**Laboratory Pattern:**
- **Dicarboxylic acids:** Elevated during crises
- **Acylcarnitines:** Elevated long-chain species (C16, C18)
- **CK:** Elevated during rhabdomyolysis

---

### 4. Carnitine-Acylcarnitine Translocase Deficiency (CACT)
**Severe, rare defect**

**Metabolic Block:**
- Cannot transport acylcarnitines across inner  membrane

**Clinical Presentation:**
- Severe neonatal onset
- Cardiac arrhythmias, cardiomyopathy
- High mortality

**Laboratory Pattern:**
- **Dicarboxylic acids:** Elevated
- **Acylcarnitine profile:** Diagnostic (elevated plasma, decreased intramitochondrial)

---

### 5. Very Long-Chain Acyl-CoA Dehydrogenase Deficiency (VLCADD)
**Variable severity**

**Clinical Forms:**
- Severe (cardiac phenotype)
- Hepatic (hypoketotic hypoglycemia)
- Later-onset myopathic

**Laboratory Pattern:**
- **Dicarboxylic acids:** May be elevated
- **Acylcarnitines:** Elevated C14-C18 species

---

## Acquired vs Genetic: Key Distinctions

| Feature | Genetic FAOD | Acquired Carnitine Deficiency |
|---------|-------------|------------------------------|
| **Onset** | Infancy/childhood | Any age |
| **Family history** | Often positive | Negative |
| **Consistency** | Persistent | Variable, correctable |
| **Carnitine response** | Partial | Often complete |
| **Genetic testing** | Mutation identified | Negative |

## Diagnostic Workup Algorithm

```
Dicarboxylic aciduria identified
            ↓
    Acylcarnitine profile
            ↓
Specific pattern? —→ Genetic testing
            ↓
    No clear pattern
            ↓
Carnitine profile + Clinical history
            ↓
Acquired cause? —→ Treat and re-test
            ↓
Persistent? —→ Expanded testing (biotinidase, mitochondrial DNA)
```
