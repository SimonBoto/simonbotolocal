## Core Claim
**Glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase (G6PD) deficiency is the most common enzyme deficiency worldwide (400M+ people), causing hemolytic anemia under oxidative stress — the X-linked mutation persists due to heterozygote advantage against malaria** — RBCs are especially vulnerable due to lack of nucleus and dependence on PPP for NADPH.

## Evidence

### Prevalence and Genetics
| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Prevalence** | 400M+ people worldwide |
| **Inheritance** | X-linked recessive |
| **Variants** | 400+ identified |
| **Distribution** | Mediterranean, African, Asian populations |

### Pathophysiology
```
G6PD deficiency → Impaired PPP → Low NADPH
→ Cannot maintain reduced glutathione (GSH)
→ RBCs vulnerable to oxidative damage → Hemolysis
```

### Triggers of Hemolysis
| Category | Examples |
|----------|----------|
| **Foods** | Fava beans (vicine, convicine) |
| **Drugs** | Primaquine, dapsone, sulfa antibiotics |
| **Infections** | Oxidative stress from illness |
| **Chemicals** | Naphthalene (mothballs) |

### Why RBCs Vulnerable?
- **No nucleus:** Limited protein synthesis
- **No mitochondria:** Dependent on PPP for NADPH
- **High oxygen exposure:** Oxidative stress risk

### Evolutionary Advantage
| Genotype | Malaria Resistance |
|----------|-------------------|
| **Normal** | Susceptible |
| **Heterozygote (female)** | **Protected** |
| **Deficient (male)** | Variable |

## Clinical Implication
- **Diagnosis:** G6PD activity assay, genetic testing
- **Management:** Avoid triggers, transfuse if severe
- **Neonatal jaundice:** Common in deficient infants
- **METHAP relevance:** G6PD deficiency affects antioxidant capacity; high-dose nutrients may increase oxidative stress — screening important in high-risk populations
