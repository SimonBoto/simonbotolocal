## Mechanism

**The transsulfuration pathway converts homocysteine to cysteine via cystathionine, serving as the primary endogenous source of cysteine for glutathione synthesis** — this B6-dependent pathway is activated by high SAM (methionine abundance), creating a metabolic switch that diverts homocysteine toward antioxidant production when methylation capacity is sufficient.

## Mechanism

**The transsulfuration pathway converts homocysteine to cysteine via cystathionine, serving as the primary endogenous source of cysteine for glutathione synthesis** — this B6-dependent pathway is activated by high SAM (methionine abundance), creating a metabolic switch that diverts homocysteine toward antioxidant production when methylation capacity is sufficient.

## Evidence

### Two-Step Enzymatic Process
| Step | Enzyme | Reaction | Cofactor |
|------|--------|----------|----------|
| **1** | CBS | Homocysteine + Serine → Cystathionine | Vitamin B6 |
| **2** | CGL | Cystathionine → Cysteine + α-Ketobutyrate + NH₃ | Vitamin B6 |

### Regulation by SAM
| SAM Level | CBS Activity | Metabolic Fate |
|-----------|--------------|----------------|
| **High SAM** | Activated | Transsulfuration favored |
| **Low SAM** | Inhibited | Homocysteine recycled to methionine |

### Tissue Distribution
- **Liver** — Primary site
- **Kidney, pancreas, brain** — Secondary sites

## Clinical Implication
- **B6 deficiency** impairs transsulfuration → elevated homocysteine + reduced glutathione synthesis
- **CBS mutations** (C677T) reduce enzyme activity → homocysteinemia risk
- **High methionine intake** without adequate B6/B12/folate can overwhelm the pathway
- **METHAP relevance:** B6 is essential for both transsulfuration steps; monitoring homocysteine assesses pathway function
