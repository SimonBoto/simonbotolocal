# MAO Enzymes MAO-A and MAO-B — Functions, Inhibitors, and Clinical Significance

## Overview

Monoamine oxidases (MAO) are mitochondrial enzymes that catalyze the oxidative deamination of monoamines, playing critical roles in neurotransmitter metabolism and drug action.

## Isoforms

### MAO-A

**Gene:** X chromosome (Xp11.3)

**Substrate Preference:**
- Serotonin (5-HT) — primary
- Norepinephrine
- Epinephrine

**Tissue Distribution:**
- Brain (raphe nuclei, noradrenergic neurons)
- Liver
- Intestinal mucosa
- Placenta
- Platelets

**Inhibition:**
- Preferentially inhibited by **clorgyline**
- Low-dose **moclobemide** (reversible)
- **Harmine** (natural alkaloid)

**Clinical Relevance:**
- Target for depression treatment
- Serotonin metabolism critical
- Dietary tyramine sensitivity (with non-selective inhibitors)

### MAO-B

**Gene:** X chromosome (Xp11.3)

**Substrate Preference:**
- Dopamine — primary
- Phenylethylamine
- Benzylamine

**Tissue Distribution:**
- Brain (astrocytes, striatum)
- Platelets
- Liver
- Lymphocytes

**Inhibition:**
- Preferentially inhibited by **selegiline (L-deprenyl)**
- **rasagiline**
- **safinamide**
- Low-dose **pargyline**

**Clinical Relevance:**
- Target for Parkinson's disease
- Dopamine preservation in striatum
- Less dietary restriction than MAO-A inhibition

## Biochemistry

### Reaction Mechanism

**Oxidative Deamination:**
```
R-CH2-NH2 + O2 + H2O → R-CHO + NH3 + H2O2
```

**Cofactor:**
- FAD (flavin adenine dinucleotide) — covalently bound

**Byproducts:**
- Aldehyde (metabolized by aldehyde dehydrogenase)
- Ammonia
- Hydrogen peroxide (H2O2)

**H2O2 Significance:**
- Reactive oxygen species generation
- Oxidative stress
- Potential neurotoxicity
- Link to neurodegeneration theories

## MAO Inhibitors (MAOIs)

### Non-Selective (MAO-A + MAO-B)

**Irreversible:**
- **Phenelzine** (Nardil)
- **Tranylcypromine** (Parnate)
- **Isocarboxazid** (Marplan)

**Clinical Use:**
- Atypical depression
- Treatment-resistant depression
- Social anxiety disorder

**Side Effects:**
- Hypertensive crisis (tyramine)
- Serotonin syndrome (with SSRIs)
- Orthostatic hypotension
- Weight gain
- Insomnia

### MAO-A Selective

**Reversible (RIMA):**
- **Moclobemide** (Manerix, Aurorix)

**Advantages:**
- Less dietary restriction
- Shorter washout
- Lower tyramine sensitivity

### MAO-B Selective

**Agents:**
- **Selegiline (L-deprenyl)** — irreversible
- **Rasagiline** — irreversible
- **Safinamide** — reversible, additional mechanisms

**Clinical Use:**
- Parkinson's disease (adjunctive)
- Early monotherapy
- Neuroprotection debated

**Advantages:**
- Minimal dietary restrictions
- Well tolerated
- Selective dopamine enhancement

**Safinamide (Xadago) Special Features:**
- Reversible MAO-B inhibition
- Sodium channel blockade
- Calcium channel modulation
- Glutamate release inhibition
- No effect on DAO

## Dietary Considerations

### Tyramine Effect

**Mechanism:**
- MAO normally degrades dietary tyramine
- Inhibition allows accumulation
- Sympathetic release of norepinephrine
- Hypertensive crisis possible

**High-Tyramine Foods:**
- Aged cheeses
- Cured meats
- Fermented foods
- Soy products
- Beer/wine (tap)
- Fava beans

**MAO-B Selectivity Advantage:**
- MAO-B doesn't metabolize tyramine significantly
- MAO-A in gut handles dietary tyramine
- Selegiline, rasagiline, safinamide: minimal dietary restriction

### Serotonin Syndrome

**Risk:**
- MAOI + SSRI/SNRI
- MAOI + tramadol
- MAOI + meperidine
- MAOI + St. John's wort

**Symptoms:**
- Mental status changes
- Autonomic instability
- Neuromuscular abnormalities
- Hyperthermia

**Prevention:**
- Washout periods (2-5 weeks)
- Avoid concurrent serotonergic drugs
- Patient education

## Pharmacogenetics

### MAO-A Gene Polymorphisms

**MAO-A-uVNTR:**
- 3-repeat: Lower expression
- 4-repeat: Higher expression
- Affects aggression, anxiety phenotypes

**Clinical Relevance:**
- Variable drug response
- Behavioral associations

### MAO-B Gene

**Less polymorphic**
- More consistent activity
- Less clinical variation

## Clinical Applications

### Depression

**MAOIs Effective For:**
- Atypical depression
- Treatment-resistant cases
- Bipolar depression
- Social anxiety

**Selection:**
- RIMAs (moclobemide): First-line MAOI
- Irreversible: Treatment-resistant

### Parkinson's Disease

**Rationale:**
- Preserve endogenous dopamine
- Reduce dopamine degradation
- Symptomatic benefit
- Possible disease modification

**Evidence:**
- Delays need for levodopa (selegiline)
- Adjunctive benefit (all MAO-B inhibitors)
- Symptom fluctuation reduction

### Neuroprotection Hypothesis

**Theory:**
- MAO inhibition reduces H2O2 production
- Less oxidative stress
- Potential disease-slowing

**Evidence:**
- Controversial
- Some support from DATATOP trial (selegiline)
- Not definitively proven

## Summary

**Key Points:**
1. MAO-A: Serotonin, norepinephrine — target for depression
2. MAO-B: Dopamine — target for Parkinson's
3. Non-selective MAOIs: Dietary restrictions, more side effects
4. MAO-B selective: Better tolerated, minimal dietary limits
5. H2O2 byproduct links to oxidative stress theories
6. Drug interactions (serotonin syndrome, tyramine) critical

**Clinical Pearl:** MAO-B inhibitors (selegiline, rasagiline, safinamide) are preferred in Parkinson's due to selectivity and lack of dietary restrictions. MAO-A remains important for specific depression subtypes.
