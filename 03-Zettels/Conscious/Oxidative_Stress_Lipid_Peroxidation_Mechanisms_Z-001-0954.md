# Oxidative Stress — Lipid Peroxidation Mechanisms

## Core Concept

Polyunsaturated fatty acids (PUFAs), particularly omega-6 linoleic acid, are **highly susceptible to oxidation** due to multiple double bonds. This lipid peroxidation generates reactive aldehydes that cause cellular damage and propagate oxidative stress.

## Why PUFAs Are Vulnerable

**Structural Factor:**
- Each double bond creates an **allylic position** (CH₂ group between double bonds)
- Allylic hydrogens are **easily abstracted** by free radicals
- Linoleic acid (18:2) has 2 double bonds → 1 allylic position
- Arachidonic acid (20:4) has 4 double bonds → 3 allylic positions

**Relative Oxidation Rates (simplified):**
| Fatty Acid | Double Bonds | Relative Oxidation Rate |
|------------|--------------|------------------------|
| Stearic acid (18:0) | 0 | 1 (baseline) |
| Oleic acid (18:1) | 1 | 10 |
| Linoleic acid (18:2) | 2 | 100 |
| Linolenic acid (18:3) | 3 | 150 |
| Arachidonic acid (20:4) | 4 | 200 |

## Lipid Peroxidation Pathways

### 1. Free Radical Chain Reaction (Classical)

```
INITIATION:
•OH (hydroxyl radical) + LH (PUFA) → L• (lipid radical) + H₂O

PROPAGATION:
L• + O₂ → LOO• (lipid peroxyl radical)
LOO• + LH → LOOH (lipid hydroperoxide) + L•
(L• continues chain — each radical generates multiple products)

TERMINATION:
LOO• + LOO• → Non-radical products
LOO• + Vitamin E-OH → LOOH + Vitamin E-O• (chain break)
```

### 2. Enzymatic Pathways

**Lipoxygenases (LOX):**
- 5-LOX, 12-LOX, 15-LOX isoforms
- Convert arachidonic acid to leukotrienes, HETEs
- Pro-inflammatory signaling molecules

**Cyclooxygenases (COX-1, COX-2):**
- Convert arachidonic acid to prostaglandins, thromboxanes
- Both pro- and anti-inflammatory products

## Toxic Aldehyde Products

### 4-HNE (4-Hydroxynonenal)

**Formation:**
- Oxidation of omega-6 PUFAs (linoleic, arachidonic)
- Major product — most studied reactive aldehyde

**Reactivity:**
- Highly electrophilic — attacks nucleophilic groups
- Forms **Michael adducts** with cysteine, histidine, lysine
- Forms **Schiff bases** with primary amines

**Protein Targets:**
- Enzyme active sites (cysteine residues)
- Structural proteins
- Membrane transporters
- Transcription factors (NF-κB, AP-1)

**Cellular Effects:**
| Target | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| Keap1-Nrf2 pathway | Nrf2 activation → antioxidant gene expression |
| Mitochondria | Cytochrome c release, apoptosis induction |
| DNA | Mutagenic adducts (etheno-DNA adducts) |
| Membranes | Disruption of protein-lipid interactions |
| Signaling kinases | JNK, p38 MAPK activation |

**Paradox:** Low levels → hormetic signaling; High levels → cytotoxicity

### MDA (Malondialdehyde)

**Formation:**
- Secondary product from arachidonic acid oxidation
- Two-step process via prostaglandin-like intermediates

**Reactivity:**
- Forms **cross-links** between proteins (lysine-lysine)
- Creates protein aggregates
- Less reactive than 4-HNE but more abundant

**Measurement:**
- **TBARS assay** (thiobarbituric acid reactive substances)
- Common but non-specific marker
- Better: LC-MS/MS methods

**Clinical Significance:**
- Elevated in metabolic syndrome, diabetes, CVD
- Correlates with disease severity
- Used as oxidative stress 

### Acrolein

**Formation:**
- Secondary product from glycerophospholipid oxidation
- Also from: amino acid , polyamine oxidation, smoking

**Toxicity:**
- Most reactive α,β-unsaturated aldehyde
- Strong irritant (respiratory, ocular)
- Forms adducts faster than 4-HNE

**Targets:**
- Cysteine residues (preferred target)
- DNA (deoxyguanosine adducts)
- Surfactant proteins (lung damage)

## Protein and DNA Damage

### Protein Modification Consequences

**Enzyme Inhibition:**
- Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase
- Ca²⁺-ATPase
- Mitochondrial complexes
- Antioxidant enzymes (catalase, SOD)

**Protein Aggregation:**
- Cross-linking creates insoluble aggregates
- Proteasome overload
- Cellular clearance mechanisms overwhelmed

### DNA Damage

**Etheno Adducts:**
- 1,N⁶-ethenoadenine
- 3,N⁴-ethenocytosine
- 1,N²-ethenoguanine

**Consequences:**
- Mutations (A→G, C→A transversions)
- Replication errors
- Cancer risk (liver, colon)

## Systemic Effects

### Atherosclerosis

**Mechanism:**
1. Oxidized LDL formation (oxLDL)
2. Scavenger receptor uptake by macrophages
3. Foam cell formation
4. Plaque progression

**Key Role:** 4-HNE modification of ApoB-100

### Insulin Resistance

**Mechanism:**
- 4-HNE modifies IRS-1, insulin receptor
- Impaired signaling
- GLUT4 translocation blocked

### Neurodegeneration

**Mechanism:**
- High brain PUFA content vulnerable to oxidation
- 4-HNE modifies proteins in Alzheimer's brain
- Mitochondrial dysfunction in neurons

## Antioxidant Defenses

**Primary:**
- **Vitamin E (α-tocopherol):** Chain-breaking in membranes
- **Vitamin C:** Water-soluble, regenerates vitamin E
- **Glutathione (GSH):** Direct scavenging, conjugation with aldehydes

**Secondary:**
- **ALDH (aldehyde dehydrogenase):** Detoxifies 4-HNE
- **GST (glutathione S-transferase):** Conjugates 4-HNE to GSH
- **GPx (glutathione peroxidase):** Reduces lipid hydroperoxides

## Clinical Assessment

**:**
| Marker | What It Measures |
|--------|------------------|
| Plasma 4-HNE protein adducts | Recent oxidative damage |
| Urinary MDA | Integrated oxidative stress |
| F₂-isoprostanes | Non-enzymatic arachidonic acid oxidation |
| Isofurans | Oxidative stress under high oxygen |
| Oxidized LDL | Cardiovascular risk |
