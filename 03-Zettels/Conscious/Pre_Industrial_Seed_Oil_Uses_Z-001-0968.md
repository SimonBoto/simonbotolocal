## Historical Context

Before industrial extraction methods, seed oils were produced through **labor-intensive mechanical pressing** and used primarily for **non-food applications.** Edible consumption was limited and local.

## Early Extraction Methods

### Traditional Mechanical Pressing

**Process:**
1. Seeds cleaned and dried
2. Ground or crushed (mortar and pestle, millstones)
3. Heated slightly to improve flow
4. Pressed using screw or lever presses
5. Oil collected, filtered through cloth

**Efficiency:**
- Yield: 60-80% of oil extracted
- Residual oil in cake: 20-40%
- Used for animal feed or fertilizer

**Types of Presses:**
- **Screw presses:** Worm screw 
- **Lever presses:** Mechanical advantage
- **Hydraulic presses:** Water-powered (later development)
- **Animal-powered:** Mills driven by oxen, horses

### Cold vs Heat

**Cold pressing:**
- No external heat applied
- Better quality but lower yield
- Preferred for finest oils

**Warm pressing:**
- Gentle heating before pressing
- Improved yield
- Slight quality reduction

## Primary Historical Uses

### 1. Illumination — Lamp Oil

**Importance:**
- Before electricity, lighting was essential
- Whale oil expensive and limited
- Seed oils provided alternative

**Preferred Oils:**
| Oil | Characteristics | Use |
|-----|-----------------|-----|
| **Rapeseed (canola)** | Burned cleanly, bright flame | Preferred in Europe |
| **Flaxseed (linseed)** | Good burning quality | Widespread use |
| **Cottonseed** | Available in South | American use |
| **Sesame** | Ancient use | Middle East, Asia |

**Challenges:**
- Rancidity over time
- Soot production
- Odor
- Less bright than whale oil

**Decline:**
- Kerosene (1850s) — brighter, cheaper, longer-lasting
- Electric light (1880s onward)
- Seed oil lamp use faded

### 2. Industrial Lubrication

**Industrial R Demand:**
- New machinery required lubrication
- Animal fats (tallow, lard) oxidized quickly
- Whale oil expensive
- Seed oils filled the gap

**Applications:**
- **Steam engines:** Cylinder lubrication
- **Textile mills:** Spinning machinery
- **Metal working:** Cutting fluids
- **Railways:** Axle grease
- **Leather tanning:** Softening oils

**Preferred Oils:**
- **Rapeseed:** High viscosity, stable
- **Castor oil:** Excellent lubricity
- **Linseed:** Drying oil properties
- **Whale oil:** Premium (when available)

**Limitations:**
- Oxidative instability (become gummy)
- Temperature sensitivity
- Shorter service life than mineral oils

**Transition:**
- Petroleum-based lubricants (late 1800s)
- More stable, cheaper
- Seed oils relegated to specialized uses

### 3. Paints, Varnishes, Coatings

**Drying Oils:**
Oils that polymerize (dry) when exposed to air

**Key Oils:**
| Oil | Use | Property |
|-----|-----|----------|
| **Linseed** | Oil paints, varnishes, linoleum | Excellent drying |
| **Tung oil** | Wood finishing | Waterproof, hard finish |
| **Walnut oil** | Artists' paints | Slow drying, clear |
| **Poppy seed oil** | White paints | Non-yellowing |

**Linseed Oil Dominance:**
- Binder in oil paints
- Varnish base
- Linoleum flooring (invented 1860)
- Printing inks
- Still used today in artists' materials

**Chemistry:**
- High linolenic acid (omega-3) content
- Triple bonds oxidize and cross-link
- Forms durable film

### 4. Soap Making

**Saponification:**
- Oil + alkali (lye) → soap + glycerol
- Cold process or hot process methods

**Oils Used:**
- **Cottonseed oil:** Cheap, abundant (later 1800s)
- **Coconut oil:** Hard soap, good lather
- **Olive oil:** Castile soap (premium)
- **Tallow:** Traditional soap (animal)

**Seed Oil Advantage:**
- Cheaper than animal fats
- Milder soap
- Mass production possible

**Procter & Gamble Connection:**
- Soap makers before Crisco
- Used cottonseed oil in soap
- Industrialized production

### 5. Food Use — Limited and Local

**Traditional Consumption:**
- **Olive oil:** Mediterranean (ancient use)
- **Sesame oil:** Asia, Middle East
- **Flaxseed oil:** Northern Europe (limited)
- **Rapeseed oil:** India, China (pungent varieties)

**Constraints on Widespread Use:**
1. **Extraction difficulty:** Labor-intensive
2. **Rancidity:** Limited shelf life
3. **Strong flavors:** Unpalatable varieties
4. **Cost:** More expensive than animal fats
5. **Availability:** Local production only

**Animal Fat Dominance:**
- Butter, lard, tallow primary cooking fats
- Stable, familiar, locally produced
- Seed oils supplementary only

## Regional Variations

### Europe

**Mediterranean:**
- Olive oil (fruit, not seed) — ancient food use
- Rapeseed oil: industrial (lamp oil, lubricant)

**Northern Europe:**
- Rapeseed oil: industrial
- Flaxseed oil: paint, varnish, limited food
- Butter, lard: primary food fats

### Asia

**India:**
- Sesame oil: food and religious use
- Mustard oil (rapeseed relative): food and medicine
- Coconut oil: food and cosmetic

**China:**
- Soybean oil: later development (20th century)
- Rapeseed oil: industrial and food
- Peanut oil: food (Southern China)

### Americas

**Pre-Columbian:**
- Limited seed oil use
- Animal fats, some nut oils

**Colonial Period:**
- Imported oils
- Local tallow, lard dominant

**19th Century:**
- Cottonseed oil: byproduct of cotton industry
- Initially industrial use

## Transition to Food Use

### Enabling Factors

**1. Industrial Extraction (late 1800s):**
- Hydraulic presses
- Higher yields
- Lower costs

**2. Refining Development:**
- Degumming, neutralization
- Bleaching, deodorization
- Removed flavors, extended shelf life

**3. Hydrogenation (1901):**
- Solid fats from liquid oils
- Crisco (1911) — first major food product

**4. Marketing:**
- Procter & Gamble campaigns
- "Scientific" modern image
- Recipe books, cooking schools

## Significance

**Pre-Industrial Paradigm:**
- Seed oils = industrial/luxury, not staple food
- Local production, limited trade
- Animal fats dominated 
- Human consumption of concentrated PUFA minimal

**Metabolic Implications:**
- Evolutionary exposure to linoleic acid: very low (<1% calories)
- Modern exposure: 10-15%+ calories
- Dramatic shift in 100 years
