# Physiological vs Pathological Ketosis

## Key Distinction
| Feature | **Physiological Ketosis** | **Pathological Ketosis (DKA)** |
|---------|---------------------------|--------------------------------|
| **Ketone levels** | 1-3 mM | >10-25 mM |
| **Blood pH** | Normal (7.35-7.45) | Low (<7.30) |
| **Bicarbonate** | Normal | Low (<18 mEq/L) |
| **** | Low but present | Absent/deficient |
| **Glucose** | Normal or low | Very high (>250 mg/dL) |
| **Clinical state** | Asymptomatic/symptomatic | Life-threatening |

## Physiological Ketosis

### al Ketosis
- **Ketogenic diet:** 70-80% fat
- **Fasting:** >24-48 hours
- **Exercise:** Prolonged endurance

**Characteristics:**
- Controlled, regulated
- pH maintained by buffering systems
- Brain adapts to use ketones
- **Safe and beneficial**

### Therapeutic Applications
- Epilepsy (especially pediatric)
- Metabolic syndrome
- Neurodegenerative diseases (investigational)
- Weight management

## Pathological Ketosis (Diabetic Ketoacidosis - DKA)

### Pathophysiology
**Type 1 :**
- Absolute insulin deficiency
- Uncontrolled lipolysis
- Massive ketone overproduction
- **Cannot be suppressed**

### Clinical Features
- **Dehydration:** Osmotic diuresis from hyperglycemia
- **Acidosis:** Ketones overwhelm buffering capacity
- **Electrolyte imbalance:** K⁺ shifts, dehydration
- **Altered consciousness:** Severe acidosis

### Treatment
1. **IV fluids:** Rehydration
2. **Insulin:** Stops ketogenesis
3. **Electrolytes:** K⁺, phosphate replacement
4. **Bicarbonate:** If pH <6.9 (controversial)

## Why the Difference?

### Insulin Presence
| State | Insulin | Effect |
|-------|---------|--------|
| **Nutritional ketosis** | Low but present | Limits lipolysis, prevents overproduction |
| **DKA** | Absent | Uncontrolled ketogenesis |

### Buffering Capacity
- **Physiological:** Bicarbonate, proteins maintain pH
- **DKA:** Buffering overwhelmed by massive ketone production

### Glucose vs Ketones
- **Nutritional:** Low glucose + moderate ketones
- **DKA:** High glucose + massive ketones (double problem)

## Starvation Ketosis vs DKA
**Starvation:**
- No insulin needed (not diabetic)
- Glucose low
- Ketones moderate (3-5 mM)
- **Safe**

**DKA:**
- Insulin required (Type 1)
- Glucose very high
- Ketones extreme (>10 mM)
- **Emergency**
