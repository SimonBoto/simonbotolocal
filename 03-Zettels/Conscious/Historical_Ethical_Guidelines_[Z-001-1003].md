# Historical Ethical Guidelines — Nuremberg Code to Declaration of Helsinki

## Overview

Modern  ethics emerged from responses to atrocities and ethical failures in human experimentation. Understanding this history is essential for appreciating current ethical frameworks.

## Nuremberg Code (1947)

### Historical Context

**Nuremberg Doctors' Trial (1946-1947):**
- 23 Nazi doctors and administrators tried
- Medical experiments on concentration camp prisoners
- No consent, extreme cruelty, pseudoscience

**Experiments Included:**
- High-altitude exposure
- Freezing experiments
- Sulfanilamide wounds
- Twins research (Mengele)
- Sterilization methods
- Poison experiments

**Victims:**
- Jews, Romani, disabled, Soviet POWs
- Estimated thousands killed
- Countless maimed and traumatized

### The Code (10 Points)

**1. Voluntary Consent:**
- Essential, primary consideration
- Legal capacity to consent
- Free from coercion
- Understanding of experiment

**2. Beneficial Results:**
- Must yield fruitful results
- Unprocurable by other means

**3. Based on Prior Knowledge:**
- Animal experimentation first
- Natural history of disease known

**4. Avoid Unnecessary Harm:**
- Avoid all unnecessary physical/mental suffering

**5. No Expectation of Death/Disability:**
- Experiment should not be conducted if death/disability expected

**6. Risk Proportionate to Importance:**
- Degree of risk never exceeds humanitarian importance

**7. Proper Preparations:**
- Adequate facilities
- Protection against injury

**8. Scientifically Qualified:**
- Only scientifically qualified persons
- Highest degree of skill and care

**9. Right to Withdraw:**
- Subject can stop at any time

**10. Termination by Investigator:**
- Scientist must terminate if continuation likely to result in injury/disability/death

### Significance

**First International Code:**
- First formal ethical code for human experimentation
- Voluntary consent as cornerstone
- Foundation for all subsequent guidelines

**Limitations:**
- Narrow focus on researcher-subject relationship
- No enforcement 
- No mention of independent review

## Declaration of Helsinki (1964-Present)

### Development

**World Medical Association (WMA):**
- Response to Nuremberg
- Medical profession self-regulation
- Periodic revisions (1964, 1975, 1983, 1989, 1996, 2000, 2008, 2013)

###  of Key Concepts

**1964 Original:**
- Basic principles for physicians
- Distinction therapeutic/non-therapeutic research
- Consent requirements

**1975 Revision:**
- Ethics committee review added
- Independent committee requirement

**1983, 1989 Revisions:**
- Expanded protections
- Greater detail

**1996 Revision:**
- Placebo use restrictions
- Use in developing countries
- Post-trial access

**2000 Revision:**
- Paragraph 29 (placebo) controversy
- Stronger protections
- Greater transparency

**2008, 2013 Revisions:**
- Post-trial obligations strengthened
- Registration and publication emphasized
- Vulnerable populations protections

### Current Key Principles (2013)

**General Principles:**
1. Physician duty to protect health and rights of subjects
2. Research must follow scientific principles
3. Research ethics committee approval required
4. Research must be conducted by qualified persons

**Risks, Burdens, Benefits:**
5. Research must have potential to produce beneficial results
6. Risk-benefit ratio favorable
7. Risks continuously monitored
8. Care provided for subjects

**Vulnerable Groups:**
9. Special protections for vulnerable
10. Research not conducted on vulnerable if can be done on non-vulnerable
11. Research essential for vulnerable group's condition acceptable

**Scientific Requirements:**
12. Based on adequate laboratory and animal experimentation
13. Research protocol reviewed

**Informed Consent:**
14. Potential subject must be adequately informed
15. Right to refuse or withdraw without penalty
16. Special provisions for incompetent persons
17. Written consent generally required

**Use of Placebo:**
18. Placebo only when methodologically necessary AND no serious harm
19. Standard therapy must be provided when withholding would cause serious harm

**Post-Trial Provisions:**
20. Participants entitled to share benefits
21. Post-trial access to beneficial intervention
22. Research registered and results published/disclosed

### Controversies

**Paragraph 29 (Placebo):**
- Debate over placebo use when standard treatment exists
- 2000 version criticized as allowing exploitation
- 2008/2013 revisions strengthened protections

**Developing Countries:**
- Standard of care debates
- Post-trial access obligations
- Exploitation concerns

**Enforcement:**
- Voluntary guidelines
- No legal force (unlike regulations)
- Moral authority

## Belmont Report (1979)

### Context

**National Commission:**
- Established 1974 (after Tuskegee Syphilis Study revealed)
- Response to research ethics scandals
- Four years of study

**Tuskegee Syphilis Study (1932-1972):**
- 600 African American men (399 syphilis, 201 control)
- No informed consent
- Treatment withheld even after penicillin available (1940s)
- Subjects deceived
- Continued 40 years

### Three Fundamental Principles

**1. Respect for Persons:**
- Individuals as autonomous agents
- Protection of those with diminished autonomy
- Informed consent essential

**Implementation:**
- Recognize autonomy
- Respect decisions
- Protect vulnerable
- Obtain consent

**2. Beneficence:**
- Do no harm (non-maleficence)
- Maximize possible benefits
- Minimize possible harms

**Implementation:**
- Systematic risk-benefit assessment
- Alternative approaches considered
- Risk minimization
- Benefit maximization

**3. Justice:**
- Fair distribution of benefits and burdens
- Who bears risks? Who receives benefits?
- No exploitation of vulnerable

**Implementation:**
- Fair subject selection
- No undue burden on vulnerable
- Benefits shared
- Not just convenient/populations unable to refuse

### Applications

**Informed Consent:**
- Information
- Comprehension
- Voluntariness

**Risk-Benefit Assessment:**
- Systematic evaluation
- Nature and probability of harms/benefits
- Alternatives

**Subject Selection:**
- Fairness
- Avoid exploitation
- Community input

### Impact

**U.S. Policy:**
- Basis for 45 CFR 46 (Common Rule)
- IRB requirements
- Federal policy

**Global Influence:**
- Widely cited
- Influenced international guidelines
- Taught in ethics education

## Comparative Summary

| Feature | Nuremberg (1947) | Helsinki (1964+) | Belmont (1979) |
|---------|------------------|------------------|----------------|
| **Origin** | War crimes trial | Medical profession | National Commission |
| **Binding** | No (judgment) | No (voluntary) | No (policy basis) |
| **Focus** | Consent | Comprehensive | Principles-based |
| **Key Innovation** | First code | Medical profession self-reg | Three principles |
| **Review Body** | Not mentioned | Ethics committee (post-1975) | IRB |

## Contemporary Relevance

### Why History Matters

**Lessons Learned:**
- Without ethical constraints, research harms
- Vulnerable populations at greatest risk
- Self-regulation insufficient
- Independent oversight essential

**Ongoing Challenges:**
- Global research ethics
- Post-trial obligations
- Biobanking and genetics
- Big data and AI

**Core Values Endure:**
- Voluntary consent
- Risk minimization
- Fair subject selection
- Independent review
