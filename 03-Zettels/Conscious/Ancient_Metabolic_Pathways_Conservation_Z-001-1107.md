## Core Claim
**Glycolysis, the pentose phosphate pathway, and core amino acid biosynthesis pathways are universally conserved across all domains of life, representing metabolic solutions developed in early evolution (possibly the RNA world) that have been maintained for billions of years** — this conservation indicates these pathways are optimal solutions to fundamental metabolic challenges.

## Evidence

### Universal Distribution
| Pathway | Distribution | Conservation Evidence |
|---------|--------------|----------------------|
| **Glycolysis** | All organisms | Same 10 steps, enzyme homologies |
| **PPP** | All organisms | Shared intermediates with glycolysis |
| **Iron-sulfur metabolism** | All life | Ancient cofactors, prebiotic plausibility |

### Why These Pathways Are Ancient
| Feature | Advantage | Evidence |
|---------|-----------|----------|
| **Anaerobic** | Preceded oxygenic photosynthesis | No oxygen required |
| **Simple substrates** | Prebiotic availability | Glucose, ribose plausible early |
| **Net energy gain** | Thermodynamic favorability | 2 ATP (glycolysis) |
| **RNA connection** | Ribose for nucleic acids | PPP generates ribose |

### The RNA World Hypothesis
- **Ribose from PPP** → RNA synthesis
- **ATP from glycolysis** → Energy for RNA replication
- **Before DNA/proteins** — metabolism and genetics unified in RNA

## Clinical Implication
- **Conservation implies optimality** — these pathways are robust, hard to improve upon
- **Genetic defects in these pathways** are often lethal (essential for life)
- **METHAP relevance:** Supporting ancient pathways (glycolysis, PPP) with cofactors (B vitamins, magnesium) ensures fundamental metabolic competence
