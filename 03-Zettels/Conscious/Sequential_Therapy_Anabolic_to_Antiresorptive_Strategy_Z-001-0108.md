# Sequential Therapy — Anabolic to Antiresorptive Strategy

## Core Principle
**Anabolic agents build bone mass but have duration limits; antiresorptive agents preserve gains.** Sequential use maximizes benefit.

## The Rationale

### Anabolic Limitations
| Limitation | Implication |
|------------|-------------|
| **Duration limits** | 2 years (PTH-based), 12 months (romosozumab) |
| **Bone loss after stop** | Rapid turnover rebound if no follow-up therapy |
| **Cost** | Expensive ($20,000–40,000 for course) |
| **Administration burden** | Daily or monthly injections |

### Antiresorptive Role in Sequence
- **Preserve:** Maintain BMD gains achieved with anabolic
- **Protect:** Prevent new fractures
- **Consolidate:** Allow continued mineralization of new bone matrix
- **Maintain:** Long-term therapy without duration limits (bisphosphonates)

## Evidence-Based Sequences

### 1. Teriparatide → Alendronate (PaTH Study)
**Design:**
- 1 year teriparatide → 1 year alendronate vs. alendronate alone

**Results:**
- **Spine BMD:** +14.3% (sequential) vs. +7.6% (alendronate alone)
- **Hip BMD:** +4.8% vs. +2.8%
- **Conclusion:** Sequential superior to antiresorptive alone

### 2. Romosozumab → Alendronate (ARCH Trial)
**Design:**
- 12 months romosozumab → 12 months alendronate vs. alendronate alone

**Results:**
- **Vertebral fractures:** 73% reduction vs. placebo at 24 months
- **BMD maintenance:** Gains preserved with alendronate
- **Risk:** Cardiovascular signal in romosozumab arm

### 3. Teriparatide → Denosumab (DATA Extension)
**Design:**
- 2 years teriparatide → 2 years denosumab

**Results:**
- **Continued BMD gain:** During denosumab phase
- **Hip BMD:** Reached +10.5% at 4 years
- **Spine BMD:** +18% at 4 years
- **Superior to either alone**

### 4. Romosozumab → Denosumab (STRUCTURE)
**Design:**
- 12 months romosozumab → 12 months denosumab vs. teriparatide → denosumab

**Results:**
- **BMD gains:** Comparable between anabolic starters
- **Transition:** Smooth, no loss between agents

##  Protocols

### Standard Sequential Approach

#### Option A: PTH-Based Sequence (24 months anabolic)
```
Months 0–24:   Teriparatide OR Abaloparatide (daily SC)
Month 24:      Transition to antiresorptive
Months 24+:    Alendronate (oral) OR Denosumab (SC q6 months)
Duration:      Long-term antiresorptive (5+ years typical)
```

#### Option B: Anti-Sclerostin Sequence (12 months anabolic)
```
Months 0–12:   Romosozumab (monthly SC)
Month 12:      Transition to antiresorptive
Months 12+:    Denosumab (SC q6 months) OR Zoledronate (annual IV)
Duration:      Long-term antiresorptive
```

### Transition Timing
- **Immediate:** No washout needed between anabolic and antiresorptive
- **DEXA monitoring:** 12–18 months after transition
- **Biochemical markers:** Optional (CTX, PINP) to assess suppression

## Choosing the Sequence

### Patient Factors Favoring Specific Anabolics

| Factor | Preferred Anabolic | Rationale |
|--------|-------------------|-----------|
| **Severe osteoporosis, need rapid gain** | Romosozumab | Fastest, dual action |
| **High CV risk** | Teriparatide/Abaloparatide | No CV signal |
| **Injection convenience priority** | Romosozumab | Monthly vs. daily |
| **Longest track record desired** | Teriparatide | Most experience |
| **Hypercalcemia concern** | Abaloparatide | Lower incidence |

### Antiresorptive Choice After Anabolic

| Follow-up | Advantages | Considerations |
|-----------|-----------|----------------|
| **Denosumab** | Potent, rapid, no GI side effects | Rebound risk if stopped; requires ongoing injections |
| **Zoledronate (IV)** | Annual dosing, potent | Acute phase reaction, renal function |
| **Alendronate (oral)** | Oral, established, inexpensive | GI side effects, adherence challenges |
| **Risedronate (oral)** | Oral, possibly better hip protection | Similar GI considerations |

## What Happens Without Sequencing?

### Anabolic Alone (No Follow-up)
- **Rapid bone loss:** Within 6–12 months of stopping
- ** fracture risk returns:** To baseline or higher
- **Wasted investment:** Expensive anabolic gains lost

### Antiresorptive Alone (No Anabolic First)
- **Prevents loss:** But doesn't build new bone
- **Limited in severe disease:** May be insufficient for multiple fractures
- **Appropriate for:** Moderate risk, not severe osteoporosis

## Monitoring Sequential Therapy

### During Anabolic Phase
| Time | Assessment |
|------|------------|
| Baseline | DEXA, calcium, creatinine,  |
| 6 months | Adherence, adverse events, calcium |
| 12 months | DEXA (if romosozumab), biochemical markers |
| 24 months | DEXA (if PTH-based), transition planning |

### Post-Transition
| Time | Assessment |
|------|------------|
| 6 months post | Adherence to antiresorptive, calcium (if denosumab) |
| 12–18 months post | DEXA to confirm preservation of gains |
| Annually | DEXA monitoring per standard osteoporosis protocol |

## Economic Considerations

### Cost Analysis (Approximate USD)
| Phase | Agent | Cost |
|-------|-------|------|
| Anabolic (2 years) | Teriparatide | ~$40,000–50,000 |
| Anabolic (1 year) | Romosozumab | ~$25,000–30,000 |
| Follow-up (5 years) | Denosumab | ~$25,000–30,000 |
| **Total 7-year course** | Sequential | ~$65,000–80,000 |

### Value Proposition
- **Fracture cost:** Hip fracture ~$40,000 acute + long-term care
- **Cost-effective:** Sequential therapy cost-effective in high-risk patients
- **QALY:** Generally favorable cost per quality-adjusted life year

## Special Populations

### Glucocorticoid-Induced Osteoporosis
- **Recommendation:** Anabolic first (teriparatide preferred)
- **Evidence:** Superior to alendronate in GIOP
- **Duration:** 12–24 months anabolic, then transition

### Men with Osteoporosis
- **Option:** Teriparatide or romosozumab
- **Evidence:** Anabolics effective but less studied than in women

### Prior Antiresorptive Failure (Fracture on Therapy)
- **Strategy:** Switch to anabolic, then continue antiresorptive
- **Rationale:** Indicates need for formation stimulation
