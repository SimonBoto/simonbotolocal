## Core Claim
**Glutamate is the metabolic crossroads of amino acid metabolism — it serves as the primary amino group acceptor from most amino acids (via transamination), the amino group donor for amino acid synthesis, a neurotransmitter in the brain, and an entry point to the Krebs cycle as α-ketoglutarate** — this central position makes glutamate essential for nitrogen metabolism, energy production, and neural function.

## Evidence

### Four Central Roles
| Role | Function | Mechanism |
|------|----------|-----------|
| **Amino group receiver** | Collects amino groups from transamination | Transaminases (B6-dependent) |
| **Amino group donor** | Provides amino groups for synthesis | Transamination |
| **Energy metabolism** | Enters Krebs cycle | Glutamate dehydrogenase → α-KG |
| **Neurotransmission** | Major excitatory neurotransmitter | Glutamate receptors (NMDA, AMPA) |

### Glutamate ↔ α-Ketoglutarate
```
Glutamate + NAD⁺ + H₂O → α-Ketoglutarate + NH₄⁺ + NADH (GDH)
Glutamate + α-keto acid ↔ α-KG + amino acid (transaminase)
```

### Glutamate as GABA Precursor
```
Glutamate → GABA (glutamate decarboxylase, B6-dependent)
```
- **GABA:** Major inhibitory neurotransmitter
- **Balance:** Glutamate/GABA balance critical for neural function

### Nitrogen Flow
```
Most amino acids → Glutamate (transamination)
     ↓
Glutamate → Urea cycle (nitrogen disposal)
     ↓
Or α-KG → Krebs cycle (energy)
```

## Clinical Implication
- **Hyperammonemia:** Impaired glutamate metabolism → neurotoxicity
- **Excitotoxicity:** Excess glutamate → neuronal damage (stroke, TBI)
- **B6 deficiency:** Impairs glutamate/GABA balance → seizures
- **METHAP relevance:** Glutamate hub requires B6; metabolic health supports neural function
