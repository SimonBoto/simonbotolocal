# SAH — Methylation Inhibitor and SAM/SAH Ratio

> After donating its methyl group, SAM becomes S-adenosylhomocysteine (SAH), a potent product inhibitor of methyltransferases. The SAM/SAH ratio serves as the cellular index of methylation capacity.

## Formation

**Reaction:**
```
SAM + Acceptor → Methylated acceptor + SAH
```

**Universal byproduct:** Every methylation reaction produces SAH

## Inhibitory Function

**Product Inhibition:**
- SAH inhibits most methyltransferases
- Competitive inhibition (structurally similar to SAM)
- **High SAH = Low methylation activity**

**:**
- Binds methyltransferase active site
- Prevents SAM binding
- Reversible inhibition

## SAH Hydrolysis

**Reaction:**
```
SAH ↔ Homocysteine + Adenosine
```

**Enzyme:** SAH hydrolase (reversible)

**Direction:** Depends on homocysteine and adenosine concentrations

## The SAM/SAH Ratio

**Methylation Index:**
| Ratio | Interpretation |
|-------|----------------|
| **High (>4)** | Good methylation capacity |
| **Low (<2)** | Impaired methylation |
| **Very low (<1)** | Severe methylation deficiency |

** Correlations:**
- **Low ratio:** Depression, cognitive decline, CVD
- **Optimal ratio:** Supports DNA repair, neurotransmitter synthesis

**Factors Affecting Ratio:**
- B12/folate status (affects SAM synthesis)
- Homocysteine clearance (affects SAH hydrolysis)
- Methylation demand (affects SAM consumption)

## Clinical Significance

**Elevated SAH:**
- B12 deficiency
- Folate deficiency
- Impaired homocysteine clearance
- Result: Global hypomethylation risk
