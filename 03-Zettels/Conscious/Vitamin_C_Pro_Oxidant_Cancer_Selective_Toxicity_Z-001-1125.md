## Core Claim
Pharmacological doses of intravenous vitamin C (achieving plasma concentrations >1,000 μM) generate hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂) through auto-oxidation, which is selectively toxic to cancer cells due to their relative deficiency in catalase—the enzyme that detoxifies H₂O₂—while normal cells remain protected, creating a therapeutic window for selective cancer cell killing.

## Key Details

### The Dose Distinction

| Parameter | Dietary (Oral) | Pharmacological (IV) |
|-----------|---------------|---------------------|
| Plasma levels | <100 μM | >1,000–10,000 μM |
| Absorption | Limited by intestinal transporters | Bypasses intestinal limits |
| Primary effect | Antioxidant | Pro-oxidant |
| Function | Essential nutrient | Therapeutic agent |

### Mechanism of H₂O₂ Generation

**Auto-Oxidation Reaction:**
```
Ascorbate + O₂ → Dehydroascorbate + H₂O₂
```

**Catalysis:**
- Transition metal ions (Fe²⁺, Cu⁺) in extracellular fluid
- Generates H₂O₂ locally at pharmacologic concentrations

**H₂O₂ as Signaling Molecule:**
- Diffuses into cells
- Oxidizes sensitive targets
- At high levels: induces cell death

### Selective Toxicity Mechanism

| Cell Type | Catalase Activity | H₂O₂ Handling | Outcome |
|-----------|------------------|---------------|---------|
| **Normal cells** | High | Rapid detoxification | Protected |
| **Cancer cells** | Reduced (downregulated) | Accumulation | Cell death |

**Why Reduced Catalase in Cancer:**
- Part of metabolic reprogramming (Warburg effect)
- Downregulated in many tumor types
- Creates therapeutic vulnerability exploitable by high-dose vitamin C

### Experimental Evidence

| Model | Findings |
|-------|----------|
| Cell culture | Cancer cells killed; normal cells survive |
| Xenografts | Tumor growth inhibition |
| Syngeneic models | Enhanced survival |
| Combination | Synergistic with chemotherapy/radiation |

**Dose-Response:**
- Cytotoxic threshold: ~1,000 μM
- Maximum tolerated: ~20,000 μM
- Therapeutic window exists

## Significance

Demonstrates that a simple nutrient, at pharmacologic concentrations, can exploit a metabolic vulnerability created by cancer's own reprogramming. Connects redox biology to cancer therapy and provides mechanistic rationale for integrative oncology approaches.

## Clinical Significance

**Administration:**
- Must be intravenous (oral cannot achieve pharmacologic levels)
- Dose: 10–100g per infusion
- Frequency: 2–3× per week
- Duration: 1–2 hours per infusion

**Monitoring:**
- Renal function (oxalate excretion)
- Glucose (interference with glucometers)
- Iron status (enhanced absorption)
- Optional: plasma vitamin C levels

**Safety:**
- Generally well-tolerated
- Rare: hemolysis in G6PD deficiency
- Caution: iron overload patients
- Oxalate kidney stones (rare at therapeutic doses)

**Contraindications:**
- G6PD deficiency
- History of oxalate kidney stones
- Iron overload (hemochromatosis)
