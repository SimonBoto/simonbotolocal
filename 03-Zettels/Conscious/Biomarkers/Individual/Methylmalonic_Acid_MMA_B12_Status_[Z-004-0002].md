# Methylmalonic Acid (MMA) — Functional B12 Status

## 1. ETYMOLOGY

**Methylmalonic Acid:** From methyl + malonic acid (dicarboxylic acid)

**Metabolic origin:** Intermediate in propionate metabolism

**Clinical significance:** Elevated when B12-dependent enzyme impaired

source-litnote: Synthesized from clinical practice
---

## 2. PHYSIOLOGY

### Biological Function

**Metabolic pathway:**
Propionyl-CoA → (B12-dependent mutase) → Succinyl-CoA → Krebs cycle

**When B12 deficient:**
- Mutase enzyme blocked
- Methylmalonyl-CoA accumulates
- Converted to MMA
- Excreted in urine

### Why MMA is Superior to Serum B12

**Serum B12 problems:**
- Measures total B12 (active + inactive analogs)
- Late marker (low only when deficiency severe)
- Normal range too broad
- Doesn't reflect tissue status

**MMA advantages:**
- Reflects functional B12 activity
- Elevates early in deficiency
- Tissue-level indicator
- Specific for B12 (not folate)

## 3. METABOLIC INTERPRETATION

### MMA Levels

| Level | Status | Interpretation |
|-------|--------|----------------|
| **<0.4 μmol/L** | Optimal | Adequate B12 function |
| **<0.6 μmol/L** | Acceptable | Normal |
| **0.6-1.0 μmol/L** | Borderline | Early functional deficiency |
| **>1.0 μmol/L** | Elevated | B12 deficiency |
| **>1.5 μmol/L** | High | Significant deficiency |

### Causes of Elevation

**B12 deficiency (primary cause):**
- Inadequate intake (vegan, elderly)
- Malabsorption (pernicious anemia, gastritis, PPIs)
- Metformin use
- Nitrous oxide exposure

**Other causes (rare):**
- Methylmalonic acidemia (genetic)
- Kidney disease (reduced clearance)

### Clinical Significance

**Neurological symptoms with elevated MMA:**
- Peripheral neuropathy
- Subacute combined degeneration of cord
- Cognitive impairment
- Dementia
- Psychiatric symptoms

**MMA elevation precedes:**
- Anemia (macrocytic)
- Neurological damage
- Homocysteine elevation

## 4. COFACTORS

### B12 Forms and MMA

| Form | Effect on MMA |
|------|---------------|
| **Cyanocobalamin** | Reduces MMA (synthetic, stable) |
| **Methylcobalamin** | Reduces MMA (active, neurological) |
| **Hydroxocobalamin** | Reduces MMA (long-acting, detox) |
| **Adenosylcobalamin** | Reduces MMA (mitochondrial) |

## 5. INTERVENTIONS

### For Elevated MMA

**B12 supplementation:**
| Form | Dose | Route |
|------|------|-------|
| **Methylcobalamin** | 1000-2000 mcg/day | Oral or sublingual |
| **Hydroxocobalamin** | 1000 mcg/week | IM injection (severe) |

**Supportive:**
- Folate (5-MTHF): 400-800 mcg/day (needed for methionine cycle)
- B6 (P5P): 25-50 mg/day
- Avoid nitrous oxide

### Monitoring

**Retest:** 8-12 weeks
**Target:** <0.4 μmol/L (optimal)

**Note:** MMA may transiently increase when B12 started (tissue uptake)

## 6. CONNECTED BIOMARKERS

| Biomarker | Relationship | Pattern |
|-----------|--------------|---------|
| **Homocysteine** | Both elevated in B12 deficiency | MMA + Homocysteine ↑ = B12 deficiency |
| **Serum B12** | Often normal when MMA elevated | MMA more sensitive |
| **MCV** | Macrocytosis late finding | MMA elevates before MCV increases |
| **Holotranscobalamin** | Active B12 fraction | Low with elevated MMA |

### Clinical Patterns

**Early B12 Deficiency:**
- MMA: Elevated
- Homocysteine: Normal or mildly elevated
- Serum B12: Normal or low-normal
- **MMA most sensitive marker**

**Established B12 Deficiency:**
- MMA: Elevated
- Homocysteine: Elevated
- Serum B12: Low
- MCV: High (if anemia developed)

**B12 Deficiency with Folate Excess:**
- MMA: Elevated
- Homocysteine: Normal (folate masks)
- **Dangerous — neurological damage without anemia**
