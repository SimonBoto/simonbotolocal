# Ferritin — Iron Storage & Systemic Status

## 1. ETYMOLOGY

**Origin:** From Italian "ferrito" (iron) + "-in" (protein suffix)

**Discovered:** 1937 by V. Laufberger — isolated from horse spleen

**Evolution of understanding:**
- 1930s: Iron storage protein
- 1970s: Acute phase reactant
- 1990s: Linked to insulin resistance
- 2000s: Mitochondrial function, thyroid connection
- Present: Systemic metabolic indicator beyond iron

## 2. PHYSIOLOGY

### Biological Function

**Primary Role: Iron Storage**
- Stores iron in soluble, non-toxic form
- Prevents iron-catalyzed free radical formation
- Releases iron on demand for:
  - Hemoglobin synthesis
  - Myoglobin production
  - Mitochondrial enzymes (ETC complexes)
  - DNA synthesis (ribonucleotide reductase)

**Structure:**
- Protein shell (apoferritin): 24 subunits
- Central cavity: stores up to 4,500 iron atoms as ferric hydroxide phosphate
- Heavy chain (H): ferroxidase activity (iron oxidation)
- Light chain (L): iron nucleation/storage

### Cellular Dynamics

**Iron Entry:**
Fe²⁺ → (ferroportin export) → Fe³⁺ (ceruloplasmin oxidation) → binds transferrin → cellular uptake via TfR1 → endosome acidification → Fe²⁺ release (STEAP3) → ferritin storage

**Iron Release:**
Ferritin → lysosomal degradation (ferritinophagy) → Fe²⁺ release via NCOA4 → cellular use or export via ferroportin

## 3. METABOLIC INTERPRETATION

### Ferritin as Iron Status Indicator

| Ferritin | Iron Status | Interpretation |
|----------|-------------|----------------|
| **<30 ng/mL** | Depleted stores | Absolute iron deficiency |
| **30-50 ng/mL** | Low-normal | Early depletion, functional deficiency possible |
| **50-100 ng/mL** | Adequate | Normal storage (women) |
| **100-300 ng/mL** | Replete | Normal storage (men) |
| **>300 ng/mL** | Elevated | Inflammation, metabolic syndrome, hemochromatosis |

### The Inflammation Trap

**Critical Concept:** Ferritin is an **acute phase reactant**

**Mechanism:**
- IL-6 → hepatocyte ferritin synthesis ↑
- Inflammation can **falsely elevate** ferritin
- Normal/high ferritin with inflammation ≠ adequate iron

**Clinical consequence:**
- Iron deficiency missed in inflammatory states
- Must check with CRP, other inflammatory markers

### Ferritin & Insulin Resistance

**Discovery:** Elevated ferritin independently associated with:
- Insulin resistance
- Type 2 diabetes risk
- Metabolic syndrome

**Mechanisms:**
1. **Iron-catalyzed oxidative stress** → β-cell damage
2. **Mitochondrial dysfunction** → impaired glucose oxidation
3. **Inflammation** → chronic low-grade activation

**Clinical threshold:**
- Women: >120 ng/mL associated with metabolic dysfunction
- Men: >300 ng/mL associated with metabolic dysfunction

### Ferritin & Thyroid Function

**Bidirectional relationship:**

**Iron required for:**
- Thyroid peroxidase (TPO) — iodine organification
- Thyroid hormone synthesis
- T4 → T3 conversion (deiodinases contain iron-sulfur clusters)

**Low ferritin → hypothyroid symptoms:**
- Fatigue (iron + thyroid)
- Cold intolerance
- Hair loss
- Cognitive slowing

**Correction:** Iron repletion often improves thyroid function even with "normal" TSH

### Mitochondrial Connection

**Iron-sulfur cluster biogenesis:**
- Requires functional ferritin-iron pool
- Essential for:
  - Complex I, II, III (ETC)
  - Aconitase (Krebs cycle)
  - DNA repair enzymes

**Low ferritin → mitochondrial dysfunction:**
- Reduced ETC efficiency
- Increased oxidative stress
- Fatigue, exercise intolerance

## 4. COFACTORS

### Required for Iron Metabolism

| Nutrient | Role | Deficiency Effect |
|----------|------|-------------------|
| **Copper** | Ceruloplasmin ferroxidase | Functional iron deficiency |
| **Vitamin A** | Hepcidin regulation, iron mobilization | Iron trapping in macrophages |
| **Vitamin C** | Non-heme iron absorption, ferroxidase | Reduced absorption |
| **B12/Folate** | Erythropoiesis demand | Functional deficiency |
| **Protein** | Transferrin, ferritin synthesis | Low binding capacity |

### Copper-Ferritin Connection

**Critical and often missed:**
- Copper required for ceruloplasmin
- Ceruloplasmin oxidizes Fe²⁺ → Fe³⁺ for transferrin binding
- Copper deficiency → **functional iron deficiency** despite adequate iron stores

**Pattern:** Low ferritin + normal/high serum iron + low TIBC = suspect copper

## 5. INTERVENTIONS

### For Low Ferritin (<30 ng/mL)

**Tier 1: Dietary**
- Heme iron: red meat, liver, oysters (best absorption)
- Non-heme iron: lentils, spinach, fortified foods + vitamin C
- Avoid: tea, coffee with meals (tannins inhibit absorption)

**Tier 2: Supplementation**
| Form | Dose | Notes |
|------|------|-------|
| Ferrous bisglycinate | 25-50mg elemental | Best tolerated |
| Ferrous sulfate | 65mg elemental | Cheap, more GI side effects |
| Heme iron | 11-22mg | From bovine source, well absorbed |

**Timing:**
- Away from calcium, thyroxine, PPIs
- With vitamin C (500mg)
- Every other day may improve absorption vs. daily

**Duration:**
- Replete stores: 3-6 months
- Maintenance: ongoing if chronic loss (menstruation, GI)

**Monitoring:**
- Recheck ferritin in 8-12 weeks
- Target: >50 ng/mL (women), >100 ng/mL (men)

### For Elevated Ferritin (Inflammation Pattern)

**Women: >120 ng/mL, Men: >300 ng/mL with inflammation**

**Address root cause:**
- CRP elevation → anti-inflammatory protocol
- Metabolic syndrome → insulin sensitization
- Infection → treat underlying

**Therapeutic phlebotomy:**
- If hemochromatosis suspected
- If ferritin >1000 ng/mL (organ damage risk)

**Dietary:**
- Reduce heme iron if excessive
- Increase polyphenols (tea between meals)
- Moderate alcohol (increases absorption)

## 6. CONNECTED BIOMARKERS

### Essential Pairings

| Biomarker | Relationship | Pattern Interpretation |
|-----------|--------------|----------------------|
| **CRP** | Ferritin is acute phase reactant | High CRP + high ferritin = inflammation, not iron overload |
| **TIBC** | Inverse relationship | High TIBC + low ferritin = iron deficiency |
| **Transferrin Saturation** | Functional assessment | <20% with low ferritin = deficiency; >50% with high ferritin = overload |
| **Serum Iron** | Immediate availability | Low with low ferritin = deficiency; normal/high with low ferritin = functional (check copper) |
| **MCV** | RBC size | Low MCV + low ferritin = iron deficiency anemia |
| **TSH** | Thyroid-iron interdependence | Hypothyroid + low ferritin = both need correction |
| **HbA1c** | Metabolic-iron link | Elevated with high ferritin = insulin resistance pattern |
| **Citrate (OAT)** | Mitochondrial function | Low citrate + low ferritin = Fe-S cluster impairment |

### Clinical Patterns

**Pattern 1: Absolute Iron Deficiency**
- Ferritin: <30
- TIBC: High
- Transferrin sat: <20%
- MCV: Low or normal
- CRP: Normal

**Pattern 2: Functional Iron Deficiency (Inflammation)**
- Ferritin: Normal/high (30-100+)
- TIBC: Low-normal
- Transferrin sat: <20%
- CRP: Elevated
- **Iron trapped, not usable**

**Pattern 3: Functional Iron Deficiency (Copper)**
- Ferritin: Low-normal
- Serum iron: Normal/high
- TIBC: Low
- Ceruloplasmin: Low
- **Copper deficiency blocking iron mobilization**

**Pattern 4: Iron Overload**
- Ferritin: >300 (men), >200 (women)
- Transferrin sat: >50%
- CRP: Normal
- **Hemochromatosis or excess intake**

**Pattern 5: Metabolic Ferritin Elevation**
- Ferritin: >120 (women), >300 (men)
- HbA1c: Elevated
- CRP: Mild elevation
- Transferrin sat: Normal
- **Insulin resistance, not iron overload**

## 7. OPTIMAL RANGE

| Population | Optimal | Rationale |
|------------|---------|-----------|
| **Women (premenopausal)** | 50-100 ng/mL | Adequate for menstruation, thyroid, mitochondrial function |
| **Women (postmenopausal)** | 80-150 ng/mL | Higher acceptable without menstrual loss |
| **Men** | 100-200 ng/mL | Adequate storage without metabolic risk |
| **Athletes** | 50-100 ng/mL | Higher turnover, monitor closely |

## 9. PROBLEMATIC RANGE

| Level | Concern | Action |
|-------|---------|--------|
| **<15 ng/mL** | Severe deficiency, anemia risk | Immediate iron supplementation, investigate cause |
| **<30 ng/mL** | Depleted stores, symptoms likely | Iron supplementation, dietary increase |
| **>200 (women), >300 (men)** | Metabolic/inflammatory concern | Check CRP, HbA1c, investigate cause |
| **>1000 ng/mL** | Organ damage risk (liver, heart) | Urgent phlebotomy, hemochromatosis workup |

## 10. CLINICAL WEIGHT

**Significance: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (5/5 — Critical)**

**Why ferritin is essential:**
1. **Most sensitive iron marker** — changes before anemia develops
2. **Systemic indicator** — reflects inflammation, metabolism, not just iron
3. **Thyroid function** — required for hormone synthesis and conversion
4. **Mitochondrial health** — Fe-S cluster biogenesis
5. **Metabolic syndrome** — independent risk marker

**Always order with:**
- TIBC or transferrin
- Serum iron
- Transferrin saturation
- CRP (to interpret elevation)
- MCV (if anemia suspected)

**Never interpret alone.**

## CLINICAL SCENARIOS

### Scenario 1: The Tired Female Executive
**Presentation:** 38-year-old, fatigue, hair loss, brain fog, normal TSH

**Labs:**
- Ferritin: 18 ng/mL
- TIBC: High
- Transferrin sat: 15%
- CRP: Normal
- TSH: 2.5

**Interpretation:** Absolute iron deficiency without anemia yet

**Action:**
1. Ferrous bisglycinate 50mg elemental daily
2. Vitamin C 500mg with iron
3. Recheck in 8 weeks
4. Investigate cause (menstrual loss, GI)

### Scenario 2: The Inflammatory Obesity
**Presentation:** 52-year-old, obesity, prediabetes, fatigue

**Labs:**
- Ferritin: 180 ng/mL
- CRP: 8 mg/L
- HbA1c: 6.2%
- Transferrin sat: 25%

**Interpretation:** Elevated ferritin from inflammation, not iron overload

**Action:**
1. Do NOT give iron
2. Address insulin resistance (berberine, diet, exercise)
3. Anti-inflammatory protocol (omega-3, curcumin)
4. Recheck ferritin after metabolic improvement

### Scenario 3: The Hypothyroid with "Normal" Ferritin
**Presentation:** 45-year-old, hypothyroid symptoms, TSH 3.5, on levothyroxine

**Labs:**
- Ferritin: 28 ng/mL
- TSH: 3.5
- Free T3: Low-normal

**Interpretation:** Suboptimal ferritin impairing thyroid function and conversion

**Action:**
1. Iron supplementation to reach >50 ng/mL
2. May improve thyroid function without dose increase
3. Recheck TSH, free T3 in 8-12 weeks
