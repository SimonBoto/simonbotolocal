# Uric Acid — Urate, Purine Metabolite

## 1. ETYMOLOGY

**Uric acid:** From Greek "ouron" (urine) — first isolated from urine

**Urate:** Salt form (uric acid dissociates at physiological pH)

**Also called:**
- UA
- Urate

**Discovery:** 1776 by Carl Wilhelm Scheele

## 2. PHYSIOLOGY

### Biological Function

**End product of purine metabolism:**
- Purines (adenine, guanine) → hypoxanthine → xanthine → uric acid
- Xanthine oxidase catalyzes final steps

**Humans lack uricase:**
- Cannot convert uric acid to allantoin
- Higher levels than most mammals
- **Antioxidant properties at moderate levels**

### Sources

**Endogenous:**
- Cellular turnover (DNA/RNA breakdown)
- ~70% of total

**Exogenous:**
- Dietary purines (organ meats, seafood, alcohol)
- ~30% of total

### Excretion

- Kidneys: 70% (primary route)
- Gut: 30%

## 3. METABOLIC INTERPRETATION

### Uric Acid Levels

| Level (mg/dL) | Status | Interpretation |
|---------------|--------|----------------|
| **<4.0** | Low | May be protective |
| **4.0-6.0** | Optimal | Ideal range |
| **6.0-7.0** | Borderline | Monitor |
| **7.0-9.0** | Elevated | Gout risk, metabolic syndrome |
| **>9.0** | High | High gout risk, kidney stones |

### Clinical Significance

**Elevated uric acid (Hyperuricemia):**

| Cause | Mechanism |
|-------|-----------|
| **Gout** | Crystal deposition in joints |
| **Kidney disease** | Reduced excretion |
| **Metabolic syndrome** | Insulin resistance reduces excretion |
| **Dehydration** | Concentrated urine |
| **High purine diet** | Increased production |
| **Alcohol** | Increases production, reduces excretion |
| **Diuretics** | Reduce excretion |
| **Tumor lysis** | Massive cell breakdown |

**Low uric acid (Hypouricemia):**
- Rare
- May indicate overexcretion (Fanconi syndrome)
- Uricosuric drugs

## 4. COFACTORS

### Uric Acid Metabolism

| Factor | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| **Fructose** | Increases production (ATP depletion) |
| **Alcohol** | Increases production, decreases excretion |
| **Insulin** | Reduces renal excretion |
| **Dehydration** | Concentrates uric acid |
| **Purine-rich foods** | Increases substrate |

## 5. INTERVENTIONS

### For Elevated Uric Acid

**Lifestyle:**
- Reduce alcohol (especially beer, spirits)
- Limit fructose (sodas, fruit juice)
- Reduce purine-rich foods (organ meats, anchovies, sardines)
- Increase water intake
- Weight loss
- Coffee (may reduce risk)

**Medications:**
| Drug | Mechanism | Use |
|------|-----------|-----|
| **Allopurinol** | Xanthine oxidase inhibitor | First line |
| **Febuxostat** | Xanthine oxidase inhibitor | If allopurinol intolerant |
| **Probenecid** | Uricosuric (increases excretion) | Underexcretors |
| **Colchicine** | Anti-inflammatory | Acute gout |
| **NSAIDs** | Anti-inflammatory | Acute gout |

**Target:** <6 mg/dL (or <5 mg/dL if tophi present)

## 6. CONNECTED BIOMARKERS

| Biomarker | Relationship | Pattern |
|-----------|--------------|---------|
| **Creatinine** | Kidney function | Elevated creatinine → elevated uric acid |
| **Glucose/Insulin** | Metabolic syndrome | Insulin resistance → hyperuricemia |
| **Triglycerides** | Metabolic syndrome | Often elevated together |
| **CRP** | Inflammation | Gout attacks elevate CRP |

### Clinical Patterns

**Metabolic Syndrome:**
- Uric acid: Elevated
- Glucose: Elevated
- Triglycerides: Elevated
- HDL: Low
- **Insulin resistance drives hyperuricemia**

**Gout:**
- Uric acid: Often elevated (may be normal during attack)
- Joint aspiration: Monosodium urate crystals
- **Needle-shaped, negatively birefringent**
