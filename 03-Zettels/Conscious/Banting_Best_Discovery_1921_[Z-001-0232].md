# Banting and Best — The 1921 Discovery of Insulin

> In the summer of 1921, surgeon Frederick Banting and medical student Charles Best conducted experiments at the University of Toronto that led to the discovery of insulin, one of medicine's most important breakthroughs.

## Frederick Banting (1891-1941)

**Background:**
- Canadian surgeon (orthopedic training)
- WWI veteran (decorated for bravery)
- Limited research experience
- Age 29 at time of discovery

**The Insight (October 1920):**
- Reading article on pancreatic stones and diabetes
- Hypothesis: Ligate pancreatic duct → acinar cells die → islets remain → extract hormone
- Recognized islets produced the "anti-diabetic" factor
- Unconventional background for discovery

**Personality:**
- Tenacious, driven
- Quick temper
- Generous (shared Nobel money with Best)
- Felt strongly about credit attribution

## Charles Best (1899-1978)

**Background:**
- Medical student (22 years old)
- BA in physiology and biochemistry
- Volunteer research assistant
- Originally planned to be alternate with another student

**Role:**
- Blood glucose measurements
- Dog surgery assistance
- Day-to-day laboratory work
- Chemical extractions

**Later Career:**
- Continued diabetes research
- Replaced MacLeod at University of Toronto
- Major figure in Canadian science

## The Discovery Experiments (Summer 1921)

**Setup:**
- Lab space: University of Toronto (Department of Physiology)
- Supervisor: Professor John MacLeod (initially skeptical)
- Animals: Dogs (controversial by modern standards)

**Protocol:**
1. **Pancreatectomy:** Remove pancreas → diabetes in dogs
2. **Blood glucose monitoring:** Daily measurements
3. **Extract preparation:** Pancreatic tissue extraction
4. **Injection:** Administer extract to diabetic dogs
5. **Observation:** Monitor blood sugar reduction

**Key Findings:**
- Extract lowered blood glucose dramatically
- Effect temporary (required repeated injections)
- Proof of principle: Pancreas contains glucose-lowering substance
- "Isletin" initially, later "insulin"

## Challenges and Setbacks

**Technical Difficulties:**
- Many dogs died during experiments
- Extract stability issues
- Inconsistent results initially
- Purification problems

**Scientific Skepticism:**
- MacLeod initially unconvinced
- Previous failed attempts by others
- Need for rigorous proof

## Significance

**Immediate Impact:**
- Proof that diabetes could be treated
- Hope for millions of patients
- Rapid progression to human trials

**Scientific Importance:**
- First hormone therapy
- Established endocrinology as field
- Model for drug discovery

**Legacy:**
- Transformed diabetes from fatal to manageable
- Demonstrated importance of persistence
- Team science example (with complexities)

## Controversies

**Credit Allocation:**
- Banting felt Best deserved Nobel recognition
- MacLeod's role disputed
- Collip's contribution underappreciated initially
- Historical accounts vary

**Modern Perspective:**
- Recognized as collaborative effort
- All four (Banting, Best, Collip, MacLeod) contributed
- Discovery > individual credit
