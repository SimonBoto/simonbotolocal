# Sirolimus mTORC1 Inhibition Suppresses PGC-1α and Mitochondrial Biogenesis

**Core Mechanism:** mTORC1 normally activates PGC-1α, the master regulator of mitochondrial biogenesis. Sirolimus inhibits this pathway, reducing mitochondrial mass.

## mTORC1 → PGC-1α Pathway

**Normal Function:**
1. mTORC1 activation (nutrient/growth signals present)
2. Direct and indirect upregulation of PGC-1α expression
3. PGC-1α activation of:
   - NRF-1/2 (nuclear respiratory factors)
   - TFAM (mitochondrial transcription factor A)
   - ERRα (estrogen-related receptor alpha)
4. Coordinated response:
   - mtDNA replication
   - Mitochondrial protein synthesis
   - Mitochondrial mass increase

**High-Energy Tissues Dependent on This Pathway:**
- Cardiomyocytes (continuous ATP demand)
- Neurons (action potentials, neurotransmission)
- Skeletal muscle (contraction)
- Pancreatic β-cells (insulin secretion)

## Sirolimus Disruption

**Inhibition Cascade:**
```
Sirolimus → mTORC1 inhibition
                ↓
        PGC-1α downregulation
                ↓
        ┌───────┴───────┐
        ↓               ↓
    Reduced mtDNA    Reduced mitochondrial
    replication      protein synthesis
        ↓               ↓
        └───────┬───────┘
                ↓
        Decreased mitochondrial mass
                ↓
        Reduced oxidative capacity
```

## Consequences

**Acute (days-weeks):**
- Reduced ATP production capacity
- Metabolic stress signals (AMPK activation)
- Compensatory glycolysis

**Chronic (months):**
- Mitochondrial depletion
- Energy failure in high-demand cells
- Functional impairment (fatigue, weakness)

## Clinical Relevance

**Transplant Patients:**
- Fatigue common complaint
- Cardiac dysfunction risk
- Muscle wasting (sarcopenia)

**Longevity Research Paradox:**
- Intermittent low-dose may preserve function
- Chronic high-dose causes depletion
- Context (dose, duration, tissue) determines outcome
