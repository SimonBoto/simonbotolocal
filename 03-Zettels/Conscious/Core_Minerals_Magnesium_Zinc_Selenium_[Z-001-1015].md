# Core Mineral Status — Magnesium, Zinc, Selenium, Copper Clinical Assessment

## Overview

Core minerals function as essential cofactors for hundreds of enzymatic reactions, particularly in energy , antioxidant defense, and hormonal regulation. Assessment of mineral status is critical for metabolic optimization.

## Magnesium — The Master Mineral

### Biological Importance

**Cofactor for 300+ Enzymes:**
- ATP synthesis (ATP-Mg complex)
- DNA/RNA synthesis
- Protein synthesis
- Muscle contraction/relaxation
- Nerve conduction
- Blood pressure regulation
- Blood glucose control
-  activation

### Forms and Testing

**Serum Magnesium:**
- Standard test
- Poor indicator of total body status
- Represents <1% of total body magnesium
- May be normal while tissues depleted

**Optimal Serum:** 2.0-2.5 mg/dL
**Conventional Normal:** 1.7-2.2 mg/dL (may be too low)

**RBC Magnesium:**
- Better indicator of tissue status
- Reflects intracellular magnesium
- Preferred for functional assessment

**Optimal RBC:** 5.5-6.8 mg/dL

**Ionized Magnesium:**
- Free, biologically active form
- Limited availability
- Most accurate

### Clinical Manifestations of Deficiency

**Neuromuscular:**
- Muscle cramps, spasms
- Tremor
- Weakness
- Tetany (severe)
- Seizures (severe)

**Cardiovascular:**
- Arrhythmias
- Hypertension
- Coronary artery spasm
- QT prolongation

**Metabolic:**
-  resistance
- Type 2 diabetes risk
- Metabolic syndrome

**Psychiatric:**
- Anxiety
- Depression
- Irritability
- Sleep disturbances

**Other:**
- Migraines
- Osteoporosis
- Fatigue
- Constipation

### Causes of Deficiency

**Dietary:**
- Processed food diet
- Low vegetable intake
- Soil depletion

**Increased Loss:**
- Alcohol
- Diuretics
- PPIs (proton pump inhibitors)
- Chronic stress
- Excessive sweating
- Diabetes (glycosuria)

**Malabsorption:**
- Celiac disease
- Crohn's disease
- Chronic diarrhea
- Gastric bypass

### Supplementation

**Forms:**
| Form | Absorption | Best For |
|------|-----------|----------|
| **Glycinate** | High | Sleep, anxiety, general |
| **Citrate** | High | Constipation, kidney stones |
| **Malate** | High | Energy, fibromyalgia |
| **Threonate** | High | Brain, cognitive |
| **Taurate** | High | Heart, cardiovascular |
| **Oxide** | Low | Avoid (laxative effect) |
| **Chloride** | Moderate | Topical, baths |

**Dosing:**
- Elemental magnesium: 200-400 mg/day
- Higher doses if deficient
- Divide doses (better absorption)
- Take with meals

**Toxicity:**
- Rare (renal excretion)
- Diarrhea is limiting factor
- Contraindicated in severe renal failure

## Zinc

### Biological Functions

**Key Roles:**
- Immune function (thymulin, T-cells)
- Wound healing
- Protein synthesis
- DNA synthesis
- Growth and development
- Taste and smell
- Hormonal (testosterone, thyroid)
- Antioxidant (Cu/Zn SOD)

### Testing

**Serum Zinc:**
- Standard test
- Affected by inflammation (acute phase reactant)
- Albumin-dependent

**Optimal:** 90-120 μg/dL

**Plasma Zinc:**
- Preferred over serum
- Less affected by hemolysis

**RBC Zinc:**
- Better long-term status
- Less affected by acute changes

**Functional Markers:**
- Alkaline phosphatase (zinc-dependent)
- Low activity suggests deficiency

### Clinical Deficiency

**Symptoms:**
- Frequent infections
- Poor wound healing
- Hair loss
- Taste/smell loss
- Skin lesions (acrodermatitis)
- Growth retardation (children)
- Delayed sexual maturation
- Night blindness

### Causes

**Dietary:**
- Vegetarian/vegan (phytates reduce absorption)
- Low animal protein
- High grain diet

**Increased Needs:**
- Pregnancy/lactation
- Growth
- Infection
- Trauma

**Malabsorption:**
- Similar to magnesium
- PPIs

### Supplementation

**Forms:**
- Zinc picolinate (well absorbed)
- Zinc gluconate
- Zinc citrate
- Zinc carnosine (gut health)
- Avoid zinc oxide (poor absorption)

**Dosing:**
- Elemental zinc: 15-30 mg/day
- Higher short-term for deficiency
- Balance with copper (8-15:1 zinc:copper ratio)

**Toxicity:**
- Nausea
- Copper deficiency (long-term high dose)
- Immune suppression (very high doses)

## Selenium

### Biological Functions

**Primary Role:** Selenoproteins (25+ identified)

**Key Enzymes:**
- **Glutathione peroxidases (GPx1-4):** Antioxidant defense
- **Thioredoxin reductases:** Redox regulation
- **Deiodinases:** Thyroid hormone activation (T4→T3)
- **Selenoprotein P:** Selenium transport, antioxidant

### Testing

**Serum Selenium:**
- Standard test
- Reflects recent intake

**Optimal:** 100-140 μg/L

**Whole Blood Selenium:**
- Longer-term status

**RBC Selenium:**
- 3-4 month status
- Best for assessing status

**Functional Markers:**
- GPx activity
- Selenoprotein P concentration

### Clinical Deficiency

**Consequences:**
- Hypothyroidism (impaired T4→T3)
- Weakened immunity
- Cognitive decline
- Cardiovascular disease risk
- Cancer risk (U-shaped relationship)
- Viral mutation (coxsackie, HIV)

### Regional Variations

**Deficient Areas:**
- Parts of China (Keshan disease)
- Parts of Europe
- New Zealand (historically)

**Excess Areas:**
- Parts of South Dakota, Wyoming
- Selenium-rich soil

### Supplementation

**Forms:**
- Selenomethionine (organic, well absorbed)
- Selenocysteine
- Sodium selenite (inorganic)
- Brazil nuts (variable, ~70-90 μg/nut)

**Dosing:**
- 100-200 μg/day
- Higher doses only if deficient
- Don't exceed 400 μg/day (toxicity risk)

**Toxicity:**
- Selenosis (garlic breath, hair loss, neuropathy)
- >400 μg/day risk
- Narrow therapeutic window

## Copper

### Biological Functions

**Key Roles:**
- Energy production (Complex IV, cytochrome c oxidase)
- Iron metabolism (ceruloplasmin, ferroxidase)
- Antioxidant (SOD1, extracellular)
- Connective tissue (lysyl oxidase)
- Neurotransmitter synthesis (dopamine β-hydroxylase)
- Melanin synthesis

### Testing

**Serum Copper:**
- Standard
- Acute phase reactant (elevated in inflammation)

**Ceruloplasmin:**
- Copper-containing protein
- 90% of serum copper
- Also acute phase reactant

**Optimal:**
- Serum copper: 90-130 μg/dL
- Ceruloplasmin: 20-40 mg/dL

**RBC Copper:**
- Better status indicator
- Less affected by acute changes

**24-Hour Urine Copper:**
- Diagnostic for Wilson's disease
- Not for deficiency

### Clinical Deficiency

**Symptoms:**
- Anemia (iron-resistant)
- Neutropenia
- Bone abnormalities
- Vascular fragility
- Neurological symptoms
- Poor immune function

### Causes

**Excess Zinc:**
- Most common cause
- Supplement imbalance
- Ratio should be 8-15:1 (zinc:copper)

**Malabsorption:**
- Gastric bypass
- Celiac disease

**Genetic:**
- Menkes disease (rare)

### Supplementation

**Forms:**
- Copper glycinate
- Copper citrate
- Copper sebacate

**Dosing:**
- 1-2 mg/day (if needed)
- Usually not needed unless high zinc intake
- Short-term only

**Toxicity:**
- Wilson's disease (genetic accumulation)
- Liver damage
- Neurological damage

## Manganese

### Functions

**Cofactor for:**
- **SOD2 (MnSOD):** Mitochondrial antioxidant
- **Arginase:** Urea cycle
- **Pyruvate carboxylase:** Gluconeogenesis
- **Glutamine synthetase:** Ammonia detoxification

### Testing

**Whole Blood Manganese:**
- Reflects recent exposure/intake

**Optimal:** 5-15 μg/L

### Deficiency

**Rare:**
- Skin rash
- Hair depigmentation
- Bone abnormalities
- Glucose intolerance

### Excess

**More common than deficiency:**
- Manganese miners/welders
- Neurological symptoms (manganism)
- Parkinson-like symptoms
- Caution with supplementation

## Clinical Assessment Strategy

### Testing Priority

**1. Magnesium:**
- RBC preferred
- Most common deficiency
- Wide-ranging effects

**2. Zinc:**
- Serum or plasma
- With copper ratio

**3. Selenium:**
- RBC or serum
- Regional/geographic consideration

**4. Copper:**
- With zinc
- Watch for excess zinc-induced deficiency

**5. Manganese:**
- If SOD2 concerns
- Or specific exposure history

### Pattern Recognition

**High Zinc/Low Copper:**
- Excessive zinc supplementation
- Immune dysfunction
- Anemia

**Low Magnesium/Low Potassium:**
- Diuretic use
- Poor intake
- Metabolic alkalosis

**Low Selenium/Low Iodine:**
- Thyroid dysfunction
- Same dietary patterns

### Supplementation Approach

**Foundation:**
- Magnesium: Almost everyone benefits
- 200-400 mg elemental daily

**Targeted:**
- Zinc: If low, vegetarian, frequent infections
- Selenium: If low, thyroid issues, viral concerns
- Copper: Only if high zinc or documented deficiency
- Manganese: Rarely needed

**Multimineral:**
- Balanced formulas
- Watch zinc:copper ratio
- Quality matters

## Summary

**Key Points:**
1. Magnesium: Most important; test RBC; supplement glycinate/citrate
2. Zinc: Immune, hormonal; test serum; balance with copper
3. Selenium: Thyroid, antioxidant; narrow window; U-shaped risk
4. Copper: Energy, connective tissue; deficiency from excess zinc
5. Manganese: SOD2 cofactor; rarely deficient

**Clinical Pearl:** Mineral deficiencies are widespread due to soil depletion and processed diets. RBC testing better than serum. Magnesium should be foundation of almost every protocol. Balance zinc:copper at 8-15:1 ratio.
