# Beta Oxidation Clinical Mechanisms

Mitochondrial fatty-acid breakdown into acetyl-CoA with production of NADH and FADH₂. The primary pathway for fat-derived energy, critical for metabolic flexibility and endurance.

## Evidence
> "Beta-oxidation is how we access the energy stored in fat. Without it, we cannot utilize our largest energy reserve." (Metabolic biochemistry)

## The Beta-Oxidation Cycle

### Overview
Fatty acids → Acetyl-CoA → Krebs cycle → ATP

### Step-by-Step
| Step | Reaction | Output |
|------|----------|--------|
| **1. Activation** | Fatty acid + CoA → Fatty acyl-CoA | (cytosol) |
| **2. Transport** | Carnitine shuttle → mitochondria | CPT1/CPT2 |
| **3. Oxidation** | FAD → FADH₂ | 1 FADH₂ per cycle |
| **4. Hydration** | Water added | - |
| **5. Oxidation** | NAD⁺ → NADH | 1 NADH per cycle |
| **6. Cleavage** | Acetyl-CoA released | 1 acetyl-CoA per cycle |

**Each cycle:** Shortens fatty acid by 2 carbons, produces 1 FADH₂, 1 NADH, 1 acetyl-CoA

## Energy Yield Calculation

### Palmitic Acid (16:0) Example
| Step | ATP Yield |
|------|-----------|
| **7 cycles of β-oxidation** | 7 FADH₂ + 7 NADH |
| **8 acetyl-CoA → Krebs** | 8 × 12 ATP = 96 ATP |
| **7 FADH₂ × 1.5 ATP** | 10.5 ATP |
| **7 NADH × 2.5 ATP** | 17.5 ATP |
| **Activation cost** | -2 ATP |
| **Total** | **~122 ATP** |

**Comparison:** Glucose (6C) → ~30-32 ATP

## Clinical Markers of Beta-Oxidation

### OAT Markers
| Marker | Elevation Indicates |
|--------|---------------------|
| **Adipate** | Carnitine deficiency, impaired β-oxidation |
| **Suberate** | Medium-chain fatty acid oxidation defect |
| **Ethylmalonate** | Riboflavin (B2) deficiency |

### Plasma Markers
| Marker | Interpretation |
|--------|----------------|
| **Free fatty acids** | ↑ with fasting, ↓ with insulin |
| **3-hydroxybutyrate** | Ketone, ↑ with fasting/ketogenic diet |
| **Acylcarnitines** | Specific defects in β-oxidation enzymes |

## Beta-Oxidation Disorders

### Clinical Presentation
| Symptom | Mechanism |
|---------|-----------|
| **Hypoketotic hypoglycemia** | Cannot mobilize fat for energy |
| **Muscle weakness** | Energy failure in muscle |
| **Cardiomyopathy** | Heart muscle energy deficiency |
| **Hepatomegaly** | Fat accumulation in liver |
| **Rhabdomyolysis** | Muscle breakdown during fasting |

### Common Defects
| Disorder | Enzyme | Key Feature |
|----------|--------|-------------|
| **MCAD deficiency** | Medium-chain acyl-CoA dehydrogenase | Most common, fasting intolerance |
| **VLCAD deficiency** | Very long-chain acyl-CoA dehydrogenase | Cardiac involvement |
| **CPT1 deficiency** | Carnitine palmitoyltransferase 1 | Hepatomegaly, hypoglycemia |

## Factors Affecting Beta-Oxidation

### Enhancers
| Factor | Mechanism |
|--------|-----------|
| **Fasting** | ↑ glucagon, ↓ insulin → ↑ lipolysis |
| **Exercise** | ↑ AMPK → ↑ fatty acid uptake |
| **Ketogenic diet** | Adaptation to fat burning |
| **Carnitine** | Essential for transport |
| **Riboflavin (B2)** | FAD cofactor for acyl-CoA dehydrogenases |

### Inhibitors
| Factor | Mechanism |
|--------|-----------|
| **High insulin** | Inhibits hormone-sensitive lipase |
| **High glucose** | Preferred fuel, suppresses fat burning |
| **Carnitine deficiency** | Impaired transport into mitochondria |
| **Malonyl-CoA** | Inhibits CPT1 (rate-limiting step) |

## Clinical Applications

### Fatigue Assessment
Low β-oxidation capacity → poor endurance, energy crashes

### Weight Management
Impaired fat burning → difficulty losing weight despite caloric restriction

### Metabolic Flexibility
Ability to switch between glucose and fat burning

### Therapeutic Targets
| Target | Intervention |
|--------|--------------|
| **Carnitine** | Supplement if deficient |
| **Riboflavin** | B2 for FAD-dependent enzymes |
| **Fasting** | Train β-oxidation capacity |
| **Exercise** | Enhance mitochondrial fat burning |
