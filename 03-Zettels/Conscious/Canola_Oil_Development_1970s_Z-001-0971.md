# Canola Oil Development 1970s — Canadian Low-Erucic Acid Rapeseed

## Background

### Traditional Rapeseed

**Plant:**
- *Brassica napus*, *B. rapa* (related to mustard, cabbage, turnip)
- Cultivated for thousands of years
- Ancient origins in Mediterranean, India, China

**Traditional Oil:**
- **High erucic acid (22:1):** 20-50% of 
- **Properties:**
  - Industrial applications excellent
  - Food use problematic (taste, potential toxicity)
  - Good lubricant (high viscosity)
  - Drying oil for paints/coatings

**Food Safety Concerns:**
- Erucic acid linked to heart lesions in animal studies (1950s-60s)
- FDA limited erucic acid in food (not banned, but discouraged)
- Limited human consumption historically

## Canadian Development

### Why Canada?

**Agricultural Context:**
- Wheat-dominated prairie provinces (Alberta, Saskatchewan, Manitoba)
- Short growing season
- Need for crop rotation (break pest/disease cycles)
- Rapeseed grew well in cold climate

**Economic Opportunity:**
- WWII: Critical shortage of industrial lubricants
- Rapeseed oil met need
- Post-war: Continued production
- Desire to expand market into food use

### The Breeding Program

**University of Manitoba (1950s-1970s):**
- Dr. Keith Downey and Dr. Baldur Stefansson
- Led rapeseed breeding research
- Goal: Reduce erucic acid for food safety

**Breakthrough (1970s):**
- Developed varieties with **<2% erucic acid** (vs 20-50% traditional)
- Maintained agronomic performance
- Suitable for food consumption

**Additional Breeding:**
- Also reduced glucosinolates (bitter compounds)
- Improved meal quality for animal feed
- Enhanced oil stability

## Naming and Branding

### "Canola"

**Coined:** 1978
**Meaning:** **Can**adian **o**il, **l**ow **a**cid
**Purpose:** 
- Distinguish from traditional rapeseed
- Positive, marketable name
- No negative "rape" association
- Trademarked by Western Canadian Oilseed Crushers Association

**Legal Definition (Canada):**
- Oil must contain **<2% erucic acid**
- Meal must contain **<30 micromoles glucosinolates/gram**
- Strictly regulated term

### Health Positioning

**Marketing Claims:**
- "Heart-healthy" (low saturated fat)
- "Good omega-3 to omega-6 ratio" (1:2)
- "High in monounsaturated fat"
- "Cholesterol-free"
- "Light taste" (neutral flavor)

**Scientific Nuance:**
- Better than high omega-6 oils (corn, safflower, sunflower)
- But still refined, processed
- ALA (omega-3) poorly converted to EPA/DHA
- Heating creates same toxins as other seed oils

## Commercial Success

### Adoption

**1980s-1990s:**
- Rapid uptake in Canada
- Exports to US, Asia, Europe
- Replaced other vegetable oils in many applications

**Global Expansion:**
- Now 3rd most consumed vegetable oil globally
- Major production: Canada, EU, China, Australia
- GMO varieties dominate (herbicide tolerant)

**Uses:**
- Cooking oil
- Salad dressings
- Margarine production
- Deep frying (restaurants)
- Industrial applications (biodiesel, lubricants)

### GMO Canola (1990s)

**Roundup Ready Canola:**
- Monsanto developed glyphosate-tolerant variety
- Licensed 1995
- Rapid adoption by farmers
- Now >90% of Canadian crop

**Controversy:**
- Environmental concerns
- Patent issues (Percy Schmeiser case)
- Consumer resistance in some markets
- Organic/non-GMO niche market remains

## High-Oleic Varieties

### Next Generation

**Development:**
- Further breeding to increase oleic acid
- Reduce linoleic and linolenic acids
- Enhanced stability (less oxidation)

**High-Oleic Canola:**
| Fatty Acid | Regular Canola | High-Oleic Canola |
|------------|---------------|-------------------|
| Oleic acid | 61% | **70-80%** |
| Linoleic acid | 21% | **5-10%** |
| Linolenic acid | 11% | **2-5%** |

**Advantages:**
- More stable (less oxidation)
- Longer fry life
- Reduced trans fat formation
- Better for high-heat applications

**Trade-offs:**
- Less omega-3 (ALA)
- Still refined/processed
- Still creates ALEs when heated

## Critical Assessment

### Advantages Over Other Seed Oils

**1. Better Fatty Acid Profile:**
- Lower omega-6 than corn, soybean, safflower
- Some omega-3 (ALA)
- High monounsaturated (stable)
- Low saturated

**2. Versatility:**
- Neutral flavor
- High smoke point (refined)
- Various applications

**3. Cost:**
- Efficient crop yields
- Established infrastructure
- Competitive pricing

### Limitations and Concerns

**1. Processing Required:**
- Typically refined, bleached, deodorized
- High heat deodorization
- Loses natural antioxidants
- Trans fat formation possible

**2. Omega-3 Conversion:**
- ALA (11%) poorly converted to EPA/DHA (<5% conversion)
- Not reliable omega-3 source
- Still contributes to omega-6 load

**3. Heating Concerns:**
- When heated: forms 4-HNE, acrolein, other ALEs
- Deep frying generates oxidized products
- Not suitable for high-heat cooking despite smoke point

**4. GMO Dominance:**
- Most canola GMO
- Glyphosate residues possible
- Environmental impact of monoculture

## Conclusion

Canola represents:
- Successful plant breeding achievement
- "Better" seed oil option (vs corn, safflower, soybean)
- But still processed, refined, omega-6 contributing
- Not "healthy" — just "less harmful"
- Best use: cold applications, not cooking

**Bottom line:** Among seed oils, canola is preferable. But olive oil, avocado oil, or whole food fats remain superior choices.
