# Kidney Stones —   Pathophysiology

## Epidemiology

**Most common kidney stone type:** ~80% of all stones  
**Composition:** Calcium oxalate (CaC₂O₄)  
**Risk factors:** Dietary oxalate, dehydration, genetic factors

## Formation s

### 1. Supersaturation
- Urine becomes supersaturated with calcium and oxalate
- Crystal nucleation occurs
- pH, concentration, and inhibitors matter

### 2. Crystal Growth
```
Ca²⁺ + C₂O₄²⁻ → CaC₂O₄ (crystal)
```
- Crystals aggregate in renal tubules
- Can cause obstruction and pain

### 3. Risk Factors

| Factor | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| High dietary oxalate | ↑ Urinary oxalate |
| Low urine volume | ↑ Concentration |
| Hypercalciuria | ↑ Calcium excretion |
| Hypocitraturia | ↓ Stone inhibitor |
| Genetic (PH) | ↑ Endogenous oxalate |

## Types of Hyperoxaluria

### Primary Hyperoxaluria (PH)
- **Genetic:** Enzyme defects (AGXT, GRHPR, HOGA1)
- **Type:** Endogenous overproduction
- **Severity:** High, often childhood onset

### Secondary Hyperoxaluria
- **Dietary:** High oxalate intake
- **Enteric:** Fat malabsorption (oxalate absorption ↑)
- **High-dose vitamin C:** Metabolic conversion

## Prevention Strategies

1. **Hydration:** >2.5 L urine/day
2. **Dietary oxalate:** Moderate intake
3. **Calcium with meals:** Binds oxalate in gut
4. **Citrate:** Stone inhibitor (lemons, potassium citrate)
5. **Limit sodium:** Reduces calcium excretion
