# [[KEYWORD-Research|research]] Ethics Case Studies

## Purpose
Learn from historical violations to prevent future abuses and strengthen protections.

---

## Tuskegee Syphilis Study (1932-1972)

### What Happened
| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Participants** | 600 African American men (399 with syphilis, 201 without) |
| **Location** | Tuskegee, Alabama, USA |
| **Duration** | 40 years |
| **Deception** | Told they were receiving treatment for "bad blood"; actually given placebo |
| **Withheld treatment** | Even after penicillin became standard cure (1947), treatment denied |
| **Coercion** | Offered incentives (meals, burial insurance) to continue participation |

### Violations
- **No informed consent:** Participants not told true nature of study
- **Withheld effective treatment:** Penicillin denied for decades
- **Exploitation:** Targeted vulnerable, disadvantaged population
- **No scientific value:** By 1940s, syphilis natural history already known
- **Racism:** Assumed African Americans would not seek treatment anyway

### Consequences
| Outcome | Impact |
|---------|--------|
| **Deaths** | 128 direct deaths; many others from syphilis complications |
| **Spousal infection** | 40 wives infected |
| **Congenital cases** | 19 children born with congenital syphilis |
| **Public exposure** | 1972 AP story; national outrage |
| **Legal response** | $10M settlement; never formally apologized by US government until 1997 |
| **Regulatory impact** | Contributed to Belmont Report, strengthened IRB system |

### Lessons
- Scientific value cannot justify exploitation
- Vulnerable populations require enhanced protection
- Independent oversight essential
- Ongoing consent process needed

## Willowbrook Hepatitis Study (1956-1980)

### What Happened
| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Participants** | Mentally disabled children at Willowbrook State School |
| **Intervention** | Intentional infection with hepatitis virus |
| **Rationale** | Study natural history; test treatments (gamma globulin) |
| **Conditions** | Overcrowded institution; high natural infection rate |

### Ethical Issues
- **Vulnerable population:** Children with intellectual disabilities
- **Intentional infection:** Exposed to disease rather than just observing
- **Institutionalized setting:** Limited freedom to refuse
- **Parental consent controversy:** Some argued coerced (school had long waiting list; research offered faster admission)

### Defenses (at the time)
- High natural infection rate anyway
- Children would get better medical care in research wing
- Some protection via gamma globulin

### Contemporary Assessment
- Would not be approved today
- Highlights challenges of research in institutional settings
- Consent in vulnerable populations remains complex

## Nazi Medical Experiments (1940s)

### What Happened
| Experiment | Details |
|------------|---------|
| **Hypothermia** | Submersion in ice water; documented death process |
| **High altitude** | Simulated altitude in decompression chambers until death |
| **Sulfanilamide** | Wounds deliberately infected; tested drug efficacy |
| **Twins** | Mengele's experiments on twins (surgery, injections) |
| **Sterilization** | X-ray/radiation sterilization without consent |

### Characteristics
- **No consent:** Prisoners had no choice
- **Torture disguised as research:** Often no scientific validity
- **Certain death:** Many experiments designed to be fatal
- **Dehumanization:** Participants treated as objects, not humans

### Legacy
- **Nuremberg Code (1947):** Direct response; established voluntary consent
- **Crimes against humanity:** Doctors tried at Nuremberg
- **Never again:** Foundation of modern research ethics

## Henrietta Lacks (HeLa Cells) (1951)

### What Happened
| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Patient** | Henrietta Lacks, African American woman with cervical [[KEYWORD-Cancer|cancer]] |
| **Sample** | Tumor cells taken without consent during treatment |
| **Discovery** | Cells could survive and multiply indefinitely in culture |
| **Impact** | HeLa cells became foundation of modern cell biology |
| **Commercialization** | Billions of dollars in research; family never compensated |

### Ethical Issues
- **No informed consent:** Not asked for tissue use
- **No benefit sharing:** Family remained in poverty while cells generated wealth
- **Privacy violation:** Medical records published without permission
- **Racial dimension:** Exploitation of African American patient

### Contemporary Developments
- 2013: NIH agreement with Lacks family for controlled access to genome data
- Ongoing discussions about benefit sharing in tissue research
- Informed consent for biospecimens now standard

## Positive Examples: Ethics Done Right

### Salk Polio Vaccine Trial (1954)
| Aspect | Implementation |
|--------|----------------|
| **Scale** | 1.8 million children (largest trial in history) |
| **Design** | Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled |
| **Consent** | Parental permission obtained |
| **Scientific value** | Definitive proof of vaccine efficacy |
| **Impact** | Polio eradication in developed world |

### Women's Health Initiative (1991-2002)
| Aspect | Implementation |
|--------|----------------|
| **Question** | Does hormone therapy prevent heart disease? |
| **Result** | Increased risks found (stroke, heart disease, cancer) |
| **Ethical strength** | **Negative results promptly published** |
| **Impact** | Medical guidelines revised; millions protected |

## Lessons Summary

### For Researchers
| Lesson | Application |
|--------|-------------|
| **Consent is ongoing** | Not one-time event; continuous process |
| **Vulnerable need protection** | Enhanced safeguards for disadvantaged |
| **Independent oversight** | IRB review prevents researcher blind spots |
| **Publish all results** | Negative findings as important as positive |
| **Benefit sharing** | Fair distribution when commercialization occurs |

### For Participants
| Right | Protection |
|-------|------------|
| **Information** | Complete, understandable disclosure |
| **Voluntariness** | Free to decline or withdraw |
| **[[KEYWORD-Safety|safety]]** | Risks minimized, benefits maximized |
| **Privacy** | Confidentiality protected |
| **Respect** | Dignity maintained throughout |
