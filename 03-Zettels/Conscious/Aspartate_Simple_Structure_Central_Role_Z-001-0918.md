## Core Claim
**Aspartate has the simplest structure of the acidic amino acids (4-carbon dicarboxylic acid with an amino group) yet plays evolutionarily central roles — it provides the second nitrogen for the urea cycle, participates in the malate-aspartate shuttle, and is interconvertible with oxaloacetate, linking amino acid metabolism to the Krebs cycle and gluconeogenesis** — its synthesis from oxaloacetate is a simple transamination (B6-dependent).

## Evidence

### Structure
| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Formula** | HOOC-CH₂-CH(NH₂)-COOH |
| **Side chain** | -CH₂-COO⁻ (additional carboxyl) |
| **Charge** | Negative at physiological pH (pKa ~3.9) |
| **Synthesis** | OAA + Glutamate ↔ Aspartate + α-KG (AST, B6-dependent) |

### Central Roles
| Role | Function | Mechanism |
|------|----------|-----------|
| **Urea cycle** | Provides second nitrogen | Argininosuccinate synthetase |
| **Malate-aspartate shuttle** | Transfers reducing equivalents | AST, mitochondrial/cytosolic |
| **Krebs cycle** | OAA ↔ Aspartate interconversion | Transamination |
| **Purine synthesis** | Nucleotide precursor | Direct incorporation |

### Evolutionary Conservation
- **Ancient pathway:** Aspartate metabolism conserved across domains
- **Simple chemistry:** Easy interconversion with OAA
- **Multiple functions:** Nitrogen transfer, energy metabolism, biosynthesis

### Aspartate Aminotransferase (AST)
| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Isozymes** | Mitochondrial (mAST) and cytosolic (cAST) |
| **Clinical marker** | AST/ALT ratio in liver disease |
| **Tissue distribution** | Heart, liver, muscle, RBCs |

## Clinical Implication
- **AST elevation:** Marker for cardiac, hepatic, or muscle damage
- **Urea cycle:** Essential for nitrogen disposal
- **Malate-aspartate shuttle:** Critical for gluconeogenesis
- **METHAP relevance:** Aspartate's central role in nitrogen and energy metabolism makes B6 (for AST) essential
