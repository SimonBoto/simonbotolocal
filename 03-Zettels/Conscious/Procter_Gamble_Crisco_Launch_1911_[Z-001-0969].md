# Procter & Gamble Crisco Launch 1911 — Marketing Revolution in Food

## Background

### Procter & Gamble

**Company History:**
- Founded 1837 by William Procter (candle maker) and James Gamble (soap maker)
- Cincinnati, Ohio
- By 1900: Major soap and candle manufacturer
- Seeking new products as electricity reduced candle demand

### The Opportunity

**Cottonseed Oil Surplus:**
- Cotton industry produced massive amounts of cottonseed as waste
- Oil extraction developed in 1880s-1890s
- Cheap, abundant raw material
- Primarily used for soap, lubricants

**Technical Innovation:**
- **Wilhelm Normann** invented hydrogenation (1901, Germany)
- Licensed to P&G
- Allowed conversion of liquid oil to solid fat

## Product Development

### Creating Crisco

**Name Origin:**
- **Crys**talized **c**ottonseed **o**il
- Later softened to "Crisco"
- Sounded modern, scientific

**Formulation:**
- Hydrogenated cottonseed oil
- Partial hydrogenation → semi-solid
- Mixed with liquid oil for texture
- Blanched, deodorized for neutral flavor

**Advantages Over Lard:**
| Feature | Crisco | Lard |
|---------|--------|------|
| Shelf life | Long (months) | Shorter (weeks) |
| Consistency | Uniform | Variable by batch |
| Appearance | White, clean | Off-white, pork association |
| Cost | Lower (industrial scale) | Higher |
| Marketing appeal | Modern, scientific | Traditional, ethnic |

### Manufacturing Innovation

**Quality Control:**
- Standardized product (unlike variable lard)
- Brand reliability
- Nationwide distribution

**Scale:**
- Mass production
- Advertising budget unprecedented in food
- Changed how food was marketed

## Marketing Revolution

### Launch Campaign (1911)

**Budget:**
- Estimated $1.5 million (massive for era)
- Equivalent to ~$45 million today
- Unprecedented for food product

**Strategies:**

**1. Scientific Authority**
- "Digestible," "pure," "modern"
- White coat imagery
- Chemical-sounding benefits
- Laboratory testing claims

**2. Cleanliness and Purity**
- White packaging
- Emphasized "pure" vs "dirty" animal fats
- Hygiene associations
- Appeal to "modern" housewife

**3. Recipe Development**
- Crisco Cook Book (1912)
- Free with purchase
- Recipes replaced lard/butter with Crisco
- Created demand through instruction

**4. Cooking Schools**
- Demonstrations in department stores
- Free samples
- Taught new techniques
- Built brand loyalty

**5. Advertising Media**
- Magazine advertisements
- Newspaper inserts
- Billboards
- Radio (later)

**6. Racial and Class Marketing**
- Positioned as upgrade from lard (associated with rural, immigrant, African American cooking)
- "Respectable" middle-class appeal
- Assimilation tool for immigrants

### Key Messaging

**vs. Animal Fats:**
- "Cleaner" than lard
- "More digestible" than butter
- "Vegetable" (healthy) vs "animal" (suspect)
- Modern vs old-fashioned

**Health Claims:**
- "Easily digested"
- "Does not upset the stomach"
- "Better for the heart" (later, implicit)
- Appealed to dyspepsia concerns (common in era)

## Market Impact

### Sales Growth

**1912-1920:**
- Immediate success
- P&G sold 2.6 million pounds in first year
- By 1920s: Dominant shortening brand
- Model for branded food products

### Competitive Response

**Other Shortenings:**
- Spry (Lever Brothers)
- Swift's Premium
- Store brands
- Market expanded but Crisco dominant

### Product Line Extension

**Evolution:**
- Original all-vegetable shortening
- Butter-flavored versions
- Stick form (vs can)
- Reduced-fat versions (later)
- Today: Multiple formulations

## Cultural Transformation

### Changing Cooking Practices

**Recipe Standardization:**
- Before: Regional variations, family recipes with lard/butter
- After: National recipes using Crisco
- "Crisco cookies," "Crisco pie crust"

**Home Economics Movement:**
- Tied to scientific cooking
- Crisco aligned with "modern" domestic science
- Institutional acceptance

### Dietary Shift

**Fat Consumption:**
- Increased availability of cheap fat
- Baking became easier (reliable fat)
- Increased consumption of fried foods
- Shift from animal to vegetable fat calories

### Social Significance

**Class and Modernity:**
- Using Crisco signaled middle-class status
- "Scientific" home management
- Participation in modern consumer economy

**Regional Spread:**
- National distribution broke down regional food patterns
- Southern, Midwestern, Northeastern cooking converged

## Scientific and Health Context

### Contemporaneous Knowledge

**What Was Known (1911):**
- Fat essential for 
- Chemistry of hydrogenation understood
- No concept of essential  (discovered 1929-1930)
- No link between fat type and heart disease
- No knowledge of trans fats

**What Was NOT Known:**
- Essential fatty acids (, omega-3)
- Trans fat dangers
- PUFA oxidation issues
- Heart disease connection
- Long-term metabolic effects

### The Essential Fatty Acid Problem

**Later Discovery (1929-1930):**
- Burr and Burr discovered essential fatty acids
- Rats deficient in linoleic acid developed disease
- Paradox: Crisco provided linoleic acid
- Interpretation: "Vegetable oils healthy"

**Misinterpretation:**
- If deficiency is harmful, more must be better
- Did not anticipate toxicity of excess
- Confounded omega-6 vs omega-3 (discovered later)

## Long-Term Consequences

### For Food Industry

**Model Established:**
- Branding commodity products
- Advertising creating demand
- Recipe marketing
- Science as selling point
- Still used today

### For Public Health

**Unintended:**
- Massive increase in omega-6 consumption
- Trans fat introduction (hydrogenation)
- Shift away from stable animal fats
- Contribution to modern metabolic disease

**Timeline:**
- 1911: Crisco launched
- 1920s-1940s: Gradual adoption
- 1950s-1970s: Acceleration (Keys, diet-heart hypothesis)
- 1980s-present: Peak consumption

## Conclusion

Crisco's 1911 launch marked:
- Birth of modern food marketing
- Industrialization of dietary fat
- Cultural shift toward "vegetable" oils
- Unwitting experiment in metabolic health

**Legacy:** Changed how the world eats, with consequences still unfolding.
