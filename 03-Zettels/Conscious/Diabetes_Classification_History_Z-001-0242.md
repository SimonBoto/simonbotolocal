## Historical Evolution of Diabetes Concepts

### Ancient Recognition (2nd Century CE)
- Aretaeus of Cappadocia described "diabetes" (meaning "to pass through")
- Recognized excessive urination and thirst
- Distinguished from other conditions causing polyuria

### 17th-18th Century
- Thomas Willis (1674): Noted sweet taste of diabetic urine
- "Diabetes mellitus" (honey-sweet) vs "diabetes insipidus" (tasteless)
- Understanding of urine composition limited

### 19th Century
- Claude Bernard's work on glycogen and liver glucose production
- Recognition of diabetes as metabolic disorder
- Differentiation between juvenile and adult onset

### 20th Century Classification
- **1930s-1940s:** Distinction between insulin-dependent and non-insulin-dependent diabetes
- **1979:** National Diabetes Data Group (NDDG) classification
  - Insulin-dependent diabetes mellitus (IDDM)
  - Non-insulin-dependent diabetes mellitus (NIDDM)
  - Other specific types

### Modern Classification (1997-2023)
- **1997:** ADA/WHO reclassification
  - Type 1 diabetes (autoimmune, insulin deficiency)
  - Type 2 diabetes (insulin resistance, progressive beta-cell failure)
  - Gestational diabetes
  - Other specific types

- **2019:** ADA Classification
  - Emphasized etiology over treatment
  - Type 1: Autoimmune beta-cell destruction
  - Type 2: Insulin resistance and relative insulin deficiency
  - Monogenic diabetes, cystic fibrosis-related, etc.

## Key Classification Milestones
- **1674:** Thomas Willis identifies sweet urine
- **1850s:** Bernard links liver and glucose metabolism
- **1921:** Insulin discovery enables distinction between types
- **1930s:** Clinical distinction between juvenile/adult onset
- **1979:** Formal IDDM/NIDDM classification
- **1997:** Type 1/Type 2 classification
- **2019:** Etiology-based classification

## Historical Significance
Diabetes classification reflects the evolution of medical understanding:
- From symptom-based (excessive urination) to metabolic (glucose homeostasis)
- From treatment-based (insulin-dependent) to etiology-based (autoimmune vs. insulin resistance)
- From clinical observation to molecular understanding
- The classification history parallels the glucose discovery timeline, showing how understanding of the molecule enabled understanding of the disease.
