## Core Claim
**Iron-sulfur (Fe-S) clusters are among the most ancient biological cofactors, essential for electron transfer, enzyme catalysis, and cellular sensing — their prebiotic plausibility and universal conservation suggest they were present in the earliest life forms** — modern mitochondria retain dozens of Fe-S proteins for energy metabolism.

## Evidence

### Structure Types
| Type | Composition | Geometry | Examples |
|------|-------------|----------|----------|
| **[2Fe-2S]** | 2 iron, 2 sulfide | Rhombic | Ferredoxins, some aconitase |
| **[4Fe-4S]** | 4 iron, 4 sulfide | Cubane | Aconitase, succinate DH, nitrogenase |
| **[3Fe-4S]** | 3 iron, 4 sulfide | Modified cubane | Oxidative damage product |

### Biological Functions
| Function | Proteins | Role |
|----------|----------|------|
| **Electron transfer** | Ferredoxins, Complex I/II | Photosynthesis, respiration |
| **Enzyme catalysis** | Aconitase, nitrogenase | Krebs cycle, nitrogen fixation |
| **Sensing** | IRP1, SoxR, FNR | Iron regulation, oxidative stress, oxygen |

### Ancient Origins
- **Prebiotic synthesis:** Fe-S clusters form spontaneously under early Earth conditions
- **Universal conservation:** Found in all domains of life
- **Mitochondrial retention:** Essential for oxidative metabolism

## Clinical Implication
- **Fe-S cluster biogenesis defects** cause rare but severe mitochondrial diseases
- **Oxidative damage** converts [4Fe-4S] to [3Fe-4S] → enzyme inactivation
- **Iron deficiency** impairs Fe-S protein synthesis
- **METHAP relevance:** Mitochondrial support includes iron (if deficient) and antioxidants to protect Fe-S clusters
