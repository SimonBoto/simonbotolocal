# Colorectal  Screening — Guidelines and Modalities

## Overview

Colorectal cancer (CRC) is the third most common cancer and second leading cause of cancer death. Screening reduces mortality through detection of early-stage cancer and removal of precancerous adenomas.

## Risk Stratification

### Average Risk

**Criteria:**
- Age ≥45 (lowered from 50)
- No personal history of CRC or adenomas
- No IBD
- No family history of CRC in first-degree relative <60
- No known genetic syndrome

### Increased Risk

**Personal History:**
- Prior CRC
- Prior adenomatous polyps
- IBD (UC or Crohn's colitis >8 years)

**Family History:**
- First-degree relative with CRC <60
- Two first-degree relatives with CRC at any age
- Hereditary syndrome in family

### High Risk (Genetic Syndromes)

**Lynch Syndrome (HNPCC):**
- MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2 mutations
- Begin colonoscopy at 20-25 or 2-5 years before youngest case
- Every 1-2 years

**FAP (Familial Adenomatous Polyposis):**
- APC mutation
- Begin at 10-12 years
- Annual sigmoidoscopy
- Colectomy when adenomas develop

**MUTYH-Associated Polyposis:**
- Begin at 25-30 years
- Colonoscopy every 1-2 years

## Screening Modalities

### 1. Colonoscopy (Gold Standard)

**Procedure:**
- Visualize entire colon and rectum
- Biopsy suspicious lesions
- Remove polyps (polypectomy)

**Advantages:**
- Most sensitive (detects >95% of cancers, >90% of polyps)
- Therapeutic (removes polyps)
- 10-year interval if normal
- Single test

**Disadvantages:**
- Invasive
- Requires bowel prep
- Sedation required
- Complications (perforation 1/1000, bleeding 1/500)
- Cost
- Access limitations

**Preparation:**
- Clear liquid diet 24 hours
- Split-dose bowel prep (PEG preferred)
- Adequate prep essential (Boston Bowel Preparation Scale ≥6)

### 2. Fecal Immunochemical Test (FIT)

**:**
- Detects human hemoglobin in stool
- Antibodies specific to human globin
- No dietary restrictions

**Procedure:**
- Single stool sample at home
- No bowel prep
- Annual testing

**Advantages:**
- Non-invasive
- No preparation
- Inexpensive
- High specificity (fewer false positives than FOBT)

**Disadvantages:**
- Lower sensitivity than colonoscopy
- Must be repeated annually
- Positive requires colonoscopy
- Misses right-sided lesions (blood degraded)

**Performance:**
- Sensitivity: 70-80% for cancer
- Specificity: 90-95%
- Adherence better than colonoscopy

### 3. Stool DNA (mt-sDNA)

**Mechanism:**
- Detects DNA mutations (KRAS, APC, etc.)
- Detects methylation markers
- Detects hemoglobin (FIT component)

**Procedure:**
- Entire bowel movement
- Every 3 years

**Advantages:**
- Non-invasive
- No prep
- Higher sensitivity than FIT alone
- Every 3 years

**Disadvantages:**
- Expensive
- Lower specificity (more false positives)
- Positive requires colonoscopy
- Optimal interval uncertain

**Performance:**
- Sensitivity: 90-95% for cancer
- Sensitivity: 40-60% for advanced adenomas
- Specificity: 85-90%

### 4. CT Colonography (Virtual Colonoscopy)

**Procedure:**
- CT scan with bowel insufflation
- 3D reconstruction
- Every 5 years

**Advantages:**
- Non-invasive (no sedation)
- Short procedure
- Detects extracolonic findings
- Good sensitivity for polyps >6mm

**Disadvantages:**
- Requires bowel prep
- Radiation exposure
- Positive requires colonoscopy
- Misses flat lesions
- Does not allow biopsy/polypectomy
- Cost

**Performance:**
- Sensitivity: ~90% for cancer
- Sensitivity: 80-90% for polyps >10mm
- 60-80% for polyps 6-9mm
- Poor for <6mm

**Follow-up:**
- Polyps <6mm: Surveillance
- Polyps 6-9mm: CTC follow-up or colonoscopy
- Polyps ≥10mm: Colonoscopy

### 5. Flexible Sigmoidoscopy

**Procedure:**
- Visualizes rectum, sigmoid, descending colon
- Every 5 years
- Or every 10 years with annual FIT

**Advantages:**
- Less invasive than colonoscopy
- Lower complication rate
- No sedation needed (usually)
- Shorter prep

**Disadvantages:**
- Misses right-sided lesions (40% of cancers)
- Biopsy/polypectomy limited
- Positive requires colonoscopy

**Combined with FIT:**
- FIT annually + sigmoidoscopy every 10 years
- Better than either alone

### 6. Capsule Colonoscopy

**Procedure:**
- Swallow camera capsule
- Images transmitted to recorder
- Every 5 years (if available)

**Advantages:**
- Minimally invasive
- No sedation
- Views entire colon

**Disadvantages:**
- Requires bowel prep
- Positive requires colonoscopy
- Cannot biopsy or remove polyps
- Risk of retention (strictures)
- Expensive

## Screening Guidelines

### USPSTF (2021)

**Average Risk:**
- Begin at age 45
- Continue to age 75
- Shared decision-making 76-85

**Recommended Strategies:**
- Colonoscopy every 10 years
- FIT annually
- Stool DNA every 3 years
- CT colonography every 5 years
- Flexible sigmoidoscopy every 5 years
- Flexible sigmoidoscopy every 10 years + FIT annually

### American Cancer Society (2018)

**Average Risk:**
- Begin at age 45
- Continue to age 75
- Individualize 76-85

### American College of Gastroenterology

**Preferred:**
- Colonoscopy every 10 years

**Acceptable:**
- FIT annually
- CT colonography every 5 years

## Post-Polypectomy Surveillance

### Risk Stratification

**Low Risk (3-6 year follow-up):**
- 1-2 tubular adenomas <10mm
- Colonoscopy in 7-10 years

**Intermediate Risk (3 year follow-up):**
- 3-4 tubular adenomas <10mm
- OR 1-2 adenomas with high-grade dysplasia
- OR 1-2 adenomas ≥10mm

**High Risk (1-3 year follow-up):**
- ≥5 adenomas
- OR ≥3 with one ≥10mm
- OR any number with villous features

**Very High Risk (short interval):**
- Piecemeal resection of large polyp (>20mm)
- Colonoscopy in 2-6 months

### Serrated Lesions

**Sessile Serrated Lesions (SSL):**
- <10mm, no dysplasia: 5-10 years
- ≥10mm or dysplasia: 3 years

**Traditional Serrated Adenoma:**
- Treat as high-risk adenoma

## Special Populations

### IBD

**Ulcerative Colitis and Crohn's Colitis:**
- Begin 8 years after diagnosis
- Colonoscopy every 1-2 years
- Random biopsies (surveillance)
- Pancolitis: higher risk

### Family History

**One first-degree relative with CRC <60:**
- Begin at age 40 or 10 years before earliest case
- Colonoscopy every 5 years

**Two first-degree relatives:**
- Same as above
- Consider genetic counseling

## Quality Indicators

### Colonoscopy Quality

**Adenoma Detection Rate (ADR):**
- Benchmark: ≥25% (men), ≥20% (women)
- Higher ADR = lower interval cancer rate

**Cecal Intubation Rate:**
- >95%
- Documented by photo

**Withdrawal Time:**
- ≥6 minutes (normal exams)
- Longer times correlate with higher ADR

**Bowel Preparation:**
- Adequate in >90%

## Barriers to Screening

### Patient Barriers
- Fear of procedure
- Bowel prep burden
- Cost/insurance
- Lack of awareness
- Fatalism

### System Barriers
- Access to colonoscopy
- Endoscopist availability
- Socioeconomic disparities
- Rural/urban differences

### Solutions
- Offer choice of tests
- Patient navigation
- Reminder systems
- Remove cost barriers
- Public education

## Summary

**Key Points:**
1. Start screening at age 45 (average risk)
2. Colonoscopy is gold standard (10-year interval)
3. FIT is acceptable alternative (annual)
4. Stool DNA every 3 years (non-invasive)
5. CT colonography every 5 years (radiation)
6. Post-polypectomy surveillance based on findings
7. IBD requires specialized surveillance (8 years post-diagnosis)
8. Family history advances screening age

** Pearl:** The best screening test is the one that gets done. Offer patients choices and encourage completion. FIT is as effective as colonoscopy at the population level due to higher adherence. Don't forget surveillance colonoscopy—missed interval cancers often occur in patients who never returned for follow-up.
