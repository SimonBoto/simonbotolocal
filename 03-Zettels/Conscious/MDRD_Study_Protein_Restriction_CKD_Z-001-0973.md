## Study Overview

**Full Name:** Modification of Diet in Renal Disease
**Design:** Randomized controlled trial
**Period:** 1989-1993 (publication)
**Patients:** 840 adults with moderate to severe renal insufficiency

## Study Design

### Two Interventions

**Study A (585 patients):**
- CKD stages 3-4 (GFR 25-55 mL/min)
- Low protein (0.58 g/kg) vs usual protein (1.3 g/kg)
- Primary endpoint: GFR decline rate

**Study B (255 patients):**
- CKD stage 4 (GFR 13-24 mL/min)
- Very low protein (0.28 g/kg) + ketoacids vs low protein (0.58 g/kg)
- Primary endpoint: GFR decline rate, time to renal failure/death

## Key Findings

### Primary Results

**Study A:**
- **No significant difference** in GFR decline between groups
- Trend favored low protein but didn't reach statistical significance
- Controversial negative result

**Study B:**
- Very low protein + ketoacids vs low protein: **No significant difference**
- Post-hoc analysis suggested benefit in specific subgroups

### Reanalysis and Long-Term Follow-Up

**Later Analysis (Levey et al., 1999):**
- Extended follow-up revealed modest benefit
- Low protein associated with slower progression in some analyses
- Conflicting interpretations

## Methodological Issues

### Compliance Problems

**Achieved vs Prescribed Intake:**
- Protein intake in "low protein" group higher than prescribed
- Crossover between groups
- Intention-to-treat vs per-protocol differences

**Measurement:**
- UUN used to verify compliance
- Still imperfect adherence

### Statistical Controversy

**Primary vs Secondary Analyses:**
- Primary analysis: Negative
- Multiple secondary analyses: Some positive
- Risk of data dredging

### Generalizability

**Limitations:**
- Highly selected population
- Intensive dietary intervention not feasible in routine care
- Short follow-up for chronic disease

##  Impact

### Guidelines Adoption

**Despite Inconclusive Results:**
- Became foundation for protein restriction recommendations
- Cited in NKF/KDIGO guidelines
- Influenced standard of care

**Rationale:**
- Mechanistic plausibility (hyperfiltration hypothesis)
- Safety demonstrated
- No better alternatives

### Subsequent Research

**Meta-Analyses Including MDRD:**
- Pooled with other trials
- Modest benefit of protein restriction confirmed
- Effect size: small but consistent

## Modern Perspective

### Critiques

**1. Underpowered:**
- Sample size calculations may have been optimistic
- Detectable effect size larger than realistic benefit

**2. Short Duration:**
- 2-3 years may be insufficient
- CKD progresses over decades

**3. Lack of Hard Endpoints:**
- GFR slope vs ESRD, mortality
- Surrogate endpoints problematic

### Lessons for Trial Design

**What Was Done Well:**
- Randomized, controlled
- Objective endpoints (GFR)
- Attempted compliance verification

**What Could Improve:**
- Larger sample
- Longer follow-up
- Hard clinical endpoints
- Real-world applicability

## Current Relevance

**Still Cited Because:**
- Largest RCT of protein restriction
- Only adequately powered trial
- No subsequent trial superseded it

**Interpreted With Caution:**
- Modest effect at best
- Must balance against mal risk
- Individualize based on patient factors
