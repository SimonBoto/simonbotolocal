# Glucose Discovery — Andreas Marggraf (1747)

> In 1747, German chemist Andreas Marggraf isolated glucose from raisins, becoming the first to identify this simple sugar as a distinct chemical entity, though its physiological significance would not be understood for over a century.

## Andreas Marggraf (1709-1782)

**Background:**
- German chemist and pharmacist
- Director of the Chemical Laboratory at the Berlin Academy of Sciences
- Pioneer of analytical chemistry
- Developed new methods for mineral and plant analysis

**The Discovery (1747):**
- Source: Raisins (dried grapes)
- Method: Extraction and purification
- Product: White crystalline substance
- Identification: Sweet-tasting, similar to honey sugar

## Scientific Context

**Pre-Discovery Understanding:**
- Sugars known as natural substances
- Honey, fruit sugars, cane sugar recognized
- No chemical differentiation
- "Sweet principle" not understood

**Marggraf's Contribution:**
- Demonstrated sugar in plants
- Showed chemical similarity between fruit sugar and honey
- Established existence of glucose as distinct entity
- Foundation for carbohydrate chemistry

## Limitations of the Era

**What Wasn't Known (1747):**
- Chemical formula (C6H12O6)
- Structure or stereochemistry
- Physiological role in 
- Connection to 
- Difference from other sugars (fructose, galactose)

**Timeline to Understanding:**
- 1747: Isolation
- 19th C: Chemical formula
- 1850s: Bernard's glycogen discovery
- 1902: Fischer's stereochemistry
- 1921:  connection

## Significance

**For Chemistry:**
- Early pure compound isolation
- Plant chemistry foundation
- Analytical methods development

**For Medicine:**
- Eventually led to diabetes understanding
- Glucose as diagnostic marker
- Foundation for metabolic disease research
