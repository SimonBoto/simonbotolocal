# Endoscopic Ultrasound (EUS) — Indications and  Applications

## Overview

Endoscopic ultrasound (EUS) combines endoscopy with high-frequency ultrasound imaging, providing detailed visualization of the GI wall layers and adjacent structures. It is the premier modality for evaluating pancreaticobiliary disorders and mediastinal pathology.

## Technical Aspects

### Equipment

**Echoendoscopes:**
- Linear array (therapeutic)
- Radial array (diagnostic imaging)
- High-frequency (5-12 MHz) for high resolution

**Imaging Capabilities:**
- 5-layer GI wall visualization
- Structures <5mm from GI tract
- Doppler for vascular assessment
- Fine needle aspiration/biopsy

### GI Wall Layers

**Five Layers on EUS:**
1. **Hyperechoic** - Interface/border echo
2. **Hypoechoic** - Mucosa (muscularis mucosae)
3. **Hyperechoic** - Submucosa
4. **Hypoechoic** - Muscularis propria
5. **Hyperechoic** - Serosa/adventitia

**T Staging Advantage:**
- Direct visualization of tumor invasion depth
- T1 vs T2 vs T3 vs T4 accuracy
- Superior to CT for early gastric/esophageal 

## Primary Indications

### 1. Pancreaticobiliary Evaluation

**Pancreatic Mass Characterization:**
- **Distinguish solid vs cystic lesions**
- Cyst wall features (thickening, nodules)
- Ductal involvement
- Vascular invasion assessment

**FNA (Fine Needle Aspiration):**
- Tissue diagnosis of pancreatic mass
- Sensitivity ~85-90% for malignancy
- Lower sensitivity for cystic lesions (avoid if mucinous cyst)
- ROSH cytology assessment

**ROSH Criteria (Rapid On-Site Evaluation):**
- Adequate cellularity
- Assessment of malignant features
- Real-time evaluation
- Reduces inadequate samples

**Biliary Evaluation:**
- Choledocholithiasis (alternative to ERCP)
- Distal CBD strictures
- Ampullary lesions

### 2. Cancer Staging

**Esophageal Cancer:**
- **T staging** (accuracy 85-90%)
- Nodal assessment (periesophageal, celiac)
- Detects T3/T4 vs T1/T2
- Guides treatment (surgery vs chemoradiation)

**Gastric Cancer:**
- Depth of invasion (T stage)
- Perigastric lymph nodes
- Assessment of resectability

**Rectal Cancer:**
- T staging
- Mesorectal lymph nodes
- CRM (circumferential resection margin)
- Guides neoadjuvant therapy

**Pancreatic Cancer:**
- Vascular invasion (portal vein, SMA, celiac)
- Resectability assessment
- Nodal staging
- Avoid unnecessary surgery

### 3. Subepithelial Lesions (SEL)

**Characterization:**
- Layer of origin (which wall layer)
- Size and morphology
- Echogenicity
- Doppler vascularity

**Common SELs:**
- **GIST** (GI stromal tumor) - layer 4 (muscularis)
- **Leiomyoma** - layer 4
- **Carcinoid** - layer 2/3
- **Lipoma** - layer 3 (hyperechoic)
- **Varices** - layer 2/3, vascular on Doppler
- **Cysts** - anechoic

**FNA/Biopsy:**
- GIST: Confirm with DOG1, CD117
- Risk stratification based on size/location

### 4. Pancreatic Cyst Evaluation

**Cyst Characterization:**
- Wall thickness
- Mural nodules
- Septations
- Solid components
- Communication with duct

**FNA for Cyst Fluid:**
- CEA, amylase
- Cytology (low yield)
- Viscosity
- DNA analysis (KRAS, GNAS)

**Caution:** Avoid FNA of mucinous cysts if resectable

### 5. Neuroendocrine Tumors (NETs)

**Localization:**
- Pancreatic NETs (small, difficult to find on CT)
- Gastric/duodenal NETs
- Peripancreatic lymph nodes

**Features:**
- Hypoechoic, well-defined
- Vascular on Doppler
- FNA for grading (Ki-67)

### 6. Fiducial Placement

**Indication:**
- Pancreatic cancer radiation planning
- Stereotactic body radiation therapy (SBRT)

**Procedure:**
- EUS-guided placement
- Gold fiducials
- CT verification
- Enables precise targeting

### 7. Therapeutic Applications

**EUS-Guided Interventions:**
- Celiac plexus neurolysis/block (pain control)
- Pseudocyst drainage
- Gallbladder drainage (EUS-GBD)
- Biliary drainage (EUS-BD)
- Gastrojejunostomy
- Tumor ablation (radiofrequency, cryo)

## Contraindications

**Absolute:**
- Severe coagulopathy (INR correction needed)
- Hemodynamic instability
- Inability to sedate safely

**Relative:**
- Severe cardiopulmonary disease
- Recent GI surgery
- Acute pancreatitis (for pancreatic procedures)
- Large hiatal hernia (esophageal EUS)

## Complications

**Overall:** 0.5-1%

**Specific Risks:**
- Perforation (0.03-0.1%)
- Bleeding (FNA-related)
- Infection (cyst FNA - rare with prophylaxis)
- Pancreatitis (1-3% after pancreatic FNA)
- Bacteremia (rare)

**Aspiration Precautions:**
- Antibiotics for cyst aspiration
- Monitor for sepsis

## Comparison with Other Modalities

### EUS vs CT

**EUS Superior:**
- T staging of GI cancers
- Small pancreatic lesions (<2cm)
- Subepithelial lesions
- Biliary stones (choledocholithiasis)
- Peripancreatic lymph nodes

**CT Superior:**
- Distant metastases
- Overall cancer staging
- Liver lesions
- Peritoneal disease

**Complementary:** Use both

### EUS vs MRCP

**EUS Superior:**
- Solid pancreatic masses
- Small stones
- Tissue acquisition
- SEL characterization

**MRCP Superior:**
- Biliary tree anatomy
- Ductal strictures
- No radiation
- Non-invasive

## Summary

**Key Indications:**
1. Pancreatic mass evaluation + FNA
2. Cancer T staging (esophageal, gastric, rectal, pancreatic)
3. Subepithelial lesion characterization
4. Pancreatic cyst assessment
5. Neuroendocrine tumor localization
6. Fiducial placement for radiation
7. Therapeutic interventions

**Clinical Pearl:** EUS is the single best test for evaluating pancreatic masses and for T staging of GI cancers. If you need tissue from a pancreatic mass or to stage esophageal/gastric cancer locally, EUS is essential.
