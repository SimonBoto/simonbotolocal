# Cirrhosis and Portal Hypertension — MELD-Na, TIPS, and Complications

## Overview

Cirrhosis represents end-stage liver disease with fibrosis, regenerative nodules, and architectural distortion leading to portal hypertension and hepatic synthetic dysfunction. Management requires understanding staging, complications, and interventional options.

## Pathophysiology

### Portal Hypertension

**Definition:**
- Portal pressure >5 mmHg (normal 1-5)
- [[KEYWORD-Clinical|clinical]]ly significant: >10 mmHg (varices form)
- Variceal hemorrhage risk: >12 mmHg

**[[KEYWORD-Mechanism|mechanism]]s:**
1. **Increased resistance** (intrahepatic)
   - Fibrosis and regenerative nodules
   - Sinusoidal capillarization
   - Endothelial dysfunction
   - Dynamic component (vasoconstriction)

2. **Increased portal flow** (splanchnic vasodilation)
   - Nitric oxide release
   - Arterial vasodilation
   - Hyperdynamic circulation
   - Increased splanchnic inflow

**Consequences:**
- Varices (esophageal, gastric, ectopic)
- Ascites
- Hepatic encephalopathy
- Portosystemic shunts
- Hepatorenal syndrome

### Cirrhosis Staging

**Child-Pugh Classification:**
| Parameter | 1 point | 2 points | 3 points |
|-----------|---------|----------|----------|
| **Bilirubin (mg/dL)** | <2 | 2-3 | >3 |
| **Albumin (g/dL)** | >3.5 | 2.8-3.5 | <2.8 |
| **INR** | <1.7 | 1.7-2.3 | >2.3 |
| **Ascites** | None | Mild | Severe |
| **Encephalopathy** | None | Grade 1-2 | Grade 3-4 |

**Classes:**
- A: 5-6 points (well-compensated, 100% 1-year survival)
- B: 7-9 points (significant compromise, 80% 1-year survival)
- C: 10-15 points (decompensated, 45% 1-year survival)

## MELD and MELD-Na Scores

### MELD (Model for End-Stage Liver Disease)

**Formula:**
```
MELD = 3.78×ln(bilirubin) + 11.2×ln(INR) + 9.57×ln(creatinine) + 6.43
```

**Variables:**
- Bilirubin (mg/dL)
- INR
- Creatinine (mg/dL)
- Dialysis (2x in past week = creatinine 4.0)

### MELD-Na (Preferred)

**Addition:**
```
MELD-Na = MELD + 1.32×(137-Na) - [0.033×MELD×(137-Na)]
```

**Why Sodium?**
- Hyponatremia reflects severity
- Independent predictor of mortality
- Better predicts waiting list mortality
- Captains portal hypertension complications

**Score Interpretation:**
| Score | 3-Month Mortality |
|-------|-------------------|
| <9 | 1.9% |
| 10-19 | 6.0% |
| 20-29 | 19.6% |
| 30-39 | 52.6% |
| ≥40 | 71.3% (most die) |

### Clinical Applications

**Liver Transplant Priority:**
- Higher MELD = higher priority
- MELD ≥15: Consider transplant
- MELD ≥30: Urgent
- MELD ≥40: Critical

**Special MELD Exceptions:**
- HCC (hepatocellular carcinoma)
- Acute liver failure
- Metabolic diseases
- Cholangiocarcinoma (protocols)

**Procedural Risk Assessment:**
- Higher MELD = higher perioperative risk
- TIPS: MELD >18-20 increases mortality
- Surgery: MELD >15 increases complications

## Variceal Hemorrhage

### Screening

**Indications:**
- All cirrhotics at diagnosis
- Every 2-3 years if no varices
- Annually if small varices
- Repeat after decompensation

**Methods:**
- Upper endoscopy (gold standard)
- Capsule endoscopy (acceptable alternative)
- Non-invasive markers (platelet count, spleen size) less reliable

### Primary Prophylaxis

**Indications:**
- Medium/large varices (regardless of bleeding risk)
- Small varices with high-risk features (Child C, red wale marks)

**Options:**
1. **Non-selective beta-blockers:**
   - Propranolol or nadolol
   - Reduce portal pressure by 20% or to <12 mmHg
   - Target HR 55-60 or 25% reduction
   - Contraindications: asthma, severe COPD, bradycardia, hypotension

2. **Endoscopic band ligation (EBL):**
   - Preferred if beta-blocker contraindicated
   - Eradicate varices over several sessions
   - Repeat every 2-4 weeks until eradicated

**Combination:**
- Beta-blocker + EBL: No added benefit in primary prophylaxis

### Acute Variceal Bleeding

**Management:**

**1. Resuscitation:**
- IV access, fluids (avoid over-resuscitation)
- Blood transfusion: Hb 7-8 g/dL target
- Platelets >50, INR correction controversial
- Antibiotic prophylaxis (ceftriaxone 7 days)

**2. Pharmacotherapy:**
- **Octreotide** or somatostatin: Start immediately
  - Reduces splanchnic blood flow
  - 50-100 mcg bolus, then 50 mcg/hr
  - Continue 2-5 days
- **Vasopressin** + nitroglycerin: Alternative (more side effects)
- **Terlipressin:** Available outside US

**3. Endoscopic therapy:**
- **EGD within 12 hours**
- Band ligation (preferred)
- Sclerotherapy (if banding difficult)
- Confirm variceal source

**4. Balloon tamponade:**
- Minnesota or Sengstaken-Blakemore tube
- Bridge to definitive therapy
- High complication rate
- ICU monitoring essential

**5. TIPS:**
- Rescue for failure
- Pre-emptive in high-risk (Child C, active bleeding)

### Secondary Prophylaxis

**After index bleed:**
- Beta-blocker + EBL (combination reduces rebleeding)
- Repeat EBL until eradicated
- Surveillance every 6-12 months
- Lifelong

## TIPS Procedure

### Transjugular Intrahepatic Portosystemic Shunt

**Principle:**
- Create connection between hepatic vein and portal vein
- Stent placement reduces portal pressure
- Diverts blood around liver

**Indications:**

**1. Acute variceal bleeding:**
- Rescue after medical/endoscopic failure
- Pre-emptive (Child C, active bleed)

**2. Secondary prophylaxis:**
- Recurrent bleeding despite optimal therapy
- Gastric varices (especially fundal)

**3. Refractory ascites:**
- Diuretic-resistant or intolerant
- Improves response, may delay transplant

**4. Hepatic hydrothorax:**
- Refractory pleural effusion
- Usually right-sided

**5. Hepatorenal syndrome:**
- Bridge to transplant
- Improves renal perfusion

**6. Budd-Chiari syndrome:**
- Portal decompression
- Bridge to transplant

### Contraindications

**Absolute:**
- Severe pulmonary hypertension
- Congestive heart failure
- Uncontrolled sepsis
- Unrelieved biliary obstruction

**Relative:**
- Hepatic encephalopathy (can worsen)
- Large hepatic mass
- Portal vein thrombosis (pre-stenting required)
- MELD >18-20 (high mortality)

### Technical Aspects

**Approach:**
- Jugular vein access
- Hepatic vein cannulation
- Portal vein puncture (ultrasound/fluoroscopy guided)
- Balloon dilation
- Stent deployment (covered preferred)

**Goal:**
- Portal pressure gradient (PPG) <12 mmHg
- Or reduction by >50%

### Complications

**Hepatic Encephalopathy (15-45%):**
- New or worsening post-TIPS
- Medical management (lactulose, rifaximin)
- May require shunt reduction

**Shunt dysfunction (10-20%):**
- Stenosis or thrombosis
- Doppler surveillance
- Revision if needed

**Liver failure:**
- Diversion worsens hepatic function
- MELD-dependent risk

**Other:**
- Bleeding
- Infection
- Cardiac overload

### Post-TIPS Management

**Surveillance:**
- Doppler ultrasound at 1, 3, 6, 12 months
- Then every 6-12 months
- Assess shunt patency
- Portal flow direction

## Ascites

### Pathophysiology

**Mechanisms:**
1. Portal hypertension (sine qua non)
2. Splanchnic arterial vasodilation
3. Reduced effective arterial blood volume
4. Sodium/water retention (RAAS, ADH activation)
5. Lymphatic overflow

### Diagnosis

**Paracentesis:**
- Indicated at diagnosis, hospitalization, deterioration
- Cell count, albumin, culture
- SAAG (Serum-Ascites Albumin Gradient)

**SAAG:**
- ≥1.1 g/dL = Portal hypertension
- <1.1 g/dL = Non-portal cause

### Treatment

**Sodium restriction:**
- <2g/day (88 mmol)
- Dietary counseling

**Diuretics:**
- **Spironolactone** (aldosterone antagonist)
  - Start 100 mg daily
  - Titrate to 400 mg
- **Furosemide** (loop diuretic)
  - Start 40 mg daily
  - Titrate to 160 mg
- Ratio 100:40 (spironolactone:furosemide)

**Monitoring:**
- Weight loss 0.5-1 kg/day (no edema)
- Weight loss 1-2 kg/day (with edema)
- Renal function
- Electrolytes

**Refractory Ascites:**
- **Large volume paracentesis** (LVP) + albumin
  - Albumin 6-8g per liter removed (if >5L)
- **TIPS**
- **Liver transplant evaluation**

## Hepatic Encephalopathy

### Pathophysiology

**Ammonia hypothesis:**
- Gut bacteria produce ammonia
- Impaired hepatic clearance
- Crosses blood-brain barrier
- Astrocyte swelling
- Neurotransmitter imbalance (GABA, glutamate)

**Other factors:**
- [[KEYWORD-Inflammation|inflammation]]
- Benzodiazepine-like substances
- Manganese deposition
- Cerebral edema

### Grading (West Haven)

| Grade | Features |
|-------|----------|
| **1** | Trivial lack of awareness, euphoria/anxiety, shortened attention span |
| **2** | Lethargy/apathy, minimal disorientation, subtle personality change |
| **3** | Somnolent but arousable, confusion, severe disorientation |
| **4** | Coma (unresponsive to pain) |

### Precipitants

**Common:**
- Infection (SBP most common)
- GI bleeding
- Constipation
- Dehydration/diuresis
- Sedatives (benzodiazepines, opioids)
- Electrolyte disturbances (hypokalemia)
- High protein intake
- Transjugular procedures (TIPS)

### Treatment

**Lactulose:**
- Dose to 2-3 soft stools/day
- 15-30 mL BID-TID
- Mechanism: Acidifies colon, reduces ammonia absorption

**Rifaximin:**
- 550 mg BID
- Non-absorbable antibiotic
- Reduces ammonia-producing bacteria
- Add to lactulose for recurrent HE

**Protein restriction:**
- No longer recommended
- Maintain 1.2-1.5 g/kg/day
- Vegetable protein preferred

**Other:**
- Treat precipitants
- Avoid sedatives
- Manage constipation
- Zinc supplementation (if deficient)

## Summary

**Key Points:**
1. Portal hypertension drives cirrhosis complications
2. MELD-Na predicts mortality and transplant priority
3. Varices: Screen all, band if medium/large, beta-blockers
4. Acute bleed: Octreotide + antibiotics + endoscopy within 12h
5. TIPS: Rescue for bleeding, definitive for refractory ascites
6. Ascites: Sodium restriction + diuretics, LVP if refractory
7. HE: Lactulose + rifaximin, treat precipitants

**Clinical Pearl:** In cirrhosis, always calculate MELD-Na, screen for varices, and think about transplant when MELD >15 or complications occur. TIPS is a bridge, not a cure.
