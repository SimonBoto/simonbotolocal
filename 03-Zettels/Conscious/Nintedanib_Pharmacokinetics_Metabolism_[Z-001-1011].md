# Nintedanib Pharmacokinetics and Metabolism — Esterases, UGTs, and CYP3A4

## Overview

Nintedanib has a unique metabolic profile primarily involving esterases, with secondary glucuronidation and minimal CYP450 involvement. Understanding these pathways is important for predicting drug interactions and managing therapy.

## Absorption

**Bioavailability:**
- ~4.7% (low)
- Due to extensive first-pass metabolism
- P-gp substrate (efflux transporter)

**Food Effect:**
- High-fat meal increases exposure by ~20%
- Recommended with food

**Timing:**
- Twice daily dosing (12 hours apart)
- With meals

## Primary Metabolism: Esterases

### Esterase-Mediated Hydrolysis

**Primary Pathway:**
```
Nintedanib (ethyl ester)
    ↓
Carboxylesterases (CES1, CES2)
    ↓
BIBF 1202 (active metabolite, carboxylic acid)
    ↓
UGT-mediated glucuronidation
```

**Carboxylesterases:**
- **CES1:** Liver, adipose tissue
- **CES2:** Intestine, liver
- Hydrolyze ester bond
- Form pharmacologically active metabolite

**Clinical Significance:**
- Major pathway (~25% of dose)
- Esterase inhibitors could increase nintedanib levels
- Limited clinically significant interactions known

## Secondary Metabolism: Glucuronidation

### UGT Enzymes

**Primary UGTs:**
| UGT | Role | Location |
|-----|------|----------|
| **UGT1A1** | Major | Liver, intestine |
| **UGT1A7** | Moderate | Liver, intestine |
| **UGT1A9** | Moderate | Liver, kidney, intestine |
| **UGT2B7** | Moderate | Liver, kidney, intestine |

**Reaction:**
```
BIBF 1202 + UDP-glucuronic acid
    ↓
UGT enzymes
    ↓
BIBF 1202-glucuronide (inactive)
```

**Clinical Significance:**
- Major elimination pathway
- UGT inhibitors could increase levels
- UGT inducers could decrease levels

### UGT Inhibitors (Potential Interactions)

**Strong UGT Inhibitors:**
- **Probenecid** (classic)
- **Valproic acid**

**Moderate:**
- **Fluconazole**
- **Ketoconazole** (also CYP/P-gp)

**Clinical Management:**
- Monitor for increased toxicity if co-administered
- Dose reduction may be needed

### UGT Inducers

**Potential:**
- **Rifampin** (strong inducer)
- **Phenytoin**
- **Carbamazepine**
- **St. John's wort**

**Effect:**
- Could reduce nintedanib exposure
- Decreased efficacy
- Avoid if possible

## Minor Pathway: CYP3A4

### Contribution

**Limited Role:**
- Minor metabolic pathway (~5%)
- CYP3A4-mediated oxidation
- Not clinically significant for most interactions

### CYP3A4 Interactions

**Inhibitors:**
- Ketoconazole
- Clarithromycin
- Itraconazole
- Grapefruit juice

**Effect:**
- Minimal on nintedanib
- P-gp inhibition more relevant

**Inducers:**
- Rifampin
- Carbamazepine
- Phenytoin

## Transport: P-glycoprotein (P-gp)

### Role in Nintedanib Disposition

**P-gp (ABCB1):**
- Efflux transporter
- Limits intestinal absorption
- Biliary excretion
- Substrate of nintedanib

**Clinical Impact:**
- P-gp inhibition increases exposure
- More significant than CYP interactions

### P-gp Inhibitors

**Strong:**
- **Ketoconazole**
- **Itraconazole**
- **Clarithromycin**
- **Verapamil**

**Effect:**
- Increased nintedanib exposure
- Increased toxicity risk
- Avoid or monitor closely

### P-gp Inducers

**Strong:**
- **Rifampin** (major)
- **St. John's wort**

**Effect:**
- Decreased nintedanib exposure
- Reduced efficacy
- Contraindicated

## Elimination

**Routes:**
- **Fecal:** ~93% (primarily)
- **Renal:** <1%
- **Biliary excretion:** Major pathway

**Half-life:**
- ~9.5 hours
- Supports twice-daily dosing

**Accumulation:**
- Steady state in ~1 week
- No significant accumulation beyond expected

## Drug Interaction Summary

### Contraindicated

**Strong P-gp Inducers:**
- Rifampin
- St. John's wort
- Significantly reduce exposure

### Use With Caution

**P-gp Inhibitors:**
- Azole antifungals
- Macrolide antibiotics
- Monitor for toxicity

**UGT Inhibitors:**
- Probenecid
- Valproic acid
- Monitor liver enzymes, diarrhea

### Generally Safe

**CYP3A4 modulators:**
- Limited effect
- Less concern than P-gp/UGT

## Special Populations

### Hepatic Impairment

**Child-Pugh A:**
- No dose adjustment
- Monitor LFTs

**Child-Pugh B:**
- Use with caution
- Monitor closely
- Dose reduction if needed

**Child-Pugh C:**
- Contraindicated

### Renal Impairment

**Mild-Moderate (CrCl 30-90):**
- No adjustment needed
- Minimal renal elimination

**Severe (<30):**
- Limited data
- Use caution

### Age/Gender

**No clinically significant differences**

## Practical Management

### Initiation

**Baseline:**
- Liver function tests
- Pregnancy test (contraindicated)
- Medication review for interactions

### Monitoring

**Regular:**
- LFTs (monthly first 3 months, then quarterly)
- Diarrhea assessment
- Bleeding events

### Dose Modifications

**For Toxicity:**
- Interrupt dose
- Reduce to 100 mg bid
- Resume 150 mg if tolerated

**For Drug Interactions:**
- Avoid strong P-gp inducers
- Monitor with P-gp inhibitors
- Consider dose reduction

## Summary

**Key Metabolic Features:**
1. Primary: Esterases (CES1/2)
2. Secondary: Glucuronidation (UGT1A1, 1A7, 1A9, 2B7)
3. Minor: CYP3A4
4. Major transporter: P-gp
5. Elimination: Biliary/fecal (93%)

**Key Interaction Risks:**
- P-gp inducers (contraindicated)
- P-gp inhibitors (caution)
- UGT inhibitors (caution)
- CYP interactions (minimal)

**Clinical Pearl:** Focus on P-gp and UGT interactions rather than CYP when managing nintedanib. Rifampin and St. John's wort are contraindicated due to P-gp induction. Monitor LFTs regularly due to hepatotoxicity risk.
