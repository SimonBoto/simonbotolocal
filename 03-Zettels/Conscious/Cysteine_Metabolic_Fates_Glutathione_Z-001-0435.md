## Core Claim
**Cysteine, derived from methionine via transsulfuration, serves as a metabolic branch point with four distinct fates: glutathione synthesis (antioxidant), taurine synthesis (bile/osmoregulation), sulfate production (detoxification), or complete oxidation to pyruvate for energy** — this distribution is regulated by cellular redox status and metabolic demand.

## Evidence

### Four Metabolic Fates
| Pathway | Product | Function | Regulation |
|---------|---------|----------|------------|
| **1. Glutathione** | GSH | Antioxidant, detoxification | High demand in oxidative stress |
| **2. Taurine** | C₂H₇NO₃S | Bile conjugation, osmoregulation | Hepatic synthesis |
| **3. Sulfate** | SO₄²⁻ | Sulfation reactions, excretion | Sulfur disposal |
| **4. Energy** | Pyruvate → ATP | ATP production | Low glutathione demand |

### Glutathione Priority
- **Rate-limiting:** Cysteine availability
- **High oxidative stress** → cysteine diverted to GSH
- **Low stress** → cysteine available for other fates

## Clinical Implication
- **Cysteine is the bottleneck for glutathione synthesis** — methionine/B6 must be adequate
- **High oxidative stress depletes cysteine** — may impair taurine and sulfate production
- **NAC (N-acetylcysteine)** bypasses transsulfuration to boost cysteine directly
- **METHAP relevance:** B6 supports transsulfuration → cysteine → glutathione; oxidative stress shifts cysteine toward GSH
