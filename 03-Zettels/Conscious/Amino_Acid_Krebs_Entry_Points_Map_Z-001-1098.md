## Core Claim
**Each amino acid enters the Krebs cycle at specific points, with 18 entering at various intermediates (glucogenic) and 2 (leucine, lysine) converted to acetyl-CoA (ketogenic)** — this mapping determines whether protein catabolism supports glucose production or ketogenesis.

## Evidence

### Entry Points by Krebs Intermediate
| Entry Point | Amino Acids | Cofactors Required |
|-------------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Pyruvate** | Ala, Ser, Gly, Cys, Trp | B6, B2, iron |
| **Acetyl-CoA** (ketogenic) | Leu, Lys, Phe, Tyr, Trp | B vitamins |
| **Alpha-ketoglutarate** | Glu, Gln, Pro, Arg, His | B6 |
| **Succinyl-CoA** | Ile, Met, Val, Thr | **B12**, biotin |
| **Fumarate** | Phe, Tyr | — |
| **Oxaloacetate** | Asp, Asn | B6 |

### Key Patterns
- **Transamination (B6)** removes amino groups from most amino acids
- **B12 required** for branched-chain and sulfur amino acids entering at succinyl-CoA
- **Leucine and lysine exclusively ketogenic** — cannot produce glucose

## Clinical Implication
- **B6 deficiency impairs most amino acid entry** — broad metabolic impact
- **B12 deficiency blocks succinyl-CoA entry** — impairs energy from 4 amino acids
- **Maple syrup urine disease:** Defect in branched-chain alpha-keto acid dehydrogenase
- **METHAP relevance:** B-vitamin adequacy ensures complete amino acid utilization
