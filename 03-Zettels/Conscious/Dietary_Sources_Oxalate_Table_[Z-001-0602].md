# Dietary Sources of 

## Very High Oxalate Foods (>100 mg/serving)

| Food | Oxalate Content | Notes |
|------|-----------------|-------|
| **Spinach** | ~750 mg/cup cooked | Highest common source |
| **Rhubarb** | ~500 mg/cup | Stem and leaves |
| **Beet greens** | ~400 mg/cup | Not roots |
| **Swiss chard** | ~350 mg/cup | Leafy green |

## High Oxalate Foods (50-100 mg/serving)

| Food | Oxalate Content | Notes |
|------|-----------------|-------|
| **Almonds** | ~75 mg/oz | Nuts |
| **Cashews** | ~60 mg/oz | Nuts |
| **Sesame seeds** | ~55 mg/oz | Seeds |
| **Buckwheat** | ~65 mg/cup | Grain |
| **Wheat bran** | ~50 mg/cup | Fiber |
| **Dark chocolate** | ~55 mg/oz | Cocoa |
| **Black tea** | ~50 mg/cup | Beverage |
| **Soybeans** | ~48 mg/cup | Legumes |
| **Navy beans** | ~45 mg/cup | Legumes |

## Moderate Oxalate Foods (10-50 mg/serving)

- Collard greens, kale
- Carrots, celery
- Cranberries, oranges
- Coffee (brewed)
- Peanuts, walnuts

## Low Oxalate Foods (<10 mg/serving)

- Most meats, fish, eggs
- Dairy products
- White rice, white bread
- Bananas, apples, grapes
- Cauliflower, cabbage
- Milk, cheese, yogurt

##  Relevance

**Kidney stone risk:** High oxalate intake increases urinary oxalate excretion, raising  oxalate stone risk.

**Management:** Low-oxalate diet (<50 mg/day) for stone formers; moderate intake for general population.
