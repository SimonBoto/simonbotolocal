# Seven Requirements for Ethical   — Emanuel Framework

## Overview

Ezekiel Emanuel, David Wendler, and Christine Grady (JAMA, 2000) proposed that informed consent alone is neither necessary nor sufficient for ethical clinical research. Seven requirements must be satisfied.

## The Seven Requirements

### 1. Value

**Definition:**
The research must enhance health or increase knowledge significantly.

**Key Points:**
- Must answer important scientific or clinical question
- Justifies risks participants undertake
- Trivial or redundant research is unethical

**Assessment Questions:**
- Does this study address an important gap?
- Will results be generalizable and useful?
- Is the research question worth the risks?

**Examples:**
- **Ethical:** Testing new  therapy with promising preclinical data
- **Unethical:** Repeating well-established findings without justification

### 2. Scientific Validity

**Definition:**
The research must use sound, rigorous methodology capable of producing reliable results.

**Rationale:**
- Invalid research wastes resources
- Exposes participants to risk without benefit
- Cannot contribute to knowledge

**Requirements:**
- Appropriate study design
- Adequate sample size
- Valid measurements
- Proper statistical analysis
- Qualified investigators

**Examples:**
- **Ethical:** RCT with adequate power, validated outcomes
- **Unethical:** Underpowered study destined to yield inconclusive results

### 3. Fair Subject Selection

**Definition:**
Subjects should be selected based on scientific objectives, not vulnerability or convenience.

**Principles:**
- Scientifically appropriate population
- Fair distribution of risks and benefits
- Avoid exploitation of vulnerable groups
- Convenience alone insufficient

**Vulnerable Populations:**
- Children
- Prisoners
- Pregnant women
- Cognitively impaired
- Economically disadvantaged
- Educationally disadvantaged

**Special Protections:**
- Must be scientifically necessary
- Additional safeguards
- Community consultation

**Examples:**
- **Ethical:** Testing pediatric vaccine in children (necessary for indication)
- **Unethical:** Testing experimental drug in impoverished population when could be done elsewhere

### 4. Favorable Risk-Benefit Ratio

**Definition:**
Potential benefits must outweigh risks, with risks minimized.

**Assessment:**
- Systematic risk identification
- Probability and magnitude of harms
- Probability and magnitude of benefits
- Risk minimization strategies

**Types of Benefits:**
- Direct to participants
- To society/generalizable knowledge
- To future patients

**Risk Categories:**
- Physical
- Psychological
- Social
- Economic
- Legal

**Hierarchy:**
1. Minimize risks (first priority)
2. Ensure benefits justify remaining risks

**Examples:**
- **Ethical:** Phase 1 cancer trial with toxicity monitoring, data informing future patients
- **Unethical:** High-risk procedure with minimal potential benefit

### 5. Independent Review

**Definition:**
A neutral body must review and approve research protocol.

**Purpose:**
- Unbiased evaluation
- Community representation
- Protection of participants
- Ensure ethical compliance

**Review Bodies:**
- Institutional Review Boards (IRB) — USA
- Research Ethics Committees (REC) — Europe
- Ethics Review Boards — Other countries

**Review Elements:**
- Scientific merit
- Risk-benefit assessment
- Informed consent process
- Subject selection fairness
- Data safety monitoring
- Privacy protections

**Authority:**
- Can approve
- Can require modifications
- Can reject
- Ongoing oversight

### 6. Informed Consent

**Definition:**
Participants provide voluntary, informed consent after understanding risks, benefits, and purpose.

**Elements (Belmont Report):**
1. **Information:**
   - Purpose of research
   - Procedures involved
   - Risks and discomforts
   - Benefits
   - Alternatives
   - Confidentiality
   - Compensation for injury
   - Right to withdraw
   - Contact information

2. **Comprehension:**
   - Appropriate for subject population
   - Consider education level
   - Consider language
   - Assess understanding

3. **Voluntariness:**
   - Free from coercion
   - Free from undue influence
   - No penalty for refusal
   - Can withdraw anytime

**Special Considerations:**
- Vulnerable populations may need additional protections
- Waivers possible in limited circumstances (minimal risk, impracticable)
- Ongoing process, not one-time event

### 7. Respect for Enrolled Subjects

**Definition:**
Ethical obligations continue after enrollment throughout study and beyond.

**Ongoing Requirements:**

**During Study:**
- Monitor well-being
- Allow withdrawal without penalty
- Maintain privacy/confidentiality
- Respond to new safety information
- Provide new information affecting consent

**Data Protection:**
- Secure storage
- De-identification
- Limited access
- Confidentiality agreements

**Post-Study:**
- Results dissemination
- Fair benefit sharing (especially in resource-limited settings)
- Post-trial access to beneficial interventions
- Long-term safety monitoring

**Vulnerable Populations:**
- Enhanced monitoring
- Additional safeguards
- Independent advocacy

## Application and Limitations

### Universal Applicability

**Framework Strengths:**
- Applies across cultures
- Adaptable to different contexts
- Comprehensive coverage
- Internationally recognized

**Contextual Adaptation:**
- Local health conditions
- Economic circumstances
- Cultural factors
- Technological capabilities

### Limitations

**Not Algorithmic:**
- Requires judgment
- Balancing competing values
- Context-dependent decisions

**Not Exhaustive:**
- Other ethical considerations exist
- Professional ethics
- Institutional policies

## Practical Application

### Research Protocol Evaluation

**Checklist:**
1. Is the research valuable? ____
2. Is the methodology valid? ____
3. Is subject selection fair? ____
4. Do benefits outweigh risks? ____
5. Has independent review occurred? ____
6. Is informed consent adequate? ____
7. Are enrolled subjects respected? ____

**All seven must be satisfied for ethical research.**

### IRB Review Application

**Alignment:**
- IRB criteria map to seven requirements
- Provides structured evaluation framework
- Supports consistent decision-making

## Summary

**Key Insight:**
Ethical research requires more than informed consent. All seven requirements must be met:

| Requirement | Core Question |
|-------------|---------------|
| Value | Is it worth doing? |
| Scientific Validity | Will it produce reliable knowledge? |
| Fair Subject Selection | Are participants selected fairly? |
| Risk-Benefit Ratio | Are risks justified by benefits? |
| Independent Review | Has neutral review occurred? |
| Informed Consent | Do participants understand and agree voluntarily? |
| Respect for Subjects | Are ongoing obligations met? |

**Clinical Pearl:** When evaluating research ethics, systematically assess all seven requirements. A deficit in any one renders the research ethically problematic.
