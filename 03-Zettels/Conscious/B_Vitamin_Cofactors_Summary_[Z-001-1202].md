# B-Vitamin Cofactors — Summary and Clinical Connections

## Overview

B-vitamins serve as essential cofactors (coenzymes) for hundreds of metabolic reactions. Understanding their interconnected roles is critical for clinical assessment and intervention.

---

## B-Vitamin Cofactor Summary

| Vitamin | Active Form | Primary Function | Key Enzymes |
|---------|-------------|------------------|-------------|
| **B1 (Thiamine)** | TPP | Decarboxylation | PDH, α-KGDH, transketolase |
| **B2 (Riboflavin)** | FMN, FAD | Redox reactions | Acyl-CoA dehydrogenases, Complex I/II |
| **B3 (Niacin)** | NAD+, NADP+ | Redox reactions | Dehydrogenases, reductases |
| **B5 (Pantothenic)** | CoA | Acyl group carrier | All acyl-CoA reactions |
| **B6 (Pyridoxine)** | PLP | Amino acid  | Transaminases, decarboxylases |
| **B7 (Biotin)** | Biotin-lysine | Carboxylation | Pyruvate carboxylase, ACC |
| **B9 (Folate)** | THF | One-carbon transfer | Serine hydroxymethyltransferase |
| **B12 (Cobalamin)** | Adenosyl-B12, Me-B12 | Rearrangement, methylation | Methylmalonyl-CoA mutase, methionine synthase |

---

## Metabolic Pathways Requiring B-Vitamins

### Energy Production
```
Glycolysis → Pyruvate → Krebs Cycle → ETC
    ↓            ↓            ↓         ↓
   Niacin       TPP         TPP       Riboflavin
                CoA         CoA       Niacin
                            Riboflavin
```

### Amino Acid Metabolism
```
Transamination → Decarboxylation → One-carbon
      ↓                ↓               ↓
    PLP               PLP         Folate, B12
```

### Fatty Acid Metabolism
```
β-Oxidation → Ketogenesis → Lipogenesis
      ↓            ↓            ↓
   Riboflavin     CoA         Biotin
    Niacin
```

## Clinical Deficiency Patterns

### Isolated Deficiencies (Rare)
- Specific dietary restriction
- Genetic disorders
- Drug-induced

### Multiple Deficiencies (Common)
**Causes:**
- Malnutrition
- Alcoholism
- Malabsorption (celiac, Crohn's)
- Bariatric surgery
- Chronic illness

**Why Multiple:**
- B-vitamins often coexist in foods
- Shared absorption mechanisms
- Similar risk factors

## Functional Assessment

### Direct Measurements
| Vitamin | Best Test | Functional Marker |
|---------|-----------|-------------------|
| B1 | Erythrocyte TPP | Transketolase activation |
| B2 | Erythrocyte glutathione reductase | EGRAC |
| B3 | Urinary N-methylnicotinamide | — |
| B6 | Plasma PLP | Erythrocyte AST activation |
| B12 | Serum B12 + MMA | Methylmalonic acid |
| Folate | Serum folate + homocysteine | Homocysteine |

### Indirect Markers
- **Homocysteine:** B6, B12, folate
- **Methylmalonic acid:** B12
- **Organic acids:** B1, B2, B3

---

## Therapeutic Considerations

### Supplementation Strategy

**Individual vs. Complex:**
- **Isolated deficiency:** Single vitamin
- **Suspected multiple:** B-complex
- **Functional medicine:** Activated forms

**Activated Forms:**
- B1: Benfotiamine (lipid-soluble)
- B6: P5P (pyridoxal-5-phosphate)
- B9: Methylfolate (5-MTHF)
- B12: Methylcobalamin, adenosylcobalamin

### Clinical Applications

**Metabolic Disorders:**
- l dysfunction: High-dose B-vitamins
-  resistance: B1, B3, biotin
- Homocysteinemia: B6, B9, B12

**Neurological:**
- Neuropathy: B1, B6, B12
- Cognitive decline: B1, B3, B6, B9, B12

**Cardiovascular:**
- Hyperhomocysteinemia: B6, B9, B12
- Heart failure: Thiamine

## Drug-Nutrient Interactions

| Drug | B-Vitamin Affected | Mechanism |
|------|-------------------|-----------|
| **Metformin** | B12 | Impaired absorption |
| **PPIs** | B12 | Reduced acid-dependent absorption |
| **Isoniazid** | B6 | Forms inactive hydrazone |
| **Methotrexate** | Folate | Folate antagonist |
| **Oral contraceptives** | B6, folate, B12 | Altered metabolism |
| **Hydralazine** | B6 | Forms hydrazone |

## Food Sources

### B-Complex Rich Foods
- **Whole grains:** B1, B2, B3, B6
- **Legumes:** B1, B6, folate
- **Leafy greens:** Folate
- **Animal products:** B12, B2, B3, B6
- **Nuts/seeds:** B1, B6, biotin
- **Nutritional yeast:** Most B-vitamins
