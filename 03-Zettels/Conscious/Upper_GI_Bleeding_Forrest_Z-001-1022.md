# Upper GI Bleeding — Forrest Classification and Management

## Overview

Upper gastrointestinal bleeding (UGIB) is a common medical emergency with significant morbidity and mortality. Endoscopic assessment using the Forrest classification guides risk stratification and management decisions.

## Classification of Bleeding

### Hematemesis
- Vomiting blood (red or coffee-ground)
- Indicates active or recent bleeding above ligament of Treitz

### Melena
- Black, tarry stools
- Indicates bleeding proximal to cecum
- Usually >50-100 mL blood

### Hematochezia (Upper Source)
- Bright red blood per rectum from UGIB
- Indicates massive bleeding (hypotension, shock)
- Usually >1000 mL rapid loss

## Forrest Classification (Peptic Ulcer)

The Forrest classification stratifies risk of rebleeding and guides endoscopic therapy decisions.

### Active Bleeding (High Risk)

**Forrest Ia: Spurting Hemorrhage**
- Arterial bleeding
- Highest risk of rebleeding (>90%)
- **Must treat endoscopically immediately**
- Mortality 5-10%

**Forrest Ib: Oozing Hemorrhage**
- Venous or capillary bleeding
- High risk of rebleeding (~80%)
- **Endoscopic therapy indicated**

### Signs of Recent Hemorrhage (Intermediate Risk)

**Forrest IIa: Visible Vessel**
- Pigmented protuberance
- Risk of rebleeding: 40-50%
- **Endoscopic therapy recommended**

**Forrest IIb: Adherent Clot**
- Clot firmly attached
- Risk of rebleeding: 15-25%
- **Endoscopic therapy controversial**
  - Some advocate removal and treatment
  - Others observe with PPI
  - High-dose PPI often sufficient

**Forrest IIc: Flat Pigmented Spot**
- Hematin on mucosa
- Low risk of rebleeding (~10%)
- **No endoscopic therapy**
- High-dose PPI

### Lesions Without Active Bleeding (Low Risk)

**Forrest III: Clean Base**
- No stigmata of recent hemorrhage
- Very low risk of rebleeding (<5%)
- **No endoscopic therapy**
- Standard PPI dose

## Initial Assessment and Resuscitation

### Risk Stratification

**Glasgow-Blatchford Score (GBS):**
| Parameter | Points |
|-----------|--------|
| BUN >18.2 mg/dL | 2-6 |
| Hemoglobin (men) <12, (women) <10 | 1-6 |
| Systolic BP <90 | 3 |
| Pulse ≥100 | 1 |
| Melena | 2 |
| Syncope | 2 |
| Hepatic disease | 2 |
| Cardiac failure | 2 |

**Interpretation:**
- GBS 0-1: Low risk (consider outpatient)
- GBS ≥2: Higher risk (admit)

**Pre-Endoscopic Rockall Score:**
- Age, shock, comorbidities
- Predicts mortality

### Resuscitation

**Immediate:**
- IV access (two large-bore)
- Fluid resuscitation (crystalloids)
- Blood transfusion if:
  - Hemoglobin <7 g/dL (most patients)
  - Hemoglobin <8 g/dL if CAD
  - Hemodynamic instability
- **Target Hb: 7-9 g/dL** (not higher)

**Platelets:**
- Transfuse if <50,000

**INR:**
- Correct if >1.5 with vitamin K, FFP, PCC

**Airway:**
- Protect airway if altered mental status
- Intubate if massive hematemesis

### Medications

**Proton Pump Inhibitors (PPIs):**
- High-dose IV PPI (80 mg bolus + 8 mg/hr infusion)
- Or intermittent high-dose (40 mg IV BID)
- Pre-endoscopy: Beneficial but not mandatory
- Post-endoscopy: Essential for high-risk stigmata

**Octreotide:**
- If varices suspected
- 50 mcg bolus + 50 mcg/hr infusion

**Antibiotics:**
- If cirrhosis (ceftriaxone 1g IV daily)

## Endoscopy

### Timing

**Urgent (<24 hours):**
- Hemodynamically unstable
- High-risk features
- Most patients with UGIB

**Emergent (<12 hours):**
- Active bleeding, hemodynamic instability
- Suspected varices

**Early (<24 hours):**
- Standard for most

### Endoscopic Therapy

**For High-Risk Lesions (Ia, Ib, IIa):**

**1. Injection:**
- Epinephrine (1:10,000)
- Volume: 6-10 mL in 4 quadrants
- Provides tamponade
- Not monotherapy (add thermal or mechanical)

**2. Thermal Coagulation:**
- Heater probe
- Bipolar/multipolar electrocautery
- Direct pressure and heat
- Effective for visible vessels

**3. Mechanical Therapy (Preferred):**
- **Endoscopic clips**
  - Through-the-scope (TTS) clips
  - Over-the-scope clips (OTSC) for large defects
  - Mechanical closure
- **Band ligation**
  - For varices
  - Similar to esophageal variceal ligation

**Combination Therapy:**
- Epinephrine injection + thermal or mechanical
- Superior to monotherapy
- Standard for visible vessels

### Post-Endoscopic Management

**High-Risk Stigmata (Ia, Ib, IIa):**
- High-dose PPI IV (80 mg bolus + 8 mg/hr x 72h)
- Monitor in ICU
- Second-look endoscopy controversial

**Low-Risk (IIc, III):**
- Standard PPI (40 mg daily)
- Ward bed
- Early feeding

## Specific Causes

### Peptic Ulcer Disease

**Most common cause (40-50%)**

**Risk Factors:**
- H. pylori infection
- NSAIDs/aspirin
- Anticoagulants
- Severe illness (stress ulcers)

**Endoscopic Therapy:**
- As above
- Test for H. pylori (biopsy or breath test later)
- Eradicate if positive

**Surgery:**
- Rarely needed
- Indications:
  - Endoscopic failure
  - Perforation
  - Penetration

### Esophageal Varices

**:**
- Portal hypertension
- Cirrhosis most common cause

**Endoscopic Therapy:**
- **Band ligation** (first-line)
  - Multiple bands applied
  - Eradicate varices over several sessions
- Sclerotherapy (alternative)

**Pharmacotherapy:**
- Octreotide
- Terlipressin (if available)
- Antibiotics

**Balloon Tamponade:**
- Minnesota tube
- Bridge to definitive therapy
- High complication rate

### Mallory-Weiss Tear

**Mechanism:**
- Mucosal laceration at GE junction
- Forceful vomiting/retching
- Usually self-limited

**Endoscopy:**
- Linear tear
- Active bleeding in minority
- Usually no intervention needed
- Rarely: clips or injection

### Esophagitis/Gastritis

**Erosive Disease:**
- Reflux esophagitis
- NSAID gastropathy
- CMV/HSV (immunocompromised)

**Management:**
- Usually medical (PPI)
- Endoscopic therapy rarely needed

### Malignancy

**Gastric , Esophageal Cancer:**
- Can present with bleeding
- Biopsy for diagnosis
- Palliative therapy if advanced

## Prevention of Rebleeding

### PPI Therapy

**High-dose:**
- 72 hours for high-risk stigmata
- Then transition to oral
- Duration: 8 weeks (ulcer healing)

**Maintenance:**
- If NSAID use continues
- If recurrent bleeding
- Consider H. pylori eradication

### NSAID Management

**If NSAID-associated:**
- Discontinue if possible
- If must continue: PPI + COX-2 inhibitor
- H. pylori eradication

### H. pylori

**Test and Treat:**
- All peptic ulcer patients
- Eradication reduces rebleeding

## Prognosis

**Mortality:**
- Overall: 2-10%
- Variceal: Higher (15-20%)
- Increases with:
  - Age
  - Comorbidities
  - Hypotension
  - Active bleeding
  - In-hospital bleeding

## Summary

**Key Points:**
1. Forrest classification predicts rebleeding risk
2. Forrest Ia/Ib/IIa: Endoscopic therapy required
3. Resuscitate before endoscopy (but don't delay unnecessarily)
4. High-dose PPI post-endoscopy for high-risk lesions
5. Combination endoscopic therapy superior (epinephrine + mechanical)
6. Test for H. pylori in all ulcer patients
7. Varices need specific therapy (banding, octreotide)

** Pearl:** The Forrest classification is essential for UGIB management. High-risk stigmata (active bleeding, visible vessel) require endoscopic therapy + high-dose PPI. Don't forget H. pylori testing and eradication to prevent recurrence.
