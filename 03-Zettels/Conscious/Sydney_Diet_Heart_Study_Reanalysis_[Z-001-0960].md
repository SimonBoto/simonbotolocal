# Sydney Diet-Heart Study Reanalysis —  and Mortality

## Study Overview

**Original Study (1966-1973):**
- Randomized controlled trial in Australian men
- 458 men, ages 30-59, with recent coronary events
- Intervention: Replace saturated fat with omega-6 linoleic acid (safflower oil)
- Control: Usual diet

**Original Findings (published 1978):**
- Cholesterol reduction in intervention group
- Cardiovascular outcomes not fully reported
- Study largely forgotten

**Ramsden Reanalysis (2013):**
- Original data recovered from National Heart Institute archives
- Full mortality data analyzed for first time
- Published: *BMJ* 2013

## Study Design

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| Design | Single-blind RCT |
| Duration | 1966-1973 (7 years) |
| Participants | 458 men, post-MI or angina |
| Intervention | Safflower oil + safflower oil margarine |
| Control | No specific dietary advice |
| Primary endpoint | Cardiovascular mortality (original) |

**Intervention Details:**
- Safflower oil provided (high linoleic acid: ~75%)
- Target: 15% of calories from omega-6
- Also given safflower oil-based margarine
- Saturated fat reduced

## Reanalysis Findings

### The Omega-6 Paradox

| Outcome | Intervention | Control | Risk Ratio |
|---------|-------------|---------|------------|
| **All-cause mortality** | 17.6% | 11.8% | **1.49 (95% CI 0.97-2.28)** |
| **Cardiovascular mortality** | 16.3% | 10.1% | **1.62 (95% CI 1.00-2.64)** |
| **CHD mortality** | 14.0% | 8.4% | **1.74 (95% CI 1.04-2.92)** |

**Key Finding:** Higher mortality in omega-6 intervention group despite cholesterol reduction.

### Cholesterol Response

- Intervention group: Significant **TC and LDL reduction**
- ~13% reduction in total cholesterol
- **Paradox:** Lower cholesterol, higher death rate

### Subgroup Analysis

**Complete Replacement vs Partial:**
- Men who fully replaced saturated with omega-6: highest risk
- Dose-response relationship observed

## Mechanistic Explanations

### 1. Increased Oxidative Stress

- High omega-6 → substrate for lipid peroxidation
- Oxidized LDL formation
- Atherosclerosis progression despite lower LDL

### 2. Pro-inflammatory Eicosanoids

- Linoleic acid → arachidonic acid → PGE2, LTB4
- Chronic  in vessel walls
- Plaque instability

### 3. Reduced Omega-3 Incorporation

- Competitive inhibition of delta-6 desaturase
- Less EPA/DHA synthesis
- Loss of cardioprotective effects

### 4. Membrane Effects

- High omega-6 in cell membranes
- Altered receptor function
- Impaired vascular reactivity

## Significance and Impact

### Challenge to Diet-Heart Hypothesis

**Traditional View:**
- Saturated fat raises LDL
- LDL causes heart disease
- Replace saturated with PUFA → benefit

**Sydney Findings:**
- PUFA replacement reduced LDL
- But increased deaths
- Suggests **non-LDL s matter**

### Meta-Analysis Integration

**Ramsden et al. Updated Meta-Analysis (2013):**
- Included recovered Sydney data
- Also recovered Minnesota Coronary Experiment data
- Combined analysis of omega-6 intervention trials

**Pooled Results:**
- No cardiovascular benefit from omega-6 reduction
- Possible harm in high omega-6 interventions without omega-3 increase

## Criticisms and Limitations

### Study Limitations

1. **Small sample size:** 458 men, limited power
2. **Single-blind:** Not fully blinded
3. **Short duration:** 7 years (longer follow-up needed)
4. **Specific population:** Post-MI men only
5. **High omega-6 dose:** ~15% calories (higher than typical)

### Counterarguments

**Defenders of PUFA guidelines argue:**
- Results may not apply to lower omega-6 intakes
- Modern PUFA sources include more omega-3
- Olive oil (MUFA) studies show benefit
- Not applicable to primary prevention

**Ramsden Response:**
- Dose-response strengthens causal inference
- Consistent with mechanistic data
- Other high omega-6 trials show similar trends

## Clinical Implications

### For Patients

**Questioning Simple Advice:**
- "Replace butter with vegetable oil" may be oversimplified
- Quality of replacement fat matters
- Omega-6 without omega-3 may be harmful

### For Guidelines

**Need for Nuance:**
- Distinguish omega-6 from omega-3
- Consider total fatty acid profile
- Don't assume all PUFA are equivalent

## Related to Minnesota Coronary Experiment

**Consistency:**
- Both show omega-6 increase without benefit
- Both had unpublished unfavorable data
- Both recovered by Ramsden team

**Combined Message:**
- High omega-6 interventions failed to show benefit
- Industry/academic bias may have suppressed negative results

## Current Status

**Guideline Response:**
- Limited impact on official guidelines
- AHA maintains omega-6 recommendations
- Some shift toward emphasizing omega-3

**Research Impact:**
- Increased scrutiny of omega-6 research
- More attention to omega-6:omega-3 ratio
- Recognition of need for better trials
