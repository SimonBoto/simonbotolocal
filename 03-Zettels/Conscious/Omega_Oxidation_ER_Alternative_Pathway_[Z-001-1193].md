# Omega-Oxidation — ER Alternative Pathway

## Overview

ω-oxidation is an alternative fatty acid  pathway in the endoplasmic reticulum. It becomes active when β-oxidation is impaired, producing dicarboxylic acids that can be excreted or further metabolized.

**"Omega" (ω) = Terminal methyl carbon (opposite end from carboxyl group)**

---

## The Three Steps

### Step 1: ω-Hydroxylation
```
R-CH₃ + O₂ + NADPH → R-CH₂-OH + NADP⁺ + H₂O
```
- Enzyme: CYP4A/F family (cytochrome P450)
- Adds hydroxyl group to terminal carbon
- Requires: NADPH, O₂
- Rate-limiting step

### Step 2: Alcohol Oxidation
```
R-CH₂-OH + NAD⁺ → R-CHO + NADH + H⁺
```
- Enzyme: Alcohol dehydrogenase
- Oxidizes alcohol to aldehyde

### Step 3: Aldehyde Oxidation
```
R-CHO + NAD⁺ + H₂O → R-COOH + NADH + H⁺
```
- Enzyme: Aldehyde dehydrogenase
- Oxidizes aldehyde to carboxylic acid

**Result:** Monocarboxylic acid converted to dicarboxylic acid

---

## Physiological Role

### Normal Conditions
- Minor pathway (< 10% of fatty acid metabolism)
- "Overflow" for excess fatty acids
- Detoxification of non-metabolizable lipids

### When Activated
1. **β-Oxidation Impairment**
   - Carnitine deficiency
   - l dysfunction
   - Genetic defects (MCAD, LCAD)

2. **Fatty Acid Overload**
   - High-fat diet
   - Uncontrolled diabetes
   - Fasting (carnitine-limited)

3. **Peroxisomal Disorders**
   - Very long-chain fatty acids
   - Branched-chain fatty acids

---

## Comparison: β-Oxidation vs ω-Oxidation

| Feature | β-Oxidation | ω-Oxidation |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| **Location** | Mitochondria | ER (microsomes) |
| **Products** | Acetyl-CoA | Dicarboxylic acids |
| **Energy yield** | High (ATP) | Low/moderate |
| **Cofactors** | NAD⁺, FAD, CoA | NADPH, NAD⁺, O₂ |
| **Regulation** | Tight (energy state) | Loose (substrate-driven) |
| **Clinical marker** | Normal function | Adipate/suberate/sebacate |

---

## Dicarboxylic Aciduria

**Definition:** Elevated dicarboxylic acids in urine

**Pattern:**
- C6 (adipate), C8 (suberate), C10 (sebacate) elevated
- Odd-chain usually normal

**Causes:**
- MCAD deficiency (genetic)
- Severe  resistance
- Riboflavin deficiency (FAD cofactor)
- Fasting in infants

---

## Clinical Significance

### Diagnostic Marker
- **Adipate elevation:** Early indicator of β-oxidation stress
- **Pattern analysis:** Distinguishes genetic vs acquired

### Therapeutic Target
- **CPT-I inhibitors:** Research for metabolic disease
- **Fenofibrate:** Induces CYP4A, increases ω-oxidation
