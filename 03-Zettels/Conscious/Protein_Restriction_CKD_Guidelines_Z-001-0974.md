# Protein Restriction CKD Guidelines — NKF KDIGO Recommendations

## Guideline Overview

**KDIGO (Kidney Disease: Improving Global Outcomes):**
- International consensus body
- Evidence-based clinical practice guidelines
- Updated periodically

**NKF KDOQI (National Kidney Foundation):**
- US-focused guidelines
- Often aligns with KDIGO
- More detailed implementation guidance

## Current Recommendations

### Non-Dialysis CKD

**Protein Intake:**

| CKD Stage | GFR | Protein Target | Evidence Strength |
|-----------|-----|----------------|-------------------|
| **1-2** | >60 | 0.8 g/kg/day (normal) | Weak |
| **3a** | 45-59 | 0.8 g/kg/day | Moderate |
| **3b** | 30-44 | 0.6-0.8 g/kg/day | Moderate |
| **4-5** | <30 | **0.6-0.8 g/kg/day** | **Strong** |

**Key Points:**
- Lower end of range for advanced CKD
- Higher end if mal risk
- Individualize based on clinical status

### Rationale

**Benefits:**
- Reduces glomerular hyperfiltration
- Decreases proteinuria
- Slows GFR decline (modestly)
- Reduces uremic symptoms (advanced CKD)

**Risks:**
- Malnutrition
- Sarcopenia
- Poor wound healing
- Increased infection risk

### Diabetic CKD

**ADA + KDIGO Alignment:**
- Similar protein targets
- Emphasis on quality (high biological value)
- Must balance with glycemic control

## Implementation

### Assessment Prior to Restriction

**Required Evaluation:**
1. Current protein intake (dietary history)
2. Nutritional status (albumin, weight, SGA)
3. CKD stage and progression rate
4. Comorbidities (, CVD)
5. Patient preferences and compliance capacity

### Monitoring on Restriction

**Every 3-6 Months:**
- Serum albumin
- Prealbumin (if albumin low)
- Weight and BMI
- Subjective Global Assessment (SGA)
- Muscle mass (clinical exam)
- GFR trend

**Red Flags:**
- Albumin <3.5 g/dL
- >5% unintentional weight loss
- Muscle wasting
- Functional decline
- Rapid GFR decline

### Adjusting Based on Response

**If Malnutrition Develops:**
- Increase protein to 0.8 g/kg
- Address anorexia (antiemetics, appetite stimulants)
- Consider nutritional supplements
- Rule out /infection

**If CKD Progresses Despite Restriction:**
- Ensure compliance (dietary recall)
- Check proteinuria control
- Optimize BP and RAAS inhibition
- Consider ketoacid supplementation (specialist)

## Special Populations

### Elderly Patients

**Considerations:**
- Higher malnutrition risk
- Sarcopenia concern
- May accept 0.8 g/kg even in advanced CKD
- Quality of life priority

### Obese Patients

**Calculation:**
- Use adjusted body weight (not actual)
- Or ideal body weight
- Prevents over-restriction

### Transplant Candidates

**Pre-Transplant:**
- Protein restriction acceptable
- Malnutrition must be avoided
- Optimize nutritional status for surgery

**Post-Transplant:**
- Liberalize protein (1.0-1.2 g/kg)
- Corticosteroids increase catabolism
- Prevent steroid-induced myopathy

## Ketoanalogue Supplementation

**Concept:**
- Very low protein (0.3-0.4 g/kg) + essential amino acid/ketoanalogue supplements
- Provides nitrogen-free precursors
- Maintains nutrition while minimizing renal load

**Evidence:**
- Some positive trials (MDRD substudy, others)
- May delay dialysis initiation
- Specialist management required

**Availability:**
- Limited in many countries
- Expensive
- Compliance challenging

## Controversies

### "Too Low" Debate

**Historical:** VLPD (0.28 g/kg) — MDRD
**Current:** Moderate restriction (0.6-0.8 g/kg)
**Trend:** Avoiding very low unless ketoacids available

### Plant vs Animal Protein

**Emerging Evidence:**
- Plant protein may be preferable
- Lower acid load
- Better metabolic profile
- Guidelines evolving

### Individualization vs Standardization

**Tension:**
- Guidelines provide targets
- But patients vary widely
- Must balance progression risk vs malnutrition risk
