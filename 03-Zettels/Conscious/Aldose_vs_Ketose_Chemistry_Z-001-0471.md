## Core Claim
**Aldoses have an aldehyde group at C1 (terminal carbonyl), while ketoses have a ketone group at C2 (internal carbonyl) — this structural difference determines ring size (pyranose vs furanose), reactivity (aldoses are stronger reducing agents), and metabolic fate** — glucose (aldose) and fructose (ketose) exemplify this fundamental biochemical distinction.

## Evidence

### Structural Comparison
| Feature | Aldose | Ketose |
|---------|--------|--------|
| **Carbonyl** | Aldehyde (-CHO) at C1 | Ketone (C=O) at C2 |
| **Ring size** | Pyranose (6-membered) | Furanose (5-membered) |
| **Reactivity** | More easily oxidized | More stable |
| **Example** | Glucose | Fructose |

### Classification by Carbon Number
| Carbons | Aldose Name | Example | Ketose Name | Example |
|---------|-------------|---------|-------------|---------|
| 3 | Aldotriose | Glyceraldehyde | Ketotriose | Dihydroxyacetone |
| 4 | Aldotetrose | Erythrose | Ketotetrose | Erythrulose |
| 5 | Aldopentose | Ribose | Ketopentose | Ribulose |
| 6 | Aldohexose | Glucose | Ketohexose | Fructose |

### Reactivity
- **Aldoses:** Strong reducing sugars (positive Benedict's test)
- **Ketoses:** Can isomerize to aldoses under basic conditions

## Clinical Implication
- **Glucose monitoring:** Aldose chemistry enables reducing-sugar assays
- **Fructose metabolism:** Ketose structure allows bypass of PFK-1 regulation
- **Glycation:** Aldoses (glucose) more reactive in protein glycation (AGE formation)
- **METHAP relevance:** Understanding aldose/ketose chemistry explains why glucose and fructose have different metabolic effects despite being isomers
