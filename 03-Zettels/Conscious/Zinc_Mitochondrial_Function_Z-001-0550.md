# Zinc Mitochondrial Function

## Core Claim
Zinc serves essential structural and catalytic roles in mitochondria, particularly as a stabilizer of manganese superoxide dismutase (MnSOD/SOD2) and as a cofactor for multiple enzymes, while maintaining mitochondrial membrane integrity through cardiolipin stabilization and preventing permeability transition pore opening.

## Evidence

### Mitochondrial Superoxide Dismutase (MnSOD/SOD2)

**Structure and Function:**
- Homotetramer (4 identical subunits) in mitochondrial matrix
- Manganese (Mn) at catalytic active site
- Zinc stabilizes protein structure (not catalytic)
- Converts superoxide to hydrogen peroxide

**Catalytic Reaction:**
```
2 O₂⁻ + 2 H⁺ → H₂O₂ + O₂
```

**Critical Role:**
- First line of defense against mitochondrial ROS
- Prevents oxidative damage to mtDNA
- Protects electron transport chain complexes
- Essential for cellular redox homeostasis

### Broader Mitochondrial Roles

**Enzyme Cofactor:**
| Enzyme | Function | Zinc Role |
|--------|----------|-----------|
| Carbonic anhydrase V | pH regulation | Catalytic |
| Matrix metalloproteinases | Mitochondrial dynamics | Structural |
| Alcohol dehydrogenase | Metabolism | Catalytic |

**Membrane Stabilization:**
- Maintains mitochondrial membrane potential
- Prevents permeability transition pore (PTP) opening
- Protects against apoptosis initiation
- Stabilizes cardiolipin (inner membrane phospholipid)

**Protein Structure:**
- Zinc finger domains in mitochondrial proteins
- TFAM (mitochondrial transcription factor A)
- POLG (mtDNA polymerase gamma)
- Structural integrity for mtDNA maintenance

### Zinc Deficiency Consequences

**Molecular Effects:**
- Reduced SOD2 activity and stability
- Increased superoxide levels
- Accumulation of mtDNA damage
- Impaired oxidative phosphorylation

**Systemic Manifestations:**
- Fatigue and exercise intolerance
- Impaired immune function
- Delayed wound healing
- Cognitive impairment
- Growth retardation (children)

### Zinc Excess (Toxicity)

**Mechanisms:**
- Displaces other metal ions (copper, iron)
- Induces oxidative stress at high concentrations
- Neurotoxicity at extreme levels

**Copper Deficiency Risk:**
- Competitive absorption with copper
- High-dose zinc (>40 mg) causes copper deficiency
- Anemia, neutropenia, neurological symptoms

## Clinical Implication

**Assessment:**
- Serum zinc (fasting): 70-120 μg/dL
- RBC zinc (longer-term status)
- Clinical signs: hair loss, skin lesions, impaired taste

**Supplementation:**
- Form: Zinc picolinate or glycinate (best absorption)
- Dose: 15-30 mg elemental zinc daily
- Timing: Away from iron supplements (competitive absorption)
- Upper limit: 40 mg/day (avoid copper deficiency)

**Dietary Sources:**
- **Animal (high bioavailability):** Oysters, beef, lamb, chicken
- **Plant (moderate bioavailability):** Pumpkin seeds, hemp seeds, cashews
- **Absorption enhancers:** Animal protein
- **Absorption inhibitors:** Phytates (grains, legumes)

## METHAP Relevance

**Protocol Integration:**
- Include zinc in micronutrient assessment
- Supplement if deficient or high-risk (vegan/vegetarian)
- Balance with copper (monitor if supplementing >30 mg)
- Support mitochondrial function alongside CoQ10, B-vitamins
