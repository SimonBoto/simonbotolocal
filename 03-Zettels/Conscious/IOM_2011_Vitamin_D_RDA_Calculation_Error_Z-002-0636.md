## Core Claim
The Institute of Medicine's 2011 RDA of 600 IU/day for vitamin D was based on a **fundamental statistical error**: using mean serum 25(OH)D concentrations instead of the 97.5th percentile to estimate population requirements.

## The Error

### What They Did Wrong
| Correct Approach | IOM 2011 Approach |
|------------------|-------------------|
| Use 97.5th percentile of dose-response distribution | Used mean response |
| Target: 97.5% of population reaches 20 ng/mL | Target: Average person reaches 20 ng/mL |
| Result: ~8,900 IU/day | Result: 600 IU/day |

### Why It Matters
- **RDA purpose:** Ensure 97.5% of population meets needs
- **Mean vs. percentile:** The mean leaves ~50% of population deficient
- **Magnitude:** Error resulted in 15-fold underestimate

## Evidence

> "The IOM estimated the RDA to be 600 IU/day for individuals aged 1-70 years, based on a target serum 25(OH)D concentration of 20 ng/mL. However, they used the mean response rather than the upper tail of the distribution."
> — Veugelers & Ekwaru, 2014

## Implications

### Public Health Impact
- Billions of people following guidelines remain deficient
- Deficiency linked to: immune dysfunction, metabolic disease, cancer mortality, autoimmune disease
- "Safe upper limit" of 4,000 IU/day is actually within physiological range

### Scientific Integrity
- Error persisted despite peer review
- Corrections published but guidelines not revised
- Demonstrates institutional inertia in public health recommendations
