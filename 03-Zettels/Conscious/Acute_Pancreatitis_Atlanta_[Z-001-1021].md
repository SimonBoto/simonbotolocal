# Acute Pancreatitis — Revised Atlanta Classification and Management

## Overview

Acute pancreatitis is acute  of the pancreas with variable involvement of other regional tissues or remote organ systems. The Revised Atlanta Classification (2012) provides standardized definitions for diagnosis, severity, and complications.

## Diagnosis (Requires 2 of 3)

**1. Abdominal pain:**
- Acute onset
- Epigastric region
- Often radiates to back
- May improve with leaning forward

**2. Serum lipase or amylase:**
- At least 3x upper limit of normal
- Lipase preferred (more specific)
- Amylase less specific (salivary, other conditions)

**3. Characteristic imaging:**
- CT, MRI, or ultrasound
- Pancreatic inflammation
- Not required if 1+2 present

## Etiology (I GET SMASHED)

| Letter | Cause | Details |
|--------|-------|---------|
| **I** | Idiopathic | 20-30% after workup |
| **G** | Gallstones | Most common (40%) |
| **E** | Ethanol | Second most common (20-30%) |
| **T** | Trauma | Abdominal, ERCP |
| **S** | Steroids | Medication-induced |
| **M** | Mumps/Malignancy | Viral, pancreatic  |
| **A** | Autoimmune | Type 1 or 2 |
| **S** | Scorpion sting | Rare (Trinidad) |
| **H** | Hyperlipidemia/Hypercalcemia | TG >1000 mg/dL |
| **E** | ERCP/Drugs | Post-ERCP (5%), medications |
| **D** | Drugs | Azathioprine, 6-MP, others |

**Common Medications:**
- Azathioprine, 6-mercaptopurine
- Didanosine
- Valproic acid
- ACE inhibitors
- Statins
- GLP-1 agonists (possible)
- SGLT2 inhibitors (possible)

## Classification (Revised Atlanta 2012)

### Morphologic Types

**1. Interstitial Edematous Pancreatitis (IEP):**
- Most common (80-90%)
- Diffuse or localized enlargement
- Homogeneous enhancement on CT
- Usually resolves spontaneously

**2. Necrotizing Pancreatitis (10-20%):**
- Pancreatic parenchymal necrosis
- And/or peripancreatic necrosis
- Non-enhancing areas on CT (>48 hours)
- Higher morbidity/mortality

### Severity Classification

**1. Mild Acute Pancreatitis:**
- No organ failure
- No local or systemic complications
- Usually interstitial edematous
- Mortality <1%
- Resolves in 1 week

**2. Moderately Severe Acute Pancreatitis:**
- Transient organ failure (<48 hours)
- AND/OR
- Local complications
- AND/OR
- Exacerbation of comorbid disease
- Most resolve without residual issues

**3. Severe Acute Pancreatitis:**
- Persistent organ failure (>48 hours)
- Single or multiple organ systems
- Mortality 20-40%
- Requires ICU

### Organ Failure Definitions

**Modified Marshall Score ≥2:**

| System | Score 0 | Score 1 | Score 2 | Score 3 | Score 4 |
|--------|---------|---------|---------|---------|---------|
| **Respiratory (PaO2/FiO2)** | >400 | 301-400 | 201-300 | 101-200 | ≤101 |
| **Renal (Cr, mg/dL)** | <1.4 | 1.4-1.8 | 1.9-3.6 | 3.6-4.9 | >4.9 |
| **Cardiovascular (SBP, mmHg)** | >90 | <90, fluid responsive | <90, not responsive | <90, pH <7.3 | <90, pH <7.2 |

## Local Complications

### Acute Peripancreatic Fluid Collections (APFC)

**Timing:** <4 weeks

**Characteristics:**
- Homogeneous
- No definable wall
- Adjacent to pancreas
- Spontaneously resolve or become pseudocyst

**No intervention unless:**
- Infected
- Symptomatic
- Obstructing

### Pancreatic Pseudocyst

**Timing:** >4 weeks

**Characteristics:**
- Well-defined wall
- Homogeneous fluid
- No solid material
- Outside pancreas (peripancreatic)

**Management:**
- Usually observe
- Drain if:
  - Infected
  - Symptomatic (pain, obstruction)
  - Enlarging
  - Rupture risk

### Acute Necrotic Collection (ANC)

**Timing:** <4 weeks (necrotizing pancreatitis)

**Characteristics:**
- Heterogeneous
- Non-liquid (solid debris)
- No definable wall
- Within or adjacent to pancreas

### Walled-Off Necrosis (WON)

**Timing:** >4 weeks

**Characteristics:**
- Mature, well-defined wall
- Heterogeneous contents (fluid + debris)
- Solid necrotic material

**Management:**
- Step-up approach
- Drainage if infected or symptomatic
- Delayed intervention preferred (>4 weeks)

### Infection

**Infected Necrosis:**
- Gas in collection (CT)
- Positive Gram stain/culture
- Clinical sepsis

**Management:**
- Antibiotics (carbapenems, quinolones + metronidazole)
- Delayed drainage (>4 weeks if possible)
- Step-up approach (percutaneous → endoscopic → surgical)

## Severity Stratification Tools

### APACHE II

- Score ≥8: Severe disease
- Multiple parameters
- Complex calculation

### BISAP Score

| Parameter | Points |
|-----------|--------|
| **B**UN >25 mg/dL | 1 |
| **I**mpaired mental status | 1 |
| **S**IRS | 1 |
| **A**ge >60 | 1 |
| **P**leural effusion | 1 |

**Interpretation:**
- 0-1: Low risk (<2% mortality)
- 2-3: Moderate risk
- 4-5: High risk (>15% mortality)

**Advantages:** Simple, early (first 24h), no imaging

### Ranson's Criteria

**At admission:**
- Age >55
- WBC >16,000
- LDH >350
- AST >250
- Glucose >200

**At 48 hours:**
- Hematocrit fall >10%
- BUN rise >5
- Calcium <8
- PaO2 <60
- Base deficit >4
- Fluid sequestration >6L

**Score ≥3:** Severe
**Score ≥6:** Very severe

**Limitations:** Late assessment (48h), complex

## Initial Management

### Fluid Resuscitation

**Critical in first 24 hours:**
- Lactated Ringer's preferred (balanced crystalloid)
- 250-500 mL/hr or 5-10 mL/kg/hr
- Goal: Decrease BUN, maintain urine output

**Aggressive vs Moderate:**
- Moderate: 5-10 mL/kg/hr appears safer
- Over-resuscitation: Harmful (pulmonary edema, abdominal compartment syndrome)
- Target: Hemoconcentration resolution, not hemodilution

**Monitoring:**
- Urine output (>0.5 mL/kg/hr)
- BUN (should decrease)
- Heart rate, blood pressure
- Fluid balance

### Nutrition

**Early Enteral Nutrition:**
- Start within 24-48 hours
- Oral or nasogastric
- Reduces infection, mortality vs TPN
- Mild: Oral as tolerated
- Severe: NG feeding preferred

**Avoid TPN:** Unless enteral contraindicated

### Pain Control

- IV opioids (morphine, hydromorphone)
- Patient-controlled analgesia if needed
- Epidural in severe cases

### ERCP

**Indications:**
- Cholangitis
- Biliary obstruction with jaundice
- Persistent bile duct stone

**Timing:**
- Urgent (<24h) if cholangitis
- Early (<72h) if obstruction

**Not routine** for all biliary pancreatitis

### Antibiotics

**Routine prophylaxis:** NOT RECOMMENDED

**Therapeutic antibiotics:**
- Infected necrosis
- Cholangitis
- Extrapancreatic infection (pneumonia, UTI, line sepsis)

**Agents:**
- Carbapenems
- Fluoroquinolones + metronidazole
- Third-gen cephalosporins + metronidazole

## Specific Interventions

### Infected Necrosis

**Step-Up Approach:**

**Step 1:** Percutaneous drainage
- CT or ultrasound guided
- 12-14 Fr drain
- May achieve resolution alone

**Step 2:** Minimally invasive necrosectomy
- Video-assisted retroperitoneal debridement (VARD)
- Sinus tract endoscopy
- Delay if possible (>4 weeks)

**Step 3:** Surgical necrosectomy
- Laparoscopic or open
- Last resort

### Pseudocyst/WON Drainage

**Indications:**
- Infected
- Symptomatic (pain, obstruction)
- Enlarging
- Complications

**Methods:**
- **Endoscopic:** EUS-guided cystgastrostomy or transduodenal
- **Percutaneous:** CT-guided
- **Surgical:** Laparoscopic cystgastrostomy

**Timing:**
- Pseudocyst: >4 weeks (mature wall)
- WON: Delay if possible, intervene if infected

## Prognosis

**Mild:**
- Mortality <1%
- Full recovery expected

**Moderately Severe:**
- Mortality 1-5%
- May require prolonged hospitalization

**Severe:**
- Mortality 20-40%
- ICU care required
- Multi-organ failure

**Predictors of Severity:**
- BISAP ≥2
- Obesity (BMI >30)
- SIRS present
- Pleural effusions
- Hemoconcentration (Hct >44)
- BUN rising

## Follow-Up

**After Discharge:**
- Etiology workup (if idiopathic)
- Gallstone: Cholecystectomy (same admission if mild)
- Alcohol: Counseling, cessation
- Hypertriglyceridemia: Fibrate, diet,  if diabetic

**Imaging:**
- CT at 5-7 days if severe or not improving
- Assess for necrosis, collections

## Summary

**Key Points:**
1. Diagnosis: 2 of 3 (pain, enzymes 3x normal, imaging)
2. Severity: Mild, moderately severe, severe (based on organ failure)
3. BISAP simple and useful for early stratification
4. Fluid resuscitation critical (balanced crystalloid, not excessive)
5. Early enteral nutrition (within 24-48h)
6. Antibiotics only for infection, not prophylaxis
7. Necrosis: Delayed intervention unless infected
8. Step-up approach for infected necrosis

**Clinical Pearl:** Most acute pancreatitis is mild and self-limited. Identify severe early (BISAP, organ failure), resuscitate appropriately but don't overdo fluids, and remember: "Don't feed the pancreas by mouth in severe cases" is outdated—early enteral feeding is beneficial.
