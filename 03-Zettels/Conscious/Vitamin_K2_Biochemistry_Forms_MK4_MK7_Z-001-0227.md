#  Biochemistry — MK-4 vs. MK-7 Forms

> Vitamin K2 exists as a family of menaquinones with varying chain lengths, with MK-4 and MK-7 being the most clinically relevant forms, differing in source, bioavailability, dosing, and therapeutic applications.

## Core Principle

## Vitamin K Family Overview

### Vitamin K1 (Phylloquinone)
- **Source:** Green leafy vegetables
- **Function:** Coagulation (liver)
- **Bioavailability:** Low, short half-life
- **Not sufficient** for extra-hepatic functions (bone, vascular)

### Vitamin K2 (Menaquinones)
**Naming:** MK-n (n = number of isoprenoid units in side chain)
- **MK-4:** Short chain
- **MK-7 through MK-10:** Long chain

## MK-4 (Menaquinone-4)

### Characteristics
| Feature | MK-4 |
|---------|------|
| **Side chain** | 4 isoprenoid units |
| **Molecular weight** | 444.6 Da |
| **Half-life** | ~1 hour |
| **Bioavailability** | Lower |

### Sources
- **Animal products:** Meat, eggs, dairy (variable content)
- **Tissue conversion:** From K1 (phylloquinone)
- **Synthetic:** Produced for supplements

### Dosing and Uses
- **Pharmacological dosing:** 45 mg/day (Japan)
- **Indications:** Osteoporosis (Japan approved drug)
- **:** Well-established efficacy
- **Cost:** Expensive at therapeutic doses

### Limitations
- Short half-life requires multiple daily doses
- Lower bioavailability
- Less cardiovascular evidence
- High cost for therapeutic dosing

## MK-7 (Menaquinone-7)

### Characteristics
| Feature | MK-7 |
|---------|------|
| **Side chain** | 7 isoprenoid units |
| **Molecular weight** | 649.0 Da |
| **Half-life** | 72 hours |
| **Bioavailability** | Higher |

### Sources
- **Fermented foods:** Natto (Japanese fermented soy) — highest concentration
- **Bacterial production:** Gut bacteria produce variable amounts
- **Cheeses:** Some fermented cheeses (Brie, Gouda)

### Dosing and Uses
- **Typical dose:** 100-200 mcg/day
- **Once-daily dosing:** Due to long half-life
- **Bone health:** Increases bone mineral density
- **Cardiovascular:** Strong evidence for vascular calcification prevention
- **Cost:** Moderate

### Advantages
- Long half-life allows daily or even weekly dosing
- Better bioavailability
- Stronger cardiovascular evidence
- Cost-effective

##  of Action (Both Forms)

### γ-Carboxylation
**Enzymatic Reaction:**
- Glutamic acid (Glu) residues → γ-carboxyglutamic acid (Gla)
- Requires vitamin K as cofactor
- Warfarin inhibits this reaction

**Proteins Activated:**
- **Osteocalcin:** Bone protein (regulates mineralization)
- **Matrix Gla Protein (MGP):** Vascular calcification inhibitor
- **Other Gla proteins:** Various tissue-specific functions

## METHAP Recommendation

**Preferred Form: MK-7**
**Rationale:**
- Better cardiovascular evidence (critical for MetS)
- Long half-life (compliance advantage)
- Cost-effective
- Once-daily dosing

**Dosing:**
- **100-200 mcg MK-7 daily**
- Can be taken with or without food
- No established upper limit (very safe)

**Alternative:**
- If MK-7 unavailable, MK-4 at 45 mg/day
- Requires divided dosing (3x daily)
- More expensive

## Interactions

**Warfarin/Anticoagulants:**
- Vitamin K antagonizes warfarin
- MK-7 longer half-life = more stable interference
- **Contraindicated** unless closely monitored by physician
- May need warfarin dose adjustment
