## Core Claim
**Oxaloacetate (OAA) is a 4-carbon α-keto acid with competing metabolic fates: it condenses with acetyl-CoA to initiate the Krebs cycle (catabolism), or is converted to phosphoenolpyruvate (PEP) for gluconeogenesis (anabolism)** — this competition determines whether acetyl-CoA is oxidized for energy or diverted to glucose synthesis, making OAA availability the "anti-ketogenic switch."

## Evidence

### Role 1: Krebs Cycle Entry
```
Oxaloacetate + Acetyl-CoA → Citrate (citrate synthase)
```
| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Function** | Entry point for acetyl-CoA |
| **Regulation** | Inhibited by ATP, citrate, NADH |
| **Turnover** | OAA regenerated each cycle |

### Role 2: Gluconeogenic Precursor
```
Oxaloacetate + GTP → PEP + GDP + CO₂ (PEPCK)
```
| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Function** | Bypasses irreversible pyruvate kinase |
| **Location** | Cytosolic PEPCK (liver, kidney) |
| **Energy cost** | 1 GTP per OAA |

### The Competition Problem
| Condition | OAA Fate | Result |
|-----------|----------|--------|
| **High energy charge** | Krebs cycle | Acetyl-CoA oxidation |
| **Low glucose, high glucagon** | Gluconeogenesis | Glucose synthesis |
| **Insufficient OAA** | — | Acetyl-CoA → ketogenesis |

### OAA Availability Determines
| OAA Status | Acetyl-CoA Fate |
|------------|-----------------|
| **Adequate OAA** | Enters Krebs cycle |
| **Low OAA** | Shunted to ketogenesis |

## Clinical Implication
- **Gluconeogenesis cost:** 6 ATP per glucose (expensive)
- **Metformin target:** Inhibits hepatic gluconeogenesis
- **Ketogenesis trigger:** Low OAA relative to acetyl-CoA
- **METHAP relevance:** OAA availability is a metabolic control point; fasting shifts OAA toward gluconeogenesis, while ketogenic diets reduce OAA demand
