## Core Principle

> The choice of magnesium salt significantly impacts absorption, tolerability, and clinical efficacy, with organic salts (citrate, glycinate) demonstrating superior bioavailability compared to inorganic forms (oxide).

## Comparative Bioavailability

### High Bioavailability Forms (Recommended)

**1. Magnesium Citrate**
- **Elemental Mg:** ~16%
- **Absorption:** 30-40% (high)
- **Pros:** Well-absorbed, affordable, mild laxative effect beneficial for constipation, kidney stone prevention (binds oxalate)
- **Cons:** May cause loose stools at higher doses
- **Best for:** General supplementation, kidney stone prevention

**2. Magnesium Glycinate (Bisglycinate)**
- **Elemental Mg:** ~14%
- **Absorption:** 35-40% (high)
- **Pros:** Gentle on GI (least laxative), chelated form, glycine has calming effect (sleep, anxiety), good compliance
- **Cons:** More expensive
- **Best for:** GI-sensitive patients, sleep support, anxiety, long-term compliance

**3. Magnesium Orotate**
- **Elemental Mg:** ~6%
- **Absorption:** High (heart tissue penetration)
- **Pros:** Cardiac tissue penetration, metabolic benefits, used in cardiac conditions (European preference)
- **Cons:** Expensive, lower elemental content (requires more pills)
- **Best for:** Cardiovascular focus, heart failure, athletic performance

**4. Magnesium Taurate**
- **Elemental Mg:** ~9%
- **Absorption:** High
- **Pros:** Taurine synergy (cardiovascular, calming), good for heart health
- **Cons:** Moderate elemental content
- **Best for:** Cardiovascular health, blood pressure, palpitations

**5. Magnesium Malate**
- **Elemental Mg:** ~6%
- **Absorption:** High
- **Pros:** Malate supports energy (Krebs cycle), fibromyalgia benefit
- **Cons:** Lower elemental content
- **Best for:** Fatigue, fibromyalgia, muscle pain

### Moderate/Low Bioavailability Forms

**6. Magnesium Chloride**
- **Elemental Mg:** ~12%
- **Absorption:** Moderate
- **Pros:** Well-studied, inexpensive
- **Cons:** Bitter taste, GI effects
- **Best for:** Topical use, IV administration

**7. Magnesium Sulfate (Epsom Salt)**
- **Elemental Mg:** ~10%
- **Absorption:** Poor (oral)
- **Pros:** Inexpensive, transdermal use (baths)
- **Cons:** Poor oral absorption, strong laxative, contraindicated in renal failure
- **Best for:** Transdermal baths (soaking), IV (medical setting)

### Avoid (Poor Absorption)

**8. Magnesium Oxide**
- **Elemental Mg:** 60% (highest)
- **Absorption:** Only ~4% (lowest)
- **Pros:** High elemental content, very inexpensive, small pills
- **Cons:** Poorly absorbed, strong laxative effect
- **Verdict:** Not recommended for therapeutic use despite popularity

## Core Principle

> The choice of magnesium salt significantly impacts absorption, tolerability, and clinical efficacy, with organic salts (citrate, glycinate) demonstrating superior bioavailability compared to inorganic forms (oxide).

## METHAP Recommendations

**Primary Choice:**
- **Magnesium citrate** (cost-effective, well-absorbed)
- **Magnesium glycinate** (if GI sensitivity or sleep issues)

**Cardiovascular Focus:**
- **Magnesium taurate** or **orotate** (if budget allows)

**Avoid:**
- **Magnesium oxide** (poor absorption)

## Practical Considerations

**Cost-Effectiveness:**
- Citrate: Most cost-effective high-absorption form
- Glycinate: Worth premium for GI-sensitive patients
- Orotate/Taurate: Expensive but may be justified for cardiac focus

**Pill Burden:**
- Oxide: Fewest pills (misleading due to poor absorption)
- Orotate/Malate: Most pills (low elemental content)
- Citrate/Glycinate: Moderate pills (optimal balance)

**Combination Approach:**
- Morning: Citrate (energizing)
- Evening: Glycinate (calming, sleep support)
