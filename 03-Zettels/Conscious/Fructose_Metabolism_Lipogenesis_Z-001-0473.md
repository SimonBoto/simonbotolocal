## Core Claim
**~90% of dietary fructose is metabolized by the liver, bypassing the PFK-1 regulatory step and insulin control — rapid phosphorylation to fructose-1-phosphate drives high metabolic flux, generating excess acetyl-CoA for fatty acid synthesis and elevating triglycerides, VLDL, and uric acid** — this unregulated entry into metabolism distinguishes fructose from glucose and links it to metabolic syndrome.

## Evidence

### Metabolic Pathway
```
Fructose → Fructose-1-phosphate (fructokinase, unregulated)
        → DHAP + Glyceraldehyde (aldolase B)
        → Glycolysis/Gluconeogenesis/Lipogenesis
```

### Bypass of Regulation
| Feature | Glucose | Fructose |
|---------|---------|----------|
| **Rate-limiting step** | PFK-1 (regulated) | None (bypassed) |
| **Insulin regulation** | Yes (GLUT4) | No |
| **Hepatic uptake** | Controlled | Rapid, unregulated |

### Lipogenic Consequences
| Parameter | Effect |
|-----------|--------|
| **Triglycerides** | ↑↑ |
| **VLDL** | ↑ |
| **Uric acid** | ↑ (AMP degradation) |
| **Insulin resistance** | ↑ |
| **Satiety signals** | ↓ (no GLP-1/GIP) |

### Dietary Sources
- **Sucrose:** 50% fructose
- **HFCS:** 42-55% fructose
- **Fruit:** Variable (with fiber slowing absorption)

## Clinical Implication
- **NAFLD:** Fructose-driven hepatic steatosis
- **Metabolic syndrome:** Fructose consumption correlates with all components
- **Gout:** Uric acid elevation from AMP degradation
- **METHAP relevance:** Limiting added sugars (especially fructose) is central to metabolic syndrome reversal; whole fruit with fiber is distinct from processed fructose
