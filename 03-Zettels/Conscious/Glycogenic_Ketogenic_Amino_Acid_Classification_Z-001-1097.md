## Core Claim
**Amino acids are classified by metabolic fate: 18 are glycogenic (convert to glucose), 2 are purely ketogenic (leucine, lysine → ketones), and 4 are both** — this classification determines whether dietary protein can support glucose production or contributes to ketogenesis.

## Evidence

### Purely Ketogenic (2)
| Amino Acid | End Product | Cannot Produce |
|------------|-------------|----------------|
| **Leucine** | Acetoacetyl-CoA, acetyl-CoA | Glucose |
| **Lysine** | Acetoacetyl-CoA | Glucose |

### Both Glycogenic and Ketogenic (4)
| Amino Acid | Glycogenic | Ketogenic |
|------------|------------|-----------|
| **Isoleucine** | Succinyl-CoA | Acetyl-CoA |
| **Phenylalanine** | Fumarate | Acetoacetyl-CoA |
| **Tryptophan** | Pyruvate | Acetoacetyl-CoA |
| **Tyrosine** | Fumarate | Acetoacetyl-CoA |

### Purely Glycogenic (14)
All others: Alanine, Arginine, Asparagine, Aspartate, Cysteine, Glutamate, Glutamine, Glycine, Histidine, Methionine, Proline, Serine, Threonine, Valine

## Clinical Implication
- **Leucine is the most ketogenic amino acid** — popular in ketogenic diets
- **Lysine cannot become glucose** — essential for protein synthesis only
- **Mixed amino acids** provide metabolic flexibility
- **METHAP relevance:** High-leucine protein sources support ketogenesis; mixed proteins support both glucose and ketone production
