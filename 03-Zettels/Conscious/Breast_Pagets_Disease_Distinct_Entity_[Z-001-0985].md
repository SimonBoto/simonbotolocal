# Breast Paget's Disease — Nipple  Distinct from Bone Paget's

## Critical Distinction

**Paget's disease of the breast** (mammary Paget's disease) is a **completely separate disease** from Paget's disease of bone. They share a name only — both were described by Sir James Paget in the 19th century, but they have different pathophysiology, presentation, and treatment.

## Pathophysiology

### Malignant Nature

**Definition:**
- Adenocarcinoma of the nipple-areolar complex
- Extension of underlying breast cancer to skin surface
- Paget cells = malignant glandular cells in epidermis

**Origin:**
- Arises from underlying ductal carcinoma in situ (DCIS) or invasive ductal carcinoma
- Malignant cells migrate through milk ducts to nipple epidermis
- NOT a skin cancer despite skin involvement

### Histology

**Paget Cells:**
- Large malignant cells with abundant pale cytoplasm
- Prominent nuclei
- "Halo" appearance
- Found within nipple epidermis
- Express cytokeratin 7 (CK7), HER2/neu, mammoglobin

**Underlying Cancer:**
- Present in 85-95% of cases
- Usually DCIS
- May be invasive (50%)
- Often multicentric

##  Presentation

### Early Signs

**Nipple Changes:**
- Erythema (redness)
- Scaling, flaking
- Crusting
- Mild irritation

**Often Misdiagnosed As:**
- Eczema/dermatitis
- Infection
- Contact dermatitis
- Psoriasis

**Key Distinguishing Feature:**
- Unilateral (eczema usually bilateral)
- Persists despite topical treatment
- Progresses
- Associated breast mass may be present

### Advanced Signs

**Progressive Changes:**
- Ulceration
- Erosion of nipple
- Inversion or flattening
- Bloody or serous discharge
- Pain or burning
- Thickened plaque

**Associated Findings:**
- Palpable breast mass (50%)
- Nipple retraction
- Axillary lymphadenopathy (if invasive)

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Suspicion

**Red Flags for Paget's vs Benign Eczema:**

| Feature | Breast Paget's | Benign Eczema |
|---------|---------------|---------------|
| **Laterality** | Unilateral | Often bilateral |
| **Response to steroids** | Poor | Good |
| **Progression** | Progressive | Variable |
| **Nipple destruction** | Present | Absent |
| **Discharge** | Bloody/serous | None or clear |
| **Mass** | May be present | Absent |

### Diagnostic Workup

**1. Physical Examination:**
- Careful inspection of both nipples
- Palpation for breast mass
- Axillary lymph node exam

**2. Biopsy (Definitive):**
- Punch biopsy of nipple skin
- Full-thickness biopsy if needed
- Histology confirms Paget cells

**3. Imaging:**

**Mammography:**
- Evaluate underlying breast tissue
- Look for associated mass, calcifications, architectural distortion
- May be negative in 10-15%

**Breast Ultrasound:**
- Characterize any masses
- Guide biopsy if needed

**Breast MRI:**
- High sensitivity for underlying cancer
- Evaluate extent
- Recommended if mammogram negative but high suspicion

**4. Immunohisto:**
- CK7 positive (distinguishes from melanoma, squamous carcinoma)
- HER2 status (therapeutic implications)
- Hormone receptors (ER/PR)

## Staging and Prognosis

### Prognostic Factors

**Better Prognosis:**
- No palpable mass
- Underlying DCIS only
- Negative nodes
- Early stage

**Worse Prognosis:**
- Invasive cancer present
- Palpable mass
- Positive lymph nodes
- HER2-positive

### Survival

**Overall:**
- Similar to other breast cancers of same stage
- 5-year survival: 80-90% (varies by stage)
- Worse if invasive component present

## Treatment

### Surgical Options

**Mastectomy:**
- Historical standard
- Recommended if:
  - Large area involved
  - Multicentric disease
  - Invasive cancer with positive nodes
  - Patient preference

**Breast-Conserving Surgery:**
- Lumpectomy (wide local excision) + radiation
- Appropriate if:
  - Limited disease
  - Underlying DCIS or small invasive cancer
  - Negative margins achievable
  - Patient desires preservation

**Nipple-Areolar Complex Resection:**
- Central lumpectomy
- Removes nipple-areola + underlying ducts
- Oncoplastic reconstruction

### Adjuvant Therapy

**Radiation Therapy:**
- Standard after breast-conserving surgery
- Reduces local recurrence
- Whole breast + boost to nipple region

**Systemic Therapy:**

**If Invasive Cancer:**
- Chemotherapy (based on stage, receptor status)
- Endocrine therapy (if ER/PR positive)
- HER2-targeted therapy (if HER2 positive)

**If DCIS Only:**
- Endocrine therapy may be considered for risk reduction
- No chemotherapy

### Follow-Up

**Monitoring:**
- Regular breast exams
- Annual mammography
- Watch for local recurrence
- Screen contralateral breast

## Why the Name Confusion Matters

### Clinical Consequences

**Misdiagnosis Risk:**
- Delayed diagnosis if treated as eczema
- Inappropriate topical steroids
- Progression of underlying cancer

**Patient Confusion:**
- "I have Paget's disease"
- Assumes bone disease
- Different prognosis and treatment

### Educational Point

**Always Specify:**
- "Paget's disease of the breast" (cancer)
- "Paget's disease of bone" (metabolic bone disorder)
- Never use just "Paget's disease" without qualifier

## Summary

**Key Points:**
1. Breast Paget's ≠ Bone Paget's — completely different diseases
2. Breast Paget's is nipple adenocarcinoma
3. Usually associated with underlying DCIS/invasive cancer
4. Presents as persistent unilateral nipple eczema
5. Diagnosis requires biopsy
6. Workup includes imaging for underlying cancer
7. Treatment: Surgery ± radiation ± systemic therapy
8. Prognosis depends on underlying cancer stage

**Clinical Pearl:** Persistent unilateral nipple eczema not responding to topical treatment → Biopsy to exclude Paget's disease of the breast.
