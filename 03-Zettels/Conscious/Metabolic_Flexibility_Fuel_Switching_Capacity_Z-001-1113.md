# Metabolic Flexibility — Fuel Switching Capacity

## Core Claim
**Metabolic flexibility is the ability to efficiently switch between glucose (fed state), fatty acids (fasting), and ketones (prolonged fasting) based on availability and demand** — this capacity, regulated by insulin, is impaired in metabolic syndrome, leading to persistent glucose dependence even when fasting.

## Evidence

### Fuel Sources
| Fuel | Storage | Primary Use | Hormonal Control |
|------|---------|-------------|------------------|
| **Glucose** | Glycogen (limited) | Brain, RBCs, muscle | Insulin-dependent |
| **Fatty acids** | Adipose (abundant) | Muscle, heart (aerobic) | Insulin-suppressed |
| **Ketones** | None (synthesized) | Brain alternative | Insulin-suppressed |

### Healthy Flexibility
| State | Glucose Oxidation | Fatty Acid Oxidation | Ketogenesis |
|-------|-------------------|----------------------|-------------|
| **Fed** | High | Suppressed | Off |
| **Fasting (12h)** | Moderate | High | Low |
| **Fasting (24h+)** | Low | High | High |

### Metabolic Inflexibility (Metabolic Syndrome)
| Feature | Consequence |
|---------|-------------|
| **Impaired fat oxidation** | Cannot switch to fat during fasting |
| **Persistent glucose dependence** | Requires frequent carbohydrate intake |
| **Elevated insulin** | Suppresses fat oxidation chronically |
| **Intramuscular lipid accumulation** | Fat cannot be oxidized, stores in muscle |

## Clinical Implication
- **Metabolic inflexibility is hallmark of metabolic syndrome** — glucose-dependent even when fasting
- **Low-carb diets and fasting** restore flexibility by forcing fat adaptation
- **Exercise training** improves mitochondrial fat oxidation capacity
- **METHAP relevance:** High-dose mitochondrial nutrients (B12, CoQ10, Mg) support flexible fuel oxidation
