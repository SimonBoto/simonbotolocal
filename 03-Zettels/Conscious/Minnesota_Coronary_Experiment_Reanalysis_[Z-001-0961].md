# Minnesota Coronary Experiment Reanalysis — Unpublished Data Recovery

## Study Overview

**Original Study (1968-1973):**
- One of largest randomized controlled dietary trials ever conducted
- 9,423 participants from mental hospitals and nursing homes
- Designed to test diet-heart hypothesis

**Original Publication (1989 — 16 years late):**
- Only serum cholesterol results reported
- No mortality data published
- Conclusion: Cholesterol-lowering but [[KEYWORD-Clinical|clinical]] outcomes "not analyzed"

**Ramsden Recovery (2016):**
- Original data tapes recovered from University of Minnesota basement
- Full dataset analyzed for first time
- Published: *BMJ* 2016

## Study Design

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| Design | Double-blind RCT |
| Duration | 4.5 years (1968-1973) |
| Participants | 9,423 men and women |
| Setting | Mental hospitals, nursing homes (institutionalized) |
| Age range | 20-97 years |
| Intervention | Corn oil + corn oil margarine |
| Control | Control margarine (saturated fat) |

**Intervention Details:**
- **Corn oil** and corn oil margarine provided
- Target: ~16% calories from [[KEYWORD-Omega_6|omega-6]] linoleic acid
- Significant reduction in saturated fat
- Double-blind design (food color matched)

## Recovery Story

### The Missing Data

**What Was Published (1989):**
- Serum cholesterol reductions
- Compliance data
- No clinical endpoints

**What Was Hidden:**
- Complete mortality data
- Cardiovascular events
- Subgroup analyses

**Discovery:**
- Ramsden et al. located original data tapes
- Stored in University of Minnesota basement
- Data format obsolete — required recovery expertise
- Successfully retrieved and analyzed

## Reanalysis Findings

### Mortality Results

| Outcome | Intervention | Control | Risk Ratio |
|---------|-------------|---------|------------|
| **All-cause mortality** | No significant difference | | |
| **Cardiovascular mortality** | No significant difference | | |
| **Overall** | **No benefit despite 14% cholesterol reduction** | | |

### The Age Interaction

**Critical Finding — Age Subgroup Analysis:**

| Age Group | Effect of Intervention |
|-----------|------------------------|
| **<65 years** | Trend toward benefit |
| **≥65 years** | **Increased mortality** (not statistically significant but concerning trend) |

**Implication:** Older adults may be harmed by high omega-6 diets

### Serum Cholesterol vs Outcomes

**Paradox:**
- Cholesterol reduced by ~14% in intervention
- But no mortality benefit
- Suggests **cholesterol is not the only driver** of CVD

### Detailed Analysis

**Per-Protocol Analysis:**
- Higher compliance → greater cholesterol reduction
- But **no mortality benefit** even in compliant participants

**Intention-to-Treat:**
- Similar results across all analyses
- Robust to various statistical approaches

## Why Was Data Suppressed?

### Possible Explanations

**1. Disappointing Results:**
- Trial designed when diet-heart hypothesis ascendant
- Null/harmful results contradicted expectations
- Easier to publish partial data

**2. Scientific Bias:**
- Researchers "knew" saturated fat was bad
- Results didn't fit paradigm
- Publication bias toward positive findings

**3. Institutional Pressure:**
- Funded by National Heart, Lung, and Blood Institute
- Unfavorable results awkward for public health messaging

**4. Genuine Complexity:**
- Data analysis genuinely difficult
- Subgroup findings messy
- Hard to draw clear conclusions

### Historical Context

**Diet-Heart Hypothesis Dominance:**
- 1970s-1980s: Ancel Keys' influence peak
- Dissenting voices marginalized
- Industry aligned with PUFA promotion

**Publication Bias:**
- Multiple large trials had unpublished unfavorable data
- Sydney, Minnesota, others
- Systematic suppression pattern

## Methodological Strengths

### Why This Trial Matters

**1. Large Sample:**
- 9,423 participants (vs typical hundreds)
- Adequately powered for mortality endpoints

**2. Double-Blind:**
- Neither participants nor investigators knew assignment
- Minimized bias

**3. Institutional Setting:**
- Controlled food environment
- High compliance
- Accurate dietary data

**4. Long Duration:**
- 4.5 years of intervention
- Sufficient time for CVD outcomes

## Limitations

### Valid Concerns

**1. Institutional Population:**
- Mental hospitals, nursing homes
- Not generalizable to free-living population?
- But high internal validity

**2. Trans Fat in Control:**
- Control margarine contained some trans fat
- May have harmed control group
- Would underestimate intervention harm

**3. Single Omega-6 Source:**
- Corn oil only
- Not all omega-6 sources

**4. No Omega-3:**
- Intervention pure omega-6
- No EPA/DHA component

## Scientific Implications

### Cholesterol Hypothesis Challenged

**Traditional Model:**
Saturated fat ↑ LDL ↑ CVD death

**MCE Results:**
Saturated fat ↓ (replaced by PUFA) → LDL ↓ → **No change in death**

**Conclusion:**
- Cholesterol reduction ≠ mortality reduction
- Missing pieces in causal chain
- Other factors matter

### Comparison with Drug Trials

**Statins:**
- Lower LDL dramatically
- Reduce mortality (modestly)
- Multiple [[KEYWORD-Mechanism|mechanism]]s beyond LDL

**Omega-6 Diet:**
- Lower LDL modestly
- No mortality benefit
- Possibly harmful in elderly

**Inference:**
- LDL may be marker, not sole cause
- Statins work through non-LDL pathways
- Dietary LDL modification insufficient

## Contemporary Relevance

### For Current Guidelines

**AHA Position:**
- Maintains omega-6 recommendations
- Dismisses MCE as old, flawed
- Cites observational data

**Critical Perspective:**
- Largest trial of its kind
- Double-blind, well-controlled
- Cannot be ignored

### Research Agenda

**Questions Raised:**
1. Is omega-6 harmful in specific populations?
2. Does omega-6:omega-3 ratio matter most?
3. Are all PUFA sources equivalent?
4. Why doesn't LDL reduction from diet match statins?

## Integration with Sydney Study

**Consistency:**
- Both show no benefit from omega-6 increase
- Both had unpublished data
- Both suggest possible harm in some groups

**Combined Weight:**
- Two major trials, >10,000 participants
- Both null/negative for omega-6
- Cannot be dismissed as isolated findings
