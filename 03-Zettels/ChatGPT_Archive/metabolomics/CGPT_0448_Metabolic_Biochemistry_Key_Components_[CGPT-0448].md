**Vitamin B2 (Riboflavin):**

- **Molecular Structure**: Riboflavin is composed of a flavin ring (isoalloxazine) attached to a ribitol (a sugar alcohol). The structure is critical for its role as a precursor for the coenzymes FMN (flavin mononucleotide) and FAD (flavin adenine dinucleotide).

- **Minerals and Metals**: While riboflavin itself doesn't contain metals, its coenzyme forms, FMN and FAD, interact closely with various metal ions in enzymes. For instance, in the electron transport chain, FAD can associate with iron-sulfur clusters to facilitate electron transfer.

- **First Discovery**: Riboflavin was first isolated in 1933 by Richard Kuhn. However, its presence was known before as a factor in preventing pellagra and ariboflavinosis. It was initially found in various foods, including milk, meat, and green vegetables.

**Vitamin B3 (Niacin):**

- **Molecular Structure**: Niacin exists in two forms: nicotinic acid and nicotinamide. Both forms have a pyridine ring, but nicotinamide has an amide group on the ring. This structure is vital for its role as a precursor to the coenzymes NAD+ (nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide) and NADP+ (nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate).

- **Minerals and Metals**: Similar to riboflavin, niacin-based coenzymes interact with metal ions, particularly in redox reactions. For example, NAD+ can interact with metalloproteins containing iron, copper, or zinc during electron transfer processes.

- **First Discovery**: Niacin was discovered in the 1930s during studies on pellagra, a disease caused by niacin deficiency. It was first found in liver, yeast, and green vegetables.

Both riboflavin and niacin are quintessential to energy metabolism, particularly in their coenzyme forms that participate in vital redox reactions.
