Assessing your risk of developing diabetes is crucial for early prevention and intervention. Several tools and questionnaires are available to help determine your risk. Here are some notable ones:

1. **ADA Type 2 Diabetes Risk Test** [[1](https://diabetes.org/diabetes/risk-test)]:
   - The American Diabetes Association offers a free online 60-second Type 2 Diabetes Risk Test. It provides a quick assessment of your risk factors for Type 2 diabetes.

2. **Diabetes Self-Management Questionnaire (DSMQ)** [[2](https://www.msm.edu/blog/2019/six-questions-to-ask-your-doctor-about-diabetes.php)]:
   - DSMQ is a comprehensive questionnaire that assesses various aspects of diabetes risk and self-management. It can provide valuable insights into your risk profile.

3. **Diabetes UK Risk Assessment** [[3](https://riskscore.diabetes.org.uk)]:
   - Diabetes UK provides a risk assessment questionnaire consisting of seven questions. It helps you determine your risk of developing Type 2 diabetes and offers guidance on what to do next based on your score.

4. **CDC Prediabetes Risk Test** [[4](https://www.cdc.gov/diabetes/takethetest/)]:
   - The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) offers a 1-minute test to assess your risk for prediabetes. Identifying prediabetes is crucial for early intervention.

5. **Prediabetes Risk Test (PDF)** [[5](https://www.cdc.gov/prediabetes/pdf/Prediabetes-Risk-Test-Final.pdf)]:
   - This is a printable risk test provided by the American Diabetes Association and the CDC. You can fill it out to determine your risk of prediabetes.

6. **Type 2 Diabetes Risk Assessment Form** [[6](https://www.diabetes.fi/files/502/eRiskitestilomake.pdf)]:
   - If you score 15 points or more in this risk test, it's recommended to have your blood glucose measured. This form can be useful for assessing your risk in more detail.

Remember that these questionnaires are valuable tools, but a definitive diagnosis of diabetes should be made by a healthcare professional. If you have concerns about your risk or health, it's essential to consult with your doctor or a diabetes specialist for personalized guidance and further evaluation.
