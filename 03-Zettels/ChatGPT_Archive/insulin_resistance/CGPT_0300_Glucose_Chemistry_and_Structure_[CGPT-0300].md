### 1. **Chemical Properties**
Glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆) is a monosaccharide, or simple sugar, that is a key source of energy in living organisms. It is an aldohexose, meaning it contains six carbon atoms and an aldehyde group. Glucose is highly soluble in water due to its hydrophilic hydroxyl groups (–OH) and its ability to form hydrogen bonds.

### 2. **Polarity**
Glucose is a polar molecule. The presence of multiple hydroxyl groups and a carbonyl group (C=O) on the first carbon atom contributes to its polarity. These groups can engage in hydrogen bonding with water molecules, enhancing its solubility. The polar nature of glucose also facilitates its transport through the aqueous environment of the bloodstream and cellular uptake.

### 3. **3D Structure**
Glucose can exist in two forms: the linear form and the cyclic form. The linear form is not commonly found in nature and is less stable. It has an extended chain structure. In aqueous solutions, glucose predominantly exists in its cyclic form—either as alpha (α) or beta (β) anomers. These forms differ in the orientation of the hydroxyl group attached to the first carbon atom in the ring.

- **Alpha (α) Glucose**: The hydroxyl group on the first carbon is on the opposite side of the ring compared to the sixth carbon's CH₂OH group.
- **Beta (β) Glucose**: The hydroxyl group on the first carbon is on the same side of the ring as the sixth carbon's CH₂OH group.

The conversion between α and β forms (mutarotation) occurs in solution and is catalyzed by the presence of acids or bases. The cyclic structure can be represented in a Haworth projection, showing a six-membered ring called a pyranose ring.
