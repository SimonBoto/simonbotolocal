## 2. LDL as a causal factor: post-2016 evidence

### 2.1 2017 EAS Consensus Statement (Ference et al.)

**“Low-density lipoproteins cause atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease: Evidence from genetic, epidemiologic, and clinical studies.”** (Eur Heart J 2017). citeturn1search1turn1search4turn1search11  

Key points:

- Synthesizes **genetic (Mendelian randomisation), prospective cohort, and randomized trial data**.  
- Concludes that LDL **meets classical causality criteria** (strength, consistency, dose–response, temporality, biological plausibility, experiment, etc.).  
- Quantifies a roughly **log-linear relationship**: each 1 mmol/L (~39 mg/dL) *lifelong* LDL reduction corresponds to ~50–55% reduction in ASCVD risk, and shorter-term pharmacologic lowering yields proportional but smaller benefits. citeturn1search1turn1search7turn1search18  

This statement is widely cited as “closing” the debate on LDL being causal rather than a mere marker, at least within mainstream cardiology.

### 2.2 Subsequent consensus and mechanistic elaborations

- **Borén et al. 2020** updated the consensus with more detailed pathobiology: retention and modification of apoB-containing lipoproteins in the arterial wall, inflammatory activation, foam cell formation, etc., again concluding LDL is **causal**. citeturn1search7turn1search18  
- **Mortensen et al. 2023 (Circulation)** reinforce LDL as a “predominant causal risk factor” using large registry and trial data. citeturn1search25  
- Several methodological papers on Mendelian randomisation (MR) refine causal inference frameworks for lipids and ASCVD (e.g. Kim 2024, Zhong 2024) and extend causal mapping to **remnant cholesterol, triglyceride-rich lipoproteins, and lipoprotein(a)**. citeturn0search1turn0search21turn1search25  

These collectively strengthen the case that **apoB-containing particles (LDL, remnants) are causal drivers**, while HDL-C is not reliably protective.

## 3. Residual cholesterol vs residual inflammatory / metabolic risk

One of DuBroff’s underlying points is that **cholesterol explains little of who gets events**, especially in the “middle” ranges. Since 2017, this has been reframed as the problem of **residual risk**.

### 3.1 Inflammation as an independent axis (CANTOS and beyond)

**CANTOS (canakinumab, 2017)** – IL-1β inhibition in post-MI patients with elevated hsCRP:  

- Significant reduction in MACE without any change in LDL cholesterol. citeturn2search9turn2search5turn2search13turn2search21  
- Strongest benefit in those achieving on-treatment hsCRP < 2 mg/L.

This demonstrates that **pure anti-inflammatory therapy can reduce events independently of LDL**, creating a “two-axis” model:

- LDL/apoB axis (cholesterol exposure)  
- Inflammatory / innate immunity axis  

Recent work explicitly compares **residual inflammatory risk** vs **residual cholesterol risk** in statin-treated populations, showing that *in some subgroups* inflammation is a stronger predictor of future events than further LDL reductions, once LDL is already low. citeturn2search3turn2search7turn2search11turn2search19turn1search24  

### 3.2 LDL lowering independent of inflammation

The SHARP-based analysis by Storey et al., mentioned above, is important here:  

- In CKD patients, **event reduction with LDL lowering was similar regardless of baseline hsCRP**, arguing that LDL has an **independent causal contribution** even when inflammation is high. citeturn2search2turn2search6turn2search10turn1search8  

Thus, the current mainstream view is **not “LDL or inflammation” but “LDL *and* inflammation”**, plus additional contributors (triglyceride-rich lipoproteins, Lp(a), blood pressure, insulin resistance, etc.).
