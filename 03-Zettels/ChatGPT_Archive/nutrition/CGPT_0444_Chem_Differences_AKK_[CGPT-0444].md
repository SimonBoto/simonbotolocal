3. **Ketoses**: This term specifically refers to a subset of carbohydrates. Ketoses are sugars that have a ketone group. An example is fructose, which is a common sugar found in fruits. In metabolism, ketoses can be important energy sources and intermediates in various pathways.

Another category worth mentioning is **Aldoses**, which are sugars with an aldehyde group. Glucose, the most ubiquitous energy source in the body, is an example of an aldose.

### 4. **ChatGPT:**

Alcohols are organic compounds characterized by the presence of one or more hydroxyl (-OH) groups attached to a carbon atom. In organic chemistry, they are classified based on the carbon to which the hydroxyl group is attached:

1. **Primary Alcohol**: Here, the carbon with the hydroxyl group is attached to only one other carbon. A classic example is ethanol.

2. **Secondary Alcohol**: In this case, the carbon with the hydroxyl group is attached to two other carbon atoms. Isopropanol is a common example.

3. **Tertiary Alcohol**: The carbon with the hydroxyl group is attached to three other carbons.

The oxidation process of alcohols depends on the type of alcohol (primary, secondary, or tertiary):

- **Oxidation of Primary Alcohols**: This process usually occurs in two steps. First, the primary alcohol is oxidized to an aldehyde. Further oxidation can convert the aldehyde to a carboxylic acid. For example, ethanol (a primary alcohol) can be oxidized to acetaldehyde (an aldehyde), which can further be oxidized to acetic acid (a carboxylic acid).

- **Oxidation of Secondary Alcohols**: Secondary alcohols are oxidized to ketones, and the reaction typically stops there. For instance, isopropanol (a secondary alcohol) can be oxidized to acetone (a ketone).

- **Tertiary Alcohols**: These are generally resistant to oxidation. They do not undergo the same type of oxidation as primary or secondary alcohols because there is no hydrogen atom attached to the carbon with the hydroxyl group that can be removed to form a double bond with oxygen.

The oxidation process of alcohols is important in both organic chemistry and biochemistry. In a clinical setting, especially in the context of metabolomics, understanding these processes is key to interpreting how different substances are metabolized in the body and their impact on various conditions like insulin resistance.

The fate of lactic acid, particularly its conversion back to non-toxic metabolites, involves a series of physiological processes known as the Cori cycle. Here's how it works:

1. **Formation and Release of Lactic Acid**:
    - During intense exercise or in conditions where oxygen supply is limited, muscles switch to anaerobic glycolysis. Pyruvate, formed from glucose breakdown, is converted to lactic acid (specifically, its ionized form, lactate) to regenerate NAD+, allowing glycolysis to continue.
    - This lactate is then released into the bloodstream.

2. **Transport to the Liver**:
    - The lactate travels in the blood to the liver. This transportation doesn’t require ATP; it relies on the concentration gradient and blood circulation.

3. **The Cori Cycle in the Liver**:
    - In the liver, lactate is converted back to pyruvate by the enzyme lactate dehydrogenase.
    - Pyruvate can then enter gluconeogenesis, a process of glucose synthesis. It’s important to note that gluconeogenesis is an energy-consuming process.
    - The newly synthesized glucose can then be released into the bloodstream, transported back to muscle cells, and used as an energy source, completing the cycle.

4. **ATP Consumption**:
    - The conversion of lactate back to glucose in the liver consumes 6 ATP molecules per glucose molecule. This process is energetically expensive, but it's crucial for maintaining blood glucose levels during prolonged exercise or fasting.

The Cori cycle is a vital mechanism in the body for managing energy under anaerobic conditions and preventing lactic acid buildup, which can lead to acidosis. NADH, when oxidized in the process of cellular respiration, contributes to the production of ATP. The exact amount of ATP produced per molecule of NADH depends on the specific conditions within the cell and the efficiency of the electron transport chain and ATP synthase. However, under typical conditions in eukaryotic cells:

1. **Theoretical ATP Yield**: Each NADH molecule can theoretically produce approximately 2.5 ATP molecules. This is based on the standard yield given by the oxidative phosphorylation process occurring in the mitochondria.

2. **Process Overview**:
   - NADH donates electrons to the electron transport chain in the inner mitochondrial membrane.
   - This transfer initiates a series of redox reactions that pump protons across the mitochondrial membrane, creating a proton gradient.
   - The flow of protons back across the membrane (down their electrochemical gradient) through ATP synthase drives the synthesis of ATP.

3. **Variability in Actual Yield**: It's important to note that the actual ATP yield can vary due to factors like the permeability of the mitochondrial membrane, the use of the proton gradient for other purposes than ATP synthesis, and the shuttle systems that transport NADH from glycolysis into the mitochondria.
