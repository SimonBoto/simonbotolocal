## 1) Guggulu (*Commiphora wightii*, syn. *Balsamodendron mukul*) — 0.26 mg
**What it tends to do**
- Traditionally used for “lipids,” inflammation, and “ama” (metabolic sludge concept).
- The famous actives are **guggulsterones**, which interact with nuclear receptors involved in bile acid/cholesterol metabolism (FXR is often mentioned in mechanistic discussions), and can shift lipid handling.

**Hormone/endocrine angles**
- **Thyroid axis:** guggul preparations have been reported to **increase peripheral thyroid hormone activity** in some contexts (historically described as increased T3/T4 effects). Clinically: this is the main endocrine “flag.”
- **Drug metabolism:** can induce/affect hepatic enzymes → indirectly changes hormone levels if you’re on hormone therapy by altering clearance (less predictable; depends on extract).

**Safety notes**
- If someone has **hyperthyroidism**, thyroid nodules with autonomy, or is on **levothyroxine**, guggul is a “be careful” ingredient.
- Can irritate GI in some users.

**Practical comment on your label dose**
- **0.26 mg** is so low that *if correct*, endocrine impact is unlikely. But if it’s a misprint (e.g., 260 mg), then thyroid relevance goes way up.

---

## 2) Shilajit (purified) — 32 mg
**What it tends to do**
- A mineral-organic exudate rich in **fulvic acids / humic substances** (composition varies wildly by source).
- Marketed for energy, mitochondrial function, fertility, “rejuvenation.”

**Hormone/endocrine angles**
- **Androgen axis:** there is some human data suggesting shilajit supplementation can **raise total/free testosterone and improve sperm parameters** in some men. That’s the headline endocrine effect.
- **HPA axis / stress:** claims exist, but robust clinical endocrine data is thinner.

**Big risk that matters more than theory**
- **Contaminants**: historically, shilajit products can be contaminated with **heavy metals**, mycotoxins, or adulterants if not truly purified and tested. If you care about endocrine disruption, contaminants are often the real “hormone meddling” mechanism (e.g., lead/cadmium can mess with gonadal function and thyroid).

**Who should be cautious**
- Anyone with **androgen-sensitive conditions** (e.g., some prostate issues) should be cautious with “testosterone-raising” supplements.
- Pregnancy: I’d avoid due to quality variability.

---

## 3) Nimba seeds (label confusion) — 14 mg
**What it tends to do**
- True neem (*Azadirachta indica*) seed extracts contain limonoids (e.g., azadirachtin-related compounds) with antimicrobial/insecticidal properties; in humans it’s used traditionally for skin, glycemic support, “detox,” etc.

**Hormone/endocrine angles (this is the biggest red flag ingredient hormonally)**
- **Fertility / reproduction:** neem seed (and neem oil particularly) has a long history of **anti-fertility/contraceptive** use in traditional medicine and experimental literature (effects on sperm function, implantation, etc. depending on preparation and dose).
- **Pregnancy risk:** because of the above, neem seed/oil is commonly treated as **contraindicated in pregnancy** (and “trying to conceive” is a caution zone).

**Safety notes**
- Neem **oil** ingestion is the scary one (toxicity reports). Seed at low mg doses is less dramatic, but still: endocrine/reproductive axis is the concern.
- The **botanical mislabel** (Melia vs Azadirachta) makes this ingredient the most “I want a COA + identity testing” item.

---

## 4) Daruharidra extract (*Berberis aristata*) — 64 mg
**What it tends to do**
- Primary active: **berberine** (though content varies).
- Strongest evidence area: **glycemic control / insulin sensitivity**, gut microbiome modulation, and lipid handling.

**Hormone/endocrine angles**
- **Insulin axis:** improving insulin sensitivity often *secondarily* improves sex hormone patterns in insulin-resistant states (e.g., lowered insulin → can reduce ovarian androgen drive in PCOS physiology). So it can “move hormones,” but usually by **metabolic upstream correction**, not direct hormone mimicry.
- **Thyroid:** not a classic thyroid stimulant, but metabolic rate changes can change thyroid labs indirectly in some people.
- **CYP/P-gp interactions:** berberine can affect drug transport/metabolism → can alter levels of many meds (including some hormone meds).

**Safety notes / interactions**
- Potential additive effects with **metformin**, **GLP-1s**, other hypoglycemics → hypoglycemia risk in susceptible people.
- Can interact with **cyclosporine/tacrolimus**, anticoagulants, and various meds via CYP/P-gp.
- GI upset is common.

## 5) Amalaki (*Emblica officinalis / Phyllanthus emblica*) — 32 mg
**What it tends to do**
- Polyphenol-rich “Amla.” Antioxidant, mild lipid/glucose support, gut-friendly.

**Hormone/endocrine angles**
- Not a classic “hormone active” botanical.
- Indirect endocrine effects would be via **reduced oxidative stress / improved glycemic control**, not via estrogenic/androgenic activity.

**Safety**
- Usually well tolerated at typical doses; 32 mg is small.

---

## 6) Haritaki (*Terminalia chebula*) — 32 mg  
## 7) Vibhitaki (*Terminalia bellirica*) — 32 mg
These + amalaki = **Triphala** core.

**What it tends to do**
- Gut motility regulation (often mild laxative/tonic depending on person), microbiome effects, antioxidant polyphenols.

**Hormone/endocrine angles**
- Mostly indirect: gut function → bile acid metabolism → hormone excretion/recycling (enterohepatic circulation).  
- If someone is very sensitive, anything that changes stool frequency can change **estrogen recycling** and medication absorption, but this is usually subtle at reasonable dosing.

**Safety**
- Watch for diarrhea/dehydration if combined with other laxatives.
- If someone is on narrow-therapeutic-index meds, chronic gut-motility changers can affect absorption.

---

## 8) Aragvadha (*Cassia fistula*) — 32 mg
**What it tends to do**
- Classically a **laxative/purgative** botanical.

**Hormone/endocrine angles**
- Not directly hormone-like. The endocrine relevance is **absorption and electrolyte effects** if it pushes diarrhea:
  - Chronic laxative effect → can reduce absorption of oral meds (including oral contraceptives, thyroid meds).
  - Electrolyte issues (if abused) can affect adrenal/RAAS signaling indirectly.

**Safety**
- Caution in IBS-D, inflammatory bowel disease flare risk, dehydration risk if dose is meaningful.

---

## 9) Kanchanara (*Bauhinia variegata*) — 32 mg
**What it tends to do**
- In Ayurveda, often used for “glandular swellings,” lymph, and thyroid-related “granthi” patterns (traditional framing).

**Hormone/endocrine angles**
- This is one of those herbs that gets *marketed* for thyroid, but modern high-quality human endocrine data is limited.
- The practical caution is: if the formula is intended for “thyroid support,” it often shows up alongside guggul, which is the more plausible thyroid-active component.

**Safety**
- Given uncertain endocrine potency, the caution is mainly for people with thyroid disease and those on thyroid meds (because the *blend* may be thyroid-directed even if single ingredient data is soft).

---

## 10) Nagkesara (*Mesua ferrea*) — 6 mg
**What it tends to do**
- Traditionally used for bleeding disorders, inflammation, sometimes “astringent” effects. Contains aromatic compounds and polyphenols.

**Hormone/endocrine angles**
- Not a common endocrine disruptor in typical discussions.  
- At **6 mg**, it’s unlikely to be a major actor unless it’s a highly concentrated extract (not specified).

# So… do they “mess with hormones”?
**Potentially yes, but in specific, predictable ways:**

### Highest endocrine-relevant ingredients
- **Neem seed**: reproductive axis / fertility risk (especially “trying to conceive” and pregnancy).
- **Shilajit**: may raise **testosterone** in some men; also quality/contaminant risk is the bigger endocrine landmine.
- **Guggul** (if the dose is real/meaningful): can push **thyroid activity**.

### Metabolic → hormonal downstream effects
- **Berberis/berberine**: shifts **insulin** and lipid metabolism; can improve PCOS-type hormone patterns indirectly, but also interacts with many meds.

### Low direct hormonal action, mostly GI/microbiome/absorption
- **Triphala components + Cassia**: can change stooling → can alter medication absorption and estrogen recycling subtly.
