1. **Hemostasis Phase (Immediate Response)**:
   - **Stress and Damage**: This is the initial injury causing tissue disruption.

2. **Inflammatory Phase**:
   - **Vasodilation and Inflammation**: Blood vessels widen (vasodilation) to increase blood flow, bringing immune cells to the wound.
   - **Immune Response**: White blood cells (neutrophils and macrophages) clean the wound by removing bacteria and debris.
   - **Signs**: Redness, heat, swelling, and pain are typical signs of inflammation.

3. **Proliferative Phase**:
   - **The Humid Phase**: A moist environment is essential for cell migration and tissue formation.
   - **Granulation Tissue Formation**: Fibroblasts produce new collagen and extracellular matrix.
   - **Angiogenesis**: New blood vessels form to supply nutrients to the healing tissue.
   - **Epithelialization**: New skin cells (epithelial cells) grow over the wound.
   - **Phase of Contraction**: Myofibroblasts cause the wound edges to contract, reducing the wound size.

4. **Maturation (Remodeling) Phase**:
   - **Scar Formation**: Collagen fibers reorganize and strengthen, leading to scar tissue development.
   - **Final Healing**: The tissue regains strength and flexibility, although it may not be as strong as the original tissue.

Understanding these stages can help in managing wounds effectively and recognizing when healing is progressing normally or if medical intervention is needed.
