The question of whether humans are innately good or bad is a topic that philosophers, theologians, psychologists, and many other scholars have debated for centuries, and it's not something that can be definitively answered as it is largely based on personal beliefs, cultural context, and philosophical or religious views.

From a philosophical perspective, thinkers such as Jean-Jacques Rousseau believed in the innate goodness of humans (a concept known as "noble savage"), arguing that we are born good and that society and its institutions corrupt us. In contrast, Thomas Hobbes viewed humans as fundamentally selfish and driven by fear of death and the desire for power.

From a theological perspective, views vary greatly among different religions and doctrines. Some Christian doctrines, for instance, espouse the idea of original sin, suggesting that humans are born with a sinful nature. In contrast, other belief systems like Buddhism, argue that at our core, humans have a fundamental nature of goodness (Buddha-nature), but this is obscured by layers of ignorance.

In terms of psychology, this debate also relates to the nature vs. nurture question. Some psychologists might argue that humans are born as blank slates (tabula rasa), and our behavior is shaped by our environment. Others may point to evidence of inherent traits, such as empathy in infants, as a sign of inherent goodness. Yet, there is also evidence of inherent self-interest and aggression.

In the field of evolutionary biology, some argue that traits such as altruism could have evolved because they helped our ancestors survive and reproduce (kin selection and reciprocal altruism theories). However, competition and aggression are also a part of our evolutionary history.

In conclusion, the question of whether humans are innately good or bad is complex, multifaceted, and depends heavily on one's perspective. It remains an open and profound question that people may answer differently based on their personal beliefs, experiences, and the disciplinary lens they view it through.

The question of whether humans are innately good or bad isn't something that can be easily answered with data or evidence, since it's largely based on personal beliefs, philosophical perspectives, and social and cultural norms. That said, there is scientific evidence that can support different aspects of this debate. Here are some examples:

Supportive Data for Innate Goodness:

1. **Empathy and Altruism**: Studies have shown that even infants and young children display signs of empathy, suggesting that it might be a fundamental human trait. For example, children as young as 18 months old have been observed to help others without any expectation of reward (Warneken & Tomasello, 2006). In the field of evolutionary biology, theories of kin selection and reciprocal altruism suggest that behaviors like altruism could have evolved because they benefited our ancestors' survival and reproduction.

2. **Cooperation and Social Bonds**: Humans are social animals and thrive in groups. Many scientists argue that our evolutionary success is largely due to our ability to cooperate, form complex social relationships, and create communities. 

Data Suggesting Innate Self-Interest or Aggression:

1. **Selfishness and Competition**: There is also evidence that humans have an innate tendency towards self-interest. Some evolutionary psychologists argue that traits such as competition and aggression could have been beneficial for our ancestors' survival and reproduction.

2. **Violence and Aggression**: Some researchers point to historical and anthropological evidence of violence and warfare as indicators of an innate capacity for aggression. For example, studies of non-state societies (like hunter-gatherer groups) have found high rates of violent death, suggesting that intergroup violence might have a long evolutionary history.

3. **The Robber's Cave Experiment**: This classic social psychology experiment conducted by Muzafer Sherif in 1954 showed how easily group conflict and hostility can be created, even among similarly-minded individuals. This may suggest an innate human potential for in-group favoritism and out-group hostility, though these tendencies can also be manipulated by situational factors.

It's important to note that these data points do not conclusively answer the question of whether humans are innately good or bad. They illustrate the complexity of human behavior and its likely roots in a combination of innate tendencies, social conditioning, and individual experiences. Also, psychology, sociology, and related fields often deal with trends and averages across populations, so individual behavior can vary widely.
