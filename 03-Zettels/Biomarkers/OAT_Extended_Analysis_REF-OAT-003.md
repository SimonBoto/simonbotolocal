## 1. DYSBIOSIS / MALABSORPTION MARKERS

### 1.1 Indoleacetic Acid (IAA)
**Source:** Tryptophan metabolism by colonic bacteria (E.Coli, Saccharomyces spp, Clostridia spp)

**Elevated causes:**
- Incomplete tryptophan absorption
- Bacterial overgrowth
- High green/black tea consumption

**Clinical significance:**
- Systemic inflammation
- Mood and concentration disorders
- Chronic cardiovascular disease
- Hartnup's genetic dysfunction

**Note:** 5-HIAA may be similarly affected

---

### 1.2 Phenylacetic Acid (PAA)
**Source:** Phenylalanine metabolism by Bacteriodetes, Clostridium spp

**Elevated causes:**
- Same as IAA above
- Dietary polyphenols (wine/grapes)

---

### 1.3 Dihydroxyphenylpropionic Acid (DHPPA)
**Source:** Phenol metabolism by Clostridia spp

**Elevated causes:**
- Increased polyphenol consumption

---

### 1.4 3 & 4 Hydroxyphenylacetic Acid
**Source:** Amino acid metabolism by bacteria, protein indigestion (E.Coli, Bacteriodetes, Clostridium spp)

**Affected by:** Polyphenols — see IAA

**Clinical interpretation:**
- Related to increased antioxidant consumption
- Inversely proportional to depressive symptomatology

**Confounding factors:**
- Wine/grapes
- Cranberries
- Green/black tea
- Orange juice

---

### 1.5 Benzoic & Hippuric Acid
**Source:** Polyphenol metabolism

**Also indicates:** Preservative consumption (sodium benzoate) — found in low-quality vitamins/supplements

---

### 1.6 D-arabinitol
**Source:** Sugar alcohol produced specifically by Candida spp

**Clinical significance:**
- Subclinical candidiasis indicator
- Suggests stool testing for Candida

---

### 1.7 Citramalic & Tartaric Acid
**Source:** Yeast/fungal metabolites

**Elevated causes:**
- Increased fruit consumption
- High sugar intake

---

## 2. CELLULAR ENERGY & MITOCHONDRIAL FUNCTION

### General Principles
**Evaluates:** Body's ability to process dietary macronutrients, enter Krebs cycle, produce energy

**Assesses:**
- Fatty acid oxidation
- Glycolysis
- Protein metabolism

**Identifies:** Enzyme dysfunction, functional nutrient deficiencies (cofactors)

**Energy production affected by:**
- Vitamin/mineral deficiencies
- Toxins
- Genetic polymorphisms
- Underlying diseases

**Krebs cycle requires:**
- Iron
- Niacin (B3)
- Magnesium
- Manganese
- Thiamine (B1)
- Riboflavin (B2)
- Pantothenic acid (B5)
- Lipoic acid

**Negative impact from:** Mercury, lead, arsenic

**Clinical manifestations of mitochondrial dysfunction:**
- Neurocognitive disorders
- Diabetes
- Malignancies
- Mood disorders (depression)
- Cardiovascular disease
- Chronic fatigue syndrome

---

### 2.1 Fatty Acid Oxidation

#### Adipic & Suberic Acid (ideally not detectable)
**Mechanism:**
- Dietary fats used for energy via β-oxidation → Acetyl-CoA
- Requires adequate carnitine levels

**Without carnitine, riboflavin (B2), vitamin C:**
- Alternative ω-oxidation pathway activated
- Produces dicarboxylic acids

**Clinical:**
- Ketosis (mainly 3-Hydroxybutyric >10 with good DGLA)
- Insulin resistance
- Diabetes
- Low carbohydrate intake + inability to utilize fats adequately

**Consequences:**
- Mitochondrial dysfunction
- Cell membrane damage
- Increased oxidative stress
- Free oxygen radicals

---

### 2.2 Glycolysis

#### Lactic & Pyruvic Acid
**Products of glycolysis**

**Process:**
- Glucose broken down to pyruvate + 2 ATP
- Pyruvate also produced from alanine, serine, threonine, glycine, tryptophan catabolism

**Aerobic conditions (normal):**
- Pyruvate enters Krebs as Acetyl-CoA (pyruvate dehydrogenase) or Oxaloacetate (carboxylase)

**Anaerobic conditions:**
- Pyruvate converted to Lactic acid

**Cofactors:** Thiamine (B1), Riboflavin (B2), Niacin (B3), Biotin (B7), Magnesium, Lipoic acid

**Elevated pyruvate:**
- Cofactor deficiency
- Increased carbohydrate consumption

**Elevated lactic:**
- COPD
- Sleep apnea
- Anemia
- Strenuous exercise
- Insulin resistance
- Alcohol

**Note:** Zinc (Zn) is cofactor for LDH (Lactic Dehydrogenase) that converts lactic back to pyruvate in liver

---

#### 2-Hydroxybutyric (2-HB)
**Oxidative stress indicator**

**Mechanism:**
- Produced from 2-Ketobutyrate via LDH or HBDH (catalyzed by niacin/B3)
- Oxidative stress disrupts NADH/NAD ratio → increases 2-HB
- Indicates increased needs for GSH (glutathione)
- 2-KB is precursor for glutathione

**Elevated in:**
- Insulin resistance
- Prediabetes
- Increased liver glutathione production
- B6-B9-B12 deficiency
- Inability to use homocysteine

**Correlation:** Check with lipid peroxides and 8-OHdG (8-hydroxydeoxyguanosine)

---

#### β-Hydroxybutyric (3-HB)
**Ketone body**

**Produced in:**
- Fasting
- Intense exercise
- Metabolic disorders with inability to utilize carbohydrates
- Low carbohydrate intake

**Clinical:**
- Early indicator of diabetes, insulin resistance, poor glycemic control

---

#### β-Hydroxy-β-methylglutaric (3-HMGA)
**Precursor of cholesterol and CoQ10**

**Product of HMG-CoA regulated by HMG-CoA reductase**

**Elevated causes:**
- Statins
- Red yeast rice
- Niacin (B3) deficiency = disrupted oxidative phosphorylation

**Note:** CoQ10 therapy for statin myopathy — high dose 100-600mg/day (daily needs = 500mg)

---

### 2.3 Krebs Cycle Detailed

#### Citric - Aconitic - Isocitric
**Mechanism:**
- Citric produced from 2-carbon transfer to oxaloacetate
- Converted to isocitric via aconitic by aconitase
- Aconitase = iron and sulfur-dependent protein

**Elevated causes:**
- Iron deficiency
- Magnesium deficiency
- Increased oxidative stress
- Increased glutathione (aconitase regulator) = glutamine deficiency
- Toxins

**Decreased causes:**
- Precursor deficiency (low consumption or malabsorption of proteins)
- Glycolysis or fatty acid oxidation disturbance

---

#### α-Ketoglutarate
**Produced from isocitric via isocitrate dehydrogenase**

**Regulates cycle rate**

**Also:**
- Basic source of glutamine and glutamic acid
- Promotes protein synthesis and bone production
- Inhibits protein catabolism in muscles
- Basic "fuel" for gastrointestinal cells

**Converted to succinyl-CoA via α-ketoglutarate dehydrogenase** (similar to pyruvate dehydrogenase)

**Elevated causes:**
- B3, Zn, Mg, Mn deficiency
- Mitochondrial dysfunction
- Oxidative phosphorylation disturbance (CoQ10)

**Decreased causes:**
- Block in precursor molecules

---

#### Succinic Acid
**Produced from Succinyl-CoA via Succinyl CoA synthetase**

**Produces NADH providing electrons directly for respiratory chain feeding**

---

#### Fumaric Acid
**Produced from Succinic via Succinate Dehydrogenase**

**Requires Riboflavin (B2) for FAD**

**Succinate Dehydrogenase:**
- Dominant role in oxidative phosphorylation
- Related to nervous system disorders and malignancies

**Elevated succinic:**
- Cofactor deficiency or low cycle supply
- Associated with DNA damage and hypermethylation

**Decreased:**
- Reduced BCAA consumption (valine, leucine, isoleucine)
- B12 deficiency (cannot convert methylmalonyl-CoA to Succinyl-CoA)

---

#### Malic Acid
**Produced from Fumaric via Fumarase**

**Converted to Oxaloacetate via Malate Dehydrogenase**

**Two types:**
- Mitochondrial
- Cytosolic (participates in malate/aspartate cycle)

**Elevated causes:**
- B3 (NAD) deficiency
- Increased fruit consumption (apples, pears) + preservative additives

---

## 3. B-VITAMIN STATUS MARKERS

### 3.1 α-Ketoadipic
**Source:** Lysine metabolism (via α-aminoadipic) and tryptophan (via α-aminomuconic)

**Converted to:** Glutaryl-CoA

**Cofactors required:** CoA, NAD (B3), B1, B2, lipoic acid

---

### 3.2 Branched-Chain Amino Acids (BCAA)

#### α-Ketoisovaleric
**Source:** Valine metabolism

**Normally converts to:** Succinyl-CoA (gluconeogenic)

---

#### α-Ketoisocaproic
**Source:** Leucine metabolism

**Normally converts to:** Acetyl-CoA and acetoacetate (ketogenic)

---

#### α-Keto-β-Methylvaleric
**Source:** Isoleucine metabolism

**Normally converts to:** Succinyl and Acetyl-CoA (gluco+ketogenic)

**BCAA process:**
- Enter bloodstream after protein consumption
- Metabolized in muscles using aminotransferases → α-ketoacids
- Return to circulation for final liver metabolism

**Used for:** All anabolic processes (proteins, energy, neurotransmitters, glucose, immune function)

**Elevated/Detectable:** B1-B2-B3-B5 and lipoic acid deficiency

---

### 3.3 Glutaric Acid
**Source:** Lysine and tryptophan metabolism via α-aminoadipic and α-ketoadipic

**Conversion:** To Glutaconyl + Crotonyl CoA by glutaryl-CoA dehydrogenase (requires B2)

**Elevated:** B2 deficiency or electron transport chain disturbance

---

## 4. METHYLATION MARKERS

### 4.1 Formiminoglutamic Acid (FIGlu)
**Intermediate in histidine → glutamic acid conversion**

**Requires:** Folic acid (B9)

**Elevated causes:**
- B9 (folate) deficiency
- B12 deficiency (folate recycling)
- Alcohol use
- Pregnancy or contraceptives

---

### 4.2 Methylmalonic Acid (MMA)
**From Propionyl-CoA → Methylmalonyl-CoA that didn't become Succinyl-CoA due to B12-2 deficiency**

**Enzyme:** Methylmalonyl-CoA mutase (cofactor: B12)

**Clinical:**
- Cognitive disorders
- Neuropathy
- Insomnia

---

### 4.3 3-Hydroxyisovaleric
**From leucine metabolism (requires biotin/B7)**

**Elevated causes:**
- Biotin deficiency
- Increased metabolism (smoking)
- Pregnancy
- Anticonvulsants

---

### 5.2 Catecholamines

#### Homovanillic Acid (HVA)
**Dopamine metabolite**

**Production:**
- From phenylalanine and tyrosine
- Cofactors: Iron, tetrahydrobiopterin (BH4), B6
- Converts to noradrenaline with copper and vitamin C (dopamine beta-hydroxylase)
- Metabolism to HVA via MAO (B2) and COMT (Mg, B6, SAM)

**Elevated causes:**
- Block in metabolism to norepinephrine (cofactor deficiency)
- Quercetin, tomatoes, onions, tea
- Neuroblastoma

**Clinical:**
- Mood regulation
- Motivation
- Stress response
- PTSD
- Mood disorders

**Decreased causes:**
- Antidepressants
- Amino acid deficiency
- Methylation disorder

---

#### Vanillylmandelic Acid (VMA)
**Formed in liver from oxidation of 3-methoxy-4-hydroxyphenylglycol**

**From catabolism of tyrosine-derived neurotransmitters**

**Indicates:** Total catecholamine production and turnover

**Elevated causes:**
- Antidepressants
- ADHD medication
- Generalized anxiety disorder
- Pheochromocytoma
- Renovascular hypertension
- Neuroblastoma
- Stress response
- Exercise
- PTSD

**Low causes:**
- Amino acid deficiency
- Cofactor deficiency
- Methylation disorder
- Copper deficiency
- Increased manganese

### 5.3 Serotonin Indicators

#### 5-Hydroxyindoleacetic Acid (5-HIAA)
**From serotonin catabolism**

**Serotonin produced from tryptophan via MAO**

**Mainly extra-CNS production:** Liver and enterochromaffin cells (GI)

**Elevated causes:**
- Microbiome disturbance
- Increased enterochromaffin and microbes due to intestinal inflammation
- Diet high in: bananas, kiwi, pineapple, nuts, tomatoes
- Carcinoid tumors (GI + Lung)
- Aspirin, paracetamol

**Decreased causes:**
- Malabsorption or reduced tryptophan consumption
- MAO inhibitors
- Metabolic syndrome
- IBS
- Migraine
- Mood disorders

## 6. ANTIOXIDANT INDICATORS

### 6.1 Pyroglutamic Acid (5-oxoproline)
**Produced and used in gamma-glutamyl cycle**

**Essential for:** Glutathione production and recycling (powerful antioxidant)

**Indicator of:** Glycine and glutamine adequacy

**Glutathione:**
- Tripeptide: glutamic acid + cysteine + glycine
- Via gGT breaks to cysteinylglycine + gamma-glutamyl
- Via pyroglutamic → glutamylcysteine + glycine = glutathione
- Requires ATP and magnesium

**Elevated causes:**
- Precursor deficiency (glycine, cysteine, glutamine)
- Cofactor deficiency (magnesium)
- Increased oxidative stress

---

### 6.2 Orotic Acid
**Intermediate in nucleotide synthesis (pyrimidine cycle)**

**Indicator of:** Urea cycle (nitrogen metabolism — arginine/glutamine adequacy)

**Produced from:** Aspartic + carbamoyl phosphate
- Introduces nitrogen into urea cycle for detoxification
- Reacts with ornithine for citrulline synthesis

**Elevated causes:**
- Hyperammonemia
- Protein diets
- Arginine metabolism disorders
- Hepatic toxins
- Liver disease
- Alcohol
- Allopurinol (Zyloric)
- Hypertension

**Leads to:** Endothelial damage and NO regulation disorders (= hypertension, cardiovascular)

**Also:** Dysbiosis — Candida (Tsouk.)

## 8. OXALATE METABOLITES

**Three organic acids as final products of glyoxalate pathway:**
- Glyceric
- Glycolic
- Oxalic (shows overall function)

**Clinically related to:**
- Nephrolithiasis (kidney stones)
- Vessel deposition (in greater dysfunction)

### 8.1 Glyceric Acid
**From serine catabolism**

**Elevated causes:**
- Rheumatoid arthritis
- B3 deficiency (glyoxylate reductase)
- Magnesium deficiency (glycerate kinase)
- Schizophrenia (?)

---

### 8.2 Glycolic Acid
**From glyoxylic acid metabolism**

**Sources:**
- Glycine metabolism
- Hydroxyproline metabolism (collagen destruction)
- Glyoxal (oxidative factor)

**Elevated causes:**
- B6 deficiency
- Oxidative stress
- Increased glutathione needs

---

### 8.3 Oxalic Acid
**Final product of glyoxylase pathway**

**From oxidation of glyoxylate** (instead of conversion to glycine or glycolic)

**Clinical significance:**
- 80% of kidney stones are calcium oxalate (!)
- Only ~40% from diet
- Depends on microbiome (Oxalobacter formigenes — friendly)
- Dietary sources: spinach, beets, cocoa, tea, wheat, strawberries

**Cooking/soaking reduces oxalates**

**Elevated causes:**
- Oxidative stress
- Glutathione depletion
- Insufficient calcium intake
- Increased collagen consumption or catabolism
- Ascorbic acid
- Metabolic syndrome
- Obesity
- Insulin resistance
- B6 deficiency
