# Iron Studies Family - B-003-0001

## 1. IDENTIFICATION

| Marker | Full Name | What It Measures |
|--------|-----------|------------------|
| **Ferritin** | Serum Ferritin | Iron storage (primary marker) |
| **Serum Iron** | Serum Iron | Circulating iron bound to transferrin |
| **TIBC** | Total Iron Binding Capacity | Transferrin available to bind iron |
| **Transferrin Saturation** | TSAT | % of transferrin saturated with iron |
| **sTfR** | Soluble Transferrin Receptor | Tissue iron demand |

**Specimen:** Serum (fasting preferred for serum iron)
**Collection:** Morning, fasting for serum iron

## 2. REFERENCE RANGES (Simos Clinical Targets)

### Ferritin - CRITICAL MARKER (Sex-Specific)

**WOMEN:**
| Range | Value | Clinical Significance |
|-------|-------|----------------------|
| **Deficiency** | **<30 ng/mL** | Iron deficiency (lowest cutoff) |
| **Low** | 30-50 | Suboptimal |
| **Optimal** | **~70** | Ideal for women |
| **Elevated** | **>120** | **Hides methylation difficulty** — check 5-methylfolate, methylmalonic, homocysteine |

**MEN:**
| Range | Value | Clinical Significance |
|-------|-------|----------------------|
| **Deficiency** | **<30 ng/mL** | Iron deficiency |
| **Optimal** | 50-100 | Ideal range |
| **Watchful** | **>300** | **Problematic — monitor closely** |
| **Overload** | >1000 | Hemochromatosis risk |

**Simos Critical Rules:**
- **Ferritin <30 = Iron deficiency (both sexes, regardless of other markers)**
- **Women: Optimal ~70; >120 check methylation axis (5-methylfolate, methylmalonic, homocysteine)**
- **Men: >300 = watchful, investigate cause**

### Serum Iron
| Range | Value | Notes |
|-------|-------|-------|
| Low | <60 μg/dL | Iron deficiency, chronic disease |
| Optimal | 60-120 | Adequate iron |
| High | >150 | Iron overload, hemolysis |

### TIBC (Total Iron Binding Capacity)
| Range | Value | Notes |
|-------|-------|-------|
| Low | <250 | Iron overload, inflammation |
| Normal | 250-400 | |
| High | >400 | Iron deficiency (liver makes more transferrin) |

### Transferrin Saturation (TSAT)
| Range | Value | Clinical |
|-------|-------|----------|
| **<20%** | Low | **Iron deficiency** |
| 20-50% | Normal | Adequate |
| **>50%** | High | **Iron overload risk** |
| >60% | Very high | Hemochromatosis likely |

**Simos Rule:** TSAT <20% = iron deficiency; >50% = overload risk

**Women-Specific Note:** Ferritin >120 may indicate impaired iron utilization despite adequate stores — always check methylation status (5-methylfolate, methylmalonic acid, homocysteine)

### Soluble Transferrin Receptor (sTfR)
| Range | Value | Notes |
|-------|-------|-------|
| Normal | 2.9-8.3 mg/L | |
| Elevated | >8.3 | Iron deficiency (tissue demand high) |
| Log(sTfR/Ferritin) | >2 | Iron deficiency (best functional marker) |

## 3. IRON METABOLISM

**Iron Cycle:**
1. **Absorption:** Duodenum (1-2 mg/day, regulated by hepcidin)
2. **Transport:** Transferrin in blood
3. **Storage:** Ferritin in liver, spleen, bone marrow
4. **Utilization:** Heme synthesis in bone marrow
5. **Recycling:** Macrophages recycle old RBCs

**Regulation:**
- **Hepcidin:** Master regulator (high hepcidin = ↓ absorption, ↓ release)
- **Iron status:** Low iron → low hepcidin → ↑ absorption
- **Inflammation:** High IL-6 → high hepcidin → functional iron deficiency

**Daily Iron:**
- Loss: 1-2 mg (shedding, menstruation)
- Absorption: 1-2 mg (10% of dietary iron)
- Recycling: 20-25 mg (from old RBCs)

## 4. CLINICAL INTERPRETATION

### Iron Deficiency Patterns

**Stage 1: Iron Depletion (Storage Iron Low)**
| Marker | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Ferritin | <30 ng/mL |
| Serum Iron | Normal |
| TIBC | Normal or ↑ |
| TSAT | Normal |
| Hb | Normal |

**Stage 2: Iron Deficiency Erythropoiesis**
| Marker | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Ferritin | <30 ng/mL |
| Serum Iron | ↓ |
| TIBC | ↑ |
| TSAT | <20% |
| Hb | Normal or ↓ |
| sTfR | ↑ |

**Stage 3: Iron Deficiency Anemia**
| Marker | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Ferritin | <15 ng/mL |
| Serum Iron | ↓↓ |
| TIBC | ↑↑ |
| TSAT | <15% |
| Hb | ↓↓ (anemia) |
| MCV | ↓ (microcytic) |

### Iron Overload Patterns

**Hemochromatosis (Primary)**
| Marker | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Ferritin | >1000 ng/mL |
| TSAT | >60% |
| Serum Iron | ↑ |
| TIBC | ↓ |
| Genetic | HFE mutation (C282Y, H63D) |

**Secondary Iron Overload**
| Cause | Mechanism |
|-------|-----------|
| Transfusional | Repeated RBC transfusions |
| Hemolytic anemia | Increased iron recycling |
| Liver disease | Impaired hepcidin regulation |
| Porphyria cutanea tarda | Hepatic iron accumulation |

### Functional Iron Deficiency (Anemia of Chronic Disease)

**Key Feature:** Iron trapped in macrophages, not available for erythropoiesis

| Marker | Value | Interpretation |
|--------|-------|----------------|
| Ferritin | Normal or ↑ | Inflammation increases ferritin |
| Serum Iron | ↓ | Not available |
| TIBC | ↓ | Inflammation suppresses |
| TSAT | <20% | Functional deficiency |
| sTfR | Normal or ↑ | Tissue demand |
| CRP | ↑ | Inflammation marker |

**Differentiation:**
- True iron deficiency: Ferritin <30, sTfR high
- Functional deficiency: Ferritin normal/high, sTfR normal/high

## 5. NUTRITIONAL CONNECTIONS

### Iron Sources
| Type | Sources | Absorption |
|------|---------|------------|
| **Heme iron** | Red meat, liver, oysters, clams | 15-35% (best) |
| **Non-heme iron** | Lentils, spinach, fortified foods | 2-20% (variable) |

### Absorption Enhancers
| Factor | Mechanism |
|--------|-----------|
| **Vitamin C** | Reduces Fe3+ to Fe2+, chelates |
| **Acidic environment** | Stomach acid solubilizes iron |
| **Meat factor** | Unknown peptide enhances |
| **Low iron stores** | Upregulates DMT1 transporter |

### Absorption Inhibitors
| Factor | Mechanism |
|--------|-----------|
| **Phytates** (grains, legumes) | Bind iron |
| **Tannins** (tea, coffee) | Bind iron |
| **Calcium** | Competes for absorption |
| **Antacids/PPIs** | ↓ Stomach acid |
| **High iron stores** | Hepcidin blocks absorption |
| **Inflammation** | High hepcidin |

### Other Nutrients
| Nutrient | Role |
|----------|------|
| **Copper** | Iron mobilization from liver |
| **Vitamin A** | Iron mobilization |
| **Vitamin B12/Folate** | RBC production (need iron too) |
| **Riboflavin (B2)** | Iron absorption |
| **Manganese** | Iron metabolism |

## 6. SUPPLEMENT INTERVENTIONS

### Iron Deficiency

**Oral Iron (First Line):**
| Form | Dose | Notes |
|------|------|-------|
| **Iron bisglycinate** | 25-50 mg elemental | Best tolerated |
| **Iron sulfate** | 65 mg elemental | Cheapest, more GI side effects |
| **Iron bisglycinate** | 25-50 mg elemental | Better absorption, less nausea |
| **Heme iron** | 11-22 mg | From animal sources, well absorbed |

**Dosing Strategy:**
- Take on empty stomach (if tolerated)
- With vitamin C (250-500 mg)
- Avoid: Tea, coffee, calcium, antacids (2 hours)
- Side effects: Constipation, nausea, dark stools

**Monitoring:**
- Recheck ferritin, Hb in 8 weeks
- Expect: Hb ↑ 1 g/dL in 1 month
- Continue 3-6 months to replete stores

**IV Iron (When Oral Fails):**
- Indications: Malabsorption, intolerance, severe deficiency, need rapid repletion
- Forms: Ferric carboxymaltose, iron sucrose, ferumoxytol
- Monitoring: Test dose, observe for anaphylaxis

### Iron Overload

**Therapeutic Phlebotomy:**
- Remove 500 mL blood weekly until ferritin <50
- Maintenance: Every 3-4 months

**Chelation (if phlebotomy contraindicated):**
- Deferoxamine (IV)
- Deferasirox (oral)
- Deferiprone (oral)

**Dietary:**
- Avoid vitamin C with meals (enhances absorption)
- Avoid alcohol (liver damage)
- Tea with meals (inhibits absorption)

## 7. RED FLAGS

🚩 **Critical:**
- Ferritin <10 ng/mL (severe deficiency)
- Ferritin >1000 ng/mL (overload, organ damage risk)
- TSAT >60% (hemochromatosis likely)
- Iron deficiency with no clear cause → GI malignancy workup

⚠️ **Urgent:**
- Ferritin <30 with anemia
- Ferritin >500 with elevated liver enzymes
- Family history hemochromatosis + high ferritin

## 8. SPECIAL POPULATIONS

### Women (Menstruating)
- Higher iron needs (1-2 mg/day loss)
- Ferritin often 20-50 (lower than men)
- Target: >50 to prevent deficiency

### Pregnancy
- Increased needs (fetus, placenta, blood volume)
- Target ferritin: >30
- Routine supplementation often recommended

### Athletes
- Foot strike hemolysis (runners)
- Increased iron needs
- Ferritin often lower
- Target: >50 for optimal performance

### Elderly
- Check for GI bleeding (colon cancer)
- Malabsorption common
- Ferritin may be elevated (inflammation) even with deficiency
