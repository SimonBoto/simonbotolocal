# BUN — Blood Urea Nitrogen

## 1. ETYMOLOGY

**BUN:** Blood Urea Nitrogen

**Urea:** From Greek "ouron" (urine) — discovered in urine by Hilaire Rouelle in 1773

**Nitrogen:** Component measured (urea contains two nitrogen atoms)

**Also called:**
- Serum urea nitrogen
- Urea (in some countries)

## 2. PHYSIOLOGY

### Biological Origin

**Protein metabolism end product:**
- Amino acids → deamination → ammonia → urea (liver)
- Urea is non-toxic form of nitrogen excretion
- Transported in blood to kidneys
- Filtered and excreted in urine

### Normal Production

**Factors increasing urea production:**
- High protein intake
- Catabolic states (fever, starvation, trauma)
- GI bleeding (blood protein digestion)
- Corticosteroids
- Tetracyclines

### Renal Handling

**Filtration and reabsorption:**
- Freely filtered at glomerulus
- Partially reabsorbed (40-50%)
- Reabsorption increases in dehydration (ADH effect)

## 3. METABOLIC INTERPRETATION

### BUN Levels

| Level (mg/dL) | Status | Interpretation |
|---------------|--------|----------------|
| **7-20** | Normal | Typical range |
| **<7** | Low | Low protein intake, liver disease, overhydration |
| **20-40** | Elevated | Mild kidney dysfunction, dehydration, high protein |
| **>40** | High | Significant kidney dysfunction, severe dehydration |
| **>100** | Very high | Severe azotemia, uremic symptoms |

### BUN:Creatinine Ratio

| Ratio | Interpretation |
|-------|---------------|
| **10-20:1** | Normal |
| **>20:1** | Prerenal azotemia (dehydration, CHF) |
| **<10:1** | Low protein intake, liver disease, tubular disease |

### Clinical Significance

**Elevated BUN causes:**
- **Prerenal:** Dehydration, CHF, shock (reduced renal perfusion)
- **Renal:** Kidney disease
- **Postrenal:** Urinary obstruction
- **Increased production:** High protein, catabolism, GI bleed

**Low BUN causes:**
- Low protein intake
- Liver disease (impaired urea synthesis)
- Overhydration
- Malnutrition

## 4. COFACTORS

### Factors Affecting BUN (Non-Renal)

| Factor | Effect | Mechanism |
|--------|--------|-----------|
| **Protein intake** | Increases | More substrate |
| **Hydration** | Concentration effect | Dehydration elevates |
| **Catabolism** | Increases | Tissue breakdown |
| **GI bleeding** | Increases | Blood protein digestion |
| **Liver function** | Decreases if impaired | Reduced urea synthesis |
| **Steroids** | Increases | Catabolic effect |

## 5. INTERVENTIONS

### For Elevated BUN

**Identify cause:**
- Dehydration → rehydration
- Kidney disease → manage CKD
- High protein → moderate if excessive
- GI bleed → stop bleeding

### For Low BUN

**Identify cause:**
- Malnutrition → nutritional support
- Liver disease → hepatic support
- Overhydration → usually not problematic

## 6. CONNECTED BIOMARKERS

| Biomarker | Relationship | Pattern |
|-----------|--------------|---------|
| **Creatinine** | Paired assessment | BUN:Creatinine ratio diagnostic |
| **eGFR** | Kidney function | Both elevated in CKD |
| **Albumin** | Nutrition | Low albumin + low BUN = malnutrition |
| **Liver enzymes** | Liver function | Elevated LFTs + low BUN = liver dysfunction |

### Clinical Patterns

**Prerenal Azotemia:**
- BUN:Creatinine >20:1
- BUN elevated more than creatinine
- Dehydration, CHF, shock
- Reversible with treatment

**Intrinsic Renal Disease:**
- BUN:Creatinine ~10-15:1
- Both elevated proportionally
- Acute or chronic kidney disease

**Postrenal Obstruction:**
- BUN and creatinine both elevated
- Urinary symptoms
- Imaging shows obstruction
