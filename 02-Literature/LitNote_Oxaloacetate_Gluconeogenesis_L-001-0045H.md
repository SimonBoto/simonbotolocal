## Oxaloacetate: The Critical Junction

### Structure
- **4-carbon dicarboxylic acid**
- **α-keto acid** (like pyruvate, but 4C)
- **Krebs cycle intermediate**
- **Gluconeogenic precursor**

### Dual Identity
**1. Krebs Cycle Component**
- Condenses with acetyl-CoA → citrate
- Regenerated at end of cycle
- Anaplerotic ("filling up") reactions maintain pool

**2. Gluconeogenic Precursor**
- Converted to phosphoenolpyruvate (PEP)
- Bypasses irreversible pyruvate kinase step
- Essential for glucose synthesis

---

## The Oxaloacetate-Ketogenesis Connection

### Competing Demands
**Liver during fasting:**
- **Gluconeogenesis:** Needs oxaloacetate → PEP → glucose
- **Krebs cycle:** Needs oxaloacetate to process acetyl-CoA
- **Conflict:** Both pathways compete for limited OAA

### Resolution: Ketogenesis Dominates
**During prolonged fasting:**
- OAA preferentially diverted to gluconeogenesis
- Less OAA available for Krebs cycle
- Excess acetyl-CoA → **shunted to ketogenesis**
- **Result:** Ketone body production increases

### Acetoacetate is NOT from Oxaloacetate
**Important distinction:**
- Acetoacetate comes from **HMG-CoA** (fatty acid-derived)
- Oxaloacetate is separate pathway
- However, OAA availability **indirectly influences** ketogenesis by determining acetyl-CoA fate

## Gluconeogenesis from Oxaloacetate

### The Mitochondrial Problem
**Oxaloacetate cannot cross mitochondrial membrane directly.**

### Solutions: Shuttle Systems

#### 1. Malate Shuttle
```
Mitochondrial OAA + NADH → Malate (malate dehydrogenase)
Malate transported to cytosol
Cytosolic Malate + NAD⁺ → OAA + NADH
```

**Benefit:** Transfers reducing equivalents (NADH) to cytosol

#### 2. Aspartate Shuttle
```
Mitochondrial OAA + Glutamate → Aspartate + α-ketoglutarate
(aspartate aminotransferase)
Aspartate transported to cytosol
Cytosolic Aspartate + α-ketoglutarate → OAA + Glutamate
```

**Benefit:** No net redox transfer, just carbon skeleton

### Cytosolic Conversion to PEP
```
Oxaloacetate + GTP → Phosphoenolpyruvate + GDP + CO₂
```
**Enzyme:** PEP carboxykinase (PEPCK)
**Cofactor:** GTP (or ATP)

**Regulation:**
- **Induced by:** Glucagon, cortisol, fasting
- **Repressed by:** Insulin

## Regulation of Gluconeogenesis

### Hormonal Control
| Hormone | Effect | Mechanism |
|---------|--------|-----------|
| **Glucagon** | ↑ Gluconeogenesis | ↑ cAMP → ↑ PEPCK, ↑ FBPase |
| **Cortisol** | ↑ Gluconeogenesis | Transcriptional activation |
| **Insulin** | ↓ Gluconeogenesis | ↓ PEPCK, ↑ glycolytic enzymes |
| **Epinephrine** | ↑ Gluconeogenesis | ↑ cAMP (emergency) |

### Substrate Availability
- **Lactate:** Cori cycle (muscle → liver)
- **Alanine:** Glucose-alanine cycle
- **Glycerol:** From lipolysis
- **Glucogenic amino acids:** From protein breakdown

### Energy Cost
**Gluconeogenesis is expensive:**
- 6 ATP per glucose (from pyruvate)
- 4 ATP + 2 GTP per glucose (from lactate)
- Must be tightly regulated

## Anaplerosis: Replenishing OAA

### Why Needed?
- Gluconeogenesis removes OAA from Krebs cycle
- Must be replenished to maintain cycle function

### Anaplerotic Reactions
| Substrate | Enzyme | Product |
|-----------|--------|---------|
| **Pyruvate** | Pyruvate carboxylase | Oxaloacetate |
| **Aspartate** | Aspartate aminotransferase | Oxaloacetate |
| **Glutamate** | Transamination | α-ketoglutarate |

**Pyruvate carboxylase:**
- Requires **biotin (B7)**
- Activated by acetyl-CoA
- Key anaplerotic enzyme

## Clinical Relevance

### Type 2 Diabetes
- **Excessive gluconeogenesis:** Major contributor to hyperglycemia
- **Insulin fails to suppress:** Hepatic glucose output
- **Metformin action:** Inhibits gluconeogenesis (via AMPK)

### Fasting Hypoglycemia
- Defects in gluconeogenic enzymes (PEPCK, glucose-6-phosphatase)
- Cannot maintain blood glucose during fasting

### Ketogenic Diet
- **Reduced gluconeogenesis:** Ketones spare glucose
- **Preserved:** Minimum needed for obligate glucose users (RBCs, brain partially)
