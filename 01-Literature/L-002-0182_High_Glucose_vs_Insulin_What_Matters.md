# Lit: High Glucose vs High Insulin — What Matters More

## Source
- **Full citation:** Zhao Y, et al. (2019). Insulin levels and cardiovascular mortality. *(Journal pending)*
- **Inbox origin:** [[Υψηλή-Γλυκόζη-Ινσουλίνη-Σημασία]]
- **Reference note:** [[Zhao_2019_Insulin_CVD_Mortality_R-002-0026]]

## Core Claim
**Elevated insulin levels are more predictive of cardiovascular mortality than elevated glucose levels** — challenging the glucose-centric model of diabetes complications and highlighting insulin resistance/hyperinsulinemia as the primary driver of cardiovascular disease in metabolic dysfunction.

---

## Zettels Extracted

### 1. Insulin More Predictive of CVD Mortality Than Glucose
- **Claim:** Elevated fasting insulin and C-peptide levels are stronger independent predictors of cardiovascular mortality than fasting glucose or HbA1c, indicating that insulin resistance and compensatory hyperinsulinemia are more pathogenic than hyperglycemia itself for cardiovascular outcomes.
- **Evidence:** Zhao 2019 analysis demonstrates that insulin levels predict CVD mortality independent of glucose levels; patients with normal glucose but elevated insulin (pre-diabetes/insulin resistance) have higher CVD risk than previously recognized — the insulin resistance state itself, not just its glycemic consequences, drives vascular disease.
- **Reference:** [[Zhao_2019_Insulin_CVD_Mortality_R-002-0026]] — cited as (Zhao et al., 2019)
- **Zettel:** [[Insulin_More_Predictive_CVD_Than_Glucose_Z-002-0681]]

### 2. Hyperinsulinemia Drives CVD Independent of Glycemia
- **Claim:** Hyperinsulinemia promotes cardiovascular disease through multiple mechanisms — including endothelial dysfunction, sympathetic nervous system activation, sodium retention, smooth muscle proliferation, and dyslipidemia — that operate independently of blood glucose levels.
- **Evidence:** Insulin at high levels (compensatory hyperinsulinemia) loses its normal vascular signaling and becomes pathogenic: promotes atherosclerosis, induces hypertension, drives inflammation, and causes dyslipidemia (high triglycerides, low HDL) — these effects occur even when glucose is normal or mildly elevated, explaining why CVD risk begins in pre-diabetes.
- **Reference:** [[Zhao_2019_Insulin_CVD_Mortality_R-002-0026]] — cited as (Zhao et al., 2019)
- **Zettel:** [[Hyperinsulinemia_Drives_CVD_Independent_Glycemia_Z-002-0682]]

### 3. Insulin-Centric vs Glucose-Centric Diabetes Paradigm
- **Claim:** The insulin-centric paradigm — focusing on insulin resistance and hyperinsulinemia as primary pathogenic drivers — better explains cardiovascular risk patterns than the glucose-centric paradigm, suggesting that interventions targeting insulin (low-carb diets, fasting, metformin) may be more cardioprotective than glucose-focused approaches alone.
- **Evidence:** The disconnect between glucose control and CVD outcomes in diabetes trials (ACCORD, ADVANCE, VADT) suggests glucose-lowering alone is insufficient; interventions that reduce insulin levels (carbohydrate restriction, weight loss, exercise) improve CVD risk factors beyond glucose reduction — supporting insulin as the primary therapeutic target for metabolic disease.
- **Reference:** [[Zhao_2019_Insulin_CVD_Mortality_R-002-0026]] — cited as (Zhao et al., 2019)
- **Zettel:** [[Insulin_Centric_vs_Glucose_Centric_Paradigm_Z-002-0683]]
