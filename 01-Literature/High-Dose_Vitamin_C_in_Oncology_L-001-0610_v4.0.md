# Lit: High-Dose Vitamin C in Oncology

## Source
- **Source type:** ChatGPT conversation archive

## Core Concepts Extracted
High-dose intravenous vitamin C represents a metabolic approach to cancer therapy—generating oxidative stress selectively in cancer cells (due to catalase deficiency) while potentially enhancing epigenetic regulation through TET enzyme cofactor activity. Best evidence supports use as adjunctive therapy rather than standalone treatment, with historical controversy stemming from pharmacokinetic misunderstandings between oral and IV routes.

## Core Concepts Extracted

### 1. Pro-Oxidant Effects at Pharmacological Doses
> "High-dose IV vitamin C generates hydrogen peroxide, selectively toxic to cancer cells due to their catalase deficiency."

**Category:** Mechanism

**What the source gives:**
Pharmacological IV vitamin C (plasma >1,000 μM) generates H₂O₂ through auto-oxidation. Cancer cells have reduced catalase activity as part of metabolic reprogramming, creating selective toxicity while normal cells remain protected.

**Why it matters:**
Establishes a therapeutic window for selective cancer cell killing based on metabolic differences between normal and malignant cells.

**Candidate Zettel:** [[Vitamin_C_Pro_Oxidant_Cancer_Selective_Toxicity_Z-001-1125]]

---

### 2. Epigenetic Modulation via TET Enzymes
> "Vitamin C is a cofactor for DNA and histone demethylases, affecting gene expression in cancer cells."

**Category:** Mechanism

**What the source gives:**
Vitamin C serves as essential cofactor for TET enzymes (DNA demethylation) and JmjC histone demethylases. This can reactivate silenced tumor suppressor genes and modulate cancer-associated epigenetic patterns.

**Why it matters:**
Provides a second, independent anti-cancer mechanism beyond oxidative stress—potential for synergistic combination with hypomethylating agents.

**Candidate Zettel:** [[Vitamin_C_Epigenetic_DNA_Histone_Demethylation_Z-001-1126]]

---

### 3. Clinical Evidence and Historical Controversies
> "Mixed results from clinical trials; promising preclinical data but need for rigorous large-scale studies."

**Category:** Evidence/Argument

**What the source gives:**
The Cameron-Pauling studies (1970s) showed promise but had methodological flaws. Subsequent Mayo Clinic RCTs using oral vitamin C found no benefit—critical misunderstanding since oral cannot achieve pharmacologic plasma levels. Modern IV studies show quality-of-life benefits and some tumor responses, but definitive Phase III evidence is lacking.

**Why it matters:**
Historical context explains why vitamin C was discredited and why the distinction between oral and IV routes is pharmacologically critical for therapeutic validity.

**Candidate Zettel:** [[Vitamin_C_Clinical_Evidence_Cancer_Controversies_Z-001-1127]]

## The Dose Distinction

| Dose Type | Route | Plasma Level | Effect |
|-----------|-------|--------------|--------|
| **Dietary** | Oral | <100 μM | Antioxidant, essential nutrient |
| **High-dose** | IV | >1,000 μM | Pro-oxidant, therapeutic |

## Administration Protocols

| Protocol | Dose | Frequency | Setting |
|----------|------|-----------|---------|
| **Standard IV** | 10-100g | 2-3x/week | Outpatient |
| **RI Protocol** | 1.5 g/kg | 3x/week | Integrated with chemo |
| **Cameron-Pauling** | 10g daily | Daily | Historical (oral) |

## Evidence Summary

| Study Type | Finding |
|------------|---------|
| **Preclinical** | Consistent anti-cancer effects at pharmacologic concentrations |
| **Phase I trials** | Safe, well-tolerated at high IV doses |
| **Phase II trials** | Mixed tumor responses; consistent quality-of-life improvements |
| **Phase III trials** | Limited; ongoing |
| **Meta-analyses** | Insufficient high-quality RCTs for definitive conclusions |
