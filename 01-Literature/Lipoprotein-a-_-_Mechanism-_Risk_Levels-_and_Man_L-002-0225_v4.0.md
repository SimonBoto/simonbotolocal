# Lit: Lipoprotein(a) — Mechanism, Risk Levels, and Management

## Core Concepts Extracted
**Lipoprotein(a) [Lp(a)] is a genetically determined cardiovascular risk factor that increases thrombosis (clot formation), heart attack, and stroke risk** — an evolutionary survival mechanism (faster bleeding cessation) now a liability with modern lifespans; levels >50 mg/dL increase risk by 50% or more, with dose-response relationship (+20 mg/dL ≈ +10% risk); no current drugs available but trials underway; management focuses on aggressive control of other modifiable risk factors.

## Zettels Extracted

### 1. Lp(a) Promotes Thrombosis — Evolutionary Survival Mechanism
- **Claim:** Lp(a) promotes clot formation through its unique apolipoprotein(a) component — an evolutionary mechanism that aided prehistoric survival through faster bleeding cessation after injury, now a liability in modern times with longer lifespans and less trauma.
- **Evidence:** Lp(a) structure: LDL-like particle + apolipoprotein(a) = thrombosis promotion; apo(a) has plasminogen-like structure → interferes with clot lysis; evolutionary function: reduced trauma mortality in prehistoric times; modern context: longer lifespans allow atherosclerosis and thrombosis to develop; same mechanism that increased survival now causes heart attacks and strokes; genetic variant frequency suggests strong historical selection pressure.
- **Reference:**  — cited as (Kamstrup et al., 2009; Lp(a) literature)
- **Zettel:** [[Lpa_Promotes_Thrombosis_Evolutionary_Origin_Z-002-0813]]

### 2. Lp(a) Risk Levels and Dose-Response Interpretation
- **Claim:** Lp(a) levels follow a dose-response relationship with cardiovascular risk — <30 mg/dL normal, 30-50 mg/dL moderate increase, >50 mg/dL significant increase (50%+ risk elevation); each +20 mg/dL increment adds ~10% risk; Lp(a) acts multiplicatively on base CVD risk.
- **Evidence:** Risk stratification: <30 mg/dL = not increased; 30-50 mg/dL = moderate increase (borderline high); >50 mg/dL = significant increase (50% or greater risk elevation); dose-response: +20 mg/dL ≈ +10% higher risk, +40 mg/dL ≈ +20% higher risk; multiplicative effect: high Lp(a) with low base risk = small absolute increase; high Lp(a) with high base risk = significant contribution; clinical implication: Lp(a) level determines intensity of other risk factor management.
- **Reference:**  — cited as (Kamstrup et al., 2009)
- **Zettel:** [[Lpa_Risk_Levels_Dose_Response_Interpretation_Z-002-0814]]

### 3. Lp(a) Management — No Current Drugs, Trials Underway, Control Base Risk
- **Claim:** No currently available drugs specifically lower Lp(a), but multiple therapies in clinical trials (pelacarsen, olpasiran) can reduce Lp(a) up to 95%; until these are approved, management focuses on aggressive control of all other modifiable CVD risk factors.
- **Evidence:** Current treatment: no approved Lp(a)-lowering drugs; clinical trials: active development of antisense therapies (pelacarsen, olpasiran) achieving 80-95% Lp(a) reduction; outcome data: insufficient to prove Lp(a) reduction improves CVD outcomes (results expected in coming years); base risk control: aggressively manage hypertension, diabetes, body weight, insulin resistance, smoking, sdLDL, triglycerides, HDL, processed food consumption, sedentary lifestyle; rationale: Lp(a) is fixed genetic risk, but other factors are modifiable; reducing overall risk burden partially offsets Lp(a) contribution.
- **Reference:**  — cited as (Kamstrup et al., 2009; Lp(a) clinical trials literature)
