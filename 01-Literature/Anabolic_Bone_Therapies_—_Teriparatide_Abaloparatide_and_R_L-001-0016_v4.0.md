# Anabolic Bone Therapies — Teriparatide, Abaloparatide, and Romosozumab

## Source
- **ChatGPT conversation:** [[CGPT_0133_Osteopenia_Osteoporosis_Bone_Remodeling_CGPT-0133]]
- **Date:** March 07, 2025
- **Scope:** Pharmacological section on anabolic agents vs. antiresorptives

---

## Core Concepts Extracted

### Overview Distinction
> "Standard anti-osteoporotic medications can be classified into antiresorptive agents (e.g., bisphosphonates, selective estrogen receptor modulators [SERMs], denosumab) and anabolic agents (e.g., teriparatide, abaloparatide, romosozumab)."

**Key Difference:**
- **Antiresorptives:** Slow bone loss by reducing resorption
- **Anabolics:** Build bone by stimulating formation

---

### 1. Teriparatide (PTH 1-34)

**Zettel extracted:** [[Teriparatide_PTH_1-34_Intermittent_Anabolic_Therapy_Z-001-0105]]

**Mechanism:**
- Recombinant human parathyroid hormone (1-34 amino acid fragment)
- **Intermittent administration** (daily subcutaneous) stimulates osteoblasts
- Unlike continuous PTH elevation (causes resorption), pulses favor formation
- Activates Wnt signaling pathway in osteoblasts

**Clinical Use:**
- Severe osteoporosis (T-score < -3.5 with fractures)
- Glucocorticoid-induced osteoporosis
- Failed antiresorptive therapy
- **Duration limit:** 2 years (osteogenic sarcoma risk in rats at high doses)

**Effects:**
- Increases bone mineral density (spine > hip)
- Reduces vertebral and non-vertebral fractures
- Improves bone architecture (trabecular connectivity)

---

### 2. Abaloparatide (PTHrP Analog)

**Zettel extracted:** [[Abaloparatide_PTHrP_Analog_Anabolic_Agent_Z-001-0106]]

**Mechanism:**
- Synthetic analog of parathyroid hormone-related protein (PTHrP)
- Binds PTH1 receptor with different kinetics than PTH
- More transient signaling → potentially less bone resorption stimulation
- Daily subcutaneous injection

**Advantages over Teriparatide:**
- Similar BMD gains with potentially fewer hypercalcemia episodes
- Non-inferior fracture reduction
- Alternative for patients intolerant to teriparatide

**Duration:** Also limited to 2 years

---

### 3. Romosozumab (Anti-Sclerostin Antibody)

**Zettel extracted:** [[Romosozumab_Anti_Sclerostin_Dual_Action_Therapy_Z-001-0107]]

**Mechanism:**
- Humanized monoclonal antibody against sclerostin
- **Dual action:**
  1. **Increases formation:** Blocking sclerostin → Wnt pathway activation → osteoblastogenesis
  2. **Decreases resorption:** Sclerostin inhibition reduces RANKL expression by osteocytes
- Monthly subcutaneous injection

**Unique Position:**
- Only therapy with simultaneous anabolic + antiresorptive effects
- Rapid BMD gains (significant improvement at 12 months)
- Bone formation stimulated early, resorption suppressed later

**Clinical Considerations:**
- Cardiovascular safety questions (increased MI/stroke signal in one trial)
- Contraindicated in patients with recent MI or stroke
- Duration typically 12 months, followed by antiresorptive

---

### 4. Sequential Therapy Strategy

**Zettel extracted:** [[Sequential_Therapy_Anabolic_to_Antiresorptive_Strategy_Z-001-0108]]

**Rationale:**
- Anabolic agents build bone but limited duration
- Antiresorptives preserve gains but don't create new bone
- **Sequence:** Anabolic first → Antiresorptive second

**Evidence:**
- Teriparatide followed by alendronate superior to either alone
- Romosozumab followed by denosumab maintains and extends gains
- Prevents rapid bone loss after anabolic discontinuation

**Protocol:**
1. **Phase 1 (12–24 months):** Anabolic agent (teriparatide, abaloparatide, or romosozumab)
2. **Phase 2 (ongoing):** Antiresorptive (bisphosphonate or denosumab)

## Clinical Indications for Anabolic Therapy

### High-Risk Candidates
- Multiple vertebral fractures
- T-score < -3.5 with fracture history
- Failure of antiresorptive therapy (fracture on bisphosphonate)
- Glucocorticoid-induced osteoporosis with high fracture risk

### When to Choose Specific Agents
- **Teriparatide:** Established track record, longest experience
- **Abaloparatide:** If hypercalcemia concern with TPTD
- **Romosozumab:** Most rapid BMD gain, severe osteoporosis, but CV risk assessment needed

## Limitations and Risks

### Duration Limits
- PTH-based therapies: 2-year lifetime maximum (theoretical osteosarcoma risk)
- Romosozumab: 12 months typically (CV safety signal)

### Cost Considerations
- All anabolic agents expensive (>$1,000/month)
- Prior authorization typically required
- Sequential therapy doubles duration of expensive treatment

### Administration Burden
- Daily injections (teriparatide, abaloparatide) vs. monthly (romosozumab)
- Patient education on injection technique
- Refrigeration requirements
