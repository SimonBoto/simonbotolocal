# Lit: Coffee Reduces Dementia Risk — 37-Year Study

## Source
- **Full citation:** Zhang Y, Liu Y, Li Y, et al. "Coffee and tea intake, dementia risk, and cognitive function." *JAMA*. 2026 Feb 9.
- **Original Greek:** "Ο καφές μειώνει τον κίνδυνο άνοιας"
- **Inbox origin:** [[INBOX-Article-41-Coffee-Dementia]]
- **Reference note:** [[Zhang_2026_Coffee_Tea_Dementia_R-001-0094]]

## Core Claim
**Higher caffeinated coffee consumption is associated with significantly lower dementia risk** — a 37-year study of 132,000 participants found optimal protection at 2-3 cups/day of coffee or 1-2 cups/day of tea, with caffeine appearing to be the key neuroprotective component (decaffeinated beverages showed no benefit).

## Zettels Extracted

### 1. Coffee 2-3 Cups/Day Provides Optimal Dementia Protection
- **Claim:** Moderate coffee consumption of 2-3 cups per day provides optimal protection against dementia — the 37-year study found this dose range associated with the lowest dementia risk, with both lower and higher intakes showing less benefit.
- **Evidence:** Study: 132,000 participants, 37-year follow-up, 11,000 dementia cases; dose-response: none = baseline risk, 1 cup/day = some benefit, 2-3 cups/day = optimal protection, >4 cups/day = benefit may plateau; tea also protective at 1-2 cups/day; consistency across long follow-up suggests robust association; clinical implication: moderate coffee consumption can be recommended as part of dementia prevention strategy.
- **Reference:** [[Zhang_2026_Coffee_Tea_Dementia_R-001-0094]] — cited as (Zhang et al., 2026)
- **Zettel:** [[Coffee_2-3_Cups_Dementia_Protection_Z-002-0816]]

### 2. Caffeine Is Key Neuroprotective Component — Decaf Shows No Benefit
- **Claim:** Caffeine appears to be the key neuroprotective component in coffee and tea — the study found protective effects only with caffeinated beverages, with decaffeinated coffee and tea showing no dementia risk reduction, indicating caffeine's specific role in brain protection.
- **Evidence:** Caffeinated coffee: ✅ significantly lower dementia risk; caffeinated tea: ✅ significantly lower dementia risk; decaffeinated coffee: ❌ no benefit observed; decaffeinated tea: ❌ no benefit observed; specificity suggests caffeine as active component; hypothesized mechanisms: adenosine receptor antagonism (neuroprotection), antioxidant effects (reduced oxidative stress), improved insulin sensitivity (metabolic brain health), reduced neuroinflammation; clinical implication: caffeine-containing beverages specifically recommended, not decaf alternatives.
- **Reference:** [[Zhang_2026_Coffee_Tea_Dementia_R-001-0094]] — cited as (Zhang et al., 2026)
- **Zettel:** [[Caffeine_Key_Neuroprotective_Component_Z-002-0817]]

### 3. Long-Term Coffee Consumption Supports Brain Health Across Decades
- **Claim:** Long-term coffee consumption over decades supports brain health and reduces dementia risk — the 37-year follow-up demonstrates that coffee's neuroprotective effects are sustained over long periods, making it a viable lifelong prevention strategy.
- **Evidence:** 37-year follow-up is exceptionally long for nutritional epidemiology; 132,000 participants provides robust statistical power; 11,000 dementia cases allows reliable risk estimation; consistency of benefit across decades suggests true protective effect rather than confounding; both coffee and tea show similar patterns (caffeine-containing beverages); practical implication: starting moderate coffee consumption in middle age may provide protection that persists into older age when dementia risk is highest; population impact: given high coffee consumption globally, even modest individual risk reduction translates to significant public health benefit.
- **Reference:** [[Zhang_2026_Coffee_Tea_Dementia_R-001-0094]] — cited as (Zhang et al., 2026)
