# Lit: Not All Calories Are Equal

## Source
- **Full citation:** Ebbeling CB, Feldman HA, Klein GL, Wong JMW, Bielak L, Steltz SK, et al. Effects of a low carbohydrate diet on energy expenditure during weight loss maintenance: randomized trial. BMJ. 2018;363:k4583. doi:10.1136/bmj.k4583
- **Inbox origin:** [[INBOX-Article-29-Calories]]
- **Reference note:** [[Calories_Not_Equal_Metabolic_R-002-0050]]

## Core Claim
**Calories are not metabolically equivalent** — the Framingham feeding study (Ebbeling et al., BMJ 2018) demonstrated that isocaloric diets with different carbohydrate content produce different energy expenditures. Low-carbohydrate diets (20% carbs) increased total energy expenditure by 278 kcal/day compared to high-carbohydrate diets (60% carbs), while thermic effect of food varies by macronutrient (protein 20-30%, carbs 5-10%, fats 0-3%).

## Zettels Extracted

### 1. Calories Are Not Equal — Metabolic and Hormonal Effects Differ
- **Claim:** Calories are not metabolically equivalent because different macronutrients have different thermic effects, hormonal impacts, and metabolic consequences; the "calories in, calories out" model fails to account for these differences, making 100 calories of protein behave differently than 100 calories of carbohydrate or fat.
- **Evidence:** Thermic effect of food (TEF) is 20-30% for protein, 5-10% for carbohydrates, and 0-3% for fats; hormonal responses differ dramatically (carbohydrates spike insulin, protein increases both insulin and glucagon, fats have minimal insulin effect); metabolic adaptation varies by diet composition with low-carb and high-protein diets preserving metabolic rate better.
- **Reference:** [[Calories_Not_Equal_Metabolic_R-002-0050]] — cited as (Ebbeling et al., BMJ 2018; TEF studies)
- **Zettel:** [[Calories_Not_Equal_Metabolic_Effect_Z-002-0768]]

### 2. Low-Carbohydrate Diets Increase Energy Expenditure — Metabolic Advantage
- **Claim:** Low-carbohydrate diets increase total energy expenditure compared to higher-carbohydrate diets with identical calorie content — the Framingham feeding study demonstrated a 278 kcal/day metabolic advantage for low-carb (20% carbs) vs high-carb (60% carbs) diets, suggesting carbohydrate restriction enhances metabolic rate through reduced insulin, increased gluconeogenesis, and ketone metabolism.
- **Evidence:** Randomized controlled trial of 164 overweight adults over 20 weeks showed energy expenditure increases of +91 kcal/day on moderate-carb (40%) and +278 kcal/day on low-carb (20%) compared to high-carb (60%), with statistical significance P<0.001; mechanisms include reduced insulin-mediated fat storage, energy-costly gluconeogenesis, inefficient ketone metabolism, and better preservation of thyroid hormone T3.
- **Reference:** [[Calories_Not_Equal_Metabolic_R-002-0050]] — cited as (Ebbeling CB, et al. BMJ 2018)
- **Zettel:** [[Low_Carb_Increases_Energy_Expenditure_Z-002-0769]]

### 3. Thermic Effect of Food — Macronutrients Differ in Processing Energy Cost
- **Claim:** The thermic effect of food (TEF) varies dramatically by macronutrient — protein requires 20-30% of its calories for digestion and processing, carbohydrates 5-10%, and fats 0-3%, meaning 100 calories of protein provides only 70-80 net calories while 100 calories of fat provides 97-100 net calories.
- **Evidence:** Protein has the highest TEF due to complex peptide bond breakdown, active transport absorption, and energy-costly nitrogen removal via the urea cycle; fat has the lowest TEF because of efficient digestion (lipase action), passive absorption, and direct storage capability; this creates an automatic 100-150 calorie difference between high-protein and low-protein diets of equal stated calories.
- **Reference:** [[Calories_Not_Equal_Metabolic_R-002-0050]] — cited as (TEF metabolic research)
- **Zettel:** [[Thermic_Effect_Macronutrients_Differ_Z-002-0770]]
