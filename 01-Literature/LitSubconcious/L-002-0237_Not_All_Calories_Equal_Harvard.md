# Lit: Not All Calories Are Equal — Harvard Study

## Source
- **Full citation:** Ebbeling CB, Feldman HA, Klein GL, Wong JMW, Bielak L, Steltz SK, et al. "Effects of a low carbohydrate diet on energy expenditure during weight loss maintenance: randomized trial." *BMJ*. 2018;363:k4583. doi:10.1136/bmj.k4583
- **Original Greek:** "Γιατί Δεν Μετράνε Όλες οι Θερμίδες το Ίδιο"
- **Inbox origin:** [[INBOX-Article-50-Not-All-Calories]]
- **Reference note:** [[Ebbeling_2018_Low_Carb_Energy_Expenditure_R-002-0050]]
- **Study:** Harvard Medical School researchers
- **Participants:** 164 adults who had already lost weight

## Core Claim
**When carbohydrates are reduced, the body burns more calories — a calorie is not just a calorie** — the Harvard study demonstrates that low-carb diets (20% carbs) increase total energy expenditure compared to high-carb diets (60% carbs), with metabolic advantages including reduced insulin signaling, increased fat oxidation, and better weight loss maintenance through preserved satiety and reduced metabolic slowdown.

## Zettels Extracted

### 1. Calorie Source Affects Metabolism — Not Just Quantity
- **Claim:** The source of calories affects metabolism, not just quantity — when carbohydrates are reduced, the body burns more calories, demonstrating that "a calorie is a calorie" is an oversimplification and macronutrient composition changes energy expenditure.
- **Evidence:** Old thinking vs new evidence: "Calories are calories" → source affects metabolism, "Only quantity matters" → quality changes energy burn, "Weight loss = calories in/out" → metabolic adaptation varies; study design: 164 adults who had already lost weight, 20 weeks duration, 3 groups (high/moderate/low carb), measurement = total energy expenditure; key finding: when carbohydrates are reduced, the body burns more calories; quote: "It is not only how many calories we eat that matters, but also where they come from. Diets with fewer carbohydrates help the body burn more energy, while diets with many carbohydrates make metabolism slower"; clinical implication: weight loss strategies must consider macronutrient composition, not just calorie counting; metabolic adaptation varies by diet type.
- **Reference:** [[Ebbeling_2018_Low_Carb_Energy_Expenditure_R-002-0050]] — cited as (Ebbeling et al., BMJ 2018)
- **Zettel:** [[Calorie_Source_Affects_Metabolism_Not_Just_Quantity_Z-002-0849]]

### 2. Low-Carb Increases Energy Expenditure — Harvard 164-Participant Study
- **Claim:** Low-carbohydrate diets increase total energy expenditure compared to high-carb diets — in the Harvard study, participants on low-carb (20% carbs) burned more calories than those on high-carb (60% carbs), with moderate-carb (40%) showing intermediate effects.
- **Evidence:** Diet comparison: high-carb (60% carbs) = slower metabolism, moderate-carb (40% carbs) = intermediate metabolism, low-carb (20% carbs) = faster metabolism; study details: 164 adults post-weight loss, 20-week trial, randomized to 3 carb levels, total energy expenditure measured; metabolic advantage of low-carb: reduced insulin → less fat storage signaling, increased fat oxidation → more fat burned for fuel, higher thermic effect → more energy expended, preserved muscle → better body composition; weight loss maintenance advantages: metabolic slowdown less pronounced on low-carb, hunger better controlled (improved satiety), weight regain tendency reduced; clinical implication: low-carb may be superior for weight maintenance after initial loss; metabolic advantage persists even after weight loss.
- **Reference:** [[Ebbeling_2018_Low_Carb_Energy_Expenditure_R-002-0050]] — cited as (Ebbeling et al., BMJ 2018)
- **Zettel:** [[Low_Carb_Increases_Energy_Expenditure_Harvard_Study_Z-002-0850]]

### 3. Metabolic Advantage of Carbohydrate Restriction for Weight Loss
- **Claim:** Carbohydrate restriction provides a metabolic advantage for weight loss and maintenance through multiple mechanisms — reduced insulin signaling decreases fat storage, increased fat oxidation promotes fat burning, higher thermic effect increases energy expenditure, and preserved muscle mass improves body composition and metabolic rate.
- **Evidence:** Mechanisms of metabolic advantage: reduced insulin → less fat storage signaling, increased fat oxidation → more fat burned for fuel, higher thermic effect → more energy expended, preserved muscle → better body composition; weight loss maintenance benefits: metabolic slowdown less pronounced (protects against adaptive thermogenesis), hunger better controlled (protein and fat improve satiety), weight regain tendency reduced; clinical applications: weight loss — consider carb restriction for metabolic advantage, weight maintenance — low-carb may be superior post-weight loss, metabolic health — carb quality and quantity both matter; key insight: "calories in, calories out" model incomplete; metabolic effects of macronutrients must be considered; low-carb creates favorable metabolic environment for fat loss; practical implication: for weight loss maintenance, low-carb approach may prevent metabolic adaptation that leads to regain.
- **Reference:** [[Ebbeling_2018_Low_Carb_Energy_Expenditure_R-002-0050]] — cited as (Ebbeling et al., BMJ 2018)
