# Lit: Tobacco and Sugar Industry Tactics

## Source
- **Full citation:** Kearns CE, Schmidt LA, Glantz SA. "Sugar Industry and Coronary Heart Disease Research: A Historical Analysis of Internal Industry Documents." *JAMA Intern Med*. 2016;176(11):1680-1685. doi:10.1001/jamainternmed.2016.5394. PMID: 27630110.
- **Inbox origin:** [[INBOX-Article-23-Industry-Tactics]]
- **Reference note:** [[Kearns_2016_Sugar_Industry_Documents_R-002-0044]]

## Core Concepts Extracted
The sugar industry employed **the same tactics as the tobacco industry** — hiding evidence, funding favorable research, attacking scientists, and delaying regulation — to shift blame from sugar to saturated fat for heart disease, corrupting nutrition science and policy for over 50 years.

---

## Zettels Extracted

### 1. Industry Playbook: Hide Evidence, Fund Doubt, Delay
- **Claim:** Industries facing health threats follow a predictable playbook: (1) hide internal evidence of harm, (2) fund research that casts doubt on risks, (3) attack independent scientists, and (4) lobby to delay regulation — tactics used by both tobacco and sugar industries successfully for decades.
- **Evidence:** UCSF Sugar Papers reveal sugar industry paid Harvard scientists $50,000+ (1960s dollars) to publish 1967 NEJM review blaming fat/cholesterol for CHD while exonerating sugar; internal memos show industry knew of sugar-CHD link but actively suppressed it.
- **Reference:** [[Kearns_2016_Sugar_Industry_Documents_R-002-0044]] — cited as (Kearns et al., 2016)
- **Zettel:** [[Industry_Playbook_Hide_Evidence_Delay_Z-002-0750]]

### 2. Tobacco and Sugar Used Same Tactics
- **Claim:** The sugar industry explicitly copied the tobacco industry's successful strategy of manipulating science and public opinion to protect profits — both industries knew their products caused disease, hid this knowledge, and spent decades creating doubt about the health risks.
- **Evidence:** Document analysis shows sugar industry hired tobacco industry consultants, used similar language in internal memos about "creating doubt," and followed the same pattern of funding favorable research while attacking unfavorable studies; both succeeded in delaying public health action by 40-50 years.
- **Reference:** [[Kearns_2016_Sugar_Industry_Documents_R-002-0044]] — cited as (Kearns et al., 2016)
- **Zettel:** [[Tobacco_Sugar_Same_Tactics_Public_Health_Z-002-0751]]

### 3. Regulatory Capture and Industry Influence
- **Claim:** Food and tobacco industries have successfully captured regulatory and policy processes through lobbying, revolving doors between industry and government, and funding research that supports industry positions — resulting in weaker regulations than scientific evidence supports.
- **Evidence:** Sugar industry influenced National Caries Program to avoid sugar restrictions, shaped FDA evaluations of sugar safety, and continues to lobby against sugar taxes and warning labels; regulatory agencies prioritize industry interests over public health due to capture.
- **Reference:** [[Kearns_2016_Sugar_Industry_Documents_R-002-0044]] — cited as (Kearns et al., 2016)
- **Zettel:** [[Regulatory_Capture_Industry_Influence_Z-002-0752]]
