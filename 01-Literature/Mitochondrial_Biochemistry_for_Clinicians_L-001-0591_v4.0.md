# Mitochondrial Biochemistry for Clinicians

## Core Concepts Extracted

### 1. Krebs Cycle Clinical Biochemistry

**Category:** Energy Metabolism

**Key Process:** 
```
Acetyl-CoA + 3NAD⁺ + FAD + GDP + Pi + 2H₂O → 2CO₂ + 3NADH + FADH₂ + GTP + 2H⁺ + CoA-SH
```

**Mechanism/Steps:**
1. Acetyl-CoA condenses with oxaloacetate to form citrate
2. Citrate is isomerized to isocitrate
3. Isocitrate undergoes oxidative decarboxylation to α-ketoglutarate
4. α-Ketoglutarate undergoes oxidative decarboxylation to succinyl-CoA
5. Succinyl-CoA is converted to succinate with GTP production
6. Succinate is oxidized to fumarate with FAD reduction
7. Fumarate is hydrated to malate
8. Malate is oxidized to oxaloacetate with NAD⁺ reduction

**Purpose/Significance:** Provides the fundamental biochemical framework for understanding mitochondrial energy production and its clinical implications in metabolic disorders

**Zettel:** [[Krebs_Cycle_Clinical_Biochemistry_Z-001-1048]]

### 2. TCA Intermediates Clinical Significance

**Category:** Metabolic Biochemistry

**Key Process:** 
```
Citrate → Isocitrate → α-Ketoglutarate → Succinyl-CoA → Succinate → Fumarate → Malate → Oxaloacetate
```

**Mechanism/Steps:**
1. Citrate regulates glycolysis and fatty acid synthesis
2. Isocitrate dehydrogenase is rate-limiting and sensitive to NAD⁺/NADH ratio
3. α-Ketoglutarate is a key amino acid metabolite and regulator of hypoxia response
4. Succinyl-CoA links TCA cycle to heme synthesis
5. Succinate accumulation inhibits prolyl hydroxylase and stabilizes HIF-1α
6. Fumarate accumulation inhibits DNA repair enzymes in fumarate hydratase deficiency
7. Malate shuttle transports reducing equivalents across mitochondrial membrane
8. Oxaloacetate serves as precursor for gluconeogenesis and aspartate synthesis

**Purpose/Significance:** Enables clinical interpretation of organic acid tests and understanding of metabolic bottlenecks in mitochondrial disorders

**Zettel:** [[TCA_Intermediates_Clinical_Significance_Z-001-1049]]

### 3. Oxidative Phosphorylation Clinical Mechanisms

**Category:** Energy Metabolism

**Key Process:** 
```
NADH → Complex I → CoQ → Complex III → Cyt c → Complex IV → O₂
FADH₂ → Complex II → CoQ → Complex III → Cyt c → Complex IV → O₂
Complex V: ADP + Pi + energy → ATP
```

**Mechanism/Steps:**
1. Complex I transfers electrons from NADH to CoQ, pumping 4H⁺
2. Complex II transfers electrons from FADH₂ to CoQ without proton pumping
3. Complex III transfers electrons from CoQH₂ to cytochrome c, pumping 4H⁺
4. Complex IV transfers electrons from cytochrome c to O₂, pumping 2H⁺
5. Complex V (ATP synthase) uses proton gradient to drive ATP synthesis
6. Oxygen serves as the final electron acceptor
7. Membrane potential maintains proton gradient across inner mitochondrial membrane

**Purpose/Significance:** Provides the mechanistic understanding needed for interpreting mitochondrial electron transport defects and oxidative phosphorylation disorders

**Zettel:** [[Oxidative_Phosphorylation_Clinical_Mechanisms_Z-001-1050]]

### 4. Redox Cofactors NAD FAD Biochemistry

**Category:** Biochemical Cofactors

**Key Process:** 
```
NAD⁺ + 2H⁺ + 2e⁻ ⇌ NADH + H⁺
FAD + 2H⁺ + 2e⁻ ⇌ FADH₂
cell
```

**Mechanism/Steps:**
1. NAD⁺/NADH cycle serves as primary electron carrier in catabolic reactions
2. FAD/FADH₂ cycle serves as electron carrier in fatty acid oxidation and TCA cycle
3. Both cofactors are derived from B-vitamins (niacin, riboflavin)
4. B-vitamin deficiencies impair redox reactions and metabolic flux
5. NAD⁺/NADH ratio reflects cellular redox state and energy status
6. FAD/FADH₂ ratio influences electron transport efficiency

**Purpose/Significance:** Enables understanding of cofactor dependencies in metabolic pathways and clinical interpretation of B-vitamin deficiency patterns

**Zettel:** [[Redox_Cofactors_NAD_FAD_Biochemistry_Z-001-1051]]

### 5. Cofactor Deficiency OAT Signatures

**Category:** Clinical Diagnostics

**Key Process:** 
```
Pyruvate → (B1) → Acetyl-CoA
Methylmalonate → (B12) → Succinyl-CoA
FIGLU → (folate) → Tetrahydrofolate
```

**Mechanism/Steps:**
1. Pyruvate accumulation indicates thiamine (B1) deficiency affecting pyruvate dehydrogenase
2. Methylmalonate accumulation indicates cobalamin (B12) deficiency affecting methylmalonyl-CoA mutase
3. FIGLU (formiminoglutamic acid) accumulation indicates folate deficiency affecting formiminotransferase
4. These patterns help differentiate between B-vitamin deficiencies in organic acid testing
5. Cofactor deficiencies create metabolic bottlenecks detectable in OAT
6. Pattern recognition enables targeted nutritional interventions

**Purpose/Significance:** Provides practical clinical framework for interpreting organic acid test patterns and guiding targeted nutritional therapy

**Zettel:** [[Cofactor_Deficiency_OAT_Signatures_Z-001-1052]]

### 6. Aconitase Oxidative Stress Sensitivity

**Category:** Oxidative Stress Response

**Key Process:** 
```
Citrate ⇌ cis-Aconitate ⇌ Isocitrate
Aconitase: [4Fe-4S] cluster → Fe²⁺ + H₂O₂ → [3Fe-4S] + OH⁻ + Fe³⁺
```

**Mechanism/Steps:**
1. Aconitase catalyzes citrate to isocitrate conversion
2. Contains iron-sulfur [4Fe-4S] cluster essential for activity
3. Oxidative stress (H₂O₂) causes cluster disassembly and enzyme inactivation
4. Inactivated aconitase releases iron, contributing to Fenton reaction
5. Citrate accumulation and isocitrate deficiency indicate oxidative stress
6. Aconitase activity serves as sensitive biomarker of mitochondrial oxidative damage

**Purpose/Significance:** Provides a mechanistic link between oxidative stress and mitochondrial dysfunction, with clinical implications for detecting early metabolic damage

**Zettel:** [[Aconitase_Oxidative_Stress_Sensitivity_Z-001-1053]]
