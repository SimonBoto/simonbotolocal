# Lit: 5 Modifiable Risk Factors Add 14.5 Years of Life — NEJM 2025

## Source
- **Full citation:** Global Cardiovascular Risk Consortium. "Global effect of cardiovascular risk factors on lifetime estimates." *N Engl J Med*. 2025;392(15):e47. doi:10.1056/NEJMoa2415879
- **Original Greek:** "5 Παράγοντες Κινδύνου που Μπορούν να Τροποποιηθούν και Συνδέονται με έως 14,5 Επιπλέον Χρόνια Ζωής"
- **Inbox origin:** [[INBOX-Article-45-Five-Risk-Factors]]
- **Reference note:** [[NEJM_2025_Global_CV_Risk_Lifetime_Estimates_R-002-0037]]
- **Study scale:** 2 million people, 39 countries

## Core Claim
**Five modifiable cardiovascular risk factors — high blood pressure, cholesterol, abnormal body weight, diabetes, and smoking — collectively account for up to 14.5 extra years of life for women and 12 years for men when optimally controlled** — the NEJM Global Cardiovascular Risk Consortium study demonstrates that smoking cessation provides the greatest longevity benefit, followed by hypertension control, while cholesterol and weight have less independent impact when other factors are controlled.

## Zettels Extracted

### 1. Five Modifiable Risk Factors Add ~14 Extra Years for Women, ~12 for Men
- **Claim:** Individuals without any of the five major cardiovascular risk factors (high blood pressure, elevated cholesterol, abnormal body weight, diabetes, smoking) live approximately 14 extra years for women and 12 extra years for men compared to those with all five factors — demonstrating the profound impact of modifiable risk factors on longevity.
- **Evidence:** NEJM 2025: 2 million people across 39 countries; five factors: (1) high blood pressure, (2) cholesterol, (3) abnormal body weight (very low or high), (4) diabetes, (5) smoking; longevity impact: women ~14 extra years, men ~12 extra years; healthy years: women 13.3 years, men 10.6 years; lifetime CVD risk with all 5 factors: women 25% (1 in 4), men 33% (1 in 3); all five factors are modifiable through lifestyle and medical intervention; population impact: widespread control of these factors would dramatically increase global longevity.
- **Reference:** [[NEJM_2025_Global_CV_Risk_Lifetime_Estimates_R-002-0037]] — cited as (Global Cardiovascular Risk Consortium, NEJM 2025)
- **Zettel:** [[Five_Risk_Factors_14_Extra_Years_Women_Men_Z-002-0828]]

### 2. Smoking Cessation and Hypertension Control Provide Greatest Longevity Benefits
- **Claim:** Among the five modifiable risk factors, smoking cessation provides the greatest longevity benefit, followed by hypertension control — these two factors have the largest individual impact on life extension, while diabetes, weight, and cholesterol have significant but lesser independent effects.
- **Evidence:** Hierarchy of impact: Rank 1 = smoking cessation (greatest benefit), Rank 2 = hypertension control (second greatest), Ranks 3-5 = diabetes, weight, cholesterol (significant but less); smoking: single most important modifiable risk factor for longevity; hypertension: silent killer, major vascular stress; modification pathways: hypertension → lifestyle, diet, micronutrient repletion; diabetes → carbohydrate restriction, weight loss; weight → dietary changes, metabolic optimization; smoking → cessation programs, metabolic support; clinical implication: prioritize smoking cessation and blood pressure control for maximum longevity gain.
- **Reference:** [[NEJM_2025_Global_CV_Risk_Lifetime_Estimates_R-002-0037]] — cited as (Global Cardiovascular Risk Consortium, NEJM 2025)
- **Zettel:** [[Smoking_Cessation_Hypertension_Greatest_Longevity_Benefit_Z-002-0829]]

### 3. Cholesterol and Weight Have Less Impact When Other Factors Are Controlled
- **Claim:** Cholesterol and body weight have less independent impact on longevity when the other three risk factors (smoking, hypertension, diabetes) are controlled — cholesterol shows a U-shaped relationship with risk (both very low and very high problematic), and weight effects are often confounded by accompanying diabetes and hypertension.
- **Evidence:** Cholesterol findings: no clear difference in longevity impact, both very low and very high associated with risk, U-shaped curve — optimal range matters, context-dependent: less important when other factors controlled; weight findings: no significant difference alone, often accompanied by diabetes and hypertension, confounded by other metabolic factors; key insight: "When the other four factors are controlled, cholesterol does not appear to have particular significance on its own"; clinical implication: prioritize smoking, hypertension, and diabetes control first; address cholesterol and weight within context of overall metabolic health; don't overtreat cholesterol in isolation.
- **Reference:** [[NEJM_2025_Global_CV_Risk_Lifetime_Estimates_R-002-0037]] — cited as (Global Cardiovascular Risk Consortium, NEJM 2025)
