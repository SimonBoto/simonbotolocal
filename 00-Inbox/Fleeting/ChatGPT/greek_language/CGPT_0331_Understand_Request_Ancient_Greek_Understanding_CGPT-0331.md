### Babylonian Cosmogony: "Enuma Elish"

The Babylonian creation epic "Enuma Elish," which dates back to the late second millennium BC, is named after its opening words, "When on high." It was recited during the Babylonian New Year's festival and played a key role in reinforcing the supremacy of Marduk, the patron god of Babylon, aligning cosmology with the city's political hierarchy.

- **Primordial Chaos**: Like the Greek narrative, "Enuma Elish" begins with primordial chaos, embodied by the mingling of fresh and salt waters represented by the gods Apsu (fresh water) and Tiamat (salt water/chaos monster).

### Comparative Insights

Both cosmogonies reflect a worldview where the universe emerges from a formless chaos through divine action and conflict, leading to a structured cosmos governed by a hierarchy of deities. The Greek version tends to focus more on the interrelations and personal motives of the gods, reflecting a complex narrative of inter-god politics and family dynamics. In contrast, the Babylonian story serves to reinforce political and religious authority through the elevation of Marduk, aligning cosmic order with societal order.

The introduction of humans in both myths also serves different functions: in Greek mythology, humans are often a byproduct or an afterthought of divine conflict, whereas in Babylonian narrative, their creation is directly linked to the maintenance of divine order and serving the gods.

Overall, these cosmogonies not only explain how the world came to be but also serve to justify and perpetuate the cultural and religious structures of their respective societies.
