### 2. **ChatGPT:**

Let’s clarify this carefully — because “baking ceramics” can mean very different things depending on what kind of **ceramic** you are referring to: clay, polymer, or glaze-finished material.  

We’ll go through each possibility systematically.

---

## 1. **Traditional (mineral) ceramics — clay, stoneware, porcelain**
These require **true vitrification** — the transformation of clay minerals into a glass-like, durable matrix through high heat (600–1300 °C depending on the type).

- **Domestic ovens** typically reach **250–300 °C**, which is **far below the sintering temperature**.  
- In such an oven, the clay will only **dry** (remove physical water), but not sinter or vitrify.  
  - You can get a fragile, chalky, porous result — sometimes used for prototypes, but not waterproof or mechanically stable.
  - If you add glaze and attempt to bake it, the glaze will never melt and bond.

**→ Conclusion:**  
A **classic kitchen oven cannot produce real ceramics.** It can only **air-dry or pre-dry** them before kiln firing.

**Safe process:**  
1. Let the clay **air-dry** for several days.  
2. Then put it in your oven at **100 °C** for 1–2 hours to drive out remaining moisture (prevent cracking in the kiln).  
3. But do **not exceed 250 °C** — it won’t help and can cause micro-fractures.

---

## 2. **Microwave baking**
Microwave ovens heat by **exciting water molecules**, not by uniform high-temperature conduction.  
Microwaving unfired clay is **risky** because:
- Uneven heating creates **internal steam pressure** → cracking or explosion.  
- Clay is often dense and wet inside, so steam cannot escape fast enough.  
- The maximum internal temperature is only around 200–300 °C — still far below vitrification.

However, you can **microwave-dry** small clay pieces (1–2 cm thick) if:
1. You start at **low power (30%)** for 1 minute.
2. Let them cool, repeat cycles gradually until dry.
3. This is only **for drying**, not true ceramic firing.

There are special **microwave kilns** (ceramic fiber domes with a refractory coating) that can reach **800–900 °C inside** even though the microwave’s air remains cool. These are used for small jewelry or test tiles — not structural ceramics.

---

## 3. **Polymer “ceramics” or clay substitutes**
If you mean **polymer clay** (e.g., Sculpey, Fimo), which hardens through polymer cross-linking, that’s a completely different story:

- These are **vinyl-based materials** cured at **110–130 °C for 15–30 min**.  
- A **domestic oven** works perfectly fine.  
- Never use a **microwave**, because polymer clay may burn or bubble from uneven heating.

---

## 4. **Improvised low-temperature craft ceramics**
If you want to make *ceramic-like crafts* (for decorative use only), you can:
- Use **air-dry clay** or **salt dough**.
- Bake gently in a **domestic oven at 100–120 °C** for a few hours.
- Then paint and seal with **acrylic varnish** for water resistance.
But these will not be true ceramics; they remain soluble and fragile under heat or water.
