### 1. **Simos:**

I have a patients with high calcium ALP pathological bone scan and normal PTH.

Is there any chance that is not Paget and is hyperparathyroidism

### 2. **ChatGPT:**

While the presentation of high calcium, elevated ALP (alkaline phosphatase), pathological findings on bone scans, and normal PTH might suggest **Paget's disease of bone**, the possibility of hyperparathyroidism cannot be ruled out entirely. Here's a detailed evaluation:

### Key Points:
1. **Normal PTH in Hyperparathyroidism**:
   - Primary hyperparathyroidism (PHPT) typically presents with elevated PTH and hypercalcemia. However, in some cases, PTH can be inappropriately normal (non-suppressed) in the presence of hypercalcemia, indicating PHPT.
   - This phenomenon can occur because the calcium feedback loop is disrupted in PHPT.

2. **Differentiating Paget's Disease**:
   - Paget's disease is primarily a disorder of bone remodeling and is often characterized by:
     - Elevated ALP (bone isoenzyme) due to increased bone turnover.
     - Focal lesions or abnormalities on bone scans (hot spots).
     - Normal calcium and PTH levels unless there's co-existing pathology, like immobilization or secondary hyperparathyroidism.
   - Hypercalcemia is **not typical** of isolated Paget's disease unless there is prolonged immobilization or malignancy-associated hypercalcemia.

3. **Imaging and Histological Considerations**:
   - **Paget's disease**:
     - Bone scans typically show areas of increased uptake in affected bones.
     - Radiographs reveal classic findings like cortical thickening, bowing, or "cotton wool" appearance in the skull.
   - **Hyperparathyroidism**:
     - Can cause generalized bone demineralization or subperiosteal bone resorption (e.g., on the radial aspect of the phalanges).
     - "Brown tumors" or cystic bone lesions may mimic Paget's findings on imaging.

4. **Calcium and ALP Elevation**:
   - Hypercalcemia combined with elevated ALP is highly suspicious of metabolic bone disease. PHPT could be a strong contender if PTH is inappropriately normal for the calcium level.

### Additional Testing to Differentiate:
1. **Calcium Profile**:
   - Ionized calcium is more reliable than total calcium. Confirm hypercalcemia with repeat tests, if needed.

2. **Bone-Specific ALP**:
   - Measure the bone isoenzyme of ALP to confirm whether the ALP elevation is bone-specific rather than liver-derived.

3. **Vitamin D Levels**:
   - Exclude severe vitamin D deficiency, which could mask PTH elevation or mimic hyperparathyroidism symptoms.

4. **Other Markers of Bone Turnover**:
   - Evaluate bone resorption markers (e.g., urinary N-telopeptide, serum C-telopeptide).
   - These are often elevated in both Paget's and hyperparathyroidism.

5. **Sestamibi Scan**:
   - If primary hyperparathyroidism is suspected, a parathyroid sestamibi scan or ultrasound can help identify an adenoma.

6. **Bone Biopsy**:
   - In rare or ambiguous cases, histological analysis may distinguish Paget’s disease from other metabolic bone disorders.

### Likely Scenarios:
- If **calcium is high and PTH is normal**, PHPT should be strongly considered unless imaging suggests otherwise.
- If calcium is normal, but ALP is elevated with focal bone scan abnormalities, Paget's disease is more likely.

### Final Recommendation:
Confirm the diagnosis with a combination of clinical assessment, imaging, and biochemical markers. Hyperparathyroidism remains possible, but the clinical context (localized vs. generalized bone involvement, degree of hypercalcemia, and PTH dynamics) is critical for differentiation.
