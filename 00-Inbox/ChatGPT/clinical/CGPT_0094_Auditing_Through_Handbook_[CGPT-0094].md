## 1. On the State of Man

**Context & Purpose:** This opening chapter establishes *why* addressing the human mind is urgent. Hubbard sketches humanity’s long journey and the discovery that our **mind** is both our greatest asset and potential Achilles’ heel【40†L239-L247】【40†L245-L253】. With civilization now in possession of destructive atomic technology, Hubbard argues, understanding and improving the mind is critical for our survival【40†L253-L258】. The section poses fundamental questions that the rest of the book will attempt to answer: *What is wrong with Man’s mind? What is the ideal state of being?*【40†L245-L253】【44†L334-L336】

> *“Man started his journey to present time some countless eons thence… grief and happiness he has progressed… into the master of the world and the lord of all kingdoms. **Key Points & Quotes:** Hubbard emphasizes that **Man’s success** as a species comes from his ability to **change and adapt** his thinking. *“He is successful because he can change.”*【44†L301-L309】 The human mind evolved over millennia as a problem-solving tool, yet it perplexes Man because it can become impaired (by “privation, injury or demons,” in Hubbard’s words【40†L239-L247】). **Clinical/Personal Utility:** *The State of Man* section serves to motivate the reader. It frames personal development not just as self-improvement, but as part of humanity’s survival. ## Act I: Sympathy Facsimiles and Self‑Determinism  
**Context & Title:** The first act (“Sympathy”) addresses the mechanism by which people unconsciously use illness or trouble to attract sympathy from others. Hubbard introduces the term **“service facsimile,”** meaning a mental image (facsimile) one holds onto to gain sympathy or justify failures【55†L15-L23】. The *purpose* of Act I is explicitly *“to pick up sympathy facsimiles.”* In other words, the practitioner examines incidents in life where they decided (however subconsciously) to be ill or unfortunate to solicit sympathy【55†L31-L38】. This ties directly into **life continuum**: taking on others’ burdens or ailments out of sympathy. 

**Key Quote:** *“The purpose of this act is to pick up sympathy facsimiles.”* Additionally, *“[the worst case] wants auditing only for the auditor’s sympathy and wants to retain the **service facsimile** because it is a sympathy source.”*【55†L15-L23】【55†L31-L38】

**Definitions & Relevance:** A **facsimile** in Dianetics is a mental recording of an experience, including pain and emotion. A *service facsimile* is a special kind of facsimile one “uses” to explain failures or gain compassion【55†L15-L23】. Clinically, this concept highlights psychosomatic illnesses: for example, Hubbard notes that *“nobody ever became ill without wanting to be ill at some earlier moment… She felt sorry for [a friend with polio] and so decided to get it herself.”* (paraphrased from text). In Act I, the preclear scans through every time they **decided to be sick** or found being ill advantageous (such as to skip school)【55†L43-L50】. By **consciously reviewing** and “desensitizing” those incidents, the individual can release the hidden decision to be an effect (suffering) and reclaim self-determinism. This has clear clinical relevance: it targets psychosomatic or “Nocebo” effects where one’s own mind contributes to illness. It’s essentially early **cognitive-behavioral insight**: recognizing one’s role (authorship) in one’s ailments.

**Effect on Tone, Perception, Authorship, Survival:** Playing sick for sympathy is considered a low-tone (apathy or grief-range) behavior on the **Tone Scale**. Indeed, *“one is peculiarly prone to sympathy bids around his family”* and will hold onto illness as an *“explanation of failure and a bid for sympathy.”* This keeps the person’s emotional **tone** depressed and perception skewed toward victimhood【55†L15-L23】【55†L31-L38】. Act I flips the script by making the person **author** of those incidents: by acknowledging *“when you made a decision, it is MADE”* (Hubbard emphasizes personal agency【55†L35-L38】), the individual stops passively “being the effect.” This improves survival potential – no longer needing illness as a crutch – and starts raising their tone (greater confidence instead of self-pity). It prepares the mind for deeper **engramic processing** by removing the “need” to hold onto certain painful memories. In Hubbard’s view, *self-determinism* is rehabilitated here: *“we are all self-determined, natively. NOTHING we do is beyond self-determined action.”*【55†L35-L38】 Act I makes that real by uncovering self-imposed illnesses and dissolving them.

**Use Cases:** Practically, Act I’s process of scanning decisions to be ill or upset can increase one’s **self-awareness**. For self-improvement, an individual might realize how often they’ve used minor ailments or emotional upsets to avoid responsibility or to get attention, and can consciously stop that pattern. For **spiritual advancement**, this act is said to free up life-force (Theta) that was tied in sympathetic suffering, allowing the person to operate with more genuine intention rather than covert cries for help. In a therapeutic or metaphysical context, running these sympathy facsimiles can purportedly alleviate psychosomatic conditions – Hubbard claimed even serious chronic ailments could improve once the underlying “sympathy decision” is discharged. For example, a chronic headache “held” to get a loved one’s care might vanish once the need for sympathy is processed. It’s akin to modern notions of secondary gain in illness, tackled head-on.

**Philosophical Insights:** Act I suggests a provocative philosophy: that **suffering is (at least partly) by choice**. This raises the idea of radical responsibility for one’s condition. It reframes the human tendency to seek pity as an error in self-determinism – implying our *“native state”* is to be cause, not effect. The **mind-body connection** is highlighted: a mental decision can produce very real physical illness, demonstrating how tightly intertwined thought and survival are. On a humanistic level, it implies compassion (sympathy) can inadvertently **entrap** both giver and receiver in a lower tone – a nuanced view of empathy’s downside. Hubbard is essentially cautioning that indulging in or soliciting pity undermines one’s autonomy and vitality. This aligns with stoic or Buddhist ideas that clinging to suffering (or others’ suffering) is an attachment that must be released.

**Red Flags & Contradictions:** A critical view would note that Hubbard’s claims here verge on **victim-blaming** if misapplied – telling someone their serious illness or misfortune is because they *decided* it, even subconsciously, is speculative and can be harmful. While mild psychosomatic links are plausible (stress causing illness etc.), Hubbard’s blanket statement that *“nobody ever became ill without wanting to”* is a **sweeping generalization【55†L43-L50】**. It lacks scientific evidence, especially for diseases like polio in his example. This approach could make someone feel guilty for their illness, which is ironic given later acts deal with guilt complexes. There’s an internal tension: Hubbard emphasizes self-determinism, yet someone in the throes of illness might not feel they “determined” it at all. Also, the concept of service facsimiles as a catch-all for psychosomatic illness is **speculative** – it posits a single mechanism (seeking sympathy) for a broad range of conditions, oversimplifying medical realities. Nonetheless, within the *Handbook*, Act I is foundational: it sets the tone that the preclear **must confront their own role** in their suffering, an idea both empowering and potentially troubling.

## Act II: ARC Triangle and Chart of Attitudes (Act II)  
**Context & Title:** The second act introduces **Affinity, Reality, and Communication (ARC)** – a triadic model that Hubbard calls *“the anatomy of the static of Life,”* meaning the fundamental qualities of a living being’s interactions【59†L20-L28】. Act II is largely theoretical and observational: the preclear studies the **Hubbard Chart of Attitudes** and assesses the people in their life against it. This act bridges Dianetics into Scientology proper by shifting focus from just engrams to the living dynamics of relationships and agreements. It provides *context* for emotional tone levels and relationships. The preclear is asked to list their family members, friends, etc., estimate each person’s tone level, and note their attitudes across various life aspects on the Chart of Attitudes【65†L1-L9】【65†L17-L25】. This external observation builds the ability to establish ARC (affinity, reality, communication) with others as well as objectify elements of their own mindset.

**Key Quotes:** *“The anatomy of the static of Life demonstrates it to have three interdependent characteristics. Each one of these three is as important as the other two. They are Reality, Affinity and Communication.”*【59†L20-L28】 In defining these: *“Reality…could be said to be agreement above 2.0 on the tone scale,”* i.e. a shared perception of what is real【60†L44-L52】. *“Affinity is love above 2.0…meaning the feeling of love or brotherhood,”* a degree of liking or cohesion【60†L50-L58】. Communication is described as the exchange of impulses or perceptions – *“When one talks to another…Life to Life via the physical universe”*【60†L59-L67】. Crucially, *“one cannot have affinity without agreement…communication without affinity,”* illustrating their interdependence【61†L33-L41】.

**Definitions & Clarity:**  
- **Affinity:** an emotional closeness or *“cohesion.”* High affinity would be warmth, love, or *“affectionate cheerfulness”* at tone 4.0, whereas low affinity is hostility or apathy【61†L68-L75】【61†L78-L87】. In clinical terms, affinity reflects the degree of positive feeling one has toward life or others.  
- **Reality:** consensus and *agreement on what is real*. Above the critical tone 2.0 (the point Hubbard marks between negative and positive attitudes), reality is solid and shared – sane people agree on the physical world【59†L33-L41】【60†L44-L52】. Below 2.0, reality warps (e.g. hallucinations, delusions). In therapy, this highlights aligning the preclear’s perceptions with observable fact (grounding them).  
- **Communication:** the exchange of ideas or perceptions, via any of the senses or directly. Hubbard frames even perception as a form of communication (e.g. seeing a rock = communicating with it by sight)【60†L59-L67】. Good communication is free and constructive at high tone, whereas at low tone it becomes lies, nagging, or complete withdrawal【61†L73-L81】. This concept is applied clinically by encouraging the preclear to communicate more openly and honestly (first by writing down observations on the chart). 

Act II uses the **Chart of Attitudes** (a detailed table of emotional “attitudes” from 40.0 down to 0.0 across various categories of life) as a tool. The preclear rates other people on this chart, which serves two purposes: (1) it externalizes their understanding of **tone levels** – they learn to *observe* tone in others without immediate judgment, and (2) it subtly sets a benchmark for themselves (often one realizes *“I am not at the top either”*【65†L19-L27】). This act is largely educational and diagnostic rather than a processing exercise per se.
