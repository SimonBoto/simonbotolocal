Primary hyperoxaluria is a group of rare inherited disorders characterized by the excessive production of oxalate, a substance that the body typically excretes in urine as a waste product. When too much oxalate is produced, it can accumulate and form crystals, leading to kidney stones and, in more severe cases, kidney damage or failure. The disorder is caused by mutations in specific genes that are responsible for oxalate production, resulting in enzymatic deficiencies that lead to its overproduction.

There are three main types of primary hyperoxaluria (PH), each corresponding to a deficiency in a different enzyme:

1. **Primary Hyperoxaluria Type 1 (PH1)**: This is the most common and severe form, caused by a deficiency in the liver enzyme alanine-glyoxylate aminotransferase (AGXT). PH1 can lead to kidney stones, nephrocalcinosis, and progressive renal failure.

2. **Primary Hyperoxaluria Type 2 (PH2)**: Caused by a deficiency in the enzyme glyoxylate/hydroxypyruvate reductase (GRHPR), PH2 is generally less severe than PH1 but can still result in significant kidney stone formation and kidney damage.

3. **Primary Hyperoxaluria Type 3 (PH3)**: This type is due to mutations in the HOGA1 gene, which encodes the enzyme 4-hydroxy-2-oxoglutarate aldolase. PH3 is typically milder than PH1 and PH2.

Diagnosis of primary hyperoxaluria typically involves clinical evaluation, urinary oxalate measurement, genetic testing to identify the specific mutation, and sometimes liver biopsy to measure enzyme activity. Management focuses on reducing oxalate levels through hydration, dietary modification, use of oxalate-binding agents, and in severe cases, may involve dialysis or organ transplantation.

Given the complications associated with oxalate accumulation, ongoing research aims to find more effective treatments, including enzyme replacement therapies and gene therapy.
