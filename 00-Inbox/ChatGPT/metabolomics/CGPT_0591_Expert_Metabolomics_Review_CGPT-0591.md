1. Introduction
Aging is a process that involves the physiological deterioration of tissues and organs, which involves the gradual accumulation of cell damage and increased susceptibility to diseases over time (Flatt, 2012; Luo et al., 2020). 
Therefore, many studies have been conducted on organs that deteriorate with age (Docherty et al., 2019; Hunt et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2018). 
Especially, brain is known to be associated with common neurodegenerative brain diseases such as Alzheimer's and Parkinson's disease (De Lima et al., 2020; Hayflick, 2000; Sarkar and Fisher, 2006). 
The brain requires a large amount of energy; thus, it is sensitive to changes in energy supply and mitochondrial function. Mitochondrial dysfunction, inflammation-related oxidative stress, and glucose metabolism may lead to brain-related energy metabolism disorders associated with age-related functional changes (Camandola and Mattson, 2017; Chung et al., 2019). 
Therefore, metabolomic studies on brain aging is essential. Metabolomics is the quantitative and comprehensive study of low-molecular-weight metabolites (< 1,000 Da) that arise during cellular processes, which can be used to monitor the physiological state of metabolism (Bino et al., 2004). 
Recently, to understand aging, metabolomics studies have been conducted to identify potential biomarkers associated with aging and age-related diseases (Camandola and Mattson, 2017; Clement et al., 2019; Havelund et al., 2017; Shi et al., 2021; Son et al., 2012). 
In particular, altered amino acids (AAs), organic acids (OAs), and fatty acids (FAs) have been reported as potential biomarkers of aging in biological samples, such as serum, plasma, dorsolateral prefrontal cortex, eyes, and liver (Huo et al., 2020; Jove et al., 2016; Seo et al., 2016; Seo et al., 2022; Son et al., 2012).
 In our previous reports, metabolomic studies have been conducted on AAs, OAs, and FAs in the plasma and eyes of aged mice (Seo et al., 2016; Seo et al., 2022). 

However, a systematic metabolomic study has not yet been conducted in various brain tissues. Therefore, we conducted a systematic metabolomic study to detect potential biomarkers and monitor altered metabolism in brain tissues (cortex, cerebellum, hypothalamus, and hippocampus) of young and old rats using gas chromatography-tandem-mass spectrometry (GC-MS/MS).
