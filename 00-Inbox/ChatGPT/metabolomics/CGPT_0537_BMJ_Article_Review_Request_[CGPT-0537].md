Abstract
Non-communicable diseases (NCDs) are a significant challenge for patients, as they face both economic and health burdens that are not satisfactorily addressed. Metabolomics is an effective tool for identifying and treating metabolic disruptions in NCDs, contributing to their management. This study aims to investigate the cost-effectiveness and impact on the quality of life of metabolomics-based treatment for patients with NCDs. 295 patients (18-65 years old, 185F/74 M) with NCDs receiving metabolomics-based treatment were included. Quality of life (QoL) was assessed using custom, self-administered questionnaires and targeted metabolomics profiling was used to measure urinary organic acids, plasma fatty acids and serum metabolites. The costs, quality-adjusted life years (QALY) gained, and incremental cost-effectiveness ratios (ICERs) for the approach were estimated. Statistically significant improvement was identified in energy levels, sleep, gastrointestinal function and physical activity (p<0.0001). In total, 67.9% of participants reported significantly improved QoL as per their self-assessment. The ICER was estimated at €49.774/QALY (95%CI-€40,110-€61,433). The mean number of QALYs after treatment was 0.735 (95% UI 0.666–0.809) vs 0.671 (95% UI 0.603–0.774) at baseline with a 0,064 (95% UI 0.050–0.078) QALY gain. 16 organic acids and 18 total fatty acids were significantly altered post-treatment (p< 0.0001). Vitamin D levels significantly increased (P=0.0036), correcting low levels in 48.1% of patients to just 1.2% post-treatment. Similarly, 5-methyltetrahydrofolate levels increased from 14.15 + 4.8 to 26.6 + 8.3 nmoL/L (p=0.036). Individualized treatment of NCDs through metabolic reprogramming of key pathways is an emerging strategy in healthcare showing promise in addressing the rising prevalence, economic burden, and loss of quality-adjusted life years.

Keywords: Metabolomics, Quality of life, Cost-effectiveness, NCDs, QALY



Introduction

Non-communicable diseases (NCDs) are chronic diseases and a significant health challenge of the 21st century, accounting for 74% (41 million) of global deaths each year and 90% in developed countries. NCDs, such as cardiometabolic conditions, autoimmune diseases, and cancer, are primarily influenced by non-genetic factors, and the World Health Organization reports that modifying these factors could prevent 80% of NCD-related deaths(1,2). Among the most important contributing factors to the rise of NCDs are unhealthy diet and micronutrient deficiency, lack of exercise, alcohol abuse and socioeconomic factors(3). Poor dietary patterns are responsible for more deaths than tobacco, high blood pressure, or any other health risk. However, they are not assessed by physicians adequately(4). In addition, current treatment approaches have only proven beneficial for a fraction of patients (30-60%) and do not consider individual risk factors or genetic variability(5). As a result, patients with NCDs often do not meet their health goals with the available treatments, either due to the low responsiveness and drug adverse effects or related complications. Specifically, increased rates of comorbidity and the presence of associated metabolic complications deteriorate the overall quality of life of patients. For instance, patients with Hashimoto's thyroiditis, the most common autoimmune disease, suffer from fatigue, weight gain, gastrointestinal problems, depression, cognitive impairment, and muscle and joint pain(6–13). These symptoms are reported even in treated patients with normal TSH levels, suggesting that other factors should be evaluated(14,15). These quality-of-life aspects are similarly affected in chronic diseases such as inflammatory bowel disease and rheumatoid arthritis(16–18). 
Besides the health challenge, NCDs are a significant economic burden to healthcare systems, patients and society. For instance, in the USA, the direct cost of chronic diseases has been estimated at $216 billion annually, causing $147 billion in lost productivity1, while in Europe, premature death from chronic diseases in the working-age population is responsible for €115 billion loss yearly. 
Overall, there is a pressing demand for effective and patient-centered solutions to improve patient’s quality of life by leveraging recent medical and technological advances.
Precision medicine is a new approach to patient-centered care that considers an individual's gene variability, lifestyle, and nutrition for early diagnosis and personalized treatment incorporating -omics technologies.  Metabolomics is the study of metabolites within an organism and provides insights into cellular metabolic health and micronutrient status, giving in-depth information on the phenotype. Metabolomics' advantage lies in metabolites' increased sensitivity to external factors and their association with complex immunopathogenic mechanisms. Patients with chronic diseases are characterized by low-grade inflammation and metabolic complications (such as insulin resistance), and their evaluation represents one of the clinical applications of metabolomics.
Here, we report the findings of a longitudinal clinical study of patients with chronic diseases who received metabolomics-based treatment focusing on the metabolic changes and the associated improvement in the patient’s quality of life. An economic analysis was also undertaken to investigate the cost-effectiveness of the metabolomics-based approach.
Materials and Methods
Study design
The present study employed longitudinal clinical data based on 765 individuals who visited the "Health clinic for autoimmune and chronic diseases" in Athens, Greece, from 15/06/2016 -15/03/2023. Patients were requested to assess their current state of quality of life (QoL at T1) by filling out a questionnaire developed by the medical team of the clinic for patients with NCDs. Details on the questionnaire design are available in the supplementary materials. Of patients who answered the questionnaire, only those who followed a metabolomics-based treatment for a reasonable time were selected. Baseline QoL levels (QoL at T0) were calculated based on the medical history of patients, which were assessed and converted to questionnaire scaling by experienced physicians according to the scale shown In Supplementary Table 2. Metabolite levels were also compared between the two time- points. Data analysis was performed on 259 participants for whom thorough medical records were available and follow-up responses along with metabolomic data (Figure 1). All the available data of the subjects were collected by trained administrative staff via the electronic platform of the clinic and assessed by physicians and biologists. 



Figure 1: Study design diagram

Study population
A total of 259 diagnosed patients with a controlled chronic or autoimmune disease under treatment (73 males and 186 females) or related symptoms were included in the present study. Newly-diagnosed patients were not included. Personal data included age, gender, weight, height, BMI, alcohol consumption and smoking status (Table I).
Eligible criteria for the study's participants were male and female adults 18-65 years old with a diagnosed chronic disease or related symptomatology who had performed metabolomic analysis and received treatment as described below. Exclusion criteria for the participants were athletes, obese, pregnant or lactating women and individuals diagnosed with cancer or have an infectious disease. Individuals who answered the questionnaire twice or misinterpreted the questions, did not adhere to treatment or were treated for less than 30 days were also excluded.
Ethics
Participants' data were processed and anonymized according to the EU General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR). All the participants signed an informed consent. The study was conducted complying with the ethical standards of the 1964 Helsinki Declaration, or comparable ethical standards and has been approved by the scientific board of the "Health clinic for autoimmune and chronic diseases" and the Ethics Committee of the University of Crete (approval no. Questionnaire and Modelling of the Utility Values
 
To assess the quality of life of patients with NCDs, a generic, self-reported, four-dimensioned, custom questionnaire was developed internally by the clinical experts of our team leveraging the clinical experience and previously published work as described in the Supplementary material. Briefly, four main categories were assessed, namely, a) the level of the  patient's energy, b) the sleep quality, c) the gastrointestinal function and d) the physical activity levels. The questionnaire was administered via a Google electronic form and was sent to participants by email. Response options were at a close form, a five-point Likert-type scale where the higher scores indicated a better QoL, while questions were simple, concise and easily understood in accordance with the guidelines7. Questions that referred to a negative feeling or effect were scored in reverse. The total questionnaire score per patient was estimated as an unweighted average of these four factors and represented the utility of patients used in the  QALY estimation as described below. Other categories assessed by the administered questionnaire included joint pain, headache frequency, stress levels and overall quality of life improvement.

Modeling the Utility Values and QALYs

Utility values refer to the quality of a patient's life (QoL) associated with different health states. Quality is measured on a scale of 0-1, or of 0-100, where 0 represents the "worst possible" and 1 is the "highest or best possible" state of health. In related literature, there are several methodological tools to value a patient's health state and quality of life, depending on the type of disease, the target population (patients/health professionals), the type of instrument (generic/specific), and the complexity and multidimensionality of questionnaires. 

A Quality Adjusted Life Years (QALY) is a generic measure combining the life expectancy and the quality of life and represents the standard approach in economic evaluations(27). In particular, the estimation of QALYs is based on the treatment time multiplied by the total utility score, which, in the present analysis, was represented by the total score of the questionnaire. This assumption was based on the disease-specific QALYs obtained by the related literature and the baseline QALYs from our dataset that were reasonably similar by our clinicians8. It was also conservatively assumed that patients who would stop treatment would have the convergence of utility back to baseline in one month. The final QALY calculations were determined by the average baseline and the final assessment of the utility, the so-called linear interpolation approach, multiplied by the duration of treatment for each participant. As a supplementary analysis, we also investigated other aspects of utility such as a) the reduction of knee pain, b) the decrease in the frequency of headaches, c) the stress level, and d) The decrease in the frequency of exacerbations in the specific disease of patients.  

Perspective of Economic Analysis

The perspective of analysis is the point of view from which the costs and benefits are recorded and assessed. In the present study, since metabolomic-based treatments are not reimbursed by the state health system, a patient perspective1 was adopted, including all medical costs and focusing on their payments. Other types of indirect costs, such as productivity loss, were not taken into consideration. 

Economic Analysis and costing Methodology

The model was set to provide deterministic and probabilistic analyses. The results are used to compute the Incremental Cost-Effectiveness Ratio (ICER), the cost-effectiveness acceptability curve (CAEC) and the Expected-Value-of-Perfect-Information (EVPI) curve. The ICER was determined as the ratio of the added costs of the metabolomics-based approach divided by the difference in QALYs. Since cost-effectiveness represents a subjective assessment, a willingness-to-pay-threshold was determined to estimate the probability of accepting to follow the metabolomic-based approach in addition to the standard treatment or not, compared to standard treatment alone.
The standard approach to illustrating this type of analysis is the CEAC, which shows the probability (on the y-axis) that the combinatorial treatment of standard treatment and metabolomics-based intervention may represent a cost-effective option compared with a conventional approach for a range (on the x-axis) of maximum monetary values that a decision-maker might be willing to pay per QALY3. The EVPI estimates the value of simultaneously eliminating all the uncertainty and thus, provides the decision-maker with an indication of the value of collecting additional information4. 

Concerning the cost calculation, patient-level resource utilization data were combined with unit cost data and these data were then aggregated to compute total treatment for each patient. Total costs included a) the cost of metabolomic exams and b) the cost of supplements (Table 2). Similar to pharmacogenomic studies, the cost of main drugs and the cost of adverse reactions were not taken into account in the analysis owing to the fact that a) has been considered identical for both groups and b) the patients represented a pool of individuals with different health statuses, different diseases and comorbidities and only the cost of metabolomic intervention could make a difference(28,29). 

Since the monitoring of patient adherence to the treatment protocol is somewhat complex, a modeling approach was utilized for the final calculation of total cost. In particular, an 80% compliance of patients was assumed based on data from a large systematic review of oral supplements5. Variations in resource utilization among patients reflected differences related to the frequency in exams and in supplements prescribed in participants. 
To deal with uncertainty in probabilistic analysis, the bootstrap method was conducted. Bootstrapping is an alternative and preferable inference technique to parametric approaches for estimating standard error and the 95% confidence intervals. In short, this method creates from the original dataset, 5000 new matrices of the same size using random sampling with replacement. The confidence intervals were estimated with the use of a straightforward percentile method. Hence, bootstrapping permitted hypothesis testing avoiding the adoption of restricting assumptions and complicated analytical formulas. All price data referred to the economic year 2023. Prices used in the model for nutritional supplements were extracted by the private sector and might be considered common across the country. 

The effect of covariates (patient characteristics) on total cost was investigated within a generalized linear regression framework. In particular, a gamma distribution was plausibly assumed for cost, and a logarithmic as a link function since heteroskedasticity and right skewness in cost data might distort the linear model- a common problem in this type of data6. In the present analysis, there were no issues with right-censored and/or missing data since the economic dataset was complete.
