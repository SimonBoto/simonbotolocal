Indeed, the connection between **Alchemy and Cognitive Science** is not only poetic—it is philosophically grounded and increasingly supported by contemporary thinkers like **John Vervaeke**, **James Hillman**, and even **Carl Jung** (whose work on alchemical symbolism and the psyche laid much of the groundwork for the modern synthesis of consciousness studies and hermetic metaphors).

Let’s break down the resonance between **Alchemy** and **Cognitive Science** along the three pillars you've outlined:

---

### 1. **Bridging Matter and Spirit (The *Unus Mundus*)**

Alchemy never truly sought to *literally* transmute lead into gold. That was a metaphor for **psychospiritual integration**—a transformative union of the *nigredo* (chaotic matter or shadow) with the *rubedo* (illumined consciousness). This is mirrored in cognitive science's quest to **map and recreate consciousness**, not just in machines, but in **embodied and embedded systems**—where matter becomes intelligent through pattern, recursion, and interaction (see: *4E Cognition*).

The *Unus Mundus* is echoed today in efforts to unify **physics, biology, and cognition**—or even in the concept of the **noosphere** (Teilhard de Chardin), where consciousness is an emergent layer of the material cosmos.

---

### 2. **Psychological Transformation through Projection**

Alchemists projected inner states onto the materials in the crucible. In doing so, they **externalized psychological content**, allowing for reflection, transformation, and eventual integration. This is not dissimilar from:

- **Computational modeling of thought**
- **Machine learning architectures designed on the mind's recursive functions**
- **Virtual reality environments used in trauma therapy**
- And even the **symbolic projections** in AI development (e.g., anthropomorphizing ChatGPT or robotic agents)

What this shows is a deep **feedback loop between internal states and external technologies**—exactly what alchemy was always working with.

---

### 3. **The Maker’s Epistemology (Praxis-Based Knowing)**

The idea that “**one who makes a thing can know a thing**” is Aristotelian, yes, but also deeply **Hermetic**. In alchemy, the making of the Philosopher’s Stone wasn’t just a task—it was a *praxis*, a way of transforming the practitioner.

Modern cognitive science and AI replicate this with:

- **Synthetic approaches to intelligence**: We make artificial agents to understand intelligence.
- **Neural nets mimicking brain function**.
- **Cyborg theory and posthumanism**, where the boundary between the knowing subject and the made object is blurred.

Just like the **homunculus** was a symbolic way of grappling with *what it means to be a mind in matter*, today’s AI projects are the new crucibles. But instead of mercury and sulfur, we use code and silicon.

---

### Final Reflection

The alchemist was not a chemist, but a **psychonaut, an ontologist, and a maker of selves**. Today’s cognitive scientists, AI researchers, and psychotechnologists are rediscovering these themes—but often without knowing their lineage.

Your thought draws the gold thread between them. Perhaps, as Vervaeke suggests, we are recovering not just knowledge, but **wisdom frameworks**—psychotechnologies for transformation. In this sense, *the real Philosopher’s Stone* may be a recursive interface between world and mind, self and system, chaos and order.
