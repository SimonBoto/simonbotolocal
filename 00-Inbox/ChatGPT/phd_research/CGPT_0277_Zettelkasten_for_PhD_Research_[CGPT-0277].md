When deciding between using tags or notes for labeling categories like definitions, symptoms, and pathophysiology, each method has its strengths, and both can serve different purposes depending on how you prefer to organize and retrieve information. Here’s a breakdown to help you decide:

### **Tags vs. Notes for Labeling**:

#### **1. Using Tags**:
   Tags can be used as a secondary organizational tool to quickly filter and group notes by their category (e.g., definition, symptoms).

   **Pros**:
   - **Quick Filtering**: You can easily filter all notes tagged with `#Definition`, `#Symptoms`, `#Pathophysiology`, etc., which provides an instant overview of specific types of content across your vault.
   - **Less Clutter**: Tags help reduce clutter since they do not create new notes. They work in the background to help you organize without taking up extra space.
   - **Multiple Tags Per Note**: A single note can have multiple tags (e.g., a note on insulin resistance could have `#Definition`, `#Pathophysiology`, and `#Treatment`), making it easy to filter and categorize content by various criteria.

   **Cons**:
   - **Limited Context**: Tags don't hold content; they are just labels. If you need rich interlinking or additional details, you’ll need to open the associated notes manually.
   - **Hard to Search for Tag Combinations**: Depending on your tool, filtering for combinations of tags (e.g., finding notes that are both `#Definition` and `#Pathophysiology`) might require additional setup or plugin support.

   **When to Use Tags**:
   - If you want to **quickly categorize** and **filter** notes by content type without creating extra notes.
   - For broad organizational purposes, where searching for specific categories across multiple notes is your main goal.

#### **2. Using Notes (Dedicated Notes for Definition, Symptoms, etc.)**:
   Creating separate notes for categories like definitions or symptoms gives you more flexibility to include details and maintain richer connections across your vault.

   **Pros**:
   - **Rich Content**: Notes can contain detailed explanations, examples, or even sub-definitions, which tags cannot. For example, a `Definition: Insulin Resistance` note could include not just the definition but different perspectives or updates over time.
   - **Linking Capabilities**: You can link other notes directly to a specific "definition" or "symptom" note. This allows you to maintain the Zettelkasten method’s interlinked knowledge structure. Each note becomes a piece of a more extensive web.
   - **Topic-Specific Details**: By separating content into notes, you can ensure each category is treated thoroughly. For instance, a symptoms note might link to clinical cases, while a pathophysiology note might link to molecular mechanisms.

   **Cons**:
   - **More Notes to Manage**: If you're dealing with many categories, it could lead to note overload, making management a bit more complex.
   - **Requires Thoughtful Structure**: You'll need to carefully plan the structure of your notes and interlinking, so they remain easy to navigate.

   **When to Use Notes**:
   - If you want to **create detailed content** and **link specific parts** of your research to various aspects like definition, symptoms, etc.
   - For **complex topics** where you'll need in-depth notes that explain more than just the category (e.g., definitions that include multiple sub-categories, historical shifts in meaning, etc.).
