**Introduction to Pathophysiology**

Metabolic Syndrome is characterized by complex, multifactorial, and multi-system pathophysiology, exhibiting modulatory effects in almost every known system of the body. Central obesity, hormonal imbalances, and insulin resistance are the core component drivers of the syndrome's development (Reaven, 1993).

**Central Obesity and its Role in Metabolic Syndrome**

Obesity is fundamental to Metabolic Syndrome and it appears to precede the emergence of other risk factors (O’Neill & O’Driscoll, 2015). Obesity is defined by an excess of body fat and measured by BMI (≥ 30 kg/m^2) or WC (≥ 102 cm in men and WC ≥ 88 cm in women), representing a later stage of overweight (Lean et al., 1995). Adipose tissue, and especially visceral fat, is not merely for passive energy storage, but is an endocrine organ with important metabolic functions, which produces various bioactive peptides (Després & Lemieux, 2006). In Metabolic Syndrome, excess in visceral fats results in dysregulation of adipokines and cytokines further promoting insulin resistance and systemic inflammation (Kahn et al., 2006). Adiponectin, interleukin-6 (IL-6), tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-alpha), leptin, and angiotensinogen are the most important among these (Lehr et al., 2012; Berg & Scherer, 2005; Grundy, 2016).

**Insulin Resistance Mechanisms**

Insulin is a peptide hormone produced and secreted by the pancreatic beta cells in response to high levels of blood glucose. Insulin resistance, one of the major hallmarks of metabolic syndromes, refers to the defective ability of cells to respond to insulin's action, ultimately leading to hyperglycemia and increased insulin production (Kahn et al., 2006). Furthermore, insulin resistance impairs fat metabolism, increasing free fatty acid flux and leading to dyslipidemia, characterized by elevated TGs and low HDL (Taskinen, 2003).

**Impact on Glucose and Lipid Metabolism**

The interplay between insulin resistance, altered adipokine profiles, and hormonal imbalances leads to significant disruptions in glucose and lipid metabolism, characterized by increased triglycerides and decreased HDL cholesterol levels (Taskinen, 2003; Kolovou et al., 2005). These alterations in lipoproteins balance and concentration constitute the basis of the atherogenic dyslipidemia, characteristic of both insulin resistance and the Metabolic Syndrome (Grundy, 1998).

**Hypertension Mechanisms**

Hypertension is also a core component of Metabolic Syndrome affecting 85% of the patients diagnosed with the syndrome (Ginsberg, 2000; Sowers, 2004). It is created as the result of insulin resistance-induced sympathetic system stimulation, vasoconstriction, and endothelial dysfunction (Grassi et al., 2007). These are caused partly by the loss of vasodilatory effect of insulin, FFA-induced vasoconstriction due to increased reactive oxygen species (ROS) and scavenging of nitric oxide (NO) (Tripathy et al., 2003).

**Adipokines and Inflammatory Pathways**

The various pathogenic pathways mentioned earlier, such as insulin resistance, dyslipidemia, and obesity, induce systemic oxidative stress and activate the downstream inflammatory pathways, explaining the elevation of Inflammatory markers in Metabolic Syndromes and the subsequent increased risk for fibrosis and atherogenesis (Hotamisligil, 2006). Adipose tissue's role in the development of obesity-induced inflammation is integral, by increasing cytokines and chemokines and dysregulating adipokines, including tumor necrosis factor-α (TNF-α), interleukin (IL)-6, IL-8, IL-1, monocyte chemoattractant protein-1 (MCP-1), leptin, PAI-1, retinol binding protein 4 (RBP4), chemerin, serum amyloid A (SSA), C-reactive protein (CRP), lipopolysaccharide-binding protein (LBP), fetuin A, and decreased adiponectin and omentin-1 (Jialal & Devaraj, 2018; Jung & Choi, 2014).

**Hormonal Imbalances and Their Implications**

Hormonal imbalances are also a major component contributing to the pathophysiology of metabolic syndrome. Recorded imbalances in the HPA axis, levels of cortisol, growth hormone, sex, and thyroid hormones affect fat distribution and metabolism, insulin sensitivity, and overall metabolic homeostasis (Pasquali et al., 2006). Cushing's syndrome, resulting from excessive glucocorticoids, is characterized by central obesity and increased visceral depots, findings common with Metabolic Syndrome (Björntorp, 1996; Wajchenberg, 2000). These effects also require the involvement of other hormones, including increased insulin, dysregulated catecholamines, decreased growth hormone, and altered androgen levels (Björntorp, 1996). Furthermore, cortisol response to the CRH challenge has been significantly correlated with insulin levels and insulin resistance in obese adults (Pasquali & Vicennati, 2000).

**Conclusion and Transition to Clinical Manifestations**

The described pathophysiological mechanisms are intricately linked, each exacerbating the other to increase the overall metabolic risk. These mechanisms collectively set the stage for the clinical manifestations and complications of metabolic syndrome, which include severe cardiovascular conditions and type 2 diabetes, topics that will be explored in the following sections.

### Clinical Manifestations

**Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus (T2DM)**

From the three major risk factors for diabetes—family history, obesity, and impaired glucose regulation—the last two are included in the Metabolic Syndrome definition. Epidemiological studies demonstrate that Metabolic Syndrome is associated with an increased cardiometabolic risk, which is exceptionally high for diabetes, ranging between a 3-fold to 20-fold increase of relative risk, depending on the number of components present (Aschner, 2010).

**Cancer**

Metabolic Syndrome has been associated with increased cancer mortality and prevalence, with relatively strong evidence correlating to colorectal cancer in both sexes, liver cancer in men, and pancreatic cancer in women. In cohort studies in men, the presence of MetS was associated with increased relative risk for liver, colorectal, and bladder cancer, while in women it increases relative risk by significant margins for endometrial, pancreatic, breast postmenopausal, rectal, and colorectal cancer (Esposito et al., 2012).

**Cardiovascular Disease**

Each component of Metabolic Syndrome independently increases the risk for cardiovascular disease, while the combination of these factors further elevates the rates and severity of the disease. These effects are mediated by a spectrum of conditions including microvascular dysfunction, atherosclerosis, and cardiac dysfunction, leading to myocardial infarction and heart failure (Tune et al., 2017). Metabolic syndrome is considered a precursor to cardiovascular disease, significantly increasing the risk for CVD, CVD mortality, and all-cause mortality (Mottillo et al., 2010).

**Non-Alcoholic Fatty Liver Disease (NAFLD)**

NAFLD is characterized by the presence of fat in the liver (>5-10%), which is not associated with other causes of steatosis, and is commonly accompanied by insulin resistance. Visceral adiposity is strongly linked to hepatic fatty infiltration, with the level of fatty acids in the hepatic parenchyma being indirectly linked to Metabolic Syndrome, being a cause and a consequence (Chalasani et al., 2012; Radu et al., 2023). NAFLD can range from simple fatty infiltration without inflammation to more severe forms like non-alcoholic steatohepatitis (NASH), cirrhosis, and may even progress to end-stage liver disease or hepatocellular carcinoma (Browning et al., 2004; Charlton et al., 2011).

**Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS)**

Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) is a prevalent endocrine disorder affecting women of reproductive age and is closely associated with numerous metabolic manifestations such as obesity, insulin resistance, and hyperandrogenism. According to current criteria, the prevalence of PCOS has risen to 20%, with a significant percentage of women with PCOS also exhibiting features of metabolic syndrome and insulin resistance (Chen & Pang, 2021; Apridonidze et al., 2005).

**Autoimmune Diseases**

The interplay between the immune system and metabolic syndrome has become a focal point of research, recognizing that disruptions in immune-metabolic communication contribute to the progression of autoimmune diseases. The prevalence of Metabolic Syndrome is notably higher in patients with autoimmune diseases, such as rheumatic diseases, systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE), ankylosing spondylitis, and vasculitis, with consequent elevated incidences of cardiovascular diseases (Medina et al., 2018; Müller et al., 2017; Haroon et al., 2016; Parker et al., 2015; Peralta-Amaro et al., 2015; de Moraes et al., 2013; Petermann Smits et al., 2013).

**Hyperuricemia and Gout**

Gout, an autoinflammatory disease characterized by the deposition of monosodium urate crystals within joints, is the most common inflammatory arthritis in adults and is often associated with metabolic syndrome. The relationship between serum uric acid levels and the components of metabolic syndrome is well-documented, with significant associations reported in numerous studies (Beyl et al., 2016; Ichikawa et al., 2011; Fam, 2002; Lee et al., 1995; Rathmann et al., 1998; Emmerson, 1998; Choi & Ford, 2007).

**Vitamin D and Metabolic Syndrome Components**

Vitamin D plays a crucial role beyond bone health, affecting glucose metabolism, insulin secretion, and sensitivity. It is synthesized in the skin through exposure to UVB radiation from the sun and undergoes further activation in the liver and kidneys (Holick, 2007; DeLuca, 2004). Vitamin D influences insulin secretion and sensitivity by enhancing insulin receptor expression and promoting insulin responsiveness, potentially reducing insulin resistance (Pittas et al., 2007). Additionally, it is involved in lipid metabolism and regulates the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS), linking it to hypertension, a component of metabolic syndrome (Jorde & Grimnes, 2011; Li et al., 2002).
