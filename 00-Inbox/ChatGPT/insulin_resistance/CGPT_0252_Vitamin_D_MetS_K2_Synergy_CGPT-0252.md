# Chapter: The Role of Vitamin D in Metabolic Syndrome and its Synergy with Vitamin K2

## Introduction

Metabolic syndrome (MetS) is a cluster of conditions—including increased blood pressure, high blood sugar, excess body fat around the waist, and abnormal cholesterol levels—that occur together, increasing the risk of heart disease, stroke, and type 2 diabetes. Vitamin D has emerged as a critical nutrient influencing various aspects of metabolic health. Numerous studies have linked low vitamin D levels to components of MetS. Additionally, vitamin K2 has gained attention for its potential synergistic effects with vitamin D, possibly enhancing metabolic outcomes. This chapter reviews the current research on vitamin D in connection with metabolic syndrome and explores its potential synergy with vitamin K2.

## 1. Vitamin D Intake and Serum Levels: Association with Metabolic Syndrome

### 1.1. Vitamin D Intake and Obesity Indicators in Children

Several studies have demonstrated that low vitamin D intake is correlated with higher measures of adiposity in children:

- **Rodríguez-Rodríguez et al. (2010)** observed that inadequate vitamin D intake was associated with increased body weight, BMI, waist circumference, and waist-to-hip ratio.
- **Alemzadeh et al. (2008)** reported similar findings, linking low vitamin D intake to higher body weight and BMI in pediatric populations.

### 1.2. Vitamin D Intake and Metabolic Risk in Adults

- **Pittas et al. (2010)**, in the Nurses' Health Study cohort, found that consuming three or more dairy servings per day—indicative of higher total vitamin D and calcium intake—was associated with a decreased risk of developing diabetes.

### 1.3. Serum Vitamin D Levels and Metabolic Syndrome Components

#### 1.3.1. Seasonal Variation and Vitamin D Levels

- **Mitri et al. (2011)** highlighted that diabetes control worsens during winter when vitamin D levels are typically lower, suggesting a possible connection between vitamin D status and glucose metabolism.
- **Jorde et al. (2010)**, in a large Norwegian study with 10,229 subjects, found an inverse association between 25(OH)D concentrations and BMI consistent across both summer and winter months, indicating that seasonality did not affect this relationship.

#### 1.3.2. Large Population Studies Linking Vitamin D Levels to Metabolic Syndrome

- **Song et al. They reported significant associations between vitamin D deficiency and MetS components, particularly triglycerides (TG), high-density lipoprotein (HDL), fasting blood sugar (FBS), insulin resistance (IR), blood pressure (BP), and waist circumference (WC).

#### 1.3.3. Vitamin D Levels and Adiposity Measures

- **Cheng et al. (2010)** observed an inverse relationship between serum vitamin D concentrations and measures of adiposity such as waist circumference and BMI in 1,882 subjects. Vitamin D deficiency prevalence was three times higher in individuals with higher fat concentrations.
- **González et al. (2015)** confirmed that individuals with higher BMI, waist circumference, and waist-to-height ratio had significantly lower vitamin D status.
- **Lenders et al. (2009)** noted that in adolescents, a decrease of 0.5 ng/mL in 25(OH)D was related to a 1% increase in body fat mass.

### 1.4. Meta-Analyses and Prospective Studies

- **Afzal et al. (2013)** conducted a Danish prospective study with a median follow-up of 20 years, including 9,841 participants. They reported that individuals with 25(OH)D levels less than 12.5 nmol/L had a hazard ratio (HR) of 1.22 for developing MetS compared to those with levels more than 50 nmol/L; the HR was 1.35 when comparing the lowest with the highest quartile.

Specifically, high vitamin D levels were significantly associated with reductions in cardiovascular disease risk (33%), type 2 diabetes (55%), and MetS (51%).

- **Hajhashemy et al. (2021)** included 43 studies with 298,187 adults in their systematic review and found that the highest serum vitamin D levels were significantly associated with a 43% decreased odds of MetS in developed countries and 40% in developing countries. Each 25 nmol/L increase in serum vitamin D level corresponded to a 15% decrease in the odds of MetS.
