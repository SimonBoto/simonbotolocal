Chapter 2: Comprehensive Overview of Metabolic Syndrome

2.1 Definition and Diagnostic Criteria of Metabolic Syndrome

Metabolic Syndrome is characterized by a cluster of commonly coexisting metabolic imbalances, which lead to increased risk of CVD and T2DM. Thus, its diagnosis and management are pivotal for public health and clinical practice. 

In 1988, Reaven first proposed the diagnostic criteria for Syndrome X, focusing on insulin resistance, dyslipidemia and hypertension (Reaven, 1988). The World Health Organisation (WHO) later proposed the first formal definition of the Metabolic Syndrome MetS in 1998, emphasizing on insulin resistance as the major underlying risk factor. For the diagnosis the presence of insulin resistance as depicted by several markers was required, along with 2 additional risk factors (obesity, hypertension, elevated triglycerides, decreased HDL, microalbuminuria) (Alberti, Zimmet & WHO, 1998). 

The next major update was presented by the ‘Third Report of National Cholesterol Education Program Adult Treatment Panel III’ (2002). The ATPIII criteria did not require any single factor's presence as obligatory, but for the diagnosis 3 out of 5 factors were needed (abdominal obesity, elevated triglycerides, decreased HDL, hypertension, elevated fasting glucose or T2DM) (“Third Report of the National Cholesterol Education Program (NCEP) Expert Panel on Detection, Evaluation, and Treatment of High Blood Cholesterol in Adults (Adult Treatment Panel III) Final Report,” 2002).

Today, after multiple updates and slight variations among approaches, the International Diabetes Federation (IDF) and the National Cholesterol Education Program Adult Treatment Panel III (NCEP ATP III) are most commonly used (Alberti et al, 2009). However, the disagreement on waist circumference to define abdominal obesity remains, with IDF recommending <94 cm for men and 80 cm for women, while American Heart Association (AHA), in contrast, sets cut points of 102 and 88 cm respectively (Eckel, Grundy & Zimmet, 2005).



2.2 Epidemiology of Metabolic Syndrome

Metabolic Syndrome has been rising worldwide, following the trends of the obesity pandemic. Obesity has reached epidemic proportions already 10 years ago, with worldwide figures estimating that 1.4 billion people are obese, with the number growing every year (O’Neill, & O’Driscoll, 2015). 

Almost 34% of the adults living in the United States meet the criteria for Metabolic Syndrome, with the prevalence rising by 35% in all socio-economic groups from 1988-1994 to 2007-2012 (Moore et al, 2017). The same trends are recorded globally, with urbanized and industrialized regions leading the number due to related lifestyle changes (O’Neill, & O’Driscoll, 2015).  

Older age, minority ethnicity and lower socioeconomic status negatively influence the prevalence rates (Grundy, 2016).  These trends, following the concurrent increases in other chronic conditions and comorbidities and combined with an aging US and world population, should raise concern and lead to united effort for prevention strategies (Hirode & Wong, 2020).

2.3 Pathophysiology of Metabolic Syndrome
Metabolic Syndrome is characterized by complex, multifactorial, and multi-system pathophysiology, exhibiting modulatory effects in almost every known system of the body. Central obesity, hormonal imbalances, and insulin resistance are the core component drivers of the syndrome's development (Reaven, 1993).
2.3.1 Central Obesity and its Role in Metabolic Syndrome
Obesity is fundamental to Metabolic Syndrome and it appears to precede the emergence of other risk factors (O’Neill & O’Driscoll, 2015). Obesity is defined by an excess of body fat and measured by BMI (≥ 30 kg/m^2) or WC (≥ 102 cm in men and WC ≥ 88 cm in women), representing a later stage of overweight (Lean et al., 1995). Adipose tissue, and especially visceral fat, is not merely for passive energy storage, but is an endocrine organ with important metabolic functions, which produces various bioactive peptides (Després & Lemieux, 2006). In Metabolic Syndrome, excess in visceral fats results in dysregulation of adipokines and cytokines further promoting insulin resistance and systemic inflammation (Kahn et al., 2006). Adiponectin, interleukin-6 (IL-6), tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-alpha), leptin, and angiotensinogen are the most important among these (Lehr et al., 2012; Berg & Scherer, 2005; Grundy, 2016).
2.3.2 Insulin Resistance Mechanisms
Insulin is a peptide hormone produced and secreted by the pancreatic beta cells in response to high levels of blood glucose. Insulin resistance, one of the major hallmarks of metabolic syndromes, refers to the defective ability of cells to respond to insulin's action, ultimately leading to hyperglycemia and increased insulin production (Kahn et al., 2006). Furthermore, insulin resistance impairs fat metabolism, increasing free fatty acid flux and leading to dyslipidemia, characterized by elevated TGs and low HDL (Taskinen, 2003).
The interplay between insulin resistance, altered adipokine profiles, and hormonal imbalances leads to significant disruptions in glucose and lipid metabolism, characterized by increased triglycerides and decreased HDL cholesterol levels (Taskinen, 2003; Kolovou et al., 2005). These alterations in lipoprotein balance and concentration constitute the basis of the atherogenic dyslipidemia, characteristic of both insulin resistance and the Metabolic Syndrome (Grundy, 1998).

### 3.4 Vitamin D Intake Studies

Numerous studies have investigated the dietary intake of vitamin D and its correlation with metabolic syndrome (MetS) and its components. Dietary sources of vitamin D are limited and include fatty fish, fortified foods, and to a lesser extent, egg yolks and certain mushrooms (Holick, 2007). However, it is challenging to meet the recommended daily allowance of vitamin D through diet alone, especially in populations with limited sun exposure.

Cross-sectional studies have demonstrated that individuals with lower dietary intake of vitamin D are more likely to exhibit features of MetS. For instance, a study by Rejnmark et al. (2006) found that low intake of vitamin D-rich foods was associated with increased waist circumference and higher fasting glucose levels in adults. Similarly, a study conducted in Spain observed that children with lower vitamin D intake had higher body mass index (BMI) and increased markers of insulin resistance (Rodríguez-Rodríguez et al., 2009).

Despite these associations, dietary intake alone may not significantly impact serum 25(OH)D levels due to factors such as absorption efficiency and individual differences in metabolism. Therefore, supplementation studies have been pivotal in understanding the role of vitamin D intake on MetS.

### 3.5 Vitamin D Level Studies

Several observational studies have assessed serum 25(OH)D levels in relation to MetS and its components. A consistent finding across these studies is the inverse relationship between vitamin D status and the prevalence of MetS.

Cheng et al. (2010) conducted a study within the Framingham Heart Study cohort and found that individuals with vitamin D deficiency had a higher prevalence of MetS. Similarly, Li et al. (2013) reported that lower serum 25(OH)D levels were significantly associated with increased risk of MetS among Chinese adults.

In a cross-sectional study involving U.S. adults, Ford et al. (2005) observed that participants with higher serum vitamin D levels had a lower prevalence of MetS. The study suggested that vitamin D might influence MetS components such as waist circumference, blood pressure, and glucose metabolism.

Moreover, Alemzadeh et al. (2008) found that obese children and adolescents with hypovitaminosis D had higher insulin resistance, suggesting a potential role of vitamin D in pediatric MetS.

### 3.6 Vitamin D Levels Meta-Analyses

Meta-analyses have consolidated the evidence linking vitamin D deficiency with MetS. Ju et al. (2014) performed a dose-response meta-analysis of observational studies and concluded that each 25 nmol/L increase in serum 25(OH)D was associated with a 13% reduced risk of MetS.

Another meta-analysis by Hajhashemy et al. (2021) included 43 studies and confirmed the inverse association between serum vitamin D levels and the risk of MetS. The analysis indicated that individuals with the highest vitamin D levels had a significantly lower risk of MetS compared to those with the lowest levels.

These meta-analyses underscore the potential protective role of adequate vitamin D status against the development of MetS.

### 3.7 Vitamin D Clinical Trials

Randomized controlled trials (RCTs) have been conducted to evaluate the effects of vitamin D supplementation on MetS and its individual components. The results, however, have been somewhat mixed.

Zittermann et al. (2009) demonstrated that vitamin D supplementation (3,320 IU/day) in overweight individuals enhanced the beneficial effects of weight loss on cardiovascular disease risk markers, including a reduction in triglycerides. Similarly, Dong et al. (2010) reported that vitamin D supplementation (2,000 IU/day) for 16 weeks improved arterial stiffness in overweight African American adolescents.

Conversely, Lemieux et al. (2019) found that 6-month vitamin D supplementation (5,000 IU/day) did not significantly improve insulin sensitivity or secretion in overweight adults. Additionally, a study by de Boer et al. (2019) concluded that vitamin D supplementation did not reduce the risk of developing type 2 diabetes in individuals with prediabetes.

These conflicting results suggest that while vitamin D supplementation may benefit certain aspects of MetS, its overall efficacy may depend on factors such as baseline vitamin D status, dosage, duration of supplementation, and individual variability.

### 3.8 Vitamin D Opposing Data

Not all studies support the beneficial role of vitamin D in MetS. Some RCTs and observational studies have reported no significant association between vitamin D supplementation and improvement in MetS components.

For instance, a study by Salehpour et al. (2012) showed that vitamin D supplementation (50,000 IU/week) for 12 weeks did not lead to significant changes in body fat mass among overweight and obese women. Similarly, the ViDA study (Scragg et al., 2017) found that monthly high-dose vitamin D supplementation did not prevent cardiovascular disease.

Moreover, some meta-analyses have suggested that vitamin D supplementation does not significantly affect glycemic control or insulin resistance in individuals without vitamin D deficiency (Seida et al., 2014). These discrepancies highlight the need for further research to elucidate the contexts in which vitamin D supplementation may be beneficial.

### 3.9 Vitamin D Conclusion

The current body of evidence suggests a significant association between vitamin D deficiency and the prevalence of MetS and its components. Vitamin D appears to influence insulin secretion, insulin sensitivity, lipid metabolism, blood pressure regulation, and inflammatory responses—all critical factors in the pathogenesis of MetS.

While observational studies and meta-analyses generally support the beneficial role of adequate vitamin D status, clinical trials have yielded mixed results regarding the efficacy of vitamin D supplementation in improving MetS outcomes. Factors such as baseline vitamin D levels, dosage, duration, and individual differences may account for these inconsistencies.

Further large-scale, well-designed RCTs are necessary to determine the optimal vitamin D supplementation strategies for preventing and managing MetS. In the meantime, ensuring sufficient vitamin D levels through safe sun exposure, diet, and supplementation as needed remains a prudent approach for overall health benefits.
