Every cell in our body contains a unique instruction manual known as DNA. Think of DNA as a recipe book that tells our body how to function, grow, and develop. **Epigenetics: The Bookmarks of Our DNA**

Imagine you're reading a cookbook, and you use bookmarks to mark the recipes you want to try. Similarly, our cells use "bookmarks" called epigenetic tags to mark which parts of the DNA should be read and which parts should be skipped. These tags can change based on various factors, leading to different types of cells. For example, a skin cell reads the part of the DNA about skin, while a brain cell reads the brain section.

---

**Page 2: How Our Environment Influences Our DNA**

Our environment and experiences can add or remove these epigenetic bookmarks. Factors like diet, stress, and exposure to toxins can change where these tags are placed. For instance, if you smoke, certain harmful tags might be added to your DNA, potentially leading to health issues.

These tags don't change the DNA itself but influence how it's read. It's like adding or removing bookmarks from our cookbook. This can affect how genes are expressed, meaning whether they're turned "on" or "off", and how they function.

**Intrinsic vs. Extrinsic Age**

- **Intrinsic Age**: This is your biological age based on your DNA and how it's read. It's like the "core" age of your body, regardless of external factors.
  
- **Extrinsic Age**: This takes into account external factors like sun exposure or smoking, which can add or remove epigenetic tags. It's how old your body appears due to both your DNA and your environment.

At the ends of our DNA strands are tiny protective caps called telomeres. Think of them as the plastic tips on shoelaces. As we age, these telomeres shorten. If they become too short, the DNA can be damaged, similar to how a shoelace frays without its plastic tip.

**Intrinsic Epigenetic Age (IEAA)**:
- Intrinsic Epigenetic Age is a measure of "pure" epigenetic aging effects that are not influenced by differences in blood cell counts. It factors out changes in cell types that occur as we age. For instance, as we age, our immune system becomes weaker and less functional, a phenomenon known as immunosenescence. This is often observed in the blood by having fewer naive T cells and a higher number of senescent T cells. IEAA takes into account the DNA of these cells that are found in unequal proportions as we age by factoring out the change of these cell types [Pages: 10].

**Extrinsic Epigenetic Age (EEAA)**:
- Extrinsic Epigenetic Age takes into account both age-related changes in blood cell composition and intrinsic epigenetic changes. It factors immune cells back into the equation. This means that EEAA tracks both the inherent aging process and the changes in blood cell composition that occur with age. Extrinsic measures seem to be better suited for assessing age-related decline of tissue performance as they exhibit stronger predictive associations with time to death than intrinsic measures of age acceleration [Pages: 10,11].

- Additionally, the measure of extrinsic age acceleration reflects aspects of immunosenescence. By its design, it correlates with age-related changes in blood cell composition, such as changes in T lymphocyte populations, which underlie much of the age-related decline in the protective immune response [Pages: 62,63].

In simpler terms:
- **Intrinsic Age** is like the core aging process of your body, based purely on epigenetic changes without considering changes in blood cell composition.
  
- **Extrinsic Age** considers both the core aging process and the changes in blood cell composition that happen as we age, making it more reflective of the overall aging process influenced by external factors.
