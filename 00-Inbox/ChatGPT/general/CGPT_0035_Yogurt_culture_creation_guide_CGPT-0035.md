## 🧫 1. Microbiological Principles

### a. Core yogurt microbes
Traditional yogurt depends on the symbiosis between:
- **Lactobacillus delbrueckii subsp. bulgaricus**
- **Streptococcus thermophilus**

They form a balanced pair: *Streptococcus* starts rapid acidification; *Lactobacillus* follows, producing texture and aroma.

### b. Additional species (from cheeses and probiotics)
Grated cheeses (especially aged goat or sheep types) contain secondary lactic acid bacteria:
- *Lactobacillus casei, Lb. plantarum, Lb. brevis*
- *Leuconostoc* species
- *Enterococcus faecium* (sometimes present, not always desirable)
These can bring complex flavor and slightly thicker texture, but you must manage pH to avoid bitterness or contamination.

Probiotic capsules usually add:
- *Lactobacillus acidophilus*, *Bifidobacterium bifidum / longum*, *L. rhamnosus*, *L. reuteri*, *L. plantarum*, etc.
Some of these are oxygen-sensitive and will only survive if pH drops moderately (≈ 4.4–4.6).

Your goal is to let the thermophiles build the base, then let mesophiles and probiotics survive the cool-down phase.

## 🥛 2. Ingredient Structure

| Ingredient | Function | Notes |
|-------------|-----------|-------|
| **Goat’s milk** (1 L) | Main substrate | Heat first to 82–85 °C for 20 min to denature whey proteins → stable gel. |
| **Yogurt A** (classic cow or goat) | Starter with *S. thermophilus* + *L. bulgaricus* | Use 2 tbsp (≈ 30 g) per liter. |
| **Yogurt B** (probiotic style, e.g. “bio” yogurt) | Adds *L. acidophilus* / *Bifidobacterium* | 2 tbsp (≈ 30 g). |
| **Grated cheeses** (mild + aged, 1–2 tbsp each) | Introduces flavor bacteria and peptides | Finely grate, add **after heating but before inoculation**, when milk is 42 °C; they help flavor but may raise salt — small amount only. |
| **Probiotic capsules** (2–3) | Add strain diversity | Open and mix in at 42 °C. Prefer strains labeled viable at body temp. |

## 🌡️ 3. Step-by-Step Fermentation Protocol

1. **Heat**
   - Warm 1 L goat milk to **82–85 °C** for 20 min (gentle simmer). Stir to avoid scorching.
   - This kills competing microbes and unfolds milk proteins for better coagulation.

2. **Cool**
   - Let temperature fall to **42–43 °C** (thermophilic range).

3. **Inoculate**
   - Whisk in yogurts A & B thoroughly.
   - Add grated cheeses (1 tbsp mild, 1 tbsp aged).
   - Stir until smooth.
   - Finally, add the contents of 2–3 probiotic capsules (open, sprinkle, stir gently).

4. **Ferment**
   - Maintain **42 °C for 6–8 h** (e.g. yogurt maker, oven with light on, or thermos).  
     Avoid higher than 45 °C — you’ll kill the bifidobacteria.
   - You may notice slight separation if left longer — that’s fine; means good acidification.

5. **Stabilize**
   - When curd sets (jiggles like soft pudding), cool rapidly to **room temp** for 30 min, then refrigerate **≥ 8 h** before eating.
   - This maturation phase allows secondary bacteria to stabilize and pH to balance.

---

## 🧬 4. Creating “Your Own Strain”

If you want to **maintain this ecosystem** for future batches:

1. **Reserve 3–4 tbsp** of the finished yogurt.
2. Store in sterile glass at 2–4 °C, up to 7 days.
3. Use that as starter for the next batch (1 L milk → 3 tbsp starter).  
   Repeat up to 5 generations before reseeding with a capsule again.

Over time, a **stable consortium** will form, often dominated by:
- *L. delbrueckii subsp. bulgaricus*
- *S. thermophilus*
- Residual *L. acidophilus* / *Bifidobacterium*
- Small presence of *L. casei / L. plantarum* (from cheese)

You can characterize this later (if you wish) via pH curve (final pH ≈ 4.4), viscosity, or smell (buttery, clean acidity = healthy).

## ⚗️ 5. Troubleshooting

| Problem | Cause | Solution |
|----------|--------|----------|
| Thin or watery | Too low temp / too short fermentation | Increase time or temp slightly. |
| Bitter taste | Overacidification or proteolytic cheese bacteria | Reduce aged cheese next time. |
| Slimy / stringy | Excess *Lactobacillus delbrueckii* or contamination | Shorter ferment, stricter hygiene. |
| No set | Starter dead / low viable cells | Fresh yogurt or new probiotics. |
