Below is a concise, evidence-based set of studies (with design, population, and main finding) where **higher LDL-C (or higher cholesterol closely tracking LDL-C) was associated with *lower* all-cause mortality or with longer survival—i.e., a positive correlation with longevity—primarily in older adults.** I also note key caveats.

### Systematic review (elderly)
- **Ravnskov et al., 2016 (BMJ Open)** — Systematic review of **19 cohort studies, n≈68,000, age ≥60**. In **14/19 cohorts**, *higher LDL-C was inversely associated with all-cause mortality*; in the remainder there was no association. Authors conclude that elderly individuals with higher LDL-C lived as long or longer than those with lower LDL-C. citeturn0search0turn0search4turn0search8turn0search20

### Large cohorts of “oldest old” / very elderly
- **Leiden 85-plus Study (The Lancet 1997; JAMA Intern Med 2003 analyses)** — In participants **≥85 y**, *higher total cholesterol* (which, at these ages, tracks LDL-C more than triglyceride-rich fractions) was associated with **lower all-cause mortality**, mainly via fewer deaths from infection and cancer; subsequent analysis reported **both low LDL-C and low HDL-C associated with increased mortality** in this age group. citeturn0search6turn0search22
- **Honolulu Heart Program (The Lancet, 2001)** — **3,572 men aged 71–93 y** with ~20-year follow-up: *persistently low cholesterol* over time was linked to **higher mortality**; conversely, **higher** or non-low levels were **protective** for survival. (LDL-C was not isolated, but in this age band LDL is the dominant component of TC.) citeturn0search7turn0search3turn0search23

### Additional observational signals consistent with the above
- **Zhou et al., 2020 (BMJ Open)** — Narrative synthesis notes **inverse associations between LDL-C and all-cause mortality** in several diseased/older cohorts (e.g., chronic heart failure, CKD), reinforcing that in certain high-age or high-burden states, lower LDL-C is **not** associated with better survival. citeturn0search16
- **Recent centenarian/long-liver literature** — Population studies of exceptional longevity frequently report a **“cholesterol paradox”** in the oldest old, where **higher cholesterol associates with longer survival**; an example from Sardinia discusses this paradox explicitly in a long-lived population. (These papers often analyze TC/LDL patterns rather than isolated LDL-C interventions.) citeturn0search1

## Important counterpoints and interpretation
- **Genetic evidence disagrees**: Mendelian-randomization work (e.g., **Postmus et al., 2015**) finds that *lifelong genetically higher LDL-C increases mortality risk even into old age*, and that a “beneficial LDL genetic risk profile” tracks with familial longevity. This suggests that **observational inverse associations in the elderly can reflect reverse causation (frailty, inflammation, malignancy lowering LDL-C), survivor bias, or confounding**, rather than a protective biological effect of LDL itself. citeturn0search10turn0search14
- **Age matters**: The inverse or null LDL-mortality associations occur **mainly after ~75–85 y**. In middle age, the preponderance of evidence links **higher LDL/ApoB to higher ASCVD risk**; the “paradox” emerges in the **oldest old**, where competing risks and non-ASCVD mortality dominate. (See the cohorts above.) citeturn0search6turn0search7
- **Mechanistic hypotheses (observational)**: LDL participates in **innate immunity** (binding/endotoxin neutralization) and may correlate with **nutritional status**; very low LDL-C in elders can mark **malnutrition, chronic inflammation, cancer, or infection**—conditions that drive mortality independent of ASCVD. The Leiden and Honolulu findings (fewer infection/cancer deaths with higher cholesterol) are consistent with this. citeturn0search6turn0search7

## Bottom line
- There **are** credible observational studies—especially in the **elderly and “oldest old”**—showing that **higher LDL-C (or higher cholesterol dominated by LDL) associates with *lower* all-cause mortality and longer survival**. Exemplars include **Ravnskov et al. 2016**, **Leiden 85-plus**, and the **Honolulu Heart Program**. citeturn0search0turn0search6turn0search7  
- However, **genetic and life-course data** indicate that **lifelong higher LDL-C shortens lifespan**, so the late-life inverse associations likely reflect **reverse causation, survivor bias, and competing risks** rather than LDL being longevity-promoting per se. For clinical inference, **context (age, comorbidity, ApoB, inflammatory status)** is crucial. citeturn0search10
