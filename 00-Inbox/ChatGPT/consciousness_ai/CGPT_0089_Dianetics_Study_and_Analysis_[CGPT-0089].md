# L. Ron Hubbard’s Foundational 1950s Works: Detailed Analysis

## **Dianetics: The Modern Science of Mental Health (1950)**  
**Title & Context:** *Dianetics* (published May 1950) is Hubbard’s seminal work that launched his mental health philosophy. Written after his experiences in hypnosis and with WWII veterans, it marks the beginning of the Dianetics movement. Hubbard framed it as a scientific breakthrough in psychology, promising a new therapy that could eradicate psychosomatic illness and aberrant behavior. This book became a bestseller and the cornerstone of Hubbard’s developmental arc, introducing key concepts (the **reactive mind**, **engrams**, the **Clear**) that all subsequent works would build upon. It represents Hubbard’s transition from a pulp fiction writer to a self-proclaimed explorer of the mind. The context is post-WWII optimism in science; Hubbard presents Dianetics as a “modern science” of the mind, positioning it in contrast to Freudian psychoanalysis and psychiatry of the time.

**Key Principles and Notable Quotes (Tone, Intention, Perception, Auditing):**  
- **The Reactive Mind and Survival Intention:** At its core, *Dianetics* posits that the mind’s dominant drive is **survival** across four “dynamics” (self, family, groups, mankind). Human nature is fundamentally rational and good, but aberrated by the reactive mind’s engrams (traumatic memory recordings). Even irrational or destructive acts, Hubbard argues, stem from a misdirected intention to survive. *“Aberrated conduct is irrational survival conduct and is fully intended to lead to survival. That the intent is not the act does not eradicate the intent.”*【29†L24-L28】 In other words, a criminal or neurotic is still deep down trying to **achieve a survival goal**, however twisted their actions; the basic **intention** is not evil, but the reactive mind causes a distorted outcome. This reflects Hubbard’s optimistic view that freeing the person from engrams will restore their inherently good intentions. He even declares “that basic nature has been found uniformly and invariably to be good. That is now an established scientific fact, not an opinion”【9†L17-L20】 – a bold claim underscoring Dianetics’ humanistic philosophy (though this “fact” was based on Hubbard’s personal observations, not mainstream science). 

- **Engrams and Auditing (Therapeutic Method):** Hubbard identifies **engrams** (unconscious traumatic memories recorded with full sensory detail during moments of pain/unconsciousness) as the sole source of mental aberration and psychosomatic ills. The therapeutic process to eliminate them is called **Dianetic auditing**. Auditing in Dianetics is done in a relaxed, focused state Hubbard termed “dianetic reverie” – similar to a light trance. The **auditor** (practitioner) guides the patient (called a “pre-clear”) to revisit and relive past incidents along the time track of memory. *“The technique of therapy is done in what is called a dianetic reverie. The individual undergoing this process sits or lies in a quiet room accompanied by a friend or professional therapist who acts as auditor. The auditor directs the attention of the patient to the patient’s self and then begins to place the patient in various periods of the patient’s life merely by telling him to go there rather than remember.”*【8†L52-L60】 In Dianetic theory, **re-experiencing** these engrams causes the reactive mind to lift its influence, allowing the analytical mind to re-file the memory without its painful charge. This often involves the patient recounting an incident multiple times until it no longer triggers distress – a process modern observers note has parallels to exposure therapy or abreaction. Notably, Hubbard insisted this is done *without* hypnosis or drugs, and with the patient **fully conscious** (able to talk to the auditor). The auditor’s role is to keep the person on track “re-experiencing” the engram in detail until it is fully discharged. This approach was novel at the time for lay therapy; *Dianetics* spawned countless “auditor” amateurs who paired up to practice this regression technique. 

- **Emotional Discharge and Tone:** While *Dianetics* does not formalize the **Tone Scale** (that comes in *Science of Survival*), it introduces the idea of emotional **tones** changing as engrams are relieved. Hubbard observed that as a painful memory is run out, a pre-clear often swings from negative emotion to laughter and relief. *“Any engram which really releases may be expected to begin somewhere between tears and boredom and end with laughter; the nearer the engram’s tone is to tears at the first contact, the more certainly laughter will appear as it is relieved.”*【2†L38-L42】 The laughter, according to Hubbard, is the physiological discharge of painful emotion and a sign the engram’s hold is broken. After fully relieving a trauma, the person’s emotional attitude about that incident rises to a neutral or positive tone: *“the situation, no matter how gruesome it was, when relieved, is in all its aspects a subject of great mirth… he can be said to be ‘Tone 3’ about it.”*【2†L59-L67】 Here Hubbard casually uses “Tone 3” (on a scale where 4.0 is enthusiasm, 0 is death) to describe a moderately cheerful state about a formerly upsetting memory. This laid groundwork for the Tone Scale concept. **Tone**, in Dianetics, thus refers to one’s emotional condition; effective auditing should raise a person’s chronic tone level by clearing out grief, fear, apathy, etc. Early Dianeticists took note that as their pre-clears progressed, they laughed more and exhibited more positive outlook – evidence, to them, of therapy success. (Hubbard even quips that “laughter is the relief of painful emotion”【2†L71-L72】 and cites folklore of laughter curing melancholy.)

- **Perception and the Clear:** *Dianetics* paints an ambitious picture of the end goal state called **Clear** – an individual freed of all engrams. A Clear, per Hubbard, possesses vastly heightened perception, memory, and rationality. The **analytical mind** (the conscious, thinking mind) is no longer impeded by reactive recordings, so it operates like a perfect computer. Hubbard claims a Clear has full recall of his entire life’s experiences with vivid perceptual detail. *“Cleared, ‘I’ is able to reach all moments of his lifetime without exertion or discomfort and perceive all he has ever sensed, recalling them in full motion, color, sound, **tone** and other senses.”*【8†L73-L80】 In other words, nothing which was ever perceived is truly forgotten; the Clear can remember any fact or event (down to the color of the auditor’s tie on a given day) if needed. This almost photographic memory and heightened **perception** is a signature ability of the Clear in Dianetics. Hubbard describes the mind as capable of recording every perception even when unconscious. For example, an engram from surgery under anesthesia might contain the sounds, smells, and exact words spoken in the operating room – all stored verbatim in the reactive bank【17†L119-L128】【17†L131-L138】. The Clear would ostensibly regain access to all such data at will. This reveals Hubbard’s view of the mind as an *almost infallible recorder* (when not blocked by trauma), and of human potential – he talks of IQ boosts, improved senses, even suggesting eyesight can improve when the mind is clear of aberrations【10†L255-L262】. While such superlative claims were not verified, they set a tone of lofty expectation. The concept of **perceiving with full sensory richness** also extends to how auditing works: the pre-clear in reverie is asked to re-experience incidents with all five senses (“What do you see? What do you hear?…”) to **increase awareness** and contact with those memories. Thus, sharpening perception is both a tool and outcome in Dianetics.

**Clinical and Personal Utility:** Despite its pseudoscientific framing, *Dianetics* contains elements a practitioner or individual can find useful: 

- *Cathartic Trauma Processing:* Dianetic auditing pioneered a form of **guided trauma reprocessing**. The idea of repeatedly recounting a traumatic memory to reduce its emotional impact is broadly akin to techniques used in modern trauma therapies (like Prolonged Exposure in PTSD treatment). A clinician might recognize that having a client revisit and **desensitize** painful memories can indeed relieve symptoms – a principle Hubbard built upon (though he lacked the safeguards therapists today use). Dianetics provided a structured, if simplistic, method for laypeople to attempt this kind of abreaction.
