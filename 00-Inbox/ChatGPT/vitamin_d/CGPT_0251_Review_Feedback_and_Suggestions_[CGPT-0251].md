**5.2 Calcium Absorption and the Role of Vitamin D, Vitamin K2, and Magnesium**

Calcium is a critical mineral required for various physiological processes, including bone mineralization, muscle contraction, and nerve transmission. Optimal calcium absorption from the diet necessitates the presence of vitamin D, which enhances intestinal calcium uptake by stimulating the expression of calcium-binding proteins in enterocytes (Christakos et al., 2016). However, for calcium to be effectively utilized and directed to the bones, vitamin K2 and magnesium are also essential.

Vitamin K2 activates osteocalcin, a protein synthesized by osteoblasts, through a process called gamma-carboxylation. Activated osteocalcin binds calcium ions and incorporates them into the bone matrix, enhancing bone strength and density (Booth et al., 2013). Magnesium serves as a cofactor for enzymes involved in vitamin D metabolism and is crucial for the conversion of vitamin D into its active form. It also plays a role in parathyroid hormone (PTH) secretion and action, further influencing calcium homeostasis (Gröber et al., 2015). Without adequate magnesium, vitamin D activation is impaired, and calcium absorption is suboptimal, underscoring the necessity of these nutrients working in concert.

**5.3 Calcium Metabolism and Bone Health: Roles of PTH, Osteocalcin, Vitamin D, Vitamin K2, Magnesium, and Cobalt**

Calcium metabolism is tightly regulated by hormonal control, primarily involving PTH and osteocalcin. PTH is secreted by the parathyroid glands in response to low blood calcium levels, stimulating osteoclast activity to release calcium from bones and increasing renal reabsorption of calcium (Fowler, 2008). Vitamin D enhances the effects of PTH by increasing intestinal calcium absorption and promoting bone resorption when necessary.
