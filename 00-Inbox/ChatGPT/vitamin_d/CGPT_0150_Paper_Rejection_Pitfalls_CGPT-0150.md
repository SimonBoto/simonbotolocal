### **2. Cohen’s d: What Does It Mean and How Is It Measured?**  
**Cohen’s d** is a statistical measure that quantifies the **effect size** (i.e., how strong a difference is between two groups). Unlike a P-value, which only tells whether a result is statistically significant, **Cohen’s d tells us if the difference is meaningful.**  

**Formula for Cohen’s d:**  

\[
d = \frac{\text{Mean}_{\text{Treatment}} - \text{Mean}_{\text{Control}}}{\text{Pooled Standard Deviation}}
\]

where:  
- \( \text{Mean}_{\text{Treatment}} \) = Mean telomerase activation in treated cells  
- \( \text{Mean}_{\text{Control}} \) = Mean telomerase activation in untreated cells  
- **Pooled Standard Deviation (SD)** = a measure of variability across both groups:

\[
SD_{\text{pooled}} = \sqrt{\frac{(SD_1^2 + SD_2^2)}{2}}
\]

✅ **Why Cohen’s d Matters:**  
- A **statistically significant result (low P-value) does not mean the effect is strong.**  
- Cohen’s d quantifies the **magnitude of the effect** and tells us **how biologically meaningful it is**.  

**Interpreting Cohen’s d:**  

| **Effect Size (Cohen’s d)** | **Interpretation** |
|-----------------------------|---------------------|
| **0.2 – 0.3** | Small effect |
| **0.5 – 0.8** | Medium effect |
| **> 0.8** | Large effect |
