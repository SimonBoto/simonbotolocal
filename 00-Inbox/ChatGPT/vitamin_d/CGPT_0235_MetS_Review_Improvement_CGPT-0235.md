Chapter 2: Metabolic Syndrome

2.1 Definition and Diagnostic Criteria of Metabolic Syndrome
Metabolic syndrome (MetS)  is characterized by a cluster of commonly coexisting metabolic imbalances, which contribute to increased risk of several chronic diseases, among which cardiovascular disease (CVD) and type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2DM). Thus its early diagnosis and effective management are pivotal for public health and clinical practice, especially when considering the 21st century’s public health challenges. 
In 1988, Reaven first proposed the diagnostic criteria for “Syndrome X”, focusing on insulin resistance, dyslipidemia and hypertension (Reaven, 1988). The World Health Organisation (WHO) later provided the first formal definition of MetS in 1998, emphasizing on insulin resistance as the major underlying risk factor. For diagnosis the presence of insulin resistance was required, along with two additional risk factors: obesity, hypertension, elevated triglycerides, decreased HDL, microalbuminuria (Alberti, Zimmet & WHO, 1998). The next major update was presented by the “Third Report of National Cholesterol Education Program Adult Treatment Panel III (ATP III)” (2002). The ATP III criteria did not require any single factor's presence as obligatory, but for the diagnosis three out of five factors were needed (abdominal obesity, elevated triglycerides, decreased HDL, hypertension, elevated fasting glucose or T2DM) (NCEP ATP III Final Report, 2002). Today, after multiple updates and slight variations, the 2005 International Diabetes Federation (IDF) and the 2005 National Cholesterol Education Program Adult Treatment Panel III (NCEP ATP III) are most commonly used (Alberti et al., 2009). However, disagreement remains on waist circumference threshold for defining abdominal obesity, with IDF recommending ≥ 94 cm for men and ≥ 80 cm for women, while the American Heart Association (AHA), in contrast, sets cut points o f≥ 102 and ≥ 88 cm respectively (Eckel, Grundy & Zimmet, 2005).

 Obesity had already reached epidemic proportions 10 years ago, with up-to-date research estimating that 1.4 billion people are obese, with the numbers growing bigger each year (O’Neill & O’Driscoll, 2015). MetS has been rising worldwide, parallel to the trends of the obesity pandemic., indicative of the deterioration of public health in developed countries. 2.3 Pathophysiology of Metabolic Syndrome
Metabolic syndrome exhibits a complex, multifactorial, and multisystem pathophysiology that affects nearly every known system of the body. Central obesity, insulin resistance and hormonal imbalances are the primary drivers of the syndrome's development.

2.3.1 Central Obesity 
Obesity is fundamental to MetS and it appears to precede the emergence of other risk factors (O’Neill & O’Driscoll, 2015). Obesity is defined by excess body fat and measured by BMI (≥ 30 kg/m^2) or WC (≥ 102 cm in men and WC ≥ 88 cm in women), representing a later stage of overweight (Lean et al., 1995). 
Adipose tissue, and especially visceral fat, is not merely for passive energy storage, but is an endocrine organ with important metabolic functions, which produces various bioactive peptides (Després & Lemieux, 2006). In MetS, excess in visceral fats results in dysregulation of adipokines and cytokines further promoting insulin resistance and systemic inflammation (Kahn et al., 2006). Adiponectin, interleukin-6 (IL-6), tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-alpha), leptin, and angiotensinogen are the most important among these (Lehr et al., 2012; Berg & Scherer, 2005; Grundy, 2016). Furthermore, adiposity has been found to influence the circulating levels, metabolism and end-target action of fat soluble vitamins (Shea et al. 2010). The interconnectedness of the components of MetS, in this case obesity with inflammation is already becoming apparent. 

2.3.2 Insulin Resistance and Dyslipidemia
Insulin, a peptide hormone, is produced and secreted by the pancreatic beta cells in response to high levels of blood glucose. Insulin resistance(IR), a major hallmark of MetS, refers to the defective ability of cells to respond to insulin's action, leading to hyperglycemia and increased insulin production (Kahn et al., 2006). IR also impairs fat metabolism, increases the flux of free fatty acids (FFA), leading to dyslipidemia, marked by elevated triglycerides (TG) and low HDL (Taskinen, 2003). The interplay between insulin resistance, altered adipokine profiles, and hormonal imbalances leads to significant disruptions in glucose and lipid metabolism (Kolovou et al., 2005), with these alterations in lipoprotein balance and concentration constituting the basis of the atherogenic dyslipidemia, characteristic of both IR and MetS (Grundy, 1998).

2.3.3 Hypertension 
Hypertension is also a core component of MetS affecting 85% of the patients diagnosed with the syndrome (Ginsberg, 2000; Sowers, 2004). It results from insulin resistance-induced sympathetic system stimulation, vasoconstriction, and endothelial dysfunction (Grassi et al., 2005). These effects are partly attributed to insulin’s diminished vasodilatory effect, FFA-induced vasoconstriction from increased reactive oxygen species (ROS) and the scavenging of nitric oxide (NO) (Tripathy et al., 2003).

2.3.4 Inflammatory Pathways and Adipokines
The pathogenic pathways mentioned earlier, such as insulin resistance, dyslipidemia, and obesity, induce systemic oxidative stress and activate the downstream inflammatory pathways. This activation contributes to the elevation of inflammatory markers in MetS, further increasing the risk for fibrosis and atherogenesis. 
Adipose tissue's role in the development of obesity-induced inflammation is integral, by elevating cytokines and chemokines and dysregulating adipokines, such as tumor necrosis factor-α (TNF-α), interleukin (IL)-1, IL-6, IL-8, monocyte chemoattractant protein-1 (MCP-1), leptin, PAI-1, retinol binding protein 4 (RBP4), chemerin, serum amyloid A (SSA), C-reactive protein (CRP), lipopolysaccharide-binding protein (LBP), fetuin A, and decreased adiponectin and omentin-1 (Jialal & Devaraj, 2018; Jung & Choi, 2014).
