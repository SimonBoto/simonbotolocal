# The Synergy of Vitamins D and K2 on Metabolic Syndrome and Its Components

## Introduction on Synergy

Nutrient synergy refers to the concept that the combined effects of two or more nutrients working together have a greater physiological impact on the body than when each nutrient is consumed individually (Davidson & Howarth, 2007; Jacobs et al., 2011, 2013; Jacobs et al., 2003, 2009; Singh et al., 2022). In other words, "the whole is greater than the sum of the parts" (Singh et al., 2022). While traditional nutrition science often employs a reductionist approach by isolating single nutrients to study their effects (Fardet & Rock, 2014; Singh et al., 2022), it is increasingly recognized that nutrients interact in complex ways, and their combined consumption can lead to additive or even synergistic effects (Davidson & Howarth, 2007; Wagner & Ulrich-Merzenich, 2009).

In pharmacology, synergy is discussed in various ways, such as through the enhancement of drug absorption, distribution, metabolism, or elimination (ADME) (Roell et al., 2017; Wagner & Ulrich-Merzenich, 2009). Changes in ADME result from interactions between drugs that affect different biological targets in the body, such as enzymes, receptors, or ion channels (Roell et al., 2017; Wagner & Ulrich-Merzenich, 2009). Synergy in pharmacology has also been interpreted more broadly, where ingredients that target differing physiological pathways work together for a greater physiological impact (Singh et al., 2022).

One aspect of synergy is antagonism, where one nutrient affects the uptake and availability of other nutrients in the body (Roell et al., 2017). However, the focus here is on the positive interactions that lead to enhanced physiological outcomes.

Traditionally, the study of single nutrients has been valuable in understanding nutrient deficiencies and their prevention, such as vitamin D to prevent rickets, vitamin C to prevent scurvy, or folic acid to prevent neural tube defects (Murphy et al., 2016; Tulchinsky, 2010). The Dietary Reference Intakes (DRIs) were established to provide nutrient reference values (Murphy et al., 2016). However, one shortcoming of the DRI framework is that it does not account for the additive effects some nutrients possess when consumed concurrently (National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine, 2021). The Food and Nutrition Board has acknowledged the need to examine evidence for nutrient "clusters" for all DRI nutrients, considering these nutrients are not consumed in isolation and have metabolic interactions (National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine, 2021).

## D + K2 Physiology

### D and K2 as Pleiotropic Factors

In their most active forms, both vitamins D and K2 are now known to be relatively pleiotropic factors due to their diverse mechanisms and consequent broadened clinical scope that eclipses their classic vitamin effects (Kidd, 2010). Most studies included during the present qualitative analysis showed that the combination of vitamin D and K2 intervention (sometimes with calcium as well) has an important and favorable outcome related to bone health as well as cardiovascular health when compared with single nutrient supplementation (Singh et al., 2022). Previous analyses indicated that sufficient vitamin D or K alone are associated with survival benefits and reduced cardiovascular disease (Meems et al., 2016; Riphagen et al., 2017).

### D Cofactors

It is well known that the metabolism and levels of different vitamin D metabolites, particularly calcitriol (the active or hormonic form of vitamin D), are influenced by numerous substances—parathormone (PTH), fibroblast growth factor-23, serum calcium (Ca) levels, magnesium (Mg), etc. Interestingly, zinc (Zn) and vitamin K2 can also have some "crosstalk" with vitamin D; however, this is less discussed in the scientific literature compared to PTH's or Mg's roles (Bleizgys, 2024).

For normal vitamin D metabolism and activity, an optimal Mg status is required. Mg acts as a cofactor in various processes, such as vitamin D binding to vitamin D-binding protein, 25-hydroxyvitamin D synthesis, calcitriol synthesis, activity of 24-hydroxylase (the enzyme deactivating 25-hydroxyvitamin D and calcitriol), and vitamin D receptor (VDR) expression for cellular effects (Matias et al., 2023). Severe Mg deficiency could contribute to reduced renal synthesis of calcitriol since PTH stimulates renal 1α-hydroxylase. Mg deficiency notably influences vitamin D levels in people with a high risk of low vitamin D, such as women, non-Hispanic African Americans, obese individuals, or persons with the highest levels of PTH in the blood (Erem et al., 2019).

Mg deficiency might affect bone directly by reducing bone stiffness, increasing osteoclasts, and decreasing osteoblasts, and indirectly by interfering with PTH and vitamin D, promoting inflammation/oxidative stress and subsequent bone loss (Castiglioni et al., 2013).

It appears that Zn homeostasis and vitamin D functioning are linked. Zn can regulate the expression of vitamin D-dependent genes via contributing to VDR conformational changes and intensifying the activity of specific vitamin D-dependent promoters; therefore, Zn is considered an essential cofactor for vitamin D activity (Amos & Razzaque, 2022; Ahsan et al., 2023). On the other hand, vitamin D can directly augment the expression of Zn transporters, such as ZnT10; an upregulation of the ZnT10 protein allows Zn to migrate out of the cytosol, making increased concentrations available for extracellular use (Amos & Razzaque, 2022; Ahsan et al., 2023).

# The Synergy of Vitamins D and K2 in Human Physiology

## Introduction to Vitamin D

### Discovery and the Vitamin D Endocrine System

Vitamin D was discovered as a dietary constituent in cod liver oil that helped cure rickets, a bone disease characterized by impaired bone mineralization (Kidd, 2010; Norman, 2008). This discovery highlighted the essential role of vitamin D in bone health.

Circulating vitamin D undergoes a two-step enzymatic transformation to form a hormone that drives the sophisticated vitamin D endocrine system (VDES) (Kidd, 2010; Norman, 2008). The VDES encompasses the liver and kidneys for vitamin D metabolism, vitamin D receptors (VDRs), and feedback mechanisms that homeostatically regulate hormone output (Kidd, 2010; Norman et al., 2001).

### Definitions: Endocrine, Paracrine, and Intracrine Actions

- **Autocrine effect**: Occurs when a hormone exits the cell where it was produced and influences that same cell from the outside.
- **Paracrine effect**: Involves influencing neighboring cells without reliance on the circulation for transport.
- **Intracrine effect**: Occurs when the hormone acts within the same cell that produced it without first exiting the cell.

Evidence suggests that 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D (1,25D) likely has all three of these actions (Kidd, 2010).

### Vitamin D Receptor (VDR) and Vitamin D-Binding Protein (VDB)

The 1,25D hormone is a core component of the VDES and interacts with VDRs, which are closely related to the classic steroid-sensitive nuclear receptors located in the cell nucleus but are also distributed outside the nucleus (Kidd, 2010; Norman et al., 2001). VDRs bind 1,25D with high affinity and can weakly bind 25-hydroxyvitamin D (25D), initiating both genomic and non-genomic responses (Kidd, 2010; Mizwicki & Norman, 2009).

VDRs are nuclear transcription factors capable of binding directly to DNA and regulating gene expression. It is estimated that VDRs can regulate up to 500 of the approximately 20,488 genes in the human genome (Kidd, 2010; Carlberg, 2003). A minimum of 35 cell types carry VDRs, and at least 17 cell types express the 1α-hydroxylase enzyme, enabling them to produce biological responses to 1,25D (Kidd, 2010).

Another component of the VDES is the vitamin D-binding protein (VDB), which transports 1,25D to its target organs (Kidd, 2010; Bishop et al., 1994).

### Vitamin D and Calcium Homeostasis

Vitamin D is primarily involved in intestinal calcium absorption. The active form, 1,25D, enhances the synthesis of TRPV6 (a calcium channel) and increases the permeability of tight junctions (Casadei et al., 2021; Mosali et al., 2014). Vitamin D deficiency decreases the availability of calcium and leads to bone structural defects similar to those occurring in osteoporosis (Aguayo-Ruiz et al., 2020; Levin et al., 1976).

Administration of vitamin D promotes intestinal absorption of calcium and phosphorus, increases renal tubular reabsorption of calcium, increases the expression of calbindin, and accelerates calcium transport in the distal convoluted tubule of the nephron (Aguayo-Ruiz et al., 2020; Dusso et al., 2005).

### Regulatory Loops in Vitamin D Metabolism

Several feedback mechanisms regulate vitamin D metabolism:

- **Autoregulation**: As circulating 1,25D levels rise above a homeostatic threshold, it downregulates its own synthesis in the kidney (Kidd, 2010).
- **Stimulation of Breakdown**: Elevated 1,25D stimulates its own breakdown by inducing the enzyme that converts it to 24,25-dihydroxyvitamin D3 (Kidd, 2010).
- **Parathyroid Hormone (PTH)**: PTH stimulates kidney production of 1,25D; as PTH levels increase, so does 1,25D production (Slatopolsky et al., 1999a).
- **Fibroblast Growth Factor 23 (FGF23)**: FGF23 responds to elevated calcium or phosphate levels by decreasing 1,25D production in the kidney and downregulating PTH release (Yokota et al., 2009).
- **VDR Feedback**: VDR occupancy rates influence the synthesis and degradation of 1,25D (Kidd, 2010).

### Vitamin D Levels and Health Outcomes

Data from representative human studies indicate various thresholds for serum 25D levels associated with health outcomes (Kidd, 2010):

- **<8 ng/mL**: Classic vitamin D deficiency—rickets or osteomalacia (Institute of Medicine [IOM], 1997).
- **<17.8 ng/mL**: Increased risk for all-cause mortality (Melamed et al., 2008).
- **<24 ng/mL**: Greater risk of death in patients receiving intensive care; patients had three times greater mortality rate than those with serum 25D >60 ng/mL (Lee et al., 2009).
- **30 ng/mL**: Greater risk for depression among hospitalized subjects (Schneider et al., 2000).
- **32 ng/mL**: Threshold for optimal calcium absorption from the intestine (Heaney, 2008).
- **38 ng/mL**: Threshold for optimal neuromuscular performance (Bischoff-Ferrari et al., 2004).

In 2005, the level of 25D required for sufficiency was suggested to be 33–80 ng/mL (Grant & Holick, 2005).

### Vitamin D Intake Guidelines and Dosing

The current U.S. intake guidelines for vitamin D were developed by the Food and Nutrition Board of the IOM in 1997 and recommend:

- **5 μg/day (200 IU)**: From birth to age 50.
- **10 μg/day (400 IU)**: Ages 51–70.
- **15 μg/day (600 IU)**: Age 71 and older (IOM, 1997).

Dosage for supplementation may vary between subjects according to age, comorbidities, preference, absorption, and baseline 25D plasma levels. Suggested dosages range from approximately 800–2000 IU/day, and up to 4000 IU/day (Casadei et al., 2021; Holick et al., 2011; Pludowski et al., 2018).

### Safety of Vitamin D Supplementation

The safety of vitamin D therapy is mainly linked to dosage and the form of supplementation. Bodily self-regulation of vitamin D activation, efficiently preserved by using non-hydroxylated forms, minimizes toxicity risk (Casadei et al., 2021; Pludowski et al., 2018).

### Special Populations: Hospitalized and Intensive Care Patients

Vitamin D insufficiency is a major health problem among hospitalized patients and institutionalized elderly subjects. An evaluation of 290 consecutively admitted patients found that 57% were severely vitamin D deficient (25D <15 ng/mL) (Thomas et al., 1998).

For patients in intensive care in Sydney, Australia, Lee et al. (2009) found that 17% had undetectable serum 25D levels, and half were deficient or insufficient (serum 25D <24 ng/mL). They calculated that the mortality rate was three times higher in those with levels <24 ng/mL compared to those with sufficient levels (>60 ng/mL).

## Vitamin K2

### Metabolism and the Vitamin K Cycle

Vitamin K undergoes a cycle of oxidation and reduction tied to the γ-carboxylation of vitamin K-dependent (VKD) proteins (Kidd, 2010; Oldenburg et al., 2008). The fully reduced vitamin K hydroquinone donates electrons to γ-glutamyl carboxylase (GGCX), enabling carboxylation of specific proteins at targeted glutamate groups. The oxidized vitamin K is then recycled back to the reduced hydroquinone form by vitamin K epoxide reductase (VKOR), using electrons primarily drawn from NADH (Kidd, 2010; Oldenburg et al., 2008).

Humans can regenerate vitamin K via the vitamin K cycle, which is essential for activating 17 VKD proteins that regulate blood coagulation, bone metabolism, and arterial calcification (Bleizgys, 2024; Piscaer et al., 2023).

### Role in Bone Health

In bones, vitamin K acts via several pathways:

1. **Activation of Osteocalcin (OC)**: Vitamin K helps bind calcium in bones by forming γ-carboxyglutamate (Gla) residues on OC, which is synthesized only in osteoblasts (Bleizgys, 2024; Piscaer et al., 2023). Carboxylated OC contributes to the formation of hydroxyapatite crystals and prevents over-mineralization (Bleizgys, 2024).

2. **Stimulation of Osteoblastogenesis and Inhibition of Osteoclastogenesis**: Vitamin K2 stimulates osteoblast differentiation and inhibits osteoclastogenesis (Bleizgys, 2024; Skalny et al., 2024).

3. **Activation of Bone Signaling Pathways**: Vitamin K2 is involved in activating bone morphogenetic protein-2 (BMP-2) and Wnt/β-catenin signaling, important in bone formation and homeostasis (Skalny et al., 2024).

4. **Inhibition of Bone Resorption**: Specifically, MK-4 (a form of vitamin K2) can decrease cyclooxygenase-2 expression and prostaglandin E2 production during osteoclast development, potentially contributing to the inhibition of bone resorption (Bleizgys, 2024).

5. **Protection Against Oxidative Stress**: Vitamin K can protect bone tissue against reactive oxygen species produced by osteoblasts (Piscaer et al., 2023).

### Matrix Gla Protein (MGP) and Vascular Health

MGP is the VKD protein most closely linked to blood vessel wall health. It operates predominantly in the extracellular matrix of soft tissues (vascular, heart, lung, kidney, cartilage) to downregulate calcium deposition (Kidd, 2010; Oldenburg et al., 2008). MGP likely inhibits vascular calcification by directly binding calcium ions to sequester them, thereby restricting their incorporation into the extracellular matrix (Kidd, 2010; Proudfoot & Shanahan, 2006).

Data suggest that vitamin K could reduce the progression of vascular calcification and provide cardiovascular benefits (Casadei et al., 2021; Vossen et al., 2019).

### Periostin and Other VKD Proteins

Periostin is a VKD protein occurring in collagen-rich connective tissues, including bone. It carries multiple Gla residues and is produced in bone marrow mesenchymal stromal cells capable of differentiating into osteoblasts (Kidd, 2010; Coutu et al., 2008). Periostin is associated with the extracellular matrix, where it plays a role in mineralization and contributes to the development and modeling of periodontal ligaments, trabecular bones, and heart valves (Kidd, 2010; Snider et al., 2008).

### Safety of Vitamin K2 Supplementation

Vitamin K2 is safe and usually well tolerated (Casadei et al., 2021; Bresson, 2008). No adverse effects have been reported at typical supplemental doses.
