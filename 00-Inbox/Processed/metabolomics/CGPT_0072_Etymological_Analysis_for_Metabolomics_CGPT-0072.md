DNA is a **biopolymer composed of repeating nucleotides**, each consisting of a deoxyribose sugar, a phosphate group, and a nitrogenous base (adenine, thymine, guanine, or cytosine). DNA encodes the information required for the synthesis of proteins and regulatory RNAs, acting as the **central template for biological identity and continuity**.

---

2. **Biological Function & Physiology**:

DNA underpins multiple essential biological functions:

- **Genetic Information Storage**:  
  The sequence of nucleotide bases encodes genes, regulatory elements (promoters, enhancers), non-coding RNAs, and structural motifs.

- **Replication**:  
  DNA is duplicated semi-conservatively during the S-phase of the cell cycle. DNA polymerases synthesize complementary strands using existing strands as templates. Origins of replication, helicases, and topoisomerases coordinate the unwinding and copying process.

- **Transcription Template**:  
  RNA polymerase binds promoter sequences, synthesizing complementary RNA from the DNA template strand. This RNA may become mRNA (coding), rRNA, tRNA, or regulatory RNAs (e.g., miRNA, lncRNA).

- **Epigenetic Regulation**:  
  Cytosine residues in CpG dinucleotides can be methylated, modulating gene expression without altering the sequence. DNA also associates with histones and other proteins in chromatin, whose structure is dynamically remodeled to control transcriptional access.

- **Recombination and Repair**:  
  Homologous recombination, non-homologous end joining, mismatch repair, and base excision repair maintain genomic integrity. Failure in these systems is a key driver of mutagenesis and cancer.

---

3. **Mechanism of Action (Molecular Detail)**:

- **Double-Helical Structure**:  
  Discovered by Watson and Crick (1953), based on Rosalind Franklin's X-ray crystallography data. The **antiparallel strands** (5′→3′ and 3′→5′) are stabilized by **hydrogen bonding** (A-T with 2 bonds, G-C with 3 bonds) and **base stacking interactions**.

- **Directionality and Polarity**:  
  The 5′ phosphate and 3′ hydroxyl groups confer directionality critical for polymerase function.

- **Supercoiling and Packaging**:  
  In eukaryotes, DNA is wrapped around histone octamers (nucleosomes), forming higher-order chromatin structures. Prokaryotic DNA is compacted via DNA-binding proteins (e.g., HU, IHF) into a nucleoid.

- **Transposable Elements**:  
  DNA harbors mobile sequences (transposons, retrotransposons) that can move within the genome, contributing to evolution and disease.

---

- **Cancer**:  
  DNA mutations in oncogenes (e.g., KRAS, MYC), tumor suppressors (e.g., TP53, BRCA1/2), and DNA repair genes drive neoplastic transformation. DNA methylation patterns also undergo malignant reprogramming.

- **Epigenetic Dysregulation**:

- **Coenzyme A (CoA)**: Acyl group carrier in fatty acid metabolism.

- **Phosphodiester bond formation** between the 3′ hydroxyl of one sugar and the 5′ phosphate of the next nucleotide.

- High-energy phosphoanhydride bonds (in ATP, GTP) release free energy upon hydrolysis:
  - ATP → ADP + Pi or AMP + PPi, ΔG°′ ≈ –30.5 kJ/mol

1. **Scientific Definition**:  
An **enzyme** is a **biological macromolecule—typically a globular protein, though some are catalytic RNAs (ribozymes)**—that accelerates the rate of a chemical reaction by **lowering its activation energy** without being consumed in the process. Enzymes exhibit **high substrate specificity**, **stereoselectivity**, and **regulatory control**, functioning as central mediators of cellular metabolism, signaling, repair, and homeostasis.

---

2. **Biological Function & Physiology**:

#### 🔹 Catalysis of Biochemical Reactions:
- Enzymes catalyze over **5,000 known reactions**, including:
  - **Hydrolysis** (e.g., proteases, amylases)
  - **Oxidation–reduction** (e.g., cytochrome oxidases, dehydrogenases)
  - **Isomerization** (e.g., phosphoglucoisomerase)
  - **Group transfer** (e.g., kinases, transaminases)
  - **Ligations** (e.g., DNA ligase)

#### 🔹 Core Physiological Functions:
- **Metabolic Pathways**: Glycolysis, β-oxidation, TCA cycle, urea cycle—all require enzyme-catalyzed steps.
- **DNA/RNA Processing**: Polymerases, helicases, ligases, nucleases.
- **Signal Transduction**: Kinases, phosphatases, cyclases.
- **Immune System**: Caspases (apoptosis), complement factors (proteolytic cascade).
- **Detoxification**: Cytochrome P450s, glutathione S-transferases.
- **Digestion**: Pepsin, trypsin, pancreatic lipase, lactase.

#### 🔹 Enzyme Classes (EC System):
The **Enzyme Commission (EC)** classifies enzymes into 7 main classes:
1. **Oxidoreductases**
2. **Transferases**
3. **Hydrolases**
4. **Lyases**
5. **Isomerases**
6. **Ligases**
7. **Translocases** (added later for membrane-active enzymes)

---

3. **Mechanism of Action (Molecular Detail)**:

#### 🔬 a) **Active Site Architecture**:
- Enzymes possess a uniquely structured **active site**, often a cleft or pocket, where:
  - The **substrate binds** via hydrogen bonds, van der Waals forces, hydrophobic interactions, or ionic interactions.
  - The enzyme stabilizes the **transition state**, thereby reducing activation energy.

#### 🔬 b) **Catalytic Mechanisms** (Examples):
- **Acid–base catalysis** (e.g., RNAse A)
- **Covalent catalysis** (e.g., serine proteases like chymotrypsin)
- **Metal ion catalysis** (e.g., carbonic anhydrase with Zn²⁺)
- **Proximity/orientation effects**

#### 🔬 c) **Cofactors and Coenzymes**:
- **Cofactors**: Metal ions (Zn²⁺, Fe²⁺, Cu²⁺)
- **Coenzymes**: Organic molecules (e.g., NAD⁺, FAD, biotin, pyridoxal phosphate)
- **Prosthetic groups**: Tightly or covalently bound (e.g., heme in catalase)

#### 🔬 d) **Kinetics**:
- **Michaelis–Menten model**:  
  \[ v = \frac{V_{\text{max}} [S]}{K_m + [S]} \]  
  where \(K_m\) = substrate concentration at ½ Vmax, reflecting affinity.

- **Allosteric regulation**: Enzymes like phosphofructokinase-1 (PFK1) exhibit cooperative kinetics and conformational modulation (T and R states).

1. **Scientific Definition**:  
*Oxidoreductases* are enzymes that catalyze **redox (oxidation–reduction) reactions**, where electrons are transferred from one molecule (the reductant or electron donor) to another (the oxidant or electron acceptor). They are the **first class (EC 1)** in the Enzyme Commission (EC) classification and encompass a vast family of enzymes integral to **bioenergetics, detoxification, metabolism, and signaling**.

---

2. **Biological Function & Physiology**:

Oxidoreductases are essential to life due to their roles in maintaining **electron flow and redox homeostasis** across multiple biological systems:

#### 🔋 a) **Cellular Respiration & Energy Metabolism**  
- **NAD⁺/NADH**, **FAD/FADH₂** cycles are driven by oxidoreductases:
  - *Lactate dehydrogenase*: Pyruvate ⇌ Lactate  
  - *Succinate dehydrogenase*: Succinate → Fumarate (also part of ETC Complex II)
  - *Cytochrome c oxidase*: Final electron acceptor in mitochondrial electron transport chain (reduces O₂ to H₂O).

- **Cytochrome P450 monooxygenases**: Introduce oxygen atoms into hydrophobic drugs/toxins for solubilization and excretion.
- **Glutathione peroxidase**: Reduces hydrogen peroxide and lipid peroxides using reduced glutathione (GSH).

#### 🌱 c) **Anabolism and Biosynthesis**  
- **Fatty acid desaturases**: Insert double bonds via oxidation.
- **3β-Hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase**: Key in steroid hormone biosynthesis.

#### 🧠 d) **Signaling**  
- **Nitric oxide synthase (NOS)**: Oxidizes L-arginine to produce nitric oxide (NO), a potent signaling molecule in vasodilation and neurotransmission.
- **NADPH oxidases (NOX)**: Generate reactive oxygen species (ROS) in immune cells (respiratory burst).

### 🔹 **Overview: Oxidoreductases (EC 1)**  
Oxidoreductases catalyze reactions of the general form:

\[
\text{AH}_2 + \text{B} \longleftrightarrow \text{A} + \text{BH}_2
\]

Where A is the **electron donor** (reductant) and B is the **electron acceptor** (oxidant). They are classified into subclasses based on **donor type, acceptor type, and mechanism**.

---

### 🔸 1. **Dehydrogenases** (EC 1.1–1.6)
These catalyze **hydride transfer** reactions, removing hydrogen (as electrons + protons) from substrates.

#### ✅ Reaction Type:
\[
\text{R-CH}_2\text{OH} + \text{NAD}^+ \longrightarrow \text{R-CHO} + \text{NADH} + \text{H}^+
\]

#### ⚙️ Examples & Roles:
- **Lactate dehydrogenase (LDH)**: Lactate ⇌ Pyruvate  
- **Glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase (G6PD)**: First step of pentose phosphate pathway  
- **Alcohol dehydrogenase**: Ethanol → Acetaldehyde

#### 🧪 Cofactors:
- **NAD⁺ / NADH**: Common in catabolism (oxidative metabolism)  
- **NADP⁺ / NADPH**: Common in anabolism (e.g., fatty acid synthesis)  
- Some use **FAD/FMN** for tighter binding or redox range

---

### 🔸 2. **Oxidases** (EC 1.1.3, EC 1.10.3)
Catalyze electron transfer to **molecular oxygen**, forming **hydrogen peroxide or water**.

#### ✅ Reaction Type:
\[
\text{Substrate}_{\text{(red)}} + \text{O}_2 \rightarrow \text{Product}_{\text{(ox)}} + \text{H}_2\text{O}_2
\]

#### ⚙️ Examples & Roles:
- **Cytochrome c oxidase**: Terminal complex of the ETC (O₂ → H₂O)  
- **Monoamine oxidase (MAO)**: Breaks down neurotransmitters (dopamine, serotonin)  
- **Xanthine oxidase**: Xanthine → Uric acid (target of allopurinol)

#### 🧪 Cofactors:
- **FAD**, **FMN**, **Copper (Cu)**, **Iron–sulfur clusters**, **Heme groups**

---

### 🔸 3. **Oxygenases** (EC 1.13, EC 1.14)  
Incorporate **oxygen atoms directly into substrates**.

#### ✅ Subtypes:
- **Monooxygenases**: Insert one O atom, reduce the second to H₂O  
- **Dioxygenases**: Insert both atoms into substrate

#### ⚙️ Examples & Roles:
- **Cytochrome P450 monooxygenases**: Metabolize steroids, toxins, drugs  
- **Tryptophan 2,3-dioxygenase**: First step in kynurenine pathway  
- **Lipoxygenase**: Inflammatory eicosanoid production

#### 🧪 Cofactors:
- **Heme iron** (P450s)  
- **Fe²⁺**, **Cu²⁺**  
- Requires **NADPH** as electron donor (especially in P450 systems)

---

### 🔸 4. **Peroxidases** (EC 1.11)
Use **hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂)** or organic peroxides as electron acceptors.

#### ✅ Reaction Type:
\[
\text{H}_2\text{O}_2 + 2 \text{AH} \rightarrow 2 \text{H}_2\text{O} + 2 \text{A}
\]

#### ⚙️ Examples & Roles:
- **Glutathione peroxidase**: Detoxifies H₂O₂ and lipid peroxides  
- **Myeloperoxidase (MPO)**: Forms hypochlorous acid (HOCl) in neutrophils  
- **Thyroid peroxidase**: Iodination of thyroglobulin (thyroid hormone synthesis)

#### 🧪 Cofactors:
- **Selenium (in GPX)**  
- **Heme**  
- **Glutathione (GSH)**: as electron donor

---

### 🔸 5. **Reductases** (Reverse Dehydrogenases or Electron Acceptors)  
Functionally reverse dehydrogenases; facilitate reduction under specific metabolic conditions.

#### ⚙️ Examples:
- **Ribonucleotide reductase**: Converts ribonucleotides → deoxyribonucleotides  
- **Methionine sulfoxide reductase**: Repairs oxidized methionine residues  
- **Dihydrofolate reductase (DHFR)**: Regenerates tetrahydrofolate

#### 🧪 Cofactors:
- **NADPH**, **NADH**  
- **Thioredoxin**, **Flavins**

---

### 🔸 6. **Hydroxylases** (EC 1.14.X.X subfamily of monooxygenases)  
Add hydroxyl groups using O₂.

#### ⚙️ Examples:
- **Phenylalanine hydroxylase**: Phe → Tyr (requires BH4)  
- **Prolyl hydroxylase**: Collagen stabilization (hydroxyproline synthesis)  
- **Dopamine β-hydroxylase**: Dopamine → Norepinephrine

#### 🧪 Cofactors:
- **Tetrahydrobiopterin (BH₄)**  
- **Fe²⁺**, **Ascorbate** (as stabilizers or regenerators)  
- **Copper** (e.g., dopamine β-hydroxylase)

---

### 🔸 7. **Superoxide Dismutases (SODs)** (EC 1.15)
Catalyze dismutation of superoxide radicals to O₂ and H₂O₂.

#### ✅ Reaction:
\[
2 \text{O}_2^{\cdot-} + 2 \text{H}^+ \rightarrow \text{O}_2 + \text{H}_2\text{O}_2
\]

#### ⚙️ Types:
- **SOD1**: Cu/Zn form (cytoplasmic)  
- **SOD2**: Mn form (mitochondrial)  
- **SOD3**: Extracellular

#### 🧪 Cofactors:
- **Cu/Zn**, **Mn**, **Fe** depending on isoform

\[
\text{Citrate} \rightleftharpoons \text{cis-Aconitate} \rightleftharpoons \text{Isocitrate}
\]

- **Mechanism**: A **two-step process**:
  1. **Dehydration** of citrate → cis-aconitate  
  2. **Rehydration** of cis-aconitate → isocitrate  
- This reaction repositions the hydroxyl group from C3 to C2, enabling subsequent **oxidative decarboxylation** by isocitrate dehydrogenase.

Aconitase contains a **[4Fe–4S] iron–sulfur cluster**, but unlike typical redox-active Fe–S centers, this one **functions in catalysis and substrate binding**, not in electron transfer:

- One Fe atom is **labile and non-cysteinyl-bound**, directly interacting with substrate carboxyl and hydroxyl groups.
- The Fe–S cluster stabilizes the **aconitate intermediate** and facilitates the stereospecific rearrangement.

#### 1. **Mitochondrial Aconitase (mAco / ACO2)**

- **Moonlighting function**:
  - With Fe–S cluster → acts as aconitase

- **Cancer**:
  - ACO2 expression can be altered in tumor cells with metabolic reprogramming

### ✅ Enzyme Classification:

Citrate synthase is classified under:

> **EC Class 2: Transferases** →  
> **Subclass 2.3: Acyltransferases** →  
> **Sub-subclass 2.3.3: Acyl groups converted into alkyl groups when transferred**

Thus, **citrate synthase is an acyltransferase**, specifically catalyzing the condensation of an acyl group (acetyl-CoA) with oxaloacetate to form citrate.

---

### 🧪 **Reaction Catalyzed**:

\[
\text{Oxaloacetate} + \text{Acetyl-CoA} + \text{H}_2\text{O} \rightarrow \text{Citrate} + \text{CoA-SH}
\]

- **Irreversible**, highly exergonic reaction: ΔG°′ ≈ –32.2 kJ/mol  
- Initiates the **tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle** by forming citrate, a six-carbon intermediate, from a four-carbon oxaloacetate and a two-carbon acetyl-CoA.

---

### 🔬 **Mechanism of Action**:

1. **Ordered Sequential Binding**:
   - **Oxaloacetate binds first**, inducing a conformational change that creates a binding site for acetyl-CoA.
   - This ordered binding prevents wasteful hydrolysis of acetyl-CoA when oxaloacetate is scarce.

2. **Formation of Citryl-CoA Intermediate**:
   - Acetyl-CoA undergoes enolization (activation of methyl group) → enolate attacks the carbonyl of oxaloacetate.
   - Forms **citryl-CoA**, a high-energy intermediate.

3. **Thioester Hydrolysis**:
   - Citryl-CoA is hydrolyzed to release **CoA-SH** and form **citrate**.
   - The energy from the **thioester bond** drives the reaction forward irreversibly.

---

### 🧪 **Cofactors**:

- **None required** (unlike many other TCA enzymes).
- **Mg²⁺ or Mn²⁺** may stabilize substrates in vitro, but **not strictly essential** in vivo.
- Relies entirely on **substrate-induced conformational catalysis**.

---

### 🧠 **Physiological Role**:

- **Gatekeeper of the TCA cycle**:
  - Controls the **entry of carbon from carbohydrates, fats, and amino acids** into central metabolism.
- **Integrates anaplerotic and cataplerotic flux**:
  - Citrate levels reflect balance between energy production, biosynthesis (lipids), and redox state.

---

### ⚖️ **Regulation**:

#### Negative Feedback:
- **High levels of ATP**, **NADH**, and **succinyl-CoA** inhibit citrate synthase.
- **Citrate** (product) also exerts feedback inhibition.

#### Substrate Availability:
- Controlled by **oxaloacetate** levels, which are tightly linked to the redox state (depends on NAD⁺ for malate dehydrogenase).

---

### 🧬 **Pathophysiological Relevance**:

#### 🧠 Metabolic Disease:
- Impaired citrate synthase activity → defective energy metabolism.
- In insulin resistance, increased fatty acid oxidation and mitochondrial overload alter TCA intermediates and flux.

#### 🧪 Cancer Metabolism:
- Citrate is exported from mitochondria (via the citrate shuttle) and cleaved by **ATP-citrate lyase** (ACL) in the cytosol:
  \[
  \text{Citrate} + \text{ATP} \rightarrow \text{Acetyl-CoA} + \text{Oxaloacetate}
  \]
  → Supports **de novo lipogenesis**, cholesterol synthesis, and histone acetylation in rapidly dividing cells.

#### 🧫 Cancer Cell Targeting:
- Some tumors upregulate citrate synthase and ACL to support **anabolic metabolism**.
- **Inhibitors** of citrate export or ACL may have therapeutic potential in metabolic oncology.

#### 🔥 ROS and Inflammation:
- Citrate accumulation promotes pro-inflammatory phenotypes in immune cells (e.g., macrophages) by supporting NO and ROS production and itaconate synthesis.

---

### 🧪 Clinical/Research Applications:

- **Citrate synthase activity** is a standard marker of **mitochondrial density and function** in research, especially in muscle and aging studies.
- Often used as a **normalization control** in studies of mitochondrial respiration or oxidative enzyme content.

Glucose does **not directly** convert into urate. | Pathway Involved | Role in Urate Production |
|------------------|--------------------------|
| **Glycolysis / Pentose Phosphate Pathway (PPP)** | Supplies ribose-5-phosphate for purine synthesis |
| **Purine de novo synthesis** | Produces AMP, GMP → degraded to xanthine → uric acid |
| **Nucleotide turnover** | Accelerated by ATP depletion, cell stress, hypoxia |

## 🔁 STEP-WISE BIOCHEMICAL PATHWAY FROM GLUCOSE TO URATE

---

### 🔹 **1. Glucose Enters Glycolysis**

Glucose → G6P → F6P → F1,6BP → GAP/DHAP → Pyruvate

- In high-glucose states (e.g., metabolic syndrome, diabetes), excess glycolytic intermediates are shunted toward **anabolic pathways**, including **nucleotide biosynthesis**

G6P → 6-P-gluconolactone → **Ribose-5-phosphate (R5P)**  
- R5P is the key sugar backbone for **de novo purine synthesis**
- PPP also generates **NADPH** (used in biosynthesis and ROS defense)

Ribose-5-phosphate → **PRPP** (5-phosphoribosyl-1-pyrophosphate)  
PRPP + amino acids + formyl groups → **IMP (inosine monophosphate)**  
IMP → AMP or GMP → **turnover or salvage**

- These processes are **ATP-dependent** and accelerated in high metabolic or proliferative states

---

### 🔹 **4. Purine Catabolism to Uric Acid**

When ATP is depleted (e.g., fructose overload, ischemia, or stress):

- AMP → **Inosine** → **Hypoxanthine**  
- GMP → **Guanosine** → **Guanine** → **Xanthine**

Then:

- **Hypoxanthine → Xanthine → Uric acid (urate)**  
  Catalyzed by **xanthine oxidoreductase (XOR)**  
  (exists as **xanthine dehydrogenase** and **xanthine oxidase**)

This reaction also produces **reactive oxygen species (ROS)**.

| **Obesity / Inflammation** | Increases nucleotide turnover and de novo synthesis |

- **Glucose fuels ribose (PPP) → purines → catabolism → uric acid**

## 🔹 1. SCIENTIFIC DEFINITION

Statins are a class of **competitive inhibitors** of the enzyme **HMG-CoA reductase**, the rate-limiting enzyme in the **mevalonate pathway**, which governs **cholesterol biosynthesis**. By inhibiting this enzyme, statins **decrease intracellular cholesterol** synthesis in hepatocytes, **upregulate LDL receptors**, and thereby **increase hepatic uptake of circulating LDL particles**, leading to a **reduction in plasma LDL cholesterol levels**.

### ➤ **HMG-CoA Reductase Reaction**:

| Substrate        | → Enzyme               | → Product           |
|------------------|------------------------|---------------------|
| HMG-CoA (β-Hydroxy β-methylglutaryl-CoA) | **HMG-CoA reductase** | Mevalonate (→ Cholesterol precursor) |

- Statins mimic the **HMG moiety** of the substrate and bind **competitively** to the enzyme’s active site.

### ➤ **Downstream effects**:
- ↓ Cholesterol synthesis → ↑ SREBP2 activation → ↑ LDL receptor gene expression → ↑ LDL uptake from plasma
