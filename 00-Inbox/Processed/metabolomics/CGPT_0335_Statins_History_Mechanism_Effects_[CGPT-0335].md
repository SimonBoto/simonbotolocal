Statins, a class of lipid-lowering medications, have been pivotal in managing cardiovascular disease. Their history dates back to the 1970s, when Akira Endo, a Japanese biochemist, discovered the first statin, mevastatin, from the fungus *Penicillium citrinum*. This discovery led to the development of lovastatin, the first statin approved for clinical use in nineteen eighty-seven.

The primary mechanism of action for statins involves the inhibition of HMG-CoA reductase, a key enzyme in the mevalonate pathway responsible for cholesterol biosynthesis in the liver. By inhibiting this enzyme, statins effectively reduce the production of cholesterol, particularly low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol, which is a significant risk factor for atherosclerosis and subsequent cardiovascular events.

Beyond their lipid-lowering effects, statins have several pleiotropic effects, including improving endothelial function, stabilizing atherosclerotic plaques, and reducing oxidative stress and inflammation. These effects contribute to their cardiovascular benefits beyond just lowering cholesterol levels.

Metabolically, statins can influence glucose homeostasis. Some studies have shown an increased risk of new-onset diabetes with statin therapy, which may be related to their impact on insulin sensitivity and beta-cell function. However, the cardiovascular benefits generally outweigh these risks, especially in individuals with high cardiovascular risk.

Further research is ongoing to better understand the full spectrum of statins' effects and to optimize their use in various populations, considering the balance between benefits and potential risks.

Ezetimibe and fibrates are both used to manage dyslipidemia, but they work through different mechanisms.

**Ezetimibe**: Ezetimibe works by inhibiting the Niemann-Pick C1-Like 1 (NPC1L1) protein on the enterocytes in the small intestine. This protein is responsible for the absorption of cholesterol. By blocking NPC1L1, ezetimibe reduces the absorption of dietary and biliary cholesterol, decreasing the amount of cholesterol delivered to the liver. As a result, the liver uptakes more cholesterol from the blood, lowering blood cholesterol levels. Ezetimibe is often used in combination with statins to achieve a greater reduction in LDL cholesterol【23†source】【34†source】.

**Fibrates**: Fibrates activate peroxisome proliferator-activated receptors (PPARs), specifically PPAR-alpha. These nuclear receptors regulate the expression of genes involved in lipid metabolism. Activation of PPAR-alpha leads to increased lipolysis and elimination of triglyceride-rich particles from the plasma by activating lipoprotein lipase and reducing the production of apolipoprotein C-III. This results in a decrease in very low-density lipoprotein (VLDL) and an increase in high-density lipoprotein (HDL). Fibrates are particularly effective in lowering triglycerides and increasing HDL cholesterol levels【25†source】【27†source】.

Both medications are valuable in comprehensive lipid management strategies, especially for patients who require additional lipid-lowering effects beyond what statins can provide alone.
