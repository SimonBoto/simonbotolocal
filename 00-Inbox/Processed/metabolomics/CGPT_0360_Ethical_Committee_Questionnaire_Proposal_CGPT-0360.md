### 2. **ChatGPT:**

It sounds like you have a well-rounded approach to your questionnaires. Using established tools like the SF36 and a food frequency questionnaire is a good foundation. Including a 24-hour recall, a NUMEX-specific metabolomics questionnaire, and the IPAC to assess physical activity will provide comprehensive data.

Here are a few considerations to ensure the Ethical Committee's approval:

1. **Informed Consent**: Clearly state that participants will be informed about each specific questionnaire and that their consent will be obtained for each one. Highlight how you will ensure participants understand what each questionnaire entails and how their data will be used.

2. **Data Privacy**: Detail how you will protect the privacy and confidentiality of participants' data. Explain your data storage, access control, and anonymization processes.

3. **Participant Burden**: Consider the total time commitment for participants to complete all questionnaires. The Ethical Committee will want to ensure that the burden on participants is reasonable and does not discourage participation.

4. **Validation and Reliability**: Provide evidence or references supporting the validity and reliability of the questionnaires you intend to use. For the NUMEX and any custom metabolomics questionnaires, explain how these tools were developed and validated, if applicable.

5. **Ethical Considerations**: Address any potential ethical issues, such as the risk of participant discomfort or fatigue due to the length or frequency of questionnaires. Describe measures you will take to minimize these risks.

6. **Pilot Testing**: Mention if you plan to pilot test your questionnaires to ensure they are clear, understandable, and not overly burdensome for participants.

If you address these points thoroughly in your proposal, it should strengthen your application to the Ethical Committee. If you have any specific questions about the wording or content of the proposal, feel free to ask.

### 4. **ChatGPT:**

As a medical doctor conducting interviews, you do have some flexibility in deciding which clinically relevant questionnaires to include. However, even if the questionnaires are administered through an interview, the Ethical Committee will still likely require some level of detail and standardization for several reasons:

1. **Informed Consent**: Participants need to know what to expect during the interview, including the types of questions they will be asked. While you may not need to list every specific questionnaire, providing a general overview of the topics and types of questions can be important.

2. **Transparency and Ethical Standards**: Ethical Committees ensure that research adheres to high ethical standards. Providing details about the questionnaires helps demonstrate that your study is well-planned and ethically sound.

3. **Data Quality and Reliability**: Describing the standardization and validation of the questionnaires helps the committee understand how you will ensure the data you collect is reliable and valid.

4. **Participant Burden**: Even in an interview format, the Ethical Committee will want to ensure that the time and effort required from participants is reasonable. Outlining the expected duration and frequency of the interviews, as well as the nature of the questions, is crucial.

To balance your need for flexibility with the Ethical Committee's requirements, you might consider the following approach in your proposal:

1. **General Description**: Provide a general description of the interview process, including the types of questionnaires you may use and the topics they will cover (e.g., dietary intake, physical activity, metabolic health).

2. **Sample Questionnaires**: Include examples of some of the questionnaires or question types you plan to use. This can give the committee a sense of the content and structure without requiring you to list every specific questionnaire.

3. **Justification**: Explain why the interview format is appropriate for your study and how it will enhance the quality of the data. Highlight your clinical expertise and experience in conducting such interviews.

4. **Flexibility Clause**: Include a statement that allows for some flexibility in the specific questionnaires used, based on the clinical relevance and needs of the study. Emphasize that all questionnaires will be chosen based on their relevance to the research questions and their validation in similar studies.

By providing this level of detail, you can demonstrate that your study is well-considered and ethically sound while maintaining the flexibility to include clinically relevant questionnaires as needed.
