Fatty acids are carboxylic acids with long hydrocarbon chains. The general chemical formula for a saturated fatty acid is \( CH_{3}(CH_{2})_{n}COOH \), where \( n \) is typically an even number that varies depending on the length of the hydrocarbon chain. In unsaturated fatty acids, one or more double bonds are present in the hydrocarbon chain, which affects the molecule's shape and properties.

### Types of Fatty Acids:

1. **Saturated Fatty Acids**: These have no double bonds between the individual carbon atoms of the fatty acid chain. That is, the chain of carbon atoms is fully "saturated" with hydrogen atoms. An example is palmitic acid with the formula \( C_{16}H_{32}O_{2} \).

2. **Monounsaturated Fatty Acids (MUFAs)**: These have one double bond in the fatty acid chain with all of the remainder carbon atoms being single-bonded. An example is oleic acid with the formula \( C_{18}H_{34}O_{2} \).

3. **Polyunsaturated Fatty Acids (PUFAs)**: These have more than one double bond in their backbone. Examples include linoleic acid (\( C_{18}H_{32}O_{2} \)) and alpha-linolenic acid (\( C_{18}H_{30}O_{2} \)).

4. **Trans Fatty Acids**: These are a type of unsaturated fatty acid, but they are configured differently due to the arrangement around the double bond. They are not commonly found in nature and are mostly produced artificially.

### Structural Representation:

- **Linear Representation**: Often written as CH3(CH2)nCOOH for saturated and CH3(CH2)n(CH=CH)(CH2)mCOOH for unsaturated.
  
- **Condensed Formula**: Written as C16H32O2 for palmitic acid, for example.
  
- **Skeletal Formula**: The carbon atoms are often implied and not explicitly drawn, focusing instead on the functional groups and double bonds, if any.

Fatty acids can exist in free form or as part of more complex lipids like triglycerides, phospholipids, or sphingolipids, which have different biological functions ranging from energy storage to forming cell membranes.
