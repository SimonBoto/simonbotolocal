Chapter 3: Vitamin D 

3.1 Introduction 
Vitamin D was discovered as a dietary constituent in cod liver oil that helped cure rickets, a bone disease characterized by impaired bone mineralization (Norman, 2008).
Vitamin D, often called the "sunshine vitamin", is synthesized in the skin through exposure to ultraviolet B (UVB, wavelength 290 - 315 nm) radiation from the sun. After UVB photons penetrate the skin and activate 7-dehydrocholesterol to previtamin D3 (photoconversion), which then isomerizes to vitamin D3 (cholecalciferol) (Holick, 2007). 
Circulating vitamin D undergoing a two-step enzymatic transformation to form a hormone that drives the sophisticated vitamin D endocrine system (VDES) (Norman, 2008).
First, in the liver (cytochrome P450) it is hydroxylated by the enzyme 25-hydroxylase (CYP2R1) to form 25-hydroxyvitamin D [25(OH)D], the primary circulating form, and the major metabolite used to determine a patient's vitamin D status (Holick, 2006). 
Secondly, in the kidneys, 25(OH)D is taken up by the proximal renal tubules through endocytic internalization mediated by megalin and cubilin, which are members of the LDL receptor family (Nykjaer et al, 1999). 
Subsequently, in the proximal renal tubules, 25(OH)D is hydroxylated by the enzyme 1α-hydroxylase (CYP27B1) to 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D [1,25(OH)₂D], the biologically active form of vitamin D.(DeLuca, 2004). 
The enzyme 1α-hydroxylase is also expressed extra-renally, documented on placenta (Gray et al, 1979) and immune cells, like monocytes and macrophages (Stoffels et al., 2007; Esteban et al, 2004) . and at least 17 cell types express the 1α-hydroxylase enzyme, enabling them to produce biological responses to 1,25D (Kidd, 2010).

Autocrine effect: Occurs when a hormone exits the cell where it was produced and influences that same cell from the outside.
Paracrine effect: Involves influencing neighboring cells without reliance on the circulation for transport.
Intracrine effect: Occurs when the hormone acts within the same cell that produced it without first exiting the cell.
Evidence suggests that 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D (1,25D) likely has all three of these actions (Kidd, 2010).

Vitamin D receptor (VDR) is a member of the steroid/thyroid hormone nuclear receptors family, with spontaneous presence in the cytosol, directly or indirectly affects many genes through transcriptional modifications (Strugnell & Deluca, 1997). 
It is estimated that VDRs can regulate up to 500 of the approximately 20,488 genes in the human genome (Carlberg, 2003)
VDR is present across more than 38 types of tissues, controlling vital genes related to oxidative stress, inflammation and chronic diseases (Haussler et al., 2008).
 Vitamin D exerts its effect by binding to VDR, which heterodimerizes with the retinoid receptor (RXR), forming a complex that controls transcription of certain target genes (Jones et al., 1998).
Vitamin D binding protein (DBP) is a specific carrier of vitamin D and its metabolites, as almost all serum 25-hydroxyvitamin D is bound to circulating DBP. (Bouillon, 2001). 
 It has been found that more than 200 genes are directly or indirectly regulated by 1,25(OH)₂D, controlling cellular proliferation, differentiation, apoptosis, and angiogenesis (Nagpal & Rathnachalam, 2005).
While vitamin D is well known for its important function in bone health, it also significantly affects pancreatic beta cells, insulin sensitivity and secretion.

3.2 Vitamin D Population Status 
The status of vitamin D in the body is most commonly determined by measuring the concentration of circulating total serum  [25(OH)D] (Holick et al, 2011). 
Currently, different organizations have  different definitions of vitamin D deficiency. It is mostly agreed that the levels of serum 25(OH)D < 30 nmol/L (12 ng/mL) is considered vitamin D deficiency, levels of 30–50 nmol/L (12–20 ng/mL) indicate insufficiency, and levels ≥50 nmol/L (20 ng/mL) is considered as being sufficient (Ross et al, 2011).
 However, in response to increased  levels of circulating PTH, the Endocrine Society designated the threshold of vitamin D deficiency at ≤ 50 nmol/L (20 ng/mL), the threshold of vitamin D insufficiency at 50–75 nmol/L (20–30 ng/mL), the threshold of vitamin D sufficiency at ≥75 nmol/L (30 ng/mL) (Bouillon et al, 2017) . 
It has been estimated that more than 30-50% of the children and adult population are at risk of vitamin D Deficiency (Holick & Chen, 2008). 
Cui et al (2023) reported that  "globally, 15.7%, 47.9%, and 76.6% of participants had serum 25-hydroxyvitamin D levels less than 30, 50, and 75 nmol/L, respectively. 
Vitamin D Levels and Health Outcomes
Data from representative human studies indicate various thresholds for serum 25D levels associated with health outcomes (Kidd, 2010):
<8 ng/mL: Classic vitamin D deficiency—rickets or osteomalacia (Institute of Medicine [IOM], 1997).
<17.8 ng/mL: Increased risk for all-cause mortality (Melamed et al., 2008).
<24 ng/mL: Greater risk of death in patients receiving intensive care; patients had three times greater mortality rate than those with serum 25D >60 ng/mL (Lee et al., 2009).
30 ng/mL: Greater risk for depression among hospitalized subjects (Schneider et al., 2000).
32 ng/mL: Threshold for optimal calcium absorption from the intestine (Heaney, 2008).
38 ng/mL: Threshold for optimal neuromuscular performance (Bischoff-Ferrari et al., 2004).

Vitamin D Suggested Intake 
As suggested by the European Food Safety Authority (EFSA), vitamin D supplementation is promoted as an effective way to restore serum 25(OH)D concentration, with doses up to 4.000 international units (IU) per day considered safe for adults (EFSA, 2012). 
The current U.S. intake guidelines for vitamin D were developed by the Food and Nutrition Board of the IOM in 1997 and recommend:
5 μg/day (200 IU): From birth to age 50.
10 μg/day (400 IU): Ages 51–70.
15 μg/day (600 IU): Age 71 and older (IOM, 1997).
Dosage for supplementation may vary between subjects according to age, comorbidities, preference, absorption, and baseline 25D plasma levels. Suggested dosages range from approximately 800–2000 IU/day, and up to 4000 IU/day (Casadei et al., 2021; Holick et al., 2011; Pludowski et al., 2018).

The safety of vitamin D therapy is mainly linked to dosage and the form of supplementation. Bodily self-regulation of vitamin D activation, efficiently preserved by using non-hydroxylated forms, minimizes toxicity risk (Casadei et al., 2021; Pludowski et al., 2018).

We highlight vitamin D importance in almost every pathophysiologic component of MetS, including insulin resistance, dyslipidemia, dysregulated immunity and hormonal balance. 
Vitamin D and Calcium 
Vitamin D’s regulation of calcium and phosphorus homeostasis is essential for bone health and normal mineralization.
Vitamin D is primarily involved in intestinal calcium absorption. The active form, 1,25D, enhances the synthesis of TRPV6 (a calcium channel) and increases the permeability of tight junctions (Casadei et al., 2021; Mosali et al., 2014). 
Vitamin D deficiency decreases the availability of calcium and leads to bone structural defects similar to those occurring in osteoporosis (Aguayo-Ruiz et al., 2020; Levin et al., 1976).
Administration of vitamin D promotes intestinal absorption of calcium and phosphorus, increases renal tubular reabsorption of calcium, increases the expression of calbindin, and accelerates calcium transport in the distal convoluted tubule of the nephron (Aguayo-Ruiz et al., 2020; Dusso et al., 2005)
 It effectively increases intestinal absorption of these important minerals and also promotes proper osteoblast function, thus supporting bone formation and remodeling through multiple ways simultaneously (Christakos et al, 2016). 
Vitamin D PTH
Parathyroid hormone (PTH) holds a primary role in regulating vitamin D activity by stimulating the transcription of 1αhydroxylase, thereby increasing production of 1,25(OH)₂D, which in turn provides negative feedback suppressing PTH synthesis and creating a loop (Christakos et al, 2010).
Osteoblasts are activated by osteocalcin and PTH, which need vitamin D and K2 for proper function. 
PTH has also been implicated in insulin resistance, by reducing glucose uptake by liver, muscle and adipose cells (Thomas et al, 1995), while another study indicated that PTH decreased insulin-stimulated glucose uptake (Reusch et al, 1991).
VItamin D Glucose 
Vitamin D is extensively involved in glucose metabolism, insulin production and insulin sensitivity, directly affecting the core metabolic dysfunction of MetS. 
A pivotal study by Ogunkolade et al. (2002) was one of the earliest works to demonstrate the association of vitamin D receptor polymorphism with insulin secretory capacity. Notably, 1,25-(OH)2D acts as an activator of the human insulin gene, playing an essential role in its transcription and subsequent excretion (Maestro et al, 2002). 
By acting directly on pancreatic beta cells and insulin-sensitive tissues, Vitamin D reduces insulin resistance, enhancing receptor expression and responsiveness (Pittas et al, 2007). 
The positive role of vitamin D is mediated by improved pancreatic β-cells function, including direct stimulation of insulin production and secretion through the extended presence of vitamin D receptors (VDR) (Johnson et al, 1994).
 Additionally, by regulating the intracellular and extracellular calcium pool of β-cells, vitamin D deficiency may disrupt normal release of insulin (Sooy et al, 1999). Calcium homeostasis is also influenced by vitamin K2, suggesting a collaborative effect.
