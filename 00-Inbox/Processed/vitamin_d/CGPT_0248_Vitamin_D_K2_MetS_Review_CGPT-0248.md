The concept of “Syndrome X” led to increased attention and understanding of the interplay and common physiological basis of its components, although visceral obesity, hypertension, dyslipidemia and hyperglycemia had already been known and associated for over a century. Metabolic syndrome’s components separately and collectively  increase the risk of developing cardiovascular disease (CVD) and type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2DM) (Eckel, Grundy & Zimmet, 2005). 
The significant rise In MetS prevalence throughout the preceding years can be attributed to lifestyle trends, unhealthy dietary habits, increasing rates of obesity and a sedentary lifestyle. In parallel, hidden hunger is rising and is currently estimated to affect around 2 billion people worldwide (Tulchinsky, 2010). Hidden hunger is defined by micronutrient deficiencies that are largely invisible, as they do not present with overt or evident symptoms, and is sometimes difficult to diagnose (Ruel-Bergeron et al., 2015).
Micronutrients, including vitamins and minerals, are compounds needed in small quantities for normal human function, which are not produced endogenously and must be taken exogenously from food and water (Mora, Iwata & Andrian, 2008). Although only micronutrients are required in small quantities, contrary to macronutrients, their role in physiological processes, such as metabolism, immune function and cellular signaling is considered pivotal. Imbalances or deficiencies in micronutrients may disrupt metabolic homeostasis, contributing to the development and the progression of metabolic syndrome (Alpert, 2017), deteriorating further the underlying metabolic dysfunction. Emerging research indicates that micronutrient deficiencies influence insulin sensitivity, lipid metabolism, blood pressure and inflammatory response, providing new potential avenues for preventive and therapeutic strategies for metabolic syndrome (Thomas-Valdés et al., 2017), characterized by safety, low production cost and ease of implementation in primary healthcare.

2.1 Definition and Diagnostic Criteria of Metabolic Syndrome
Metabolic syndrome (MetS)  is characterized by a cluster of commonly coexisting metabolic imbalances, which contribute to increased risk of several chronic diseases, among which cardiovascular disease (CVD) and type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2DM). Thus its early diagnosis and effective management are pivotal for public health and clinical practice, especially when considering the 21st century’s public health challenges. 
In 1988, Reaven first proposed the diagnostic criteria for “Syndrome X”, focusing on insulin resistance, dyslipidemia and hypertension (Reaven, 1988). The World Health Organisation (WHO) later provided the first formal definition of MetS in 1998, emphasizing on insulin resistance as the major underlying risk factor. For diagnosis the presence of insulin resistance was required, along with two additional risk factors: obesity, hypertension, elevated triglycerides, decreased HDL, microalbuminuria (Alberti, Zimmet & WHO, 1998). The next major update was presented by the “Third Report of National Cholesterol Education Program Adult Treatment Panel III (ATP III)” (2002). The ATP III criteria did not require any single factor's presence as obligatory, but for the diagnosis three out of five factors were needed (abdominal obesity, elevated triglycerides, decreased HDL, hypertension, elevated fasting glucose or T2DM) (NCEP ATP III Final Report, 2002). 3.1 Introduction 
Vitamin D, often called the "sunshine vitamin", is synthesized in the skin through exposure to ultraviolet B (UVB, wavelength 290 - 315 nm) radiation from the sun. After UVB photons penetrate the skin and activate 7-dehydrocholesterol to previtamin D3 (photoconversion), which then isomerizes to vitamin D3 (cholecalciferol) (Holick, 2007). Vitamin D3 undergoes two hydroxylation reactions to become fully active. First, in the liver (cytochrome P450) it is hydroxylated by the enzyme 25-hydroxylase (CYP2R1) to form 25-hydroxyvitamin D [25(OH)D], the primary circulating form, and the major metabolite used to determine a patient's vitamin D status (Holick, 2006). Vitamin D binding protein (DBP) is a specific carrier of vitamin D and its metabolites, as almost all serum 25-hydroxyvitamin D is bound to circulating DBP. (Bouillon, 2001). 
In the kidneys, 25(OH)D is taken up by the proximal renal tubules through endocytic internalization mediated by megalin and cubilin, which are members of the LDL receptor family (Nykjaer et al, 1999). Subsequently, in the proximal renal tubules, 25(OH)D is hydroxylated by the enzyme 1α-hydroxylase (CYP27B1) to 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D [1,25(OH)₂D], the biologically active form of vitamin D.(DeLuca, 2004). The enzyme 1α-hydroxylase is also expressed extra-renally, documented on placenta (Weisman et al, 1979; Gray et al, 1979) and immune cells, like monocytes and macrophages (Stoffels et al., 2007; Esteban et al, 2004) . 
Vitamin D receptor (VDR) is a member of the steroid/thyroid hormone nuclear receptors family, with spontaneous presence in the cytosol, directly or indirectly affects many genes through transcriptional modifications (Strugnell & Deluca, 1997). VDR is present across more than 38 types of tissues, controlling vital genes related to oxidative stress, inflammation and chronic diseases (Haussler et al., 2008). Vitamin D exerts its effect by binding to VDR, which heterodimerizes with the retinoid receptor (RXR), forming a complex that controls transcription of certain target genes (Jones et al., 1998).
While vitamin D is well known for its important function in bone health, it also significantly affects pancreatic beta cells, insulin sensitivity and secretion. It has been found that more than 200 genes are directly or indirectly regulated by 1,25(OH)₂D, controlling cellular proliferation, differentiation, apoptosis, and angiogenesis (Nagpal et al., 2005). Parathyroid hormone (PTH) holds a primary role in regulating vitamin D activity by stimulating the transcription of 1αhydroxylase, thereby increasing production of 1,25(OH)₂D, which in tuen provides negative feedback suppressing PTH synthesis and creating a loop (Christakos et al, 2010).

3.3 Vitamin D Mechanism of Action 
	In this section, we will explore the mechanisms implicated for vitamin D in major metabolic processes, particularly in the context of MetS. We highlight vitamin D importance in almost every pathophysiologic component of MetS, including insulin resistance, dyslipidemia, dysregulated immunity and hormonal balance. 
Vitamin D’s regulation of calcium and phosphorus homeostasis is essential for bone health and normal mineralization. It effectively increases intestinal absorption of these important minerals and also promotes proper osteoblast function, thus supporting bone formation and remodeling through multiple ways simultaneously (Christakos et al, 2016). Osteoblasts are activated by osteocalcin and PTH, which need vitamin D and K2 for proper function. PTH has also been implicated in insulin resistance, by reducing glucose uptake by liver, muscle and adipose cells (Thomas et al, 1995), while another study indicated that PTH decreased insulin-stimulated glucose uptake (Reusch et al, 1991). Beyond bone health, vitamin D exhibits potent anti-inflammatory properties, influencing both the innate and adaptive immunity (Aranow, 2011). These anti-inflammatory effects are significant in the context of MetS, where low-grade inflammation is present. Additionally, vitamin D, found to affect proliferation and differentiation of cells, has been implicated in cancer prevention and CVD (Wand et al, 2008).
Vitamin D is extensively involved in glucose metabolism, insulin production and insulin sensitivity, directly affecting the core metabolic dysfunction of MetS. A pivotal study by Ogunkalade et al. (2002) was one of the earliest works to demonstrate the association of vitamin D receptor polymorphism with insulin secretory capacity. Notably, 1,25-(OH)2D acts as an activator of the human insulin gene, playing an essential role in its transcription and subsequent excretion (Maestro et al, 2002). By acting directly on pancreatic beta cells and insulin-sensitive tissues, Vitamin D reduces insulin resistance, enhancing receptor expression and responsiveness (Pittas et al, 2007). The positive role of vitamin D is mediated by improved pancreatic β-cells function, including direct stimulation of insulin production and secretion through the extended presence of vitamin D receptors (VDR) (Johnson et al, 1994). Additionally, by regulating the intracellular and extracellular calcium pool of β-cells, vitamin D deficiency may disrupt normal release of insulin (Sooy et al, 1999). Calcium homeostasis is also influenced by vitamin K2, suggesting a collaborative effect. 
Vitamin D affects lipid metabolism at the genetic level, being involved both in lipid synthesis and clearance. Adequate levels of Vitamin D are associated with favorable lipid profiles, including lower triglycerides (Jorde et al, 2011). A potential mechanism for increased lipogenesis associated with vitamin D deficiency is mediated by elevated PTH secretion, which stimulates lipogenesis by promoting an influx of calcium into the adipocytes (McCarthy & Thomas, 2003). PTH and osteocalcin are also regulated by vitamin K2. 
Vitamin D deficiency leads to upregulation of renin, a mechanism implicated in Metabolic Syndrome hypertension, proving its regulatory role in the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS) (Li et al, 2002). 
Vitamin D exhibits immune-modulating effects by downregulating pro-inflammatory cytokines and upregulating anti-inflammatory cytokines, reducing chronic inflammation in adipose tissue and, thus, regulating a key component of metabolic syndrome (Guillot, 2010). Moreover, vitamin D deficiency is connected to increased activity of PPARγ and C/EBPα receptors, components that are involved in the maturation of pre-adipocytes, leading to increased volume of adipose tissue ready to accumulate fat (Wood, 2008).

4.1 Vitamin K2 Introduction 
Vitamin K occurs naturally under two main forms: phylloquinone (vitamin K1) and various menaquinones (collectively known as vitamin K2) (Mahdinia et al, 2019). It was first discovered in 193 by Henrik Dam, who recognised the crucial role of vitamin K in blood clotting, after observing severe bleeding in chickens fed a low-fat diet (Dam, 1935). Vitamin K consists of a group of menadione derivatives, with phylloquinone (vitamin K1) being the the main form found mainly in green leafy vegetables (e.g., spinach, cabbage, kale), fruits (e.g., avocado, kiwi, grapes), as well as in some plant oils (e.g., soybean oil) (Halder et al., 2019).  Menaquinones, structurally analogous to phylloquinone, were later discovered and collectively termed vitamin K2 (Zhao et al., 2021). Vitamin K2, being a fat soluble vitamin, has a lipophilic nature that allows its crossing through the blood brain barrier (BBB) (Talaei Firozjaei et al., 2018). Vitamin K2 has multiple forms, all sharing a common moiety in their chemical structure called menadione, which is a 2-methyl 1,4-naphthoquinone ring. They differ from each other to the degree of saturation and the length of side chains (Iwamoto et al., 2006). The repetitive unsaturated isoprene units of vitamin K2 provide strong rigidity and result in distinct cis- and trans-isomers, notably with trans-isomers being the only biologically active (Lal & Berenjian, 2020). 
Menaquinones are also referred to as MK-n, with 'n' denoting the length of its side chain varying from 4 to 15 isoprene units (Beulens et al., 2013). Each form of Vitamin K2 has distinct pharmacodynamic and pharmacokinetic properties, exhibiting unique potency and absorption efficiency (Booth & Rajabi, 2008). Vitamin K2 has two main sources: dietary intake from meat, dairy and fermented foods, and endogenous synthesis by probiotic gut bacteria (Shearer & Newman, 2008). Calcium metabolism is tightly regulated by hormonal control, primarily involving PTH and osteocalcin. **5.2 Mechanisms of Synergy between Vitamin D and Vitamin K2**

**5.2.1 Shared Pathways in Calcium Metabolism**

Vitamin D and Vitamin K2 both play crucial roles in calcium homeostasis but act through different mechanisms that complement each other. Vitamin D enhances the intestinal absorption of calcium, increasing serum calcium levels (Aaseth et al., 2024). However, without proper regulation, elevated calcium can deposit in vascular tissues, leading to calcification. Vitamin K2 activates matrix Gla protein (MGP) and osteocalcin through gamma-carboxylation, directing calcium to the bone and inhibiting vascular calcification (Capozzi et al., 2020). The synergy lies in Vitamin D's ability to increase calcium availability and Vitamin K2's role in directing calcium to appropriate sites, thus promoting bone health while preventing calcification of soft tissues.

**5.2.2 Modulation of Gene Expression**

Both vitamins influence gene expression through nuclear receptors—Vitamin D via the Vitamin D Receptor (VDR) and Vitamin K2 through the Steroid and Xenobiotic Receptor (SXR), also known as the Pregnane X Receptor (PXR) (Bleizgys, 2023). These receptors can form heterodimers and bind to Vitamin D Response Elements (VDREs) in the DNA, affecting the transcription of genes involved in glucose and lipid metabolism. The cross-talk between VDR and SXR may potentiate the metabolic effects of each vitamin, enhancing insulin sensitivity and lipid regulation, which are critical in MetS.

**5.2.3 Anti-Inflammatory and Immunomodulatory Effects**

Chronic low-grade inflammation is a hallmark of MetS. Vitamin D exhibits anti-inflammatory properties by downregulating pro-inflammatory cytokines like interleukin-6 (IL-6) and tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-α) (Visser et al., 2022). Vitamin K2 also exerts anti-inflammatory effects by inhibiting nuclear factor-kappa B (NF-κB) signaling pathways, which are pivotal in cytokine production (Capozzi et al., 2020). Together, they may synergistically reduce inflammation, improving insulin sensitivity and endothelial function.
