**Common Features of Engineering and Medicine: The Human as a System**

Both engineering and medicine can be seen as disciplines that deal with complex systems. In engineering, systems are often mechanical or electronic, designed and built with precision to perform specific functions. In medicine, the human body itself is viewed as an intricate system, comprising various interrelated components such as organs, tissues, and cells. Both fields require a deep understanding of how individual components operate and how they interact within the larger system. Problem-solving in both disciplines often involves diagnosing malfunctions and finding ways to restore or improve system functionality.

**The Distinction: Building vs. Healing**

Despite these similarities, a fundamental difference exists between engineering and medicine. Engineers create and build systems, often from scratch, using known materials and principles. They have control over the design, construction, and modification of these systems. In contrast, medical professionals do not build the human body; instead, they work with a pre-existing, incredibly complex system that is not fully understood. The human body’s capacity for self-regulation and healing is something that cannot be replicated artificially.
