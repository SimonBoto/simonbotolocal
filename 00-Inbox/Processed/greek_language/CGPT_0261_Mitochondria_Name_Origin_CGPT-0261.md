The term "mitochondria" derives from the Greek words *mitos*, meaning "thread," and *chondros*, meaning "granule" or "grain." This name was chosen due to the thread-like and granular appearance of mitochondria observed under a microscope in the early studies of cell biology.

Mitochondria were first described in the 1850s and later named by Carl Benda in 1898. The naming reflects the structure as seen through the technology of the time, where the components of the cell were identified by their shapes and forms under microscopic examination. This thread-granular appearance was characteristic in tissues, especially muscle tissues, where mitochondria are abundant and active, which led to their distinctive naming.
