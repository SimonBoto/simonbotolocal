---

## 1. **Definition**
**PFAS = Per- and Polyfluoroalkyl Substances**  
- A very large family of **synthetic chemicals** (over 12,000 identified).  
- Characterized by a **carbon backbone where hydrogen atoms are fully (per-) or partially (poly-) replaced with fluorine**.  
- The **C–F bond** is one of the strongest in organic chemistry → gives PFAS their **extreme persistence** in the environment.  

Hence their nickname: **“forever chemicals.”**

---

## 2. **Major Subclasses**
- **PFOA** (*perfluorooctanoic acid*): historically used in Teflon production.  
- **PFOS** (*perfluorooctane sulfonate*): used in Scotchgard, firefighting foams.  
- **PFHxS, PFNA, PFBA**, etc.: shorter- or longer-chain variants with similar properties.  
- **Fluoropolymers** (e.g., PTFE/Teflon) are technically PFAS but less bioavailable.  

---

## 3. **Industrial Uses**
PFAS have been used since the 1950s because they are:  
- **Water-resistant**  
- **Oil/grease-resistant**  
- **Heat-stable**  

Common applications:  
- Nonstick cookware (Teflon)  
- Waterproof clothing (Gore-Tex)  
- Food packaging (pizza boxes, burger wrappers)  
- Firefighting foams (AFFF)  
- Cosmetics, insect repellents, medical devices, paints  

---

## 4. **Environmental Fate**
- Extremely **persistent**: do not degrade under sunlight, microbes, or typical chemical processes.  
- **Mobile**: dissolve in water, spread globally (detected in Arctic ice, rainwater).

# 1. **Chemical Form of PFAS**

## Core Structure

## Properties
- **Hydrophobic tail (fluorocarbon chain)**: repels water and oil, chemically inert.  
- **Hydrophilic head (acidic group)**: makes PFAS soluble and able to bind proteins.  

This amphiphilic structure is why PFAS act like **super-detergents** and also why they cross membranes, bind albumin, and mimic natural fatty acids.

PFAS are not random toxins; they *mimic nature* in key ways:

  - Bind to **fatty acid–binding proteins (FABPs)**.  
  - Activate **PPARα and PPARγ nuclear receptors** (which normally regulate lipid metabolism, insulin sensitivity, and inflammation).  
  - Disrupt mitochondrial **β-oxidation** by competing with acyl-CoA derivatives.  

🔑 This is how PFAS cause **dyslipidemia, fatty liver, and insulin resistance** → directly tying into your PhD domain.

### **B. Sulfonate/Phosphate Mimicry**
- PFOS has a **sulfonate group** (–SO₃⁻), chemically similar in charge distribution to **phosphate (–PO₄²⁻)** groups.  
- This allows PFOS to **interfere with phospholipid membranes**, signaling cascades, and transporters.  
- It can integrate into **cell membranes**, altering fluidity and ion channel activity.  

🔑 This contributes to **neurotoxicity, endocrine disruption, and altered cell signaling**.

### **C. Albumin Binding**
- Natural fatty acids bind tightly to **serum albumin** for transport.  
- PFAS (esp. PFOS, PFOA) **bind even more strongly** → displacing natural ligands (hormones, drugs, fatty acids).  
- This alters hormone transport (e.g., thyroid, sex steroids) and drug pharmacokinetics.
