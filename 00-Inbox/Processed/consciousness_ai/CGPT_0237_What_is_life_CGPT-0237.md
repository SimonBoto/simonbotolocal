Life is a characteristic that distinguishes physical entities with biological processes from those that do not have such processes. From a biological perspective, life is defined by a set of characteristics that include:

- **Organization**: Living organisms have complex and organized structures, starting from cells—the basic unit of life.

- **Metabolism**: They undergo chemical processes to transform energy from the environment into forms usable for cellular activities.

- **Growth and Development**: Living beings grow and develop according to specific instructions coded in their DNA.

- **Adaptation**: Through evolution, life adapts to its environment over generations.

- **Response to Stimuli**: Organisms can respond to environmental changes or stimuli.

- **Reproduction**: They have the ability to produce new individual organisms, either sexually or asexually.

Philosophically, the question "What is life?" delves into the meaning and purpose of existence. Different cultures, religions, and philosophical traditions offer varied interpretations, ranging from life as a journey toward a specific goal to life as an experience to be valued in itself.

In the scientific community, defining life can be complex, especially when considering entities like viruses, which exhibit some but not all characteristics of life. The study of life and living organisms is known as biology, which explores the structure, function, growth, origin, evolution, and distribution of living things.

Overall, life encompasses the processes and qualities that set living beings apart from inanimate matter, involving both physical and, for some, existential dimensions.
