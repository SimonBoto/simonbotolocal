The current dietary guidelines in the United States, as outlined by the USDA and the Department of Health and Human Services (HHS), are presented through the MyPlate model, which replaced the food pyramid in 2011. MyPlate is designed to provide a simpler, more intuitive guide for healthy eating by visually dividing a plate into four sections: fruits, vegetables, grains, and protein, with a side of dairy.

### Key Elements of MyPlate:
1. **Vegetables**: A significant portion of the plate, emphasizing the importance of a variety of vegetables.
2. **Fruits**: Encourages whole fruits, making up about a quarter of the plate.
3. **Grains**: Stresses the importance of whole grains, with half of the grains consumed being whole grains.
4. **Protein**: Includes a variety of protein sources, such as lean meats, poultry, seafood, beans, and nuts.
5. **Dairy**: Recommends low-fat or fat-free dairy options.

### Harvard's Healthy Eating Plate:
Harvard's T.H. Chan School of Public Health introduced the Healthy Eating Plate as an alternative to MyPlate, aiming to provide more specific and detailed nutritional guidance based on current scientific evidence.

### Key Differences of Harvard's Healthy Eating Plate:
1. **Vegetables**: Emphasizes a larger portion of vegetables, highlighting the importance of variety and the benefits of consuming more non-starchy vegetables.
2. **Fruits**: Similar to MyPlate but with a stronger emphasis on a variety of colors and whole fruits over fruit juices.
3. **Whole Grains**: Places a stronger emphasis on whole grains, explicitly discouraging refined grains.
4. **Healthy Proteins**: Recommends healthy protein sources such as fish, poultry, beans, and nuts, while advising limited red meat and avoiding processed meats.
5. **Healthy Fats**: Includes a separate section for healthy fats, advising the use of oils such as olive and canola oil and avoiding trans fats.
6. **Water**: Recommends water as the primary beverage, suggesting limited dairy intake and minimal consumption of sugary drinks.
7. **Physical Activity**: Incorporates physical activity into the plate's visual guide, emphasizing the importance of an active lifestyle.

### Differences from the Classical Food Pyramid:
1. **Simplified Visual**: Both MyPlate and Harvard's Healthy Eating Plate offer a more straightforward and less hierarchical visual guide compared to the layered food pyramid.
2. **Whole Foods Focus**: Both current models place a stronger emphasis on whole foods and healthy eating patterns, moving away from the high carbohydrate, low-fat emphasis of the original food pyramid.
3. **Healthy Fats and Proteins**: There's a greater emphasis on the quality of fats and proteins, highlighting the benefits of healthy fats and recommending varied, lean protein sources.
4. **Reduced Refined Grains and Sugars**: Both models advise minimizing refined grains and added sugars, which contrasts with the earlier pyramid's broader categorization of all carbohydrates.

### Conclusion:
The shift from the food pyramid to MyPlate and the introduction of Harvard's Healthy Eating Plate reflect a growing understanding of nutrition science. These newer models focus on promoting whole, unprocessed foods, healthy fats, and balanced meals, aiming to provide clearer and more actionable dietary guidance to improve public health outcomes.
