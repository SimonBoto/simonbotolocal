5. **Storage and Release**: The iodinated thyroglobulin is stored in the follicles of the thyroid gland. When the body requires thyroid hormones, thyroglobulin is taken back into the thyroid cells and digested by enzymes, releasing T4 (and T3) into the bloodstream.

6. **Conversion of T4 to T3**: In peripheral tissues, T4 can be converted to the more active T3 (triiodothyronine) through the removal of an iodine atom. This conversion is mainly catalyzed by deiodinase enzymes.

### 54. **ChatGPT:**

Bile salts and bile acids are key components of bile, playing a critical role in the digestive process. Here's a more detailed look at these substances:

1. **Bile Acids**
   - Bile acids are synthesized in the liver from cholesterol. The primary bile acids in humans are cholic acid and chenodeoxycholic acid. 
   - They are amphipathic molecules, meaning they have both hydrophobic (water-repelling) and hydrophilic (water-attracting) parts. This unique structure allows them to interact with both fats and water.

2. **Bile Salts**
   - Bile salts are formed when bile acids are conjugated (chemically bonded) with amino acids, like glycine or taurine, in the liver. This conjugation increases their solubility in the intestinal environment.
   - The main bile salts are glycocholate and taurocholate, derived from cholic acid, and glycochenodeoxycholate and taurochenodeoxycholate, derived from chenodeoxycholic acid.

3. **Role in Digestion**
   - Bile salts and acids play a crucial role in the digestion and absorption of fats and fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) in the small intestine.
   - They are secreted into the small intestine in response to the ingestion of food, especially fats.

4. **Emulsification**
   - Emulsification is the process by which large fat droplets are broken down into smaller droplets. It's a crucial step in fat digestion.
   - Bile salts and acids facilitate this by surrounding fat droplets. Their hydrophobic sides interact with the fats, and their hydrophilic sides interact with the aqueous environment in the intestine.
   - This action creates a stable emulsion, increasing the surface area of the fats, making them more accessible to pancreatic lipases, the enzymes that break down fats.

5. **Importance of Emulsification**
   - Emulsification is essential because it allows enzymes to work more effectively. Without it, the fat droplets would be too large for enzymes to act upon efficiently.
   - By increasing the surface area of fats, emulsification speeds up lipid digestion, ensuring that fats and fat-soluble vitamins are adequately absorbed.

6. **Recycling of Bile Salts**
   - After participating in fat digestion, most bile salts are reabsorbed in the ileum of the small intestine and returned to the liver via the hepatic portal vein.
   - They are then re-secreted in bile, a process known as enterohepatic circulation. This efficient recycling means only a small fraction of bile salts needs to be synthesized each day.

The process of emulsification by bile salts and acids is a key aspect of the digestive system, enabling the body to efficiently process and absorb dietary fats and vital fat-soluble vitamins.
